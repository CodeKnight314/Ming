# **Transgender Representation in Middle Eastern and North African Cinema: Visibility, Theory, and Context**

## **I. Introduction: Navigating Visibility and Silence**

The cinematic landscape of the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) presents a complex and often challenging terrain for the representation of transgender lives and experiences. Analyzing these representations is critically important, not only for understanding the evolving dynamics of gender and sexuality within the region but also for appreciating the role cinema plays in reflecting, shaping, and sometimes contesting socio-political realities. This field is marked by significant silences, pervasive censorship challenges, and the slow emergence of often courageous cinematic voices that seek to articulate identities frequently marginalized or rendered invisible.1 A critical examination must move beyond surface-level depictions to explore the intricate interplay between identity construction, cultural specificities, political constraints, and the unique affordances of cinematic form. This paper aims to provide a theoretically grounded overview of transgender themes, characters, and narratives within MENA cinema, culminating in detailed analyses of three distinct films that illuminate different facets of this representational history.

The landscape of transgender representation in MENA film is characterized by its relative scarcity, particularly when compared to the cinematic treatment of gay and lesbian narratives, which, despite often relying on stereotypes or coded language, have a longer history of visibility.1 Explicitly transgender stories have emerged more tentatively and often face significant hurdles related to production, distribution, and reception.1 Historically, queer representation frequently relied on coded signifiers – specific ways of dressing, speaking, or behaving – to imply non-normative identities without explicit declaration, a strategy sometimes employed to navigate censorship but also, problematically, used to link queerness with villainy or comedy.1 Documentary and experimental film forms have often proven crucial in addressing transgender themes, perhaps offering avenues less constrained by commercial or state pressures.6 Furthermore, the transnational nature of much contemporary MENA filmmaking, involving diaspora creators, international funding, and festival circuits, plays a significant role.1 While this can enable the production of challenging works, it also exposes filmmakers to accusations of catering to Western expectations or distorting local realities for international audiences.2

To navigate this complex terrain, this paper employs a dual theoretical framework. Transgender studies provides essential concepts for understanding identity formation, the lived experience of embodiment, the mechanisms of social and political marginalization, and the critique of dominant, often pathologizing, narratives.10 Key thinkers such as Sandy Stone, Viviane Namaste, Judith Butler, Julia Serano, Susan Stryker, and Jack Halberstam offer invaluable tools for analyzing how transgender identities are constructed, experienced, and represented.10 Complementing this, film theory offers methodologies for analyzing cinematic representation, narrative structures, spectatorship, and the power dynamics inherent in the act of looking, particularly through concepts like Laura Mulvey's 'male gaze'.19 These theoretical lenses will be used synergistically to interpret not only the content of the films but also their formal strategies and their engagement with specific socio-cultural contexts.

This paper argues that MENA films engaging with transgender themes navigate restrictive socio-political environments through diverse cinematic strategies, often revealing profound tensions between individual embodied experience, prescriptive societal norms, the weight of tradition, and the complex politics of visibility. By examining the historical trajectory and theoretical underpinnings of representation, and through in-depth analysis of three distinct case studies – Mohamed Soueid's early documentary *Cinema Fouad* (1993, Lebanon), Randa Maroufi's experimental reimagining *The Great Safae* (2014, France/Morocco), and Ahmad Alyaseer's contemporary drama *Our Males and Females* (2023, Jordan) – this paper seeks to illuminate the nuances, challenges, and critical potential of transgender representation in MENA cinema.6 These films are selected for their historical positioning, generic diversity, and the specific ways they engage with transgender identity, embodiment, and social context.

## **II. Theoretical Foundations: Lenses for Analysis**

Understanding transgender representation in MENA cinema requires engaging with theoretical frameworks developed within both trans studies and film theory. These fields provide critical tools for analyzing identity, embodiment, power dynamics, and the specific ways cinema constructs meaning and engages spectators.

### **A. Trans Studies Frameworks**

Transgender studies emerged as a distinct academic field in the early 1990s, moving beyond medical or purely sociological models to center the experiences, histories, and cultural productions of transgender and gender-variant people.11

Identity, Embodiment, and Erasure:  
A foundational moment in the field is often traced to Sandy Stone's 1987 essay "The Empire Strikes Back: A Posttranssexual Manifesto".10 Stone critiqued existing medical and autobiographical narratives that often forced trans individuals into predefined scripts, arguing instead for self-defined expression rooted in the specificities of lived embodiment.10 This emphasis on embodiment – the lived, subjective experience of the body as a source of identity and knowledge – became central to trans theory.10 Trans studies foregrounds how identity is integrated through physical experience, self-construction, and social interaction, challenging purely social constructivist views that might neglect the material reality of the body.13 Embodiment involves processing information through senses, affects, emotions, and interactions with the environment.25  
This focus on embodiment gains political significance when juxtaposed with Viviane Namaste's concept of "erasure".10 In *Invisible Lives*, Namaste argues that transgender people are often rendered invisible not by medical production but by the denial of their existence and experiences within various institutional and cultural settings, including academic theory, healthcare, and legal systems.10 This erasure operates at micro-levels, through discourse and taken-for-granted practices.26 Namaste's work highlights the need to analyze how media representations (or their absence) contribute to this systemic invisibility, particularly in contexts like the MENA region where legal and social structures actively deny or pathologize trans identities.28 In such environments, cinematic representations that center the lived, physical reality of trans individuals, even if coded or fragmented, can function as acts of resistance against this erasure. The very act of depicting trans embodiment challenges the systemic denial inherent in many legal and social frameworks across the region.

Performativity and Challenging Binaries:  
Judith Butler's theory of gender performativity, though originating in queer theory more broadly, profoundly impacted trans studies.11 Butler posits that gender is not an internal essence but is constituted through the stylized repetition of acts, gestures, and behaviors regulated by social norms.11 Gender is something one does, rather than something one is. This framework is crucial for trans studies because it deconstructs the notion of a fixed gender binary tied inherently to assigned sex at birth, opening space for understanding gender identity as fluid and socially constructed.11 It validates transgender identities by showing how all gender is performed and maintained through citation of existing norms, allowing for the possibility of subversive or alternative performances.11  
This resonates with Jack Halberstam's work, particularly *Female Masculinity*, which decouples masculinity from male bodies.11 By examining diverse expressions of masculinity in female-assigned bodies (including butch lesbians and trans men), Halberstam further challenges binary constraints and offers ways to analyze characters who defy conventional gender presentations.18

Critiquing Dominant Narratives: The 'Wrong Body' and Transmisogyny:  
Trans studies actively critiques dominant, often harmful, narratives about transgender experience. One pervasive trope is the "wrong body" narrative, which frames trans identity as a misalignment between an internal self and an external body, often emphasizing suffering and the necessity of medical intervention to achieve "authenticity".12 Critics argue this narrative can pathologize trans lives, reinforce a binary model of gender, impose a standardized script on diverse experiences, and overlook possibilities for affirmative embodiment and resistance.12 Susan Stryker, for instance, explores how figures deemed "monstrous" by society can embody resistance to normative structures.12  
Julia Serano introduced the crucial concept of "transmisogyny" to name the specific intersection of transphobia and misogyny directed against trans women and trans feminine individuals.11 Serano argues this stems from a broader societal devaluation of femininity ("traditional sexism") coupled with the rigid enforcement of gender boundaries ("oppositional sexism").14 Transmisogyny explains why trans women often face unique forms of hostility, ridicule, and violence, being seen as embodying a devalued femininity or as "deceitful" men.14 This concept is vital for analyzing how trans feminine characters are represented and the specific prejudices they might encounter on screen and within the narrative.

Filmmakers attempting to create positive or nuanced representations navigate a complex space. Striving to avoid the "wrong body" narrative might still inadvertently reproduce transmisogynistic stereotypes if trans femininity is depicted negatively or solely through suffering.14 Conversely, focusing only on successful binary transitions might erase non-binary or gender non-conforming realities, falling into the trap of erasure highlighted by Namaste.10 This highlights the inherent difficulties in achieving "positive" representation within existing power structures and narrative conventions.

### **B. Film Theory Perspectives**

Film theory provides tools to analyze how meaning is constructed visually and narratively, and how films position spectators.

Representation, Power, and the Politics of Visibility:  
Film is understood not merely as entertainment but as an "advanced representation system" 21 that reflects, reinforces, and sometimes challenges societal power structures and ideologies.35 Choices about representation – who gets seen, how they are portrayed, whose stories are told – are inherently political.19 For marginalized groups, media representation (or misrepresentation) significantly impacts social perception and legitimacy.37 Transgender individuals have historically faced problematic media portrayals, often characterized by stereotypes (villains, victims, objects of ridicule, "tricksters"), pathologization, sensationalism, or complete invisibility.12 Accurate, complex, and humanizing representation is thus crucial not only for cultural understanding but also for advancing social justice claims.37  
Spectatorship, Identification, and the Gaze:  
Film theory explores spectatorship – the act and experience of viewing films.19 Identification, the process by which viewers relate to or project themselves onto characters, is a key mechanism of engagement.35 Laura Mulvey's highly influential 1975 essay, "Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema," published in Screen, utilized psychoanalysis to argue that classical Hollywood cinema structures visual pleasure primarily around a heterosexual male perspective – the "male gaze".19 This gaze typically positions the male protagonist as the active bearer of the look and controller of the narrative, while the female character is presented as a passive object of desire, embodying "to-be-looked-at-ness".20 Mulvey identified three interrelated looks that construct this dynamic: the look of the camera, the look of the characters within the diegesis, and the look of the spectator, who is encouraged to identify with the male protagonist's perspective.23  
While Mulvey's theory has been critiqued and expanded to account for female spectatorship, queer desire, masochism, and different genres 22, the concept of the gaze remains a powerful tool for analyzing power dynamics in visual representation. When applied to transgender characters, the gaze can become even more complex. Are trans characters subjected to a controlling, objectifying gaze similar to Mulvey's formulation? Does this gaze intersect with a medical or pathologizing scrutiny, focusing on the body as a site of difference or "abnormality"? Is there potential for an alternative, empathetic, or even "oppositional" gaze?41 Furthermore, the "imperial gaze," which represents marginalized groups from a dominant cultural perspective, often perpetuating stereotypes, is relevant when considering Western filmmakers or audiences viewing MENA subjects.40

The cinematic gaze directed at trans bodies, particularly trans feminine ones, can thus become a site where scopophilic pleasure potentially intersects with transphobia, medical curiosity, and the transmisogyny identified by Serano.14 This intensifies the objectification, as the act of looking is laden with societal anxieties and power dynamics that go beyond the heterosexual matrix Mulvey initially described. The scrutiny might focus on perceived "authenticity," signs of transition, or conformity to binary norms, making the trans body a target of a multifaceted and often hostile gaze.

Narrative Analysis in MENA and Queer Cinema:  
Narrative analysis examines how stories are constructed, whose perspectives are privileged, and how plot structures shape meaning and audience understanding.19 While generalizations are difficult, MENA cinema often engages with themes of family, tradition versus modernity, social pressures, national identity, and post-colonial legacies.3 Queer narratives, globally and within the MENA region, frequently disrupt conventional storytelling by centering non-normative desires, exploring chosen families, depicting experiences of marginalization and resistance, or employing coded language to navigate hostile environments.1 Analyzing the narrative strategies used in films with transgender themes – whether they adopt mainstream conventions, employ coded signification, or utilize experimental structures – is crucial for understanding how these stories are told and what meanings they produce.

## **III. Mapping the Terrain: Transgender Representation in MENA Cinema \- A Broad Overview**

Charting the representation of transgender individuals in MENA cinema reveals a landscape shaped by historical silence, socio-political constraints, and recent, albeit tentative, moves towards greater visibility.

### **A. Historical Trajectories: Erasure, Coding, and Emergence**

Historically, explicit transgender characters and narratives have been largely absent from mainstream MENA cinema. When non-normative gender expressions appeared, they were often subsumed under broader, frequently negative, portrayals of homosexuality or used for comedic effect, reinforcing harmful stereotypes.3 Early Egyptian cinema, for instance, sometimes depicted gay characters as sexual predators or psychologically disturbed individuals.3 While pioneering directors like Youssef Chahine included coded gay representations (e.g., suggestive glances, shared dances) 1, these often operated within the realm of "connotative homosexuality," relying on signifiers like dress, speech, or behavior rather than explicit identification.1 This coding served partly as a strategy to circumvent censorship but could also perpetuate ambiguity or link queerness to negative traits.4

The post-millennial era has witnessed a gradual shift, particularly within independent filmmaking circles, towards more direct and nuanced explorations of queer lives, although transgender themes often appear later or less frequently than gay and lesbian stories.3 Films began to move away from depicting queerness solely as pathology or deviance.3 Countries perceived as having relatively more open social environments, such as Lebanon, became early sites for the emergence of a more visible queer cinema, creating space for filmmakers to tackle previously taboo subjects, although significant challenges remain.3

### **B. Socio-Political and Legal Constraints**

The production and reception of films dealing with transgender themes in the MENA region are profoundly shaped by a challenging legal and social environment.28 Across most of the region, there is a significant lack of clear legal frameworks for legal gender recognition (LGR), the process by which individuals can change their name and gender marker on official documents to align with their gender identity.28 This legislative vacuum forces trans individuals seeking LGR into often lengthy, costly, and arbitrary judicial processes where outcomes depend heavily on individual judges' interpretations of law, medical reports, and often, religious principles or personal biases.29

Many countries retain laws inherited from colonial periods or based on conservative interpretations of Islamic law that criminalize vaguely defined acts related to "public morality," "indecency," or "imitating the opposite sex".28 Security forces often conflate gender identity with sexual orientation and use these laws to harass, arbitrarily arrest, and prosecute transgender people, particularly trans women.28 In detention, trans individuals face misgendering, placement in facilities not corresponding to their gender identity, solitary confinement, and are vulnerable to ill-treatment, torture, and sexual violence.29

Furthermore, a problematic distinction, often influenced by religious fatwas, is frequently made in legal and medical spheres between "sex correction" surgery for intersex individuals (often deemed permissible or necessary) and "sex change" surgery for transgender individuals (often deemed impermissible).28 This distinction directly impacts access to gender-affirming healthcare and can be used by courts to deny LGR, as seen in Egypt and the UAE.28 Countries like Egypt, Jordan, Oman, Morocco, and the UAE have implemented varying degrees of restrictions on gender-affirming surgeries based on this logic.28 Even in Lebanon, where a positive court ruling allowed LGR for a trans man in 2016, the process remains difficult, often requiring expensive surgery (sometimes sought abroad) and facing judicial discretion.29 Tunisia similarly lacks clear pathways for LGR and gender-affirming care, and trans people face arrest under laws criminalizing same-sex relations or "indecency".28

Beyond the legal sphere, pervasive social discrimination affects nearly every aspect of life for transgender individuals, including employment, housing, healthcare access, and family relations.28 Fear of violence from family members (including honor-based violence) or the public (mob attacks, harassment) is a constant reality for many.47 The mismatch between official documents and gender expression exacerbates these vulnerabilities.29 Adding another layer of threat, authorities in several MENA countries increasingly use digital means – monitoring social media, using dating apps for entrapment, and exploiting illegitimately obtained digital information – to target, harass, extort, and prosecute LGBTQ+ individuals.48

The absence of accessible LGR pathways creates a particularly difficult situation. Without documents reflecting their gender identity, trans people are constantly vulnerable to exposure, discrimination in accessing basic services like jobs and healthcare, and arbitrary arrest under various "morality" or "imitation" laws.28 However, the path to obtaining LGR, where it exists at all, often involves navigating opaque and biased judicial systems or undergoing costly and potentially unwanted medical procedures, including surgeries that may not be accessible or affordable locally.29 This legal and procedural impasse traps many trans individuals in a state of heightened precarity, a crucial backdrop for understanding the challenges often depicted or implied in cinematic narratives.

### **C. The Role of Documentary, Experimental Film, and Transnational Factors**

Given the constraints of censorship and potential social backlash, documentary and experimental film forms have often served as vital spaces for exploring transgender lives and themes in the MENA region.6 Documentaries like *Cinema Fouad* 6 or *A Jihad for Love* (which includes trans Muslim experiences 53) offer platforms for testimony and visibility, albeit sometimes mediated through the filmmaker's lens and facing challenges of anonymity or safety for participants.56 Experimental films like *The Great Safae* 6 utilize non-realist aesthetics to engage with memory, identity, and social critique in ways that might circumvent the limitations of direct narrative representation.

The influence of transnational connections is undeniable. Many filmmakers addressing queer and transgender themes work in diaspora or rely on international co-production funding, often from European sources.1 This support can be crucial for realizing projects that might not find backing domestically. However, it also opens avenues for criticism, with some arguing that Western funding encourages portrayals that fit preconceived notions or cater to an "imperial gaze," potentially misrepresenting or exoticizing local realities.2 International film festivals also play a critical role, providing essential platforms for exhibition, circulation, and critical discussion for films that may face censorship or limited distribution in their home countries.9 Regional initiatives like the Mawjoudin Queer Film Festival in Tunisia also contribute significantly to fostering local queer cinematic expression.9

### **A. *Cinema Fouad* (1993, Lebanon): Archiving a Life**

**Production Context & Synopsis:** Directed by Lebanese filmmaker Mohamed Soueid, *Cinema Fouad* (1993) stands as a significant early example of documentary filmmaking in the region focusing on a transgender subject.7 The film presents a portrait of Khaled El Kurdi, a Syrian trans woman who migrated to Beirut.6 Using techniques associated with cinéma vérité, Soueid follows Khaled in her daily life, documenting her work as a domestic servant and belly dancer, her interactions, and her candid reflections on her experiences and her expressed desire for gender reassignment surgery.6 The film captures the duality of her existence, living as a woman at home and often navigating public spaces as a man.64 It emerged in the context of post-civil war Lebanon, a period of social flux and reconstruction.

**Theoretical Analysis (Trans Studies):** *Cinema Fouad* directly confronts the potential for erasure identified by Namaste 10 by bringing Khaled's marginalized life into visibility. The film centers her embodied experience 10, showing her navigating the complexities of gender presentation in different social spheres.64 This focus on the lived reality of a trans individual in this specific time and place offers a counter-narrative to societal invisibility. However, questions arise regarding agency and self-narrative, central to Stone's call for posttranssexual expression.10 While Khaled speaks openly, the extent to which she controls her portrayal within the cinéma vérité framework remains ambiguous. Her desire for surgery 6 could be interpreted through the lens of the "wrong body" narrative 12, though the film may present it simply as a personal aspiration without necessarily endorsing the pathologizing aspects of that trope. Analyzing her experiences through Serano's concept of transmisogyny 14 could also illuminate potential prejudices faced due to her gender expression, particularly in her interactions and work.

**Theoretical Analysis (Film Theory):** The film's cinéma vérité style 62 raises ethical questions about the power dynamic between the filmmaker (Soueid) and the subject (Khaled). How does the camera's observational stance construct the spectator's gaze?19 Is the audience positioned as an empathetic witness, a detached observer, or potentially drawn into a form of voyeurism given the intimate nature of the subject matter? The film's narrative structure is primarily that of a portrait, weaving together scenes from Khaled's life rather than imposing a strong plot.62 This structure allows for a focus on character and experience but also relies heavily on the filmmaker's selection and editing choices.

**Socio-Cultural Reading:** *Cinema Fouad* must be understood within the specific context of early 1990s post-war Beirut, a city grappling with trauma and rebuilding. Khaled's positionality is intersectional: she is not only transgender but also a Syrian migrant and a working-class individual employed in domestic labor and entertainment.6 These factors likely shape her experiences of marginalization and resilience. The film's existence itself was a notable event, representing a rare instance of transgender visibility in Arab cinema at that time.7

As an early work of documentary representation, *Cinema Fouad* functions as a complex archive of a specific trans life. Its significance lies not just in making Khaled visible but also in the questions it raises about the nature of that visibility. The cinéma vérité approach, while aiming for immediacy, inevitably involves framing and mediation by the filmmaker. This tension between providing a platform for a marginalized voice (countering erasure 10) and the potential for objectification inherent in the documentary gaze highlights the ethical complexities of representing trans lives, particularly in pioneering works within restrictive contexts.

### **B. *The Great Safae* (2014, France/Morocco): Reimagining Identity**

**Production Context & Synopsis:** Directed by Randa Maroufi, a Moroccan filmmaker based in France whose work often explores politics and gender 61, *The Great Safae* (2014) is an experimental documentary short.6 The film is inspired by the director's memory of a transgender woman, known as Safae, who worked as a domestic servant for Maroufi's family in Casablanca for a period, with the family reportedly unaware of her gender identity at the time.6 Rather than attempting a traditional biographical account, Maroufi uses actors and non-actors to reimagine scenes from Safae's daily routine and her transition, blending these stylized reconstructions with snippets of recorded family conversations discussing societal views on gender non-conformity.6 The film was produced through Le Fresnoy \- Studio National des Arts Contemporains in France.57

**Theoretical Analysis (Trans Studies):** The film directly engages with the concept of erasure 10 by attempting to reconstruct and give presence to a life lived largely in concealment within the domestic sphere. It explores gender performance 11 through its stylized reenactments, questioning how identity is constructed and perceived, particularly when hidden. The narrative highlights the intersection of gender identity with class and labor, positioning Safae within the specific context of domestic work.6 The fragmented nature of the representation, relying on memory and imagination, implicitly acknowledges the difficulty of accessing an "authentic" trans past, perhaps aligning with critiques of simplistic identity narratives. While the sources use terms like "transvestite" and "trans woman" 6, applying Halberstam's work on diverse masculinities 18 might be relevant depending on the specifics of Safae's reimagined presentation, though the primary focus seems to be on her hidden female identity.

**Theoretical Analysis (Film Theory):** The film's experimental documentary form 57 is central to its meaning. By eschewing realism in favor of staged scenes and fragmented memories, Maroufi foregrounds the constructed nature of representation itself. This approach prompts a different kind of spectatorship 19 than traditional documentary, potentially encouraging critical distance and reflection on the processes of memory and social perception rather than straightforward identification. The director's personal connection to the subject matter (Safae worked for her family 6) introduces a complex layer to the gaze – it is simultaneously intimate and mediated, potentially recuperative but also shaped by the family's past unawareness. The visual style and sound design contribute significantly to the film's affective and interpretive dimensions.

**Socio-Cultural Reading:** The film offers a lens onto Moroccan societal attitudes towards gender non-conformity, particularly within the private, domestic space of the family.6 Safae's hidden identity speaks volumes about the potential risks and lack of acceptance faced by transgender individuals. The film's production context (France/Morocco) 57 also reflects the transnational dimensions of contemporary MENA filmmaking and the perspectives offered by diaspora artists engaging with their heritage.

The choice of an experimental, reimagined format in *The Great Safae* can be interpreted as an ethically conscious response to the inherent challenges of representing a historical figure whose own voice is absent and whose life was marked by concealment. Instead of claiming an impossible authenticity through traditional documentary methods, Maroufi employs stylized performance and fragmented recollections 6 to explore the *themes* surrounding Safae's life – gender identity, labor, social invisibility, memory – while acknowledging the mediation involved. This resonates with post-structuralist critiques of naive realism in film and echoes Sandy Stone's call for new forms of trans expression 10, even as the narrative is constructed by a non-trans director grappling with a family memory. The form itself becomes part of the commentary on visibility and the limits of representation.

### **C. *Our Males and Females* (2023, Jordan): Rituals and Reckoning**

**Production Context & Synopsis:** Directed by Jordanian filmmaker Ahmad Alyaseer, *Our Males and Females* (2023) is a short drama that confronts the intersection of transgender identity, family grief, and religious tradition.6 The film portrays the agonizing dilemma faced by the parents of a deceased transgender woman in Jordan as they prepare for her burial according to Islamic rites.6 A central conflict arises when community members refuse to perform the ritual washing of the body, forcing the father to confront his own grief, societal judgment, and his relationship with his child's identity.6 The director has stated the film was inspired by the real-life struggles of transgender individuals in the Middle East, like Bousi, who faced discrimination even after legal transition, prompting the question of whether acceptance is denied even in death.67 Alyaseer's stated aim is to provoke discussion, challenge cultural taboos, and foster empathy, with plans potentially to develop the short into a feature exploring the daughter's life more fully.67

**Theoretical Analysis (Trans Studies):** The film powerfully illustrates the persistence of transphobia and the struggle for recognition extending even beyond death, embedding this conflict within the specific framework of religious ritual.66 A key theme is erasure 10: the transgender daughter is the narrative's focal point, yet she is physically absent, her identity contested and negotiated by the living. Her absence underscores the societal forces that seek to deny or invalidate her existence. The film foregrounds the clash between deeply held religious/cultural traditions (burial rites) and the reality of transgender identity, forcing a confrontation within the family unit.66 The father's internal conflict and ultimate action 6 can be analyzed in terms of challenging or potentially reconfiguring patriarchal expectations around duty, honor, and acceptance. Applying Butler's concept of performativity 11 to the burial ritual itself reveals how these acts attempt to definitively assign and fix gender onto the body, making the refusal or insistence on performing the ritual a highly charged political act.

**Theoretical Analysis (Film Theory):** The narrative structure is notable for centering the experience and dilemma of the cisgender parents, particularly the father, rather than depicting the life of the transgender daughter directly.6 This strategy likely aims to engage a broader audience by focusing on relatable themes of grief and familial duty, forcing cisgender spectators to confront the implications of acceptance or rejection from the parents' perspective.19 The film's tone is described as harrowing and painful 66, employing pathos to underscore the injustice faced. The mise-en-scène – the setting of the home, the interactions around the body – visually embodies the tension between tradition, personal grief, and the contested identity of the deceased.

**Socio-Cultural Reading:** The film is explicitly set within a Jordanian and Islamic cultural context, engaging directly with the specific requirements and social significance of burial rites.66 It highlights the power of community judgment and the immense pressure faced by families navigating non-normative identities within conservative social structures. The director's intention to tackle taboos 67 positions the film as a form of social commentary aimed at fostering dialogue within the region.

*Our Males and Females* makes a significant intervention by shifting the narrative focus from the lived experience of transition to the societal and familial reckoning with transgender identity *after* death. This strategic choice underscores the deeply entrenched nature of gender policing, demonstrating how it permeates even the most sacred cultural and religious rituals.66 By staging the conflict within the specific context of Islamic burial practices, the film leverages cultural resonance to make a powerful statement about the enduring fight for recognition and the devastating consequences of transphobia. Centering the narrative on the parents' struggle 6 serves as a potent, perhaps confrontational, mirror for cisgender audiences, compelling them to consider their own position within dynamics of acceptance and rejection.

## **V. Synthesis and Conclusion: Intersections and Implications**

The exploration of transgender representation in Middle Eastern and North African cinema reveals a field characterized by profound challenges, innovative strategies, and evolving, albeit slowly, narratives. The overview and detailed analyses of *Cinema Fouad*, *The Great Safae*, and *Our Males and Females* demonstrate the diverse ways filmmakers engage with transgender themes amidst significant socio-political and legal constraints. *Cinema Fouad* provided crucial early visibility through documentary, though framed by the inherent power dynamics of the genre. *The Great Safae* utilized experimental aesthetics to grapple with memory, hidden identity, and the ethics of representing an absent subject. *Our Males and Females* employed narrative drama, focusing on cisgender grief and religious ritual, to confront the persistence of transphobia even after death.

Integrating perspectives from trans studies and film theory illuminates the specificities of these representations. Trans studies concepts like embodiment, erasure, performativity, transmisogyny, and the critique of the 'wrong body' narrative provide frameworks for understanding the lived experiences being depicted (or omitted) and the political stakes involved.10 Film theory, with its attention to the gaze, spectatorship, narrative structure, and genre conventions, allows for analysis of *how* these experiences are mediated cinematically and the power dynamics inherent in the act of representation.19 Using these toolkits together reveals, for example, how the cinematic gaze directed at trans bodies can become intensified through intersections of scopophilia, medical scrutiny, and transmisogyny, or how experimental forms can serve as ethical responses to the impossibility of 'authentic' representation in certain contexts.

A clear interplay exists between the restrictive socio-political realities across much of the MENA region and the cinematic forms chosen by filmmakers. The prevalence of documentary and experimental approaches, alongside coded narratives in fiction, suggests strategies for navigating censorship, expressing dissent indirectly, or fostering empathy through non-traditional means.1 The legal precarity engendered by the lack of LGR and the enforcement of morality laws 28 forms a crucial, often unspoken, context that shapes the narratives of vulnerability, concealment, and the high stakes of visibility. Transnational factors, including funding and festival circuits, undeniably enable some productions but also introduce complex dynamics related to audience expectations and potential accusations of Western influence.2

In conclusion, transgender representation in MENA cinema remains a nascent and contested field. While visibility is slowly increasing, driven by courageous filmmakers often working outside mainstream channels, significant challenges persist. The films analyzed demonstrate a range of approaches, from direct testimony to experimental reconstruction to allegorical drama, each grappling with the complexities of identity, embodiment, and social acceptance in unique ways. The theoretical frameworks of trans studies and film theory are indispensable for critically engaging with these works, understanding their political implications, and recognizing the ways they negotiate the difficult terrain between silence and speech. The future likely lies in the continued emergence of diverse narratives, particularly those driven by transgender creators themselves, offering self-determined representations that move beyond externally imposed scripts or gazes, fulfilling the call for authentic voice central to trans liberation movements.10 The power of cinema to foster empathy and challenge prejudice is undeniable, but in this context, visibility remains both a vital goal and a precarious condition.
