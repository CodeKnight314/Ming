# **Manufacturing Technology Options for Hollow Motor Shafts in New Energy Vehicle (NEV) Electric Drive Units**

## **1\. Introduction and Functional Requirements for NEV Hollow Motor Shafts**

### **1.1 Role and Importance in NEV Electric Drive Units**

The transition towards electrified mobility necessitates continuous innovation in powertrain components to meet demanding performance, efficiency, and cost targets. The electric drive unit (EDU), often referred to as the e-axle or e-drive, stands as a core system in New Energy Vehicles (NEVs), encompassing battery electric vehicles (BEVs), plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (PHEVs), and fuel cell electric vehicles (FCEVs). Modern EDUs increasingly integrate the electric motor, power electronics (inverter), and reduction gearbox into a single, compact housing, often termed a "three-in-one" system. This integration strategy aims to reduce overall weight and volume while enhancing power and torque density.

Within this integrated unit, the motor shaft serves a critical function: transmitting the rotational power and torque generated by the electric motor's rotor to the subsequent stages of the drivetrain, typically a reduction gearbox. Unlike shafts in many industrial applications, NEV motor shafts operate under unique and demanding conditions characterized by high rotational speeds, frequent torque fluctuations, and stringent requirements for efficiency (low loss), low weight, and minimal noise, vibration, and harshness (NVH). The evolution of EDU design, including changes in power characteristics, component configuration (rigidity, inertia), and mounting requirements, places strong emphasis on optimizing every component, including the motor shaft, for safety, reliability, cost-effectiveness, and enhanced performance metrics like reduced energy consumption (loss reduction, lighter weight), improved packaging (smaller size), low NVH, and potential for added functionality. The adoption of hollow shaft designs is a key strategy in addressing these demands, particularly for weight reduction and potentially altering dynamic characteristics.

### **1.2 Key Functional Requirements**

The successful operation of an NEV electric drive unit relies heavily on the motor shaft meeting several critical functional requirements:

* **Torque Transmission:** The primary function is the reliable transmission of torque from the motor rotor. NEV motors, particularly permanent magnet synchronous motors (PMSM) and induction motors commonly used, generate high peak torque for acceleration and substantial continuous torque for cruising. The shaft must withstand these loads without yielding or failing, necessitating careful consideration of torsional and shear stresses during the design phase.  
* **Fatigue Strength:** NEV operation involves highly variable loading conditions, with frequent acceleration, deceleration, and regenerative braking events. This subjects the shaft to high-cycle fatigue loading. Fatigue failure is recognized as a major failure mode for shafts operating under such conditions, often initiating at stress concentration points. Therefore, exceptional fatigue strength and durability are paramount requirements.  
* **Rotational Speed Capability:** Modern NEV electric motors operate at significantly higher speeds compared to their internal combustion engine (ICE) counterparts, with maximum speeds reaching or exceeding 16,000 rpm being increasingly common. The shaft must be designed and manufactured to operate reliably at these speeds, demanding excellent dimensional accuracy, concentricity, and balance to minimize vibrations and ensure stability.  
* **Stiffness and NVH Performance:** The motor shaft contributes to the overall stiffness of the powertrain. Its dynamic characteristics, including resonant frequencies, influence the NVH performance of the entire vehicle. While hollow shafts offer weight benefits, their resonant frequencies differ from solid shafts, requiring careful design and manufacturing control to avoid excitation within the operating speed range and minimize noise transmission. High rigidity is often a desired characteristic for low NVH.  
* **Integration and Geometric Features:** The shaft must seamlessly integrate with surrounding components. This includes precise interfaces for mounting the rotor laminations or magnets, seating bearings, accommodating seals, and connecting to the gearbox input, often via splines or other torque transfer features. The shaft geometry may incorporate steps, varying diameters, internal and external splines, keyways, or specific fillet radii at transitions, which are critical for stress management and fatigue life.

### **1.3 Material Specifications**

Meeting the demanding functional requirements necessitates careful selection of materials with appropriate mechanical and physical properties. Key material specifications include:

* **Strength and Hardness:** High yield strength and ultimate tensile strength (UTS) are essential to handle the applied torques without permanent deformation or fracture. Adequate surface hardness is also required, particularly at bearing journals and seal surfaces, to resist wear. Studies on materials like EN-GJS-500-14 (ductile cast iron, used for fatigue studies) show typical UTS around 450 MPa and yield strength around 350 MPa. Research on SCr420 steel shafts formed by radial forging demonstrated how the process influences tensile strength and hardness distribution.1 High strength allows for potentially smaller cross-sections, contributing to weight reduction.  
* **Fatigue Life:** Given the cyclic nature of the loads, a high fatigue endurance limit is arguably one of the most critical material properties. The inherent fatigue strength of the chosen material is crucial, but it is significantly influenced by the manufacturing process. Factors like grain flow direction, microstructure refinement, surface finish quality, and the presence of residual stresses play a major role.1 Heat treatments (quenching, tempering, carburizing, nitriding) and surface strengthening processes (rolling friction, shot peening) are commonly employed to enhance fatigue resistance.  
* **Weight Targets (Lightweighting):** Reducing vehicle mass is a primary objective in NEV design to extend driving range, improve handling, and enhance overall efficiency. The motor shaft, while needing to be strong, is a target for weight reduction. Shifting from solid to hollow shafts provides significant weight savings due to the removal of material from the low-stress central region. Further weight reduction is pursued through the use of higher specific strength materials or alternative materials altogether. Even seemingly small weight savings per component can accumulate to substantial vehicle-level benefits; an 18 kg reduction in total vehicle weight was estimated to potentially increase range by 1-2.5 km, or allow for an equivalent weight of additional battery cells, boosting range by 5%. This intense focus on lightweighting drives exploration beyond traditional steels.  
* **Material Candidates:**  
  * **Steels:** Remain a common choice due to their favorable balance of strength, fatigue resistance, toughness, and cost. Various grades are employed, often selected based on hardenability and strength requirements. Examples include medium carbon steels (like No. 45 steel / AISI 1045, noted for good fatigue properties and cost-effectiveness), chromium steels (like SCr420, used in EV shaft forging studies 1), chromium-molybdenum steels (like 30CrMoA used in skew rolling studies 3, or 4130/4140 Chromoly known for high strength-to-weight ratio), manganese steels (like 42SiMn), and other low-alloy steels. The properties of steel shafts are heavily dependent on heat treatment (e.g., normalizing, hardening, tempering 2) and the forming process used (e.g., forging enhances grain flow 1; cold working increases strength).  
  * **Aluminum Alloys:** Offer significant density reduction compared to steel (approx. 1/3rd the density), making them attractive for lightweighting. High-strength alloys like 7075 have been considered for automotive drivetrain components. However, aluminum generally has lower stiffness (Young's modulus) and fatigue strength compared to steel, requiring careful design considerations (e.g., potentially larger diameters to maintain stiffness). Extrusion and drawing are common manufacturing routes for aluminum tubes.  
  * **Magnesium Alloys:** Even lighter than aluminum, magnesium offers further weight reduction potential (up to 25-33% lighter than equivalent aluminum parts). Historically used in aerospace and increasingly in automotive components like transmission cases and seat frames. Challenges include lower strength than steel, potential flammability concerns (though largely addressed by modern alloys), and susceptibility to corrosion, requiring protective coatings. Manufacturing processes would need to be adapted.  
  * **Composite Materials (CFRP):** Carbon Fiber Reinforced Plastics (CFRP) provide exceptional specific strength and stiffness (strength-to-weight and stiffness-to-weight ratios), enabling potentially dramatic weight reductions (up to 50% claimed for hybrid shafts vs. steel). Hollow CFRP shafts benefit from high material utilization due to their geometry. Manufacturing typically involves processes like filament winding or automated fiber placement. Often, CFRP shafts are designed as hybrid components, with metallic end pieces joined to the composite tube to handle load introduction and interfaces. While offering significant performance benefits, CFRP solutions generally involve higher material and manufacturing costs compared to metals and introduce different design considerations regarding failure modes and joining techniques.

The strong drive for lightweighting in NEVs makes the interplay between material selection and manufacturing process particularly critical. Choosing a lighter material like aluminum or CFRP fundamentally changes the viable manufacturing options compared to traditional steel shafts. For instance, aluminum lends itself well to extrusion and drawing, while CFRP necessitates processes like filament winding. This interdependence means that the selection process cannot consider material and manufacturing route in isolation; rather, it must evaluate specific *material-process combinations* against the multi-objective requirements of weight, strength, fatigue life, and cost.

### **1.4 Typical Geometric Characteristics**

While specific dimensions vary widely based on the motor power, EDU architecture, and vehicle platform, NEV hollow motor shafts share common geometric characteristics:

* **Hollow Structure:** The defining feature is the central bore, introduced primarily for weight and inertia reduction. The wall thickness resulting from the bore can be constant along the length or intentionally varied to optimize strength and weight distribution.3 The ratio of outer diameter to wall thickness influences formability and structural behavior.4  
* **Dimensions:** Overall shaft diameter and length are dictated by the motor size (power/torque rating) and the packaging constraints within the integrated EDU. While general industrial motor standards like NEMA and IEC provide dimensional references for shafts based on frame size (e.g., NEMA 140T frame having a 3.5-inch shaft height, NEMA 280T having a 7-inch height), these often relate to solid shafts for broader applications. NEV shafts, being highly optimized, frequently deviate from these standards, often falling under special 'Y' or 'Z' designations indicating non-standard dimensions or features. Manufacturing processes cater to a wide range of potential sizes, with rotary swaging handling hollow parts up to ø120 mm and flow forming capable of diameters up to 48 inches, though typical NEV motor shafts likely fall in the range of perhaps 20 mm to 80 mm outer diameter.  
* **Features:** Beyond the basic hollow cylinder, NEV motor shafts incorporate various functional features. These commonly include:  
  * **Steps:** Changes in outer diameter to accommodate bearings, rotor components, or seals.  
  * **Splines:** Internal or external splines are frequently used for torque transmission to the rotor hub or the gearbox input gear. These can be formed during the primary manufacturing process (e.g., extrusion, forging, flow forming) or machined subsequently.  
  * **Keyways:** Alternative method for torque transmission, though potentially less common than splines in high-torque NEV applications.  
  * **Bearing Journals & Seal Surfaces:** Precisely dimensioned and finished surfaces where bearings and seals interface with the shaft. High accuracy and smooth surface finish are critical here.  
  * **Fillets and Chamfers:** Smooth transitions between different diameters or features are essential to minimize stress concentrations and improve fatigue life.

The highly customized nature of NEV components, driven by the intense focus on optimization, means that standard motor shaft dimensions serve more as a general reference than a strict specification. Manufacturing processes must therefore offer flexibility to produce these custom geometries efficiently, rather than being limited to standard sizes.

### **1.5 Fatigue Performance as a Primary Design Constraint**

The operational profile of an NEV, characterized by rapid torque changes and high rotational speeds, places immense emphasis on fatigue resistance. Fatigue failure is a critical concern for rotating components like shafts. This elevates the importance of manufacturing processes that inherently enhance fatigue performance. Processes like forging, which can create favorable grain flow aligned with stress paths 1, and cold working processes (such as flow forming, rotary swaging, and cold drawing) that induce compressive residual stresses in the surface layers, are particularly advantageous. Compressive residual stresses counteract tensile stresses applied during operation, effectively increasing the fatigue limit. Conversely, processes that might introduce material defects (e.g., porosity, inclusions), unfavorable residual stresses (e.g., tensile stresses from rapid cooling or certain machining operations), or disrupt beneficial grain flow (e.g., machining features across the flow lines of a forging 2) can be detrimental to fatigue life. The precise control of geometric features, especially fillet radii at diameter transitions, is also crucial, as these act as stress concentrators. Therefore, the selection and control of the manufacturing process are not just about achieving the desired shape and dimensions but are fundamentally linked to ensuring the shaft's long-term durability under cyclic loading.

## **2\. Overview of Manufacturing Technologies for Hollow Metal Components**

### **2.1 Survey of Potential Forming Processes**

A broad spectrum of manufacturing processes exists for producing hollow metal components, particularly shafts and tubes. These can be broadly categorized based on the starting material form and the deformation mechanism:

* **Bulk Forming (Starting from Solid Billet/Bloom):** These processes involve significant plastic deformation of a solid starting workpiece to achieve the desired shape, including the internal cavity.  
  * **Forging:** This includes traditional open-die and closed-die forging, as well as specialized techniques relevant to hollow shafts. **Radial Forging** uses multiple hammers acting radially on a rotating workpiece.1 **Hollow Die Punching** involves forging operations followed by punching a central hole within a die cavity to create a near-net hollow shape.2 These are often hot or warm working processes but can have cold variants.  
  * **Rotary Compression / Skew Rolling:** These methods use rotating rolls to progressively reduce the diameter and form features (like steps or gears) on solid or hollow stock.3 They are often suited for axisymmetric parts and can be performed hot or warm.  
* **Tube Manufacturing & Forming (Starting from Tube/Pipe):** These processes begin with a pre-existing tube, which is then further shaped or refined.  
  * **Primary Tube Production:** Creating the initial hollow tube can be done via **Seamless Processes** (piercing a solid billet followed by extrusion and/or drawing 6) or **Welded Processes** (roll forming flat strip into a tube and longitudinally welding the seam using Electric Resistance Welding (ERW) or Laser Beam Welding (LBW) 4).  
  * **Secondary Tube Forming:** Modifying the shape or properties of the pre-formed tube. This includes **Bending** 4, **Tube Hydroforming** (using internal fluid pressure to expand the tube into a die), **Flow Forming** (using rollers to reduce wall thickness and elongate the tube over a mandrel), and **Rotary Swaging** (using hammers to reduce the diameter, often over a mandrel).  
* **Sheet Metal Forming:** Primarily involves shaping thin sheets or plates.  
  * **Deep Drawing:** Forming a flat sheet blank into a hollow shape (like a cup or box) using a punch and die. Typically used for components with relatively thin walls compared to their diameter/length.  
* **Incremental Forming:** Processes that shape the workpiece gradually through localized deformation. Flow forming, rotary swaging, and skew rolling 3 fall into this category.  
* **Advanced/Non-Traditional Methods:** These represent alternative approaches using different materials or forming principles.  
  * **Composite Manufacturing:** Techniques like **Filament Winding**, **Automated Fiber Placement (AFP)**, and **Automated Tape Laying (ATL)** are used for creating hollow components from fiber-reinforced polymers.  
  * **Plastic Molding:** Processes like **Blow Molding** (Extrusion Blow Molding, Injection Blow Molding) and **Injection Molding** (including water-assisted variants for hollow parts) are standard for high-volume plastic components.

### **2.2 Initial Filtering for Automotive Shaft Applications**

Not all processes capable of producing hollow shapes are suitable for the specific demands of NEV motor shafts. A preliminary filtering based on typical applications, material capabilities, and the required performance characteristics (high strength, precision, fatigue resistance) allows for focusing the detailed analysis on the most promising candidates:

* **Likely Excluded/Limited Applicability:**  
  * **Standard Deep Drawing:** While capable of forming hollow shapes from sheet metal, it is generally best suited for thinner-walled components (e.g., automotive body parts, cooking pots) and may struggle to achieve the required wall thickness, precision, and strength for a high-torque motor shaft. Its applicability seems limited, perhaps only for very specific, lower-load shaft designs or integrated features.  
  * **Plastic Molding Techniques:** Processes like blow molding or standard injection molding are unsuitable for the primary torque-transmitting shaft due to the significantly lower strength and stiffness of plastics compared to the required engineering metals (steels, high-strength aluminum). Composites offer a potential alternative, but standard plastics do not meet the mechanical demands.  
* **Retained for Detailed Analysis:**  
  * **Advanced Forging Techniques:** Radial Forging and Hollow Die Punching 1 are retained due to their potential for excellent material properties and near-net shape capabilities for shafts. Rotary Compression/Skew Rolling 3 are also included as relevant near-net forging variants for axisymmetric parts.  
  * **Extrusion and Drawing Variants:** Both seamless and welded tube production followed by drawing 4 are critical, as drawn tubes often serve as precursors for other forming processes or can be used directly after machining features.  
  * **Flow Forming:** Retained due to its ability to produce high-precision, high-strength seamless hollow parts with controlled wall thickness, and its documented use for motor shafts.  
  * **Tube Hydroforming:** Kept for analysis due to its use in automotive structural components and ability to form complex shapes from tubes, although its suitability for the relatively simple geometry of a shaft body needs evaluation.  
  * **Rotary Swaging:** Included because it is a high-precision, cold-forming process widely used for automotive shafts (drive shafts, steering shafts) offering strength enhancement and cost-efficiency.  
  * **Welding/Joining Methods:** While not a primary forming method for the entire shaft, welding (especially advanced techniques like friction or laser welding) is crucial as an enabling technology for joining shaft sections produced by different methods, creating hybrid designs. ERW and Laser welding are also fundamental to welded tube production.4  
  * **Composite Manufacturing:** Will be discussed as a separate, high-potential but distinct alternative pathway, focusing on processes like filament winding.

### **2.3 Fundamental Process Differences and Trends**

Categorizing the potential manufacturing processes reveals fundamental differences that impact their suitability. Processes starting from a solid billet (bulk forming) inherently involve massive deformation, offering significant potential for controlling grain structure and flow 1, which is beneficial for fatigue strength. However, they often require substantial energy input (hot working) and complex tooling. Processes starting from a tube are highly dependent on the quality and cost of the initial tube blank – the choice between seamless and welded precursors is a major decision point.6 Incremental forming processes like flow forming and swaging typically involve cold working, which enhances strength and precision but may have limitations on the amount of deformation achievable in a single pass.

Across these diverse methods, a strong trend emerges towards **near-net shape manufacturing**. Techniques like forging combined with hollow punching 2, radial forging 1, rotary compression/skew rolling 3, flow forming, and rotary swaging all aim to produce a hollow shaft geometry that is very close to the final dimensions *during* the forming stage. This contrasts sharply with traditional methods relying on extensive machining (deep hole drilling, boring) from solid bar stock. The consistent emphasis on material savings (yield improvement) and reduction in subsequent machining time cited as key advantages for these near-net processes 2 indicates a significant industry push driven by cost reduction and resource efficiency – factors highly relevant to the mass production economics of NEVs.

## **3\. Detailed Analysis of Promising Manufacturing Processes for NEV Hollow Shafts**

This section provides a detailed examination of the manufacturing processes identified as most promising for producing hollow motor shafts for NEV applications, evaluating each against key technical and economic criteria.

### **3.1 Deep Drawing (Revisited)**

* **Process Description:** Deep drawing transforms a flat sheet metal blank into a hollow, cup-like, or box-shaped component by forcing it into a die cavity with a punch. A blank holder is typically used to control material flow and prevent wrinkling. Hydraulic presses are often used for control.  
* **Suitable Materials:** Commonly used with unalloyed thin and extra-thin sheets, aluminum, stainless steel, copper, brass, and bronze. Primarily suited for materials with good ductility.  
* **Cost-Effectiveness:** Tooling costs can be relatively low for simple shapes, but the initial press setup cost is high, making it most cost-effective for large production volumes (thousands to millions of parts). Raw material costs can be advantageous compared to machining from bulk. It can potentially reduce assembly costs by forming complex parts in one piece.  
* **Required Subsequent Processing:** Trimming of excess flange material is almost always necessary. Finishing operations like deburring or polishing may be needed. Multiple drawing stages (redrawing) might be required for deep parts.  
* **Achievable Quality:** The process is susceptible to defects like wrinkling (due to compressive stresses in the flange), tearing/fracturing (due to excessive tensile stress in the wall), and non-uniform wall thickness (thinning) if process parameters (punch force, blank holder force, lubrication, material properties) are not carefully controlled. It is generally not considered suitable for high-precision parts compared to other methods.  
* **Impact on Material Properties:** The process involves significant plastic deformation (tensile and compressive stresses), leading to work hardening of the material, which increases the strength of the final part.  
* **Specific Pros & Cons for NEV Shafts:**  
  * *Pros:* Potentially low raw material cost, high production rates for large volumes, work hardening effect.  
  * *Cons:* Primarily suited for thin materials, difficulty in achieving thick walls typical of motor shafts, challenges in controlling precise wall thickness and concentricity, potential for defects, generally lower precision than required for shaft features like bearing journals.  
* **Applicability Confirmation:** Deep drawing's limitations regarding material thickness, precision control, and defect susceptibility make it generally unsuitable for manufacturing the primary structural body of a high-performance NEV motor shaft. Its use would likely be confined to auxiliary, non-structural hollow components within the EDU, if at all.

### **3.2 Extrusion and Drawing Variants (Seamless/Welded Precursors)**

These processes are fundamental to producing the tubular raw material or semi-finished product used in many subsequent shaft manufacturing routes.

* **Process Description:**  
  * **Extrusion:** Pushes a heated billet (typically aluminum or other non-ferrous metals, but also steel) through a die orifice to create a continuous profile, including hollow tubes (using porthole or mandrel dies). Can create complex cross-sections.  
  * **Seamless Tube Production:** Often starts with piercing a solid billet (rotary piercing) followed by elongation and sizing using rolling or drawing processes.  
  * **Welded Tube Production:** Involves roll forming flat strip into a tube shape and then longitudinally welding the seam using ERW (Electric Resistance Welding) or Laser Welding.4  
  * **Cold Drawing:** Pulls a tube through a die (reducing OD) and often over an internal mandrel or plug (controlling ID and wall thickness) at room temperature. This refines dimensions, improves surface finish, and enhances mechanical properties through cold work. Drawn-Over-Mandrel (DOM) tubing is a common high-quality product made this way, often starting from ERW tube.  
* **Suitable Materials:** Wide range, including Carbon Steels (e.g., 1010, 1018, 1026, 1045), Alloy Steels (e.g., Chromoly 4130/4140), Stainless Steels, Aluminum Alloys, Copper Alloys. Material choice influences process parameters and resulting properties.  
* **Cost-Effectiveness:** Seamless tubing is generally significantly more expensive than welded tubing due to more complex manufacturing. ERW is a cost-effective welding method. Laser welding may be more expensive but offers potential quality benefits. Cold drawing adds cost compared to hot finished or as-welded tube but provides superior quality. Extrusion can be cost-effective, especially for aluminum where dies can be relatively affordable. Lead times can be shorter for standard extruded/welded tubes compared to custom seamless runs. For custom sizes, seamless might be more economical in smaller quantities.  
* **Required Subsequent Processing:** Extruded or welded tubes often serve as the *input material* (preform) for processes like hydroforming, flow forming, or rotary swaging. They always require cutting to length. Depending on the material, amount of cold work (drawing), and final application requirements, heat treatment (annealing, stress relieving, hardening & tempering) may be necessary. Precision machining is typically required to add features like splines, steps, bearing seats, unless formed directly via specialized extrusion.  
* **Achievable Quality:**  
  * *Seamless:* Offers inherent homogeneity, avoiding potential weld defects and associated variations in properties or corrosion resistance.  
  * *Welded:* Quality depends heavily on the welding process (ERW vs. Laser) and subsequent processing. The weld zone is inherently different from the base metal (different microstructure, potential for impurities, segregation).4 Post-weld drawing (like DOM) significantly refines the weld zone, reducing inhomogeneity, improving dimensional consistency, and smoothing the internal weld bead. Laser welding may produce a narrower HAZ and potentially fewer defects than ERW.  
  * *Drawing:* Significantly improves dimensional accuracy (OD, ID, wall thickness, concentricity) and surface finish compared to hot-finished or as-welded tubes. DOM tubing is known for tight tolerances.  
  * *Extrusion:* Accuracy depends on die design and process control. Precision extrusion can meet tight tolerances, potentially matching drawn tube accuracy for smaller diameters.  
* **Impact on Material Properties:**  
  * *Extrusion (Hot):* Imparts grain flow; properties depend on alloy and post-extrusion cooling/treatment.  
  * *Welding:* Creates a Heat-Affected Zone (HAZ) with altered microstructure (e.g., martensite or Widmanstätten structures in Zircaloy welds) and potentially different hardness/strength compared to the base metal.4 Can introduce residual stresses.  
  * *Drawing (Cold):* Induces significant strain hardening (cold work), increasing yield strength, tensile strength, and hardness, while typically reducing ductility.4 Creates beneficial compressive residual stresses on the surface.  
* **Specific Pros & Cons for NEV Shafts:**  
  * *Pros:* Established high-volume processes for producing tubular stock. Drawing provides excellent dimensional control and strength enhancement needed for shafts. Welded+Drawn (DOM) offers a cost-effective route to high-quality tubes. Seamless offers maximum material integrity. Extrusion offers shape flexibility, especially for aluminum.  
  * *Cons:* Often requires significant subsequent forming or machining. Weld integrity in welded tubes remains a critical consideration for high-fatigue applications. Seamless tubes are costly.  
* **Seamless vs. Welded Implications:** The choice between a seamless or a high-quality welded-and-drawn tube as the starting point for an NEV shaft remains a crucial decision, balancing the inherent material uniformity and potentially superior fatigue/corrosion resistance of seamless against the significantly lower cost of welded options. The viability of welded tubes hinges on the ability of advanced welding (e.g., laser) and post-weld processing (drawing) to mitigate potential weaknesses in the weld zone and ensure reliability under demanding NEV operating conditions. This trade-off directly impacts the overall cost structure.  
* **Drawing as an Enabling Step:** Cold drawing stands out not just as a tube production method but as a vital *intermediate or finishing step* for many potential NEV shaft manufacturing routes. It provides the necessary dimensional precision (tolerances, concentricity, surface finish) and mechanical property enhancement (strength via work hardening) that might be lacking in as-extruded, as-welded, or even some as-forged hollows. Its application is explicitly noted for automotive shafts.

### **3.3 Flow Forming**

* **Process Description:** Flow forming is an incremental cold metal forming process where rollers exert localized pressure on a rotating cylindrical preform (which can be a thick-walled tube or a forged/machined blank) positioned over a mandrel. This pressure reduces the wall thickness while elongating the preform axially along the mandrel. It is capable of producing seamless components with potentially varying wall thicknesses and contours along the length.  
* **Suitable Materials:** Effective for a variety of metals, including stainless steels and other alloys that might be difficult to form by other methods. It is well-suited for the types of steels typically used in shafts and potentially applicable to aluminum alloys.  
* **Cost-Effectiveness:** Generally considered cost-effective, particularly for medium to high volumes. Cost savings stem from several factors:  
  * *Material Efficiency:* It's a near-net shape process with minimal material waste, as material is displaced rather than removed. This reduces raw material costs.  
  * *One-Piece Construction:* Can produce complex profiles or parts with integrated flanges in a single piece, eliminating the need for welding multiple components and associated costs (welding, inspection, inventory).  
  * *Reduced Machining:* Often produces parts with high accuracy and good surface finish, minimizing the need for subsequent machining operations.  
  * Tooling (mandrel, rollers) represents an investment, but can be durable.  
* **Required Subsequent Processing:** Depending on the final requirements and the preform condition, subsequent processing may be minimal. Heat treatment (stress relief or tempering) might be necessary due to the significant cold work imparted. Finish machining may be needed for extremely tight tolerance features or specific surface requirements not achieved directly.  
* **Achievable Quality:** Flow forming offers excellent control over dimensions:  
  * *Wall Thickness:* Precise control, allowing for uniform or accurately varying wall thickness along the part length. Thicknesses from 0.015 to 0.50 inches (0.38 to 12.7 mm) are mentioned.  
  * *Tolerances:* Achieves dimensional tolerances comparable to machined parts.  
  * *Surface Finish:* Typically produces a high-quality surface finish, often eliminating the need for grinding or polishing.  
  * *Profiles:* Capable of producing accurate profiles and contours. Diameters from 0.75 to 48 inches (19 mm to 1219 mm) are feasible.  
* **Impact on Material Properties:** The process involves significant cold working (strain hardening):  
  * *Strength & Hardness:* Increases tensile strength, yield strength, and hardness of the material.  
  * *Fatigue Resistance:* Enhanced due to cold work and potentially favorable residual stress patterns.  
  * *Microstructure:* Refines the grain structure of the metal.  
  * *Ductility:* Elongation (ductility) may increase or be maintained depending on the material and process parameters, contributing to shock resistance.  
* **Specific Pros & Cons for NEV Shafts:**  
  * *Pros:* Excellent dimensional accuracy and surface finish. Enhanced strength and fatigue properties due to cold work. High material utilization (near-net shape). Ability to create variable wall thickness for optimized weight/strength. Proven application for hollow motor shafts. Cost-effective for volume production.  
  * *Cons:* Requires a suitable preform (tube or forged blank). Initial equipment investment can be significant. Process limits exist based on material, diameter, length, and thickness combinations.  
* **Strong Suitability Indication:** Flow forming appears exceptionally well-suited for NEV hollow motor shafts. Its ability to deliver high precision, enhanced mechanical properties through cold work, efficient material usage, and its specific application to NEV motor shafts directly address the core requirements of performance, lightweighting, quality, and potentially cost-effectiveness.

### **3.4 Hydroforming (Tube Hydroforming)**

* **Process Description:** Tube hydroforming uses high-pressure hydraulic fluid internally to expand a tubular blank (often pre-bent) outwards against the cavity walls of a surrounding die. Axial cylinders may also apply force to feed material into the expanding sections. This allows the formation of complex cross-sectional shapes and contours along the tube's length.  
* **Suitable Materials:** Requires materials with good formability. Commonly used with steels, stainless steels, aluminum alloys, and titanium. Higher strength materials can be challenging, especially if significant expansion (stretching) is required, as this can lead to thinning or splitting if the material's forming limit is exceeded. Extruded aluminum profiles are sometimes hydroformed.  
* **Cost-Effectiveness:**  
  * *Tooling:* Often uses a single-sided forming die, which can be cheaper than matched punch/die sets used in stamping or deep drawing. Tooling can sometimes be made from lower-cost materials like cast iron.  
  * *Equipment:* Hydroforming presses and high-pressure fluid systems represent a significant capital investment.  
  * *Cycle Time:* Generally slower than conventional stamping or potentially flow forming/swaging, which can impact high-volume production economics.  
  * *Part Consolidation:* A major advantage is the ability to form complex, integrated structures in one piece, reducing the number of components, assembly steps, and associated costs.  
  * *Material Utilization:* Can be efficient, producing near-net shapes with reduced scrap compared to machining or multi-stage stamping/welding. Overall process cost can be significantly lower than deep drawing for suitable parts.  
* **Required Subsequent Processing:** Pre-bending of the tube is often necessary before hydroforming. Post-forming operations typically include cutting/trimming ends and potentially piercing holes or other features. Finishing operations may be reduced due to good surface quality.  
* **Achievable Quality:** Can produce parts with complex geometries, smooth surfaces, and good dimensional accuracy. Uniform pressure application helps maintain consistent wall thickness compared to stamping. However, controlling material flow, especially during significant expansion or around tight bends, is critical to avoid excessive thinning, wrinkling, or splitting. Springback can be a concern, particularly with materials like aluminum, requiring careful process control (e.g., higher pressure, stretching beyond yield point). Tight tolerances are achievable.  
* **Impact on Material Properties:** Work hardening occurs during deformation, but potentially less severely than in deep drawing. The uniform pressure application generally leads to more evenly distributed stresses compared to mechanical stamping. Prior work hardening from pre-bending operations affects the material state before hydroforming.  
* **Specific Pros & Cons for NEV Shafts:**  
  * *Pros:* Excellent for complex shapes and part consolidation. Good surface finish. Potential for weight reduction through efficient section design. Lower tooling cost than matched dies.  
  * *Cons:* High equipment cost. Slower cycle times. Potential limitations with very high-strength materials or large expansions. May require pre-bending. Less suited for simple axisymmetric shapes compared to more specialized processes like flow forming or swaging.  
* **Potential Niche Role:** While hydroforming is a powerful technology for automotive structures (frames, subframes, exhaust components), its advantages seem less pronounced for the main body of a typical hollow motor shaft, which is often relatively simple geometrically (axisymmetric with steps/splines). The slower cycle times and high equipment costs, coupled with potential challenges in forming high-strength shaft materials with significant expansion, might make processes like flow forming or swaging more competitive for this specific application. Hydroforming's niche for shafts might lie in producing highly integrated designs where complex non-axisymmetric features need to be formed directly onto the hollow body, or in lower volume scenarios where tooling cost savings outweigh cycle time considerations.

### **3.5 Rotary Swaging**

* **Process Description:** Rotary swaging is an incremental cold forming process that uses a set of reciprocating dies (hammers) arranged radially around the workpiece. As the spindle holding the dies rotates at high speed, the hammers strike the workpiece rapidly, reducing its diameter. For hollow parts, a stationary or moving mandrel is typically inserted to control the internal diameter and profile. The workpiece can be fed axially through the dies (in-feed swaging) or formed in specific sections (recess swaging). It is essentially a high-speed multi-hammer forging process performed cold.  
* **Suitable Materials:** Applicable to a wide range of metals, including steels (low alloy, carbon), stainless steels, aluminum alloys, brass, bronze, and even difficult-to-deform materials like tungsten or magnesium alloys due to the compressive stress state during forming. Well-suited for both solid and hollow components.  
* **Cost-Effectiveness:** Positioned as a cost-efficient alternative to traditional forging and, especially, machining operations. Key cost benefits include:  
  * *Near-Net Shape:* Produces parts with high precision, significantly reducing or eliminating the need for subsequent machining, saving material and process time.  
  * *High Speed:* The process itself is rapid due to the high hammering frequency.  
  * *Material Savings:* Minimal material waste compared to subtractive processes.  
  * *Automation:* Suitable for mass production and integration into automated production lines. Modern machines offer flexibility for smaller batches as well.  
* **Required Subsequent Processing:** Often produces net or near-net shapes. Trimming ends might be necessary. Depending on the degree of cold work and material requirements, heat treatment (stress relief, tempering) may follow. Finish machining is typically minimal but might be needed for ultra-precision features.  
* **Achievable Quality:** Known for high precision and excellent surface quality:  
  * *Tolerances:* Tight dimensional control is achievable. Outer diameter tolerances range from 0.01 mm to 0.1 mm (increasing with diameter). Inner diameter tolerance when using a mandrel is approximately 0.03 mm.  
  * *Surface Finish:* Can achieve very smooth surfaces, with recess swaging capable of Ra values down to 0.1 µm. In-feed swaging typically yields Ra around 1.0 µm.  
* **Impact on Material Properties:** The significant cold working involved leads to:  
  * *Strength Enhancement:* Substantial increase in yield strength, tensile strength, and hardness due to work hardening. This allows for lightweight design by using thinner walls or replacing solid parts with stronger hollow ones.  
  * *Microstructure:* Refines grain structure and improves material density.  
  * *Fatigue Life:* Generally improves fatigue performance due to cold work and favorable surface characteristics.  
* **Specific Pros & Cons for NEV Shafts:**  
  * *Pros:* High precision and excellent surface finish. Significant strength enhancement via cold work. Near-net shape capability reduces machining costs and material waste. Cost-effective for mass production. Proven technology for various automotive shafts (steering, drivetrain, transmission). Suitable for hollow components.  
  * *Cons:* Equipment investment required. May have limitations on the complexity of profiles achievable compared to hydroforming or forging. Mandrel design is critical for internal features.  
* **Strong Candidacy:** Rotary swaging emerges as a very strong candidate for manufacturing NEV hollow motor shafts. Its combination of high precision, significant strength improvement through cold work, cost-efficiency compared to machining, and established track record in producing high-volume automotive shafts aligns extremely well with the key requirements identified for this application. It presents a profile of benefits very similar to flow forming, and the final choice between them might depend on specific geometric details, required surface finish, material compatibility nuances, or existing manufacturing infrastructure.

### **3.6 Advanced Forging Techniques (e.g., Radial Forging, Hollow Die Punching)**

This category encompasses several methods that use forging principles to create hollow shafts, often starting from solid billets and aiming for near-net shapes.

* **Process Description:**  
  * **Hollow Die Punching:** This multi-step process typically starts with a solid concast bloom or ingot. It involves heating, initial forging operations (upsetting, drawing) to pre-shape the billet, potentially booster heating, and then a critical step where the preform is placed in a hollow die and a central hole is created by punching.2 The result is a near-net shaped hollow preform.  
  * **Radial Forging:** Uses 2, 3, 4, or more radially opposed hammers that simultaneously strike a rotating and often axially feeding workpiece (which can be solid or hollow).1 By controlling hammer stroke, rotation, and feed, complex external and internal profiles (using a mandrel) can be formed. Can be performed hot, warm, or cold.  
  * **Rotary Compression / Skew Rolling:** Specialized processes using rotating rolls (often 3\) to incrementally reduce the diameter and form steps or other features on tubular or solid bars.3 Often used for axisymmetric parts and typically performed hot or warm. Requires specialized machinery.5  
* **Suitable Materials:** Primarily suited for forgeable materials, with steels being the most common.1 Specific examples include SCr420 for EV shafts via radial forging 1, C45 steel for rotary compression 5, and 30CrMoA for skew rolling.3 Aluminum alloys and other non-ferrous metals can also be forged.  
* **Cost-Effectiveness:** These near-net shape forging processes offer significant potential for material savings compared to machining from solid (yield improvements of 30-50% claimed for hollow punching 2). They also drastically reduce subsequent machining time (40-45% reduction claimed 2). However, the initial investment in forging presses, specialized machines (like radial forges or rotary compression machines 5), and complex tooling (dies, punches, mandrels) can be very high. Process development can also be costly and time-consuming, sometimes relying on trial-and-error or extensive simulation. Therefore, these methods are typically most economical for high production volumes where tooling and equipment costs can be amortized.  
* **Required Subsequent Processing:** While producing near-net shapes, these processes generally require subsequent steps. Heat treatment (e.g., normalizing, quenching, tempering) is almost always necessary to achieve the final desired microstructure and mechanical properties.1 Proof machining (turning, boring, grinding) is needed to achieve final tolerances and surface finishes on critical features like bearing journals and seal surfaces.2 A finishing allowance is typically required.5 Stress relieving may also be applied.2  
* **Achievable Quality:** Forging processes can produce parts with good dimensional control, although perhaps not as tight as cold drawing, flow forming, or swaging without finish machining. The major quality advantage lies in microstructural control. Forging creates continuous grain flow lines that follow the contour of the part, which significantly enhances strength, toughness, and particularly fatigue resistance compared to methods that cut across grain flow (like machining from bar stock).1 However, controlling the process is critical to avoid defects like laps, folds, or internal cracks, especially in complex geometries or large parts.5 Hardness distribution can vary within the part, influenced by local deformation and cooling rates.1  
* **Impact on Material Properties:**  
  * *Microstructure:* Refines grain size and creates favorable grain flow patterns.1 Hot forging allows significant deformation without work hardening but results in coarser surface finish and lower dimensional accuracy. Cold or warm forging variants induce work hardening.1  
  * *Mechanical Properties:* Significantly enhances toughness and fatigue strength due to grain flow optimization.1 Overall strength and hardness are controlled primarily by material selection and subsequent heat treatment.  
* **Specific Pros & Cons for NEV Shafts:**  
  * *Pros:* Potential for superior fatigue strength and toughness due to optimal grain flow. Excellent material utilization compared to machining from solid. Suitable for high-strength steels. Can form complex geometries including integrated features.  
  * *Cons:* High initial investment in equipment and tooling. Process complexity can be high, requiring significant expertise and potentially simulation.5 Typically requires extensive subsequent heat treatment and machining. May not achieve the same level of dimensional precision or surface finish as cold finishing processes without machining.  
* **Performance vs. Cost/Complexity Trade-off:** Advanced forging routes represent a high-performance option, potentially offering the best intrinsic material properties (especially fatigue resistance 1) critical for the demanding NEV application. The near-net shape aspect addresses the material waste issue of traditional forging/machining.2 However, this performance comes at the cost of higher process complexity, significant capital investment, and the continued need for substantial post-forming operations (heat treatment, machining). This positions forging as a premium option, likely justified only when the absolute highest performance is required and production volumes are sufficient to offset the high initial costs.

### **3.7 Welding/Joining Methods (as Enabling Technology)**

While not typically used to form the main hollow body of the shaft due to concerns about weld integrity under high cyclic loads, welding and joining play a crucial role as enabling technologies for creating optimized, multi-piece shaft designs.

* **Process Description:** Involves joining two or more pre-fabricated components together. For shafts, this often means joining a hollow tubular section (produced by e.g., drawing, flow forming, or swaging) to solid end pieces that may contain complex features like splines or flanges (produced by e.g., forging or machining). Relevant high-integrity joining processes include:  
  * **Friction Welding:** Generates heat through friction between rotating and stationary parts, creating a strong, forged bond without melting or filler material. Well-suited for joining dissimilar metals and creating high-integrity joints in shafts.  
  * **Laser Beam Welding (LBW):** Uses a focused laser beam to create a deep, narrow weld with a minimal heat-affected zone (HAZ). Offers high precision, speed, and potential for automation. Can be superior to ERW for certain applications.  
  * **Electron Beam Welding (EBW):** Similar to LBW but uses an electron beam, typically requiring a vacuum environment. Can achieve very deep penetration and high-quality welds.  
  * *(ERW is primarily used for manufacturing the initial welded tube, not typically for joining shaft components 4).*  
* **Suitable Materials:** Depends on the specific process and the materials being joined. Compatibility is key. Friction welding is versatile for joining dissimilar metals. Laser and electron beam welding work well with most engineering metals used in shafts.  
* **Cost-Effectiveness:** Adds a process step, requiring investment in specialized equipment (welder, fixtures) and potentially skilled labor. However, it enables a hybrid design approach, potentially lowering overall cost by allowing optimization of manufacturing for each section (e.g., using a cheaper tube for the main body and only using more expensive processes/materials for the complex ends).  
* **Required Subsequent Processing:** Post-weld heat treatment (PWHT) may be necessary to relieve stresses and temper the weld zone/HAZ. Non-destructive testing (NDT) such as ultrasonic or radiographic inspection is critical to ensure weld integrity, especially for fatigue-critical applications. Machining is often performed after joining to ensure final dimensional accuracy and concentricity across the assembly.  
* **Achievable Quality:** The quality and integrity of the weld are paramount. The goal is to achieve strength and fatigue properties in the joint that are comparable to the base materials. Poor weld quality can create a significant weak point. Advanced processes like friction welding, LBW, and EBW generally offer higher quality and consistency than traditional arc welding methods for these types of joints. The HAZ will always have different properties than the base metal.  
* **Impact on Material Properties:** Creates a weld nugget and HAZ with altered microstructure, hardness, and potentially reduced corrosion resistance compared to the parent materials. Can introduce residual stresses that may need mitigation through PWHT.  
* **Specific Pros & Cons for NEV Shafts:**  
  * *Pros:* Enables optimized hybrid designs combining different materials or manufacturing processes for different sections. Allows complex features (e.g., forged splined ends) to be joined to simpler, cost-effective hollow bodies (e.g., drawn tubes). Potential for overall cost reduction compared to single-piece complex forgings or extensive machining.  
  * *Cons:* Introduces a joint, which is a potential failure point if not properly designed, executed, and inspected. Adds process steps, complexity, and cost. Requires careful control and NDT. HAZ properties must be acceptable for the application.  
* **Critical Role in Hybrid Designs:** Given the complexity of NEV shaft requirements (hollow body, precise bearing seats, high-torque splines), a single manufacturing process may struggle to deliver optimal results cost-effectively. Welding/joining provides the crucial capability to combine the strengths of different processes. For example, a cost-effective drawn or flow-formed tube could form the main shaft body, while the ends requiring intricate splines could be forged or machined from solid and then reliably joined using friction or laser welding. This hybrid strategy, enabled by robust joining techniques, offers a pragmatic and potentially highly cost-effective route to meeting the multifaceted demands of NEV hollow motor shafts.

Tube-based routes (starting with Extrusion/Drawing) often need machining for features. Flow Forming and Rotary Swaging offer the potential for the least subsequent machining due to their near-net shape capabilities. Hydroforming may require trimming and potentially pre-bending. ### **4.3 Discussion of Key Trade-offs**

The comparative analysis highlights several critical trade-offs:

* **Performance vs. Cost:** Advanced forging techniques 1 promise the highest potential fatigue performance due to grain flow control but come with significant complexity and cost implications.5 Cold working processes like Flow Forming and Rotary Swaging offer a compelling balance, providing substantial strength enhancement and precision at potentially lower overall costs than forging, especially when considering reduced subsequent machining. The lowest cost options often involve welded tube precursors, but careful process control and validation are needed to ensure performance targets, particularly fatigue life, are met. The intense cost pressure in the NEV market makes this trade-off central to process selection.  
* **Near-Net Shape vs. Machining:** The clear trend towards near-net shape processes (Forging+Punching, Flow Forming, Swaging) is driven by the desire to reduce material waste and costly machining time.2 While traditional machining from solid bar or thick-walled tube offers flexibility, its inefficiency makes it less viable for high-volume NEV production. The trade-off lies in the higher initial investment required for specialized near-net shape equipment and tooling versus the ongoing costs of material waste and machining time.  
* **Seamless vs. Welded Precursors:** This remains a fundamental choice for processes starting with tubes. Seamless tubes offer the highest assurance of material homogeneity and freedom from weld-related defects, crucial for reliability. However, their higher cost is a significant drawback. Advances in welding (e.g., laser) and post-weld drawing (DOM process) are narrowing the quality gap, making high-quality welded tubes a strong contender for achieving a better cost-performance balance.6 The decision often depends on the specific fatigue requirements and the level of risk tolerance.  
* **Hot vs. Cold Forming:** While hot forming allows for greater deformation with lower forces, the resulting lower dimensional accuracy and poorer surface finish generally necessitate more extensive post-processing for precision components like motor shafts. Cold forming processes (Drawing, Flow Forming, Swaging) provide superior precision, surface finish, and the benefit of work hardening, making them more aligned with the final requirements of the shaft, despite requiring higher forming forces or multiple stages. The prevalence of successful cold working techniques for shafts suggests their advantages outweigh the benefits of hot forming for this application.

### **4.4 Process Optimization and Simulation**

The inherent complexity of material flow, stress development, and thermal effects in many of these advanced forming processes necessitates sophisticated process design and control. Techniques like radial forging 1, rotary compression 5, skew rolling 3, and hydroforming involve intricate interactions between tooling, workpiece material, and process parameters (temperature, speed, feed rate, pressure). Optimizing these processes solely through physical trials can be extremely costly and time-consuming. Consequently, the use of Finite Element Method (FEM) simulation has become indispensable. Simulation allows engineers to model material behavior, predict potential defects (like cracking in forging, non-uniform wall thickness in rotary compression 5, or thinning/splitting in hydroforming), optimize tool geometries, and refine process parameters virtually before committing to expensive hardware and trials.1 This reliance on simulation underscores the technical sophistication required to successfully implement and control these advanced manufacturing routes for high-performance components.

Furthermore, the optimal manufacturing solution is rarely a single forming operation but rather an integrated **process chain**. For instance, a common route might involve starting with a tube produced by extrusion or welding, followed by cold drawing for precision and initial strengthening, then a final shaping/strengthening step like flow forming or swaging, followed by heat treatment and minimal finish machining. Optimizing this entire sequence – considering the quality output of each step, its cost, and its impact on subsequent operations – is crucial for achieving both the required technical specifications and the target production cost. The quality of the initial precursor, whether a seamless or a high-quality welded tube 6, significantly influences the downstream processing requirements and final part quality.

### **5.4 Key Considerations for Process Selection**

The analysis consistently highlights the strong alignment between the capabilities of **cold incremental forming processes** – specifically **Flow Forming** and **Rotary Swaging** – and the demanding requirements of NEV hollow motor shafts. These methods offer a synergistic combination of benefits:

* **Enhanced Mechanical Properties:** Significant strength and hardness increases via controlled cold work, contributing directly to torque capacity and fatigue resistance.  
* **High Precision and Surface Quality:** Capable of achieving tight dimensional tolerances and excellent surface finishes, crucial for high-speed operation, bearing/seal interfaces, and minimizing NVH.  
* **Material Efficiency:** Near-net shape capabilities minimize material waste and subsequent machining, aligning with cost and sustainability goals.  
* **Cost-Effectiveness:** Offer a potentially lower overall cost compared to extensive machining from solid or complex multi-step forging routes, making them suitable for automotive volumes.

While advanced forging techniques 1 offer the theoretical advantage of optimal grain flow for potentially maximizing fatigue life, this benefit must be weighed against their higher complexity, cost, and the need for significant post-processing. The high level of performance achieved through well-controlled cold working in flow forming or swaging may represent a more pragmatic and economically viable solution for many NEV applications. As NEV production volumes increase and cost pressures intensify, processes delivering "good enough" or excellent performance at a competitive cost are likely to be favored over those pursuing ultimate performance at a premium. The "good enough" threshold, however, must still meet the stringent reliability and durability requirements of automotive powertrains.

## **6\. Recommended Manufacturing Routes and Conclusion**

### **6.1 Identification and Justification of Most Suitable Routes**

Based on the comprehensive analysis of functional requirements, process capabilities, and suitability for the NEV context, the following manufacturing routes emerge as the most promising for producing hollow motor shafts:

1. **Route 1: Flow Forming (from High-Quality Tube Preform):**  
   * **Justification:** This route leverages the exceptional capabilities of flow forming to achieve high precision, excellent surface finish, and significant strength enhancement through cold work. Its ability to produce variable wall thicknesses allows for weight optimization. It is a near-net shape process with high material yield and potential for reduced subsequent machining, contributing to cost-effectiveness at volume. Its documented use for motor shafts further supports its suitability. The quality of the starting tube (seamless or high-quality welded/drawn) is critical.  
   * **Process Chain Example:** Tube Production (Seamless or Welded+Drawn) \-\> Cut to Length \-\> **Flow Forming** \-\> Heat Treatment (Optional/Specific) \-\> Finish Machining (Features/Critical Surfaces).  
2. **Route 2: Rotary Swaging (from High-Quality Tube/Blank Preform):**  
   * **Justification:** Similar to flow forming, rotary swaging offers high precision, excellent surface finish, and substantial strength increase via cold work. It is a proven, cost-effective technology for high-volume automotive shaft production. Its near-net shape capability minimizes waste and machining. The choice between flow forming and swaging may depend on specific geometric requirements, achievable tolerances/finishes for the application, and supplier capabilities.  
   * **Process Chain Example:** Tube Production (Seamless or Welded+Drawn) / Preform Forging \-\> Cut/Prepare Preform \-\> **Rotary Swaging** (with Mandrel) \-\> Heat Treatment (Optional/Specific) \-\> Finish Machining (Features/Critical Surfaces).  
3. **Route 3: Advanced Forging (e.g., Radial Forging or Hollow Punching) \+ Machining:**  
   * **Justification:** This route is recommended for applications where maximizing fatigue strength and toughness through optimal grain flow is the absolute priority, and cost is a secondary concern.1 Near-net forging minimizes material waste compared to traditional forging and boring.2 However, it involves high capital investment, process complexity, and requires significant subsequent heat treatment and precision machining to achieve final dimensions and tolerances.2  
   * **Process Chain Example:** Billet Preparation \-\> Heating \-\> **Near-Net Forging (Radial/Punching)** \-\> Heat Treatment (Normalizing, Hardening, Tempering) \-\> Proof Machining (Turning, Boring, Grinding) \-\> Stress Relieving.  
4. **Route 4 (Cost-Driven): High-Quality Welded Tube \+ Cold Drawing (DOM) \+ Feature Machining:**  
   * **Justification:** This represents the most cost-sensitive approach, relying on the affordability of welded tube production combined with the quality enhancements of cold drawing. Success hinges critically on utilizing advanced welding techniques (e.g., Laser) and rigorous process control during welding and drawing to ensure the integrity and consistency of the weld zone meet the fatigue demands. Extensive NDT would be essential. Features like splines would typically be added via machining.  
   * **Process Chain Example:** Strip Roll Forming \-\> **Laser/High-Quality ERW Welding** \-\> **Cold Drawing (DOM)** \-\> Cut to Length \-\> Heat Treatment (Optional/Specific) \-\> Machining (Splines, Steps, Journals).

### **6.2 Discussion of Process Chains**

It is crucial to recognize that manufacturing an NEV hollow motor shaft is rarely accomplished through a single forming step. The recommended routes are, in practice, **process chains**. The selection and optimization of the entire sequence, from raw material or initial preform to the finished, heat-treated, and machined component, is vital. The quality achieved at each stage directly impacts the requirements and cost of subsequent stages. For instance, starting with a high-precision, high-strength flow-formed or swaged blank significantly reduces the scope and cost of final machining compared to starting with a rough forging. Similarly, the choice between a seamless or a welded tube precursor affects not only cost but potentially the required intensity of NDT and the achievable fatigue performance.6

### **6.3 Concluding Remarks**

NEV hollow motor shafts are critical components operating under demanding conditions of high torque, high speed, and cyclic loading, with stringent requirements for lightweighting, efficiency, and reliability. The analysis reveals that while traditional methods like machining from solid bar are inefficient, several advanced manufacturing technologies offer viable solutions.

**Flow Forming** and **Rotary Swaging** emerge as particularly strong candidates, providing an excellent balance of enhanced mechanical properties (through cold work), high dimensional precision, good surface finish, near-net shape efficiency, and cost-effectiveness suitable for automotive volumes. **Advanced Forging** techniques offer the potential for superior fatigue performance via grain flow control but entail higher costs and complexity. For cost-driven applications, routes based on **high-quality welded and drawn tubing** are feasible, provided weld integrity can be rigorously assured.

The optimal choice is not universal but depends on a detailed evaluation of the specific requirements of the application, including performance targets (torque, fatigue life), weight constraints, geometric complexity, required precision, production volume, and overall cost targets. Future developments may see increased use of lightweight materials like aluminum alloys or composites (requiring processes like extrusion or filament winding, respectively), further advancements in joining technologies for hybrid shafts, and broader application of process simulation to optimize design and manufacturing efficiency. Selecting the most appropriate manufacturing route requires a holistic approach, considering the entire process chain and leveraging available supplier expertise and capabilities.
