# **Horizontal Gene Transfer in Plants and Animals: Mechanisms, Significance, and Evolutionary Implications**

## **I. Introduction: Horizontal Gene Transfer Beyond Microbes**

### **A. Defining Horizontal Gene Transfer (HGT): A Fundamental Evolutionary Process**

Horizontal gene transfer (HGT), also known as lateral gene transfer (LGT), refers to the transmission of genetic material, primarily DNA, between different organisms through mechanisms other than vertical inheritance from parent to offspring.1 This process stands in contrast to vertical gene transfer (VGT), the conventional mode of inheritance associated with reproduction.1 HGT is now widely recognized as a fundamental evolutionary process, particularly in the microbial world.

In prokaryotes (Archaea and Bacteria), HGT is not merely an occasional event but a major driving force shaping genome evolution, adaptation, and the diversification of lineages.2 Its pervasive nature is evident in estimates suggesting that a significant fraction, potentially over 80%, of genes in prokaryotic genomes may have been subject to horizontal transfer at some point in their history.11 The profound impact of HGT in microbes is exemplified by its role in the rapid spread of antibiotic resistance, the evolution of virulence factors, and the acquisition of novel metabolic capabilities, such as the degradation of human-made compounds or adaptation to extreme environments.1 The high frequency and impact of HGT in prokaryotes have led some researchers to question the adequacy of a strictly bifurcating "Tree of Life" model for microbial evolution, suggesting that a "web" or "network" metaphor, reflecting extensive inter-lineage gene flow, might be more appropriate.3

### **B. HGT in Eukaryotes: A Rarer, More Complex Phenomenon**

While the significance of HGT in prokaryotes is well-established, its role in the evolution of eukaryotes (organisms with cells containing a defined nucleus) has historically been considered less prominent and is still a subject of ongoing research and debate.2 Nonetheless, evidence accumulated over recent decades clearly demonstrates that HGT does occur in eukaryotes. This includes transfers between prokaryotes and eukaryotes, between different eukaryotic lineages, and even between the different DNA-containing organelles within eukaryotic cells (nucleus, mitochondrion, and chloroplast).1

However, compared to its prevalence in prokaryotes, HGT is generally regarded as a rarer and significantly more complex process in eukaryotes, particularly in multicellular organisms like plants and animals.2 The structural and reproductive complexities of eukaryotes present substantial barriers to the successful transfer, integration, and inheritance of foreign genetic material. Despite these challenges, the advent of large-scale genome sequencing and sophisticated bioinformatics analyses has led to the identification of a growing number of HGT events in diverse eukaryotic lineages, suggesting that its frequency and importance might have been underestimated in the past.4

### **C. Report Objectives and Scope**

This report aims to provide a comprehensive exploration of HGT specifically within the context of non-microbial eukaryotes – plants and animals. It will delve into the current understanding of the mechanisms facilitating such transfers, present well-documented examples from both kingdoms, analyze the functional consequences and evolutionary significance of acquired genes, evaluate estimates of HGT frequency and scale, discuss the inherent barriers, and synthesize these findings to assess the overall role of HGT in eukaryotic evolution. Throughout the report, comparisons will be drawn with the more frequent and mechanistically distinct HGT processes observed in prokaryotes, drawing upon recent research findings to address the complexities and nuances of gene flow across eukaryotic species boundaries.

## **II. Contrasting HGT: Eukaryotes vs. Prokaryotes**

The differences in frequency and apparent impact of HGT between prokaryotes and eukaryotes are rooted in fundamental distinctions in their cellular organization, reproductive strategies, and genomic architecture.

### **A. Cellular Architecture: The Eukaryotic Challenge**

A primary factor contributing to the relative rarity and complexity of HGT in eukaryotes is their intricate cellular structure. Prokaryotic cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus; their genetic material (typically a circular chromosome and often plasmids) resides directly in the cytoplasm.1 This arrangement facilitates relatively straightforward mechanisms of gene exchange like the uptake of environmental DNA (transformation) or direct transfer via conjugation or bacteriophages.1

In contrast, eukaryotic cells are defined by their compartmentalization. The main repository of genetic information, the nuclear genome, is enclosed within a double membrane – the nuclear envelope.1 This envelope represents a formidable physical barrier that any incoming foreign DNA must traverse to access the chromosomes.1 Furthermore, eukaryotes possess complex endomembrane systems and cytoskeletal networks.3 Successful delivery of foreign DNA to the nucleus likely requires hijacking or navigating sophisticated subcellular transport and signaling pathways, adding layers of complexity absent in prokaryotes.1 While eukaryotes also possess DNA in mitochondria and, in plants and algae, chloroplasts, transfer *into* the primary nuclear genome remains a significant hurdle.20

### **B. Multicellularity and the Germline Barrier**

In multicellular animals, another major constraint is the sequestration of the germline. For a horizontally acquired gene to become evolutionarily significant – that is, heritable across generations – it must be integrated into the genome of germline cells (the precursors to sperm and eggs).14 Transfers into somatic cells, which constitute the vast majority of cells in a complex animal, are typically evolutionary dead ends as they are not passed on to offspring.

This contrasts sharply with prokaryotes, which reproduce asexually, and with many unicellular eukaryotes or multicellular organisms capable of vegetative propagation (like many plants) or regeneration from somatic tissues. In these cases, the distinction between germline and soma may be less rigid or irrelevant, increasing the probability that an HGT event occurring in any cell can be passed on.14 The lack of a strictly segregated germline in many protists is considered a contributing factor to potentially higher rates of HGT compared to complex animals.17

### **C. Mechanisms and Frequency: A Tale of Two Domains**

The well-characterized prokaryotic HGT mechanisms – transformation, conjugation, and transduction – are highly efficient and contribute to the observed high frequency of gene exchange in microbial communities.1 Plasmids and bacteriophages act as potent vectors for mobilizing genes within and between species.1

Eukaryotic HGT, while utilizing some analogous principles (e.g., viral vectors, mobile elements), relies on mechanisms that are generally less frequent, less efficient, or less well understood (detailed in Section III). Consequently, the overall frequency of HGT events that become fixed and contribute significantly to the long-term evolution of eukaryotic genomes appears much lower than in prokaryotes.2

The difference extends beyond mere frequency to the intricacies of the integration process itself. Prokaryotic HGT often involves homologous recombination between closely related sequences or the relatively simple maintenance of extrachromosomal elements like plasmids.1 In eukaryotes, successful HGT necessitates not only overcoming the physical barriers to entry and reaching the nucleus but also integrating into a complex environment of chromatin. Furthermore, for a transferred gene to be functional (functional HGT, or fHGT), it must adapt to the host's gene expression machinery, potentially requiring the acquisition of introns and splice sites recognized by the host spliceosome, adaptation to host codon usage biases, and acquisition of appropriate regulatory elements for transcription.23 These post-entry requirements act as additional filters, meaning that even if foreign DNA successfully enters a eukaryotic cell, its chances of becoming a stable, functional, and heritable part of the genome are significantly reduced compared to the prokaryotic scenario.

## **III. Mechanisms Enabling Gene Flow Across Eukaryotic Species**

Despite the barriers, various mechanisms have been identified or proposed that facilitate the movement of genetic material across eukaryotic species boundaries.

### **A. Mobile Genetic Elements as Vectors**

Mobile genetic elements, particularly transposable elements (TEs), are increasingly recognized as significant players in mediating HGT in eukaryotes.1 The movement of TEs between the genomes of different species, distinct from vertical inheritance, is termed Horizontal Transposon Transfer (HTT).2

Certain types of TEs seem particularly prone to HTT. DNA transposons and LTR (Long Terminal Repeat) retroelements, both of which utilize stable double-stranded DNA intermediates during their transposition cycle, are thought to be more robust and capable of surviving the journey between hosts compared to non-LTR retroelements, which rely on more fragile single-stranded RNA intermediates.2 Specific examples of TEs implicated in HTT across diverse eukaryotic lineages are numerous:

* **Helitrons:** These unique rolling-circle DNA transposons have shown evidence of repeated HTT across an exceptionally broad range of animals, including mammals (bats), reptiles (lizards), fish (lampreys), various invertebrates (insects, nematodes, molluscs), and even insect viruses (polydnaviruses), indicating their capacity to cross major phylogenetic divides.24  
* ***P*** **elements:** The relatively recent invasion of *Drosophila melanogaster* by *P* elements, likely transferred horizontally from species in the *Drosophila willistoni* group, is a classic example of HTT in animals.18  
* **BovB:** These LINE (Long Interspersed Nuclear Element) retrotransposons show evidence of extensive horizontal transfer among various mammals and other vertebrates.25  
* **hAT superfamily:** DNA transposons belonging to this superfamily have been implicated in HTT between plants (e.g., Brassicaceae to Orobanchaceae) and between *Drosophila* species.25  
* **LTR retrotransposons (Copia superfamily):** Elements from the Ale and Ivana lineages within this superfamily appear particularly prone to HTT between different plant species.2  
* **Starships:** These are massive, gene-rich transposons found in fungi, hypothesized to act as vectors for large-scale eukaryote-eukaryote HGT, potentially transferring hundreds of kilobases of sequence, including multiple genes, in single events.26

The movement of TEs across species boundaries is not just an accidental byproduct of their mobility. It can be viewed as an evolutionary strategy for the TEs themselves, allowing them to escape host defense mechanisms (like silencing pathways), avoid accumulation of deleterious mutations, or colonize new genomic environments when they face purging within a particular host lineage.2 Furthermore, because TEs, especially elements like Helitrons, are known to occasionally capture fragments of host genes during their transposition process, their horizontal transfer can inadvertently lead to the horizontal transfer of host genes as well.24 This suggests a dual role for TEs: they are vehicles facilitating their own spread, and in doing so, they can become vectors for the horizontal transfer of other genetic material, which may then be subject to selection in the recipient genome.

### **B. Viral Mediation**

Viruses, acting as natural vectors for genetic material, are another plausible mechanism for HGT in eukaryotes.6 The process is analogous to transduction in prokaryotes, where bacteriophages transfer bacterial DNA.1 Eukaryotic viruses, particularly those that integrate into the host genome (like retroviruses) or establish long-term infections, could potentially pick up host genetic material and transfer it to a new host upon subsequent infection.

Examples supporting viral mediation include the association of *Helitron* HTT with polydnaviruses (symbiotic viruses of wasps) 24, suggesting these viruses may act as vectors for transposon movement between insects. Additionally, studies on the complex patterns of gene sharing and modularity among different groups of small DNA tumor viruses (e.g., papillomaviruses, polyomaviruses) suggest that HGT has played a significant role in their own evolution, potentially creating a network of gene flow that could occasionally spill over to their eukaryotic hosts.27 The inherent ability of many viruses to infect a range of hosts could make them particularly effective conduits for HGT, potentially bridging wider evolutionary distances than mechanisms requiring direct cell contact. The modular nature of many viral genomes might reflect this history of gene exchange, making them adept at acquiring and transferring functional genetic units.27

### **C. Intimate Associations: Parasitism, Symbiosis, Endosymbiosis**

Close and prolonged physical contact between different species, as occurs in parasitic, symbiotic, or endosymbiotic relationships, provides opportunities for genetic material exchange.6

* **Parasitism:** Parasitic plants offer compelling examples. Many possess specialized feeding structures called haustoria that penetrate host tissues, establishing intimate cellular connections. These connections serve as direct conduits not only for nutrient transfer but also potentially for the transfer of DNA and RNA between parasite and host.15 Studies in the Orobanchaceae family, which includes parasitic plants with varying degrees of host dependence, have documented numerous HGT events from host plants to the parasites.15  
* **Endosymbiosis:** The origins of mitochondria and chloroplasts represent ancient, massive HGT events where entire bacterial genomes were integrated and subsequently reduced.17 This process continues on a smaller scale with more recent endosymbionts. A prime example involves bacteria of the genus *Wolbachia*, which live inside the cells (often including germline cells) of numerous arthropod and nematode species.8 Their intracellular location and frequent presence in egg cytoplasm facilitate maternal inheritance and provide a direct pathway for bacterial DNA fragments to be transferred to the host nuclear genome and potentially become heritable.14 The accumulation of nuclear genes of organelle or endosymbiont origin can be conceptualized through an "HGT ratchet" model, where transfers to the nucleus are followed by loss from the endosymbiont, creating dependency.18  
* **Symbiosis:** Organisms living in close association, such as those in microbial mats, biofilms, or the gut microbiomes of animals, also have increased opportunities for DNA exchange, although transfers specifically into the host germline might be less direct than in endosymbiosis.12

The specific nature of these intimate associations likely dictates the characteristics of the HGT events. Endosymbionts residing within germ cells, like *Wolbachia*, offer a relatively direct route for heritable HGT.14 In contrast, the intercellular connections formed by parasitic plant haustoria allow transfer between unrelated species but still require the transferred DNA to eventually reach the parasite's germline for long-term evolutionary impact.15 This implies that different selective pressures and integration challenges exist depending on whether the transfer occurs intracellularly or intercellularly, and whether it initially lands in somatic or germline-proximal tissues.

### **D. Other Potential Mechanisms**

Several other mechanisms might contribute to eukaryotic HGT, although their general frequency or significance may be less clear:

* **Phagocytosis:** The engulfment of prey cells by phagotrophic eukaryotes (primarily protists) could lead to the release of prey DNA within the predator's cell, potentially near the nucleus.3 While highly relevant for unicellular organisms, its role in HGT into the germline of multicellular plants and animals is less certain, though phagocytic immune cells exist.  
* **Grafting:** In plants, the artificial or natural fusion of tissues during grafting creates intimate contact that could theoretically allow for DNA exchange between the stock and scion.6  
* **Direct Cell Contact/Fusion:** While prokaryotic conjugation involves direct cell-cell transfer 1, analogous dedicated mechanisms are not well-defined in multicellular eukaryotes. However, cell fusion events occur naturally (e.g., fertilization) and might occur aberrantly. Mitochondrial HGT in plants, for instance, is thought to be potentially driven by the fusion of entire mitochondria from donor and recipient cells.28  
* **Transformation:** The uptake of free DNA from the environment, a major HGT route in bacteria 1, is generally considered less common or efficient in eukaryotes.2 However, it might be relevant under specific circumstances. For example, the compromised state of cell membranes during rehydration after severe desiccation (anhydrobiosis) in organisms like bdelloid rotifers has been proposed as a mechanism allowing the uptake of environmental DNA.29

## **IV. Horizontal Gene Transfer Shaping Plant Genomes**

HGT is increasingly recognized as a factor influencing the evolution and adaptation of plants, with transfers documented from microbial donors and between different plant lineages.6 While nuclear HGT occurs, the mitochondrial genome appears to be a particular hotspot for HGT events in the plant kingdom.15

### **A. Overview: HGT in the Plant Kingdom**

Evidence points to HGT playing roles in plant evolution, including potentially facilitating early land plant evolution and enabling adaptations like ferns acquiring photoreceptors from bryophytes to thrive in low-light conditions.15 Transfers often involve mtDNA, such as the movement of group I introns within the *coxI* gene or the incorporation of large fragments or even entire mitochondrial genomes from diverse sources.15

### **B. Case Study: The *Amborella* Mitochondrial Genome \- A Hotspot of HGT**

*Amborella trichopoda*, a unique shrub endemic to New Caledonia, holds a pivotal position as the sister lineage to all other extant flowering plants.30 Its genome, therefore, offers insights into the early evolution of angiosperms. Sequencing of its mitochondrial genome revealed a truly remarkable situation: an enormous genome size of approximately 3.9 megabases (Mb), vastly larger than typical plant mitochondrial genomes.31 This immense size is largely attributable to an unprecedented amount of foreign DNA acquired via HGT, estimated to correspond to roughly six entire mitochondrial genome equivalents.17

The origins of this horizontally acquired mtDNA are exceptionally diverse, including substantial contributions from other angiosperms, green algae, and, perhaps most strikingly, mosses.28 The moss-derived DNA is particularly notable. Phylogenetic and synteny analyses indicate that *Amborella* acquired sequences corresponding to nearly the entire mitochondrial genome of a moss.31 Further detailed analysis suggests this may have resulted from two independent whole-mitochondrial-genome transfer events from distinct moss lineages (one related to Hookeriales, the other sister to Ptychomniales), followed by recombination between these acquired genomes within the *Amborella* mitochondrion, and subsequent fragmentation and rearrangement.28 Despite the massive scale of these transfers, most of the foreign moss genes identified appear to be pseudogenes, suggesting they are not currently functional.31

Several factors might contribute to *Amborella*'s extraordinary accumulation of foreign mtDNA. The plant is often covered in epiphytes (including mosses and algae), providing a rich source of potential donor DNA. It also readily produces vegetative suckers from wounds, potentially facilitating the uptake of foreign material. Furthermore, *Amborella* might possess an unusually low rate of mitochondrial DNA loss or efficient integration mechanisms.32

The *Amborella* case dramatically underscores the capacity of plant mitochondria to incorporate and retain foreign genetic material on a massive scale. While the direct functional contribution of these specific moss and algal transfers appears limited due to pseudogenization, the phenomenon highlights the mitochondrion as a potential 'melting pot' or reservoir for genetic diversity from disparate sources. This high receptivity, perhaps linked to mitochondrial fusion dynamics or specific DNA repair pathways 28, makes plant mitochondria unique compartments for HGT. Even if not immediately functional, the accumulated foreign DNA could potentially influence mitochondrial evolution indirectly or serve as raw material for subsequent transfer to the nucleus over longer evolutionary timescales.

### **C. Case Study: Parasitic Plants \- HGT as an Adaptive Strategy**

Parasitic plants, such as members of the Orobanchaceae family (e.g., *Striga*, *Phelipanche*) and dodders (*Cuscuta*), provide some of the clearest examples of adaptive HGT in eukaryotes.15 Their haustorial connections create direct pathways for molecular exchange with their hosts.15 Transcriptome and genome analyses of parasitic Orobanchaceae have revealed numerous high-confidence HGT events, with genes transferred from their host plant lineages.15 One study identified 52 such events across three parasitic species.15

Crucially, the frequency of detected HGT events correlates positively with the degree of parasitic dependence (heterotrophy) of the plant species.15 Species that rely more heavily on their hosts for nutrition tend to show evidence of more HGT. This suggests that the intimate contact provided by the parasitic lifestyle facilitates transfer, and potentially, that there is selection favoring the retention of acquired genes.

The functional implications strongly support an adaptive role for HGT in these plants. Many of the horizontally acquired genes are actively expressed, often showing preferential expression in the haustorium – the very organ mediating the parasitic interaction.15 Furthermore, the predicted functions of these transferred genes are significantly biased towards roles likely beneficial for parasitism, including plant defense responses (potentially mimicking or manipulating host defenses), nutrient acquisition, metabolism, and development.15 Examples include genes encoding cysteine-rich receptor-like kinases, disease resistance proteins, proteasome subunits, and enzymes involved in amino acid metabolism.15 Specific documented cases include the transfer of the *ShContig9483* gene from *Sorghum* into the parasitic witchweed *Striga hermonthica* 25, and the acquisition of an albumin 1 KNOTTIN-like protein gene by dodder (*Cuscuta*) and broomrape (*Phelipanche*) from their legume hosts.25

In stark contrast to the *Amborella* mitochondrial scenario where widespread pseudogenization suggests limited direct function, HGT in parasitic plants appears to be a potent and targeted evolutionary mechanism. The combination of a clear physical mechanism (haustorial connections) and strong selective pressure to effectively exploit host resources seems to have driven the repeated acquisition and functional integration of host genes. This suggests HGT is not just a source of random genetic novelty but a key strategy shaping the co-evolutionary arms race between parasitic plants and their hosts.

### **D. Case Study: *Agrobacterium* T-DNA Transfers \- Nature's Genetic Engineers**

The bacterium *Agrobacterium tumefaciens* is well-known for its natural ability to transfer a specific segment of its DNA (the T-DNA, located on the Ti plasmid) into the genomes of host plants, inducing the formation of crown gall tumors.7 This natural genetic engineering system forms the basis of much plant biotechnology. Research has revealed that this process is not limited to laboratory manipulations or disease states. Evidence indicates that *Agrobacterium*\-mediated T-DNA transfers have occurred naturally in the evolutionary history of several plant lineages, with the transferred DNA becoming stably integrated into the host germline and inherited vertically thereafter.7 Such "natural GMOs" have been identified in species of *Nicotiana* (tobacco relatives), *Linaria* (toadflax), and notably *Ipomoea* (sweet potato and its wild relatives).7 Interestingly, in these cases, the integrated T-DNA genes often do not cause the typical disease symptoms associated with *Agrobacterium* infection. Instead, it is hypothesized that these naturally acquired genes may have been co-opted by the plants and potentially played roles in their subsequent evolution, although their precise functions remain largely under investigation.7

### **E. Case Study: Fungus-to-Plant and Fungus-to-Oomycete Transfers**

HGT events involving fungi as donors have also been documented. Transfers from fungi to plants have been implicated in angiosperm evolution, such as the movement of mitochondrial *coxI* introns.15 Perhaps more dramatic are the transfers identified from fungi into oomycetes.16 Oomycetes (or water molds), such as species of *Phytophthora* (responsible for diseases like potato late blight and sudden oak death), are fungus-like eukaryotes but belong to a distinct lineage (Stramenopiles). Despite this evolutionary distance, extensive HGT from true fungi into oomycetes has occurred.16 Genome-wide phylogenetic analyses revealed dozens of gene families transferred from fungi to oomycetes, potentially involving hundreds of individual genes.16

The functional significance of these fungus-to-oomycete transfers appears profound, particularly concerning the evolution of plant pathogenicity in oomycetes. A large proportion of the transferred genes encode secreted proteins, including enzymes capable of degrading plant cell walls (e.g., cellulases, pectinases), proteins involved in nutrient uptake (sugars, amino acids, phosphate), factors potentially involved in overcoming plant defense mechanisms, and effector proteins used to attack plant cells.16 In some pathogenic *Phytophthora* species, it is estimated that a substantial fraction (ranging from approximately 3% to over 7%) of their entire predicted secretome (the set of secreted proteins) consists of proteins derived from fungal HGT.16 This strongly suggests that the acquisition of genes from fungi, which often share similar saprophytic or pathogenic lifestyles involving the breakdown of plant tissues, was a key factor enabling the evolutionary success of plant parasitic oomycetes.16

## **V. Horizontal Gene Transfer Influencing Animal Evolution**

HGT also occurs in the animal kingdom, although its overall frequency and impact, particularly in vertebrates, are generally considered to be lower than in prokaryotes, protists, or even plants.2 Many documented cases involve invertebrates, potentially reflecting their diverse lifestyles, common associations with endosymbionts, different reproductive modes (including asexuality), and perhaps less stringent germline sequestration in some groups.8 Transfers from bacteria to animals appear largely confined to invertebrate lineages.14

### **A. Overview: HGT in the Animal Kingdom**

While less pervasive than in microbes, HGT has contributed novel genes and functions to various animal lineages. Phylogenetic analyses often reveal genes in animal genomes whose evolutionary history is incongruent with the species tree, pointing towards horizontal acquisition.8 These transfers have occurred from bacteria, fungi, plants, and other animals.

### **B. Case Study: Endosymbiont Contributions \- *Wolbachia* and Beyond**

As mentioned previously, transfers from endosymbionts represent a significant category of HGT in animals.14 The intracellular bacterium *Wolbachia* is a prime example, having transferred genetic material into the nuclear genomes of a wide range of its arthropod and nematode hosts.8 These hosts include insects like pea aphids, mosquitoes, the bean beetle (*Callosobruchus chinensis*), fruit flies (*Drosophila*), and parasitoid wasps, as well as various nematode worms.8 The scale of these transfers can sometimes be substantial; for instance, nearly a third of the *Wolbachia* genome was found integrated into the X chromosome of a strain of the bean beetle, likely resulting from a single large HGT event.18 The close, often intracellular association, particularly within host gonadal tissues, facilitates such transfers and increases the likelihood of germline integration and heritability.14 While the functional significance of many integrated *Wolbachia* fragments remains unclear, the potential exists for these sequences to be co-opted by the host.22 The "HGT ratchet" model, where gene transfer to the host nucleus followed by loss from the symbiont creates dependence, may also be applicable here.18

### **C. Case Study: Bdelloid Rotifers \- Asexuality, Desiccation, and HGT**

Bdelloid rotifers are microscopic freshwater invertebrates famous for two unusual biological features: their apparent long-term evolutionary persistence (tens of millions of years) without sexual reproduction (they are considered "ancient asexuals"), and their remarkable ability to survive extreme desiccation (anhydrobiosis) at any stage of their life cycle.8 Genome and transcriptome analyses of bdelloid species (e.g., *Adineta vaga*, *Adineta ricciae*, *Rotaria* species) have consistently revealed an extraordinarily high proportion of genes that appear to have been acquired via HGT from non-metazoan sources, primarily bacteria, but also fungi and plants.8 Estimates suggest that roughly 8% to 14% of the functional gene repertoire in bdelloids originates from such HGT events, a level significantly higher than observed in almost any other animal group studied to date.8 This high level of foreign gene acquisition appears to be a general feature of bdelloids, found in both desiccating and non-desiccating species, and is robustly supported by various analytical methods.19

A compelling hypothesis links the bdelloids' desiccation tolerance to their high levels of HGT.29 The process of rehydration after severe water loss is thought to temporarily compromise the integrity of cell membranes, making them permeable to large molecules, including fragments of foreign DNA present in their environment (e.g., from ingested bacteria or fungi).29 This foreign DNA could then be accidentally incorporated into the rotifer genome during the repair of DNA damage induced by desiccation and rehydration. Empirical studies have demonstrated that bdelloid rotifers can indeed readily internalize environmental DNA, lending support to this proposed mechanism.29

The evolutionary significance of this extensive HGT in bdelloids is thought to be profound. In the absence of sexual recombination, HGT may serve as an alternative source of genetic diversity, introducing novel genes and functions that allow bdelloids to adapt to changing environments and potentially overcome the long-term disadvantages typically associated with asexuality (such as the accumulation of deleterious mutations or reduced ability to adapt to co-evolving pathogens).8 Evidence suggests these acquired genes are functional and contribute significantly to bdelloid biology. For example, many HGT-derived genes are involved in metabolic processes, potentially expanding the range of substrates bdelloids can utilize.22 Furthermore, recent research demonstrated that hundreds of foreign genes, acquired from bacteria and fungi, are specifically upregulated when bdelloids are challenged with a fungal pathogen (*Rotiferophthora globospora*), suggesting HGT plays a major role in their immune defense.19 Some of these defense-related HGT genes encode proteins with functions not previously associated with animal immunity.19

While the high level of non-metazoan HGT in bdelloids is well-established 19, some related claims, such as the idea that HGT between different rotifer individuals might effectively replace sexual recombination, have been challenged due to potential issues with sample contamination in the underlying studies.36 Nevertheless, bdelloid rotifers stand out as a remarkable example where HGT from distant organisms appears to be a frequent and functionally integrated aspect of their biology, likely contributing significantly to their unique evolutionary success as ancient asexuals capable of surviving extreme conditions.

### **D. Case Study: Transposable Elements Mediating Animal HGT**

As in plants, TEs are important vectors for HGT in animals.

* ***Helitrons***: As previously noted, these elements exhibit extraordinary mobility, having transferred horizontally across diverse animal phyla (vertebrates like bats, lizards, lampreys; invertebrates like insects, nematodes, annelids, molluscs) and even into insect viruses.24 Their propensity to capture and amplify host gene fragments during transposition means their horizontal movement could directly facilitate the horizontal transfer of functional host genes between species.24  
* ***P elements***: The transfer of these DNA transposons from the *D. willistoni* group to *D. melanogaster* within the last century is a well-studied case of recent HTT.18  
* ***BovB***: These LINE elements have spread horizontally among a wide range of mammals (including ruminants, elephants, rodents, marsupials) and other vertebrates (reptiles, possibly fish), indicating frequent cross-species transmission.25  
* ***Wolbachia*****\-associated elements:** Some of the DNA transferred from *Wolbachia* to their hosts may include mobile elements originating from the bacterial genome.8

The widespread HTT observed for elements like Helitrons and BovB suggests that TEs can overcome species barriers more readily than cellular genes, potentially acting as pervasive agents of inter-genomic exchange in animals.

### **E. Case Study: The Tardigrade HGT Controversy**

Tardigrades, or "water bears," are microscopic invertebrates renowned for their ability to survive extreme environmental conditions, including desiccation, freezing, radiation, and the vacuum of space, by entering a state of suspended animation called cryptobiosis.10 In 2015, the first published draft genome of a tardigrade species, *Hypsibius dujardini*, made the startling claim that an unprecedented proportion of its genes – approximately 17.5%, or over 6,600 genes – were acquired via HGT from non-metazoan sources, primarily bacteria.10 The authors speculated that this massive influx of foreign genes might be key to the tardigrade's extraordinary resilience.23

However, this claim quickly became controversial. An independent group, also sequencing the *H. dujardini* genome (using animals from the same supplier), published results shortly after, reporting drastically different findings.23 Their analysis, using improved assembly methods and rigorous contamination filtering techniques, found evidence for only a very low level of HGT, estimating that at most 1-2% of tardigrade genes were derived from horizontal transfer.23 They demonstrated that the vast majority of the sequences identified as HGT candidates in the initial study were likely derived from bacterial and other microbial contaminants present in the tardigrade cultures, which had been mistakenly incorporated into the tardigrade genome assembly.10 Cross-comparison of the assemblies revealed that most putative HGTs resided on scaffolds identified as contaminants.23

The tardigrade HGT story serves as a critical cautionary tale in the field of genomics and evolutionary biology. It highlights the significant methodological challenges inherent in identifying genuine HGT events, particularly when sequencing organisms collected from environments rich in microbial life. It underscores the absolute necessity of employing stringent contamination detection and removal protocols, robust genome assembly techniques, and multiple lines of validation before concluding that large-scale HGT has occurred.10 While tardigrades remain fascinating for their extremotolerance, the idea that this resilience is primarily due to massive acquisition of foreign genes is not supported by current evidence. Their adaptations likely rely more on unique, vertically inherited genes and physiological mechanisms.37

### **F. Other Notable Examples in Animals**

Beyond endosymbionts, bdelloids, and TEs, several other intriguing cases of HGT have been reported in animals:

* **Carotenoid Biosynthesis:** Aphids possess genes for synthesizing carotenoids (pigments typically made by plants, fungi, and bacteria), which were acquired via HGT from fungi. These pigments contribute to aphid body color, which can influence interactions with predators and parasitoids.8 Similar fungal-derived carotenoid synthesis genes have also been reported in spider mites.8  
* **Antifreeze Proteins:** Certain fish species inhabiting cold waters, such as smelt and herring, possess highly similar antifreeze glycoprotein genes. Phylogenetic analyses suggest these genes may have spread between lineages via HGT, conferring resistance to freezing.8  
* **Nematodes:** Parasitic nematodes, particularly plant parasites, appear to have acquired genes beneficial for their lifestyle via HGT. Examples include genes encoding enzymes that degrade plant cell walls (e.g., cellulases, pectinases), likely acquired from bacteria or fungi.8 Some nematodes may have also acquired genes involved in vitamin B biosynthesis.8 The close proximity of the gut and ovary in some nematode groups might provide a pathway for foreign DNA ingested with food to potentially reach the germline.8  
* **Cnidarians:** Basal metazoans like the sea anemone *Nematostella vectensis* and *Hydra* live in close association with microbes. Genome analyses suggest they have acquired bacterial genes involved in novel metabolic pathways (e.g., parts of the shikimic acid and glyoxylate pathways), enzymes for modifying sugars, and genes encoding pore-forming toxins.8  
* **Sponges (Porifera):** Evidence suggests possible ancient HGT of bacterial genes involved in biomineralization processes in sponges.8  
* **Urochordates (Tunicates):** These marine invertebrates possess genes for cellulose biosynthesis, a capability typically found in plants and bacteria. Phylogenetic evidence points to the acquisition of these genes via HGT from bacteria.8

## **VI. Frequency, Barriers, and Detection Challenges in Eukaryotic HGT**

Understanding the true extent and impact of HGT in eukaryotes requires considering its variable frequency across lineages, the significant barriers that limit successful transfer, and the methodological hurdles involved in its detection.

### **A. Estimating the Scale and Frequency Across Lineages**

A consistent finding across numerous studies is that the overall frequency of HGT shaping eukaryotic genomes is considerably lower than that observed in prokaryotes.2 While estimates suggest over 80% of prokaryotic genes may have experienced HGT 11, the proportion in most animal genomes is estimated to be much lower, perhaps in the range of 0.5% to 2%.8

However, HGT frequency is far from uniform across the eukaryotic domain. Certain groups or genomic compartments exhibit significantly higher rates:

* **Unicellular Eukaryotes (Protists):** Generally experience more HGT than multicellular organisms, possibly due to phagocytosis and less stringent germline separation.2  
* **Parasitic Plants:** Show elevated levels of HGT from their hosts, correlated with their degree of dependency.15  
* **Bdelloid Rotifers:** Exhibit exceptionally high levels of HGT from non-metazoan sources, estimated at 8-14% of their genes.8  
* **Plant Mitochondria:** Can accumulate vast amounts of foreign DNA, as exemplified by *Amborella*.15  
* **Oomycetes:** Have acquired a significant portion of their secretome (\~3-8%) via HGT from fungi.16  
* **Complex Animals (especially Vertebrates):** Generally show the lowest frequencies of detectable HGT.8

This variability indicates that there is no single characteristic "eukaryotic HGT rate." Instead, the frequency is highly context-dependent, influenced by factors such as the organism's lineage, lifestyle (e.g., parasitism, asexuality, extremophily), cellular structure, reproductive mode, and the specific genomic compartment being considered (nucleus vs. organelles). The existence of HGT hotspots in certain groups strongly suggests that specific biological features or selective pressures in those contexts either facilitate transfer or favor the retention of acquired genes.

### **B. Barriers to Successful HGT in Eukaryotes**

The relative rarity of widespread, functional HGT in many eukaryotic lineages stems from a series of formidable barriers that foreign DNA must overcome:

* **Physical Barriers:** The initial entry into the cell is often hindered by structures like the cell wall (in plants and fungi) and the cell membrane. Subsequently, in eukaryotes, the nuclear envelope presents a major obstacle to accessing the primary genome.1  
* **Germline Integration:** For HGT to be heritable and evolutionarily significant in sexually reproducing animals with a segregated germline, the transferred DNA must integrate specifically into the germline cells. Integration into somatic cells is typically inconsequential for subsequent generations.14 This barrier is less pronounced in organisms with alternative reproductive strategies (asexuality, vegetative propagation) or less strict germline sequestration.14  
* **Host Defense Mechanisms:** Eukaryotic cells possess various defense systems to detect and eliminate foreign nucleic acids. These can include restriction enzyme systems (though less characterized than in bacteria), general DNA degradation pathways involving DNases 4, immune surveillance mechanisms, and RNA interference (RNAi) pathways that can target and silence foreign genetic elements like viruses and transposons.  
* **Genomic and Functional Integration:** Even if foreign DNA reaches the nucleus and avoids degradation, successful HGT requires stable integration into a host chromosome.1 Furthermore, for the gene to be functional, it must be compatible with the host's complex gene expression machinery. This includes having recognizable promoter and regulatory elements, being correctly transcribed, having its mRNA processed (including proper splicing if introns are present or acquired), being translated efficiently (potentially requiring adaptation to host codon usage bias and GC content), and yielding a correctly folded, non-toxic protein product.23

Successfully navigating this multi-layered gauntlet of physical, reproductive, defensive, and functional barriers is a low-probability event. Each step acts as a selective filter, drastically reducing the number of initial DNA entry events that ultimately result in stable, functional, and heritable HGT. This filtering process largely explains why such events are observed much less frequently in complex eukaryotes compared to the more permissive environment for gene exchange in prokaryotes.

### **C. Methodological Challenges: Detecting True HGT**

Accurately identifying genuine HGT events amidst the complexities of eukaryotic genomes presents significant methodological challenges:

* **Primary Detection Method:** The most common approach relies on identifying phylogenetic incongruence. This involves constructing a phylogenetic tree for a specific gene of interest and comparing its topology to the accepted phylogenetic tree of the species involved. If a gene from species A robustly groups within a clade of distantly related species B, rather than with its expected relatives, HGT from B to A (or vice versa) is inferred.8 This method requires accurate and well-supported species and gene trees.  
* **Supporting Evidence:** Phylogenetic inference is often supplemented by other methods. Parametric methods look for atypical sequence characteristics within a gene compared to the rest of the host genome, such as unusual GC content or codon usage patterns, which might reflect a foreign origin.2 Synteny analysis examines the conservation of gene order in the regions flanking the putative HGT candidate, comparing it to potential donor and recipient lineages.10 Patterns of gene presence/absence across related species can also provide clues.  
* **Major Confounder: Contamination:** Perhaps the most significant challenge, especially when sequencing organisms sampled directly from the environment or those harboring endosymbionts or microbiomes, is contamination. DNA from co-existing microbes can easily be co-extracted and sequenced along with the target organism's DNA. During genome assembly, these contaminant sequences can be mistakenly incorporated into the final assembly, leading to spurious HGT inferences.10 The tardigrade controversy serves as a stark reminder of this pitfall.10 Rigorous bioinformatic cleaning of sequence data and validation of assembly contiguity are essential.10  
* **Other Confounding Factors:** Distinguishing true HGT from other evolutionary phenomena that can cause gene tree/species tree incongruence can be difficult. Incomplete lineage sorting (ILS), where ancestral polymorphisms persist through speciation events, and differential gene loss across lineages can mimic HGT signals, particularly for ancient events or when taxon sampling is sparse.12 Varying rates of evolution across different lineages can also mislead phylogenetic analyses.12 Furthermore, pinpointing the exact donor lineage of an HGT event can be challenging, as the true donor may be extinct or simply not yet sequenced.13  
* **Validation Criteria:** Given these challenges, robust claims of HGT typically require multiple lines of evidence. This includes strong phylogenetic support, ideally corroborated by parametric or synteny data. Evidence of adaptation to the eukaryotic host environment, such as the presence of spliceosomal introns acquired after the transfer event, provides strong validation.10 Experimental validation, such as using PCR to confirm the linkage of the transferred gene to flanking native host genes across the integration junction, or using techniques like Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (FISH) to physically locate the gene on a host chromosome, adds further confidence.10 Evidence of functionality, such as confirmed gene expression (e.g., via RNA-seq data), also strengthens the case for significant HGT.15 Recognizing these difficulties, guidelines have been proposed emphasizing the need to initially treat genome assemblies as potential metagenomes and demanding rigorous validation before concluding HGT has occurred.10

## **VII. Functional Impact and Evolutionary Significance of Eukaryotic HGT**

Despite its relative rarity compared to prokaryotes, HGT has demonstrably contributed important novel functions and adaptive advantages to various eukaryotic lineages, playing a significant role in their evolution.

### **A. Acquisition of Novel Functions and Adaptive Advantages**

Horizontally transferred genes have enabled eukaryotes to acquire diverse capabilities, often linked directly to ecological challenges or opportunities:

* **Metabolic Innovation:** HGT has provided recipients with new metabolic pathways or enzymes. Examples include the acquisition of carotenoid biosynthesis genes from fungi by aphids, enabling pigment production 8; the transfer of cellulose synthase genes from bacteria to tunicates, allowing them to build their protective outer layer 8; the acquisition of genes for specific amino acid synthesis pathways or the glyoxylate cycle in cnidarians from bacteria 8; the potential acquisition of nitrogen utilization genes in nectar yeasts 13; and the transfer of ATP/ADP translocases, likely from plants, to intracellular parasitic bacteria like *Chlamydia* and *Rickettsia*, facilitating energy parasitism.12  
* **Stress Resistance:** Genes conferring tolerance to environmental extremes have been implicated in HGT. While the massive HGT initially proposed for tardigrades is now discounted 23, HGT might still contribute to the stress tolerance of bdelloid rotifers.8 A clearer example is the likely HGT of antifreeze protein genes between fish species, providing adaptation to cold marine environments.8  
* **Parasitism and Pathogenicity:** HGT has been a major factor in the evolution of parasitic lifestyles. Parasitic plants acquired genes from their hosts involved in defense manipulation, nutrient uptake, and haustorium function.15 Oomycete plant pathogens acquired fungal genes encoding cell wall degrading enzymes, effectors, and defense-related proteins, crucial for their virulence.16 Plant-parasitic nematodes obtained genes for breaking down plant cell walls from microbes.8 Pathogenic bacteria like *Chlamydia* acquired proteases possibly involved in pathogenesis.12 Conversely, bdelloid rotifers utilize hundreds of HGT-derived genes to defend against fungal pathogens.19  
* **Toxin Resistance/Production:** HGT can mediate the transfer of resistance mechanisms, such as the transfer of eukaryotic-type isoleucyl-tRNA synthetases to bacteria, conferring resistance to certain antibiotics.12 It can also involve the acquisition of toxin genes, like the bacterial pore-forming toxins found in cnidarians.8  
* **Niche Adaptation:** HGT can facilitate the colonization of new ecological niches. The acquisition of a bryophyte photoreceptor enabled ferns to adapt to low-light forest understories.15 The evolution of plant parasitism in Orobanchaceae, oomycetes, and nematodes was heavily influenced by HGT.8 Adaptation to environmental extremes or specific nutrient sources can also be driven by HGT.13

A recurring theme is that HGT often provides solutions to specific ecological problems or opens up new ecological opportunities. It allows for rapid evolutionary leaps by introducing pre-adapted functional modules, a process potentially much faster than relying solely on the gradual accumulation of mutations and selection within the vertically inherited gene pool.14 This capacity for rapid acquisition of complex traits makes HGT a particularly potent force in scenarios involving strong selective pressures, such as host-parasite co-evolution or adaptation to novel or extreme environments.

### **B. Role in Diversification and Genome Evolution**

Beyond conferring specific adaptive traits, HGT contributes to eukaryotic evolution in broader ways:

* **Source of Genetic Variation:** HGT introduces novel genes and alleles into recipient lineages, increasing their genetic repertoire and adaptive potential. This may be particularly important for lineages with limited genetic variation from other sources, such as ancient asexuals like bdelloid rotifers.8  
* **Gene Family Expansion:** HGT can introduce entirely new gene families or add members (paralogs) to existing families, contributing to genomic complexity.12  
* **Genome Restructuring:** The horizontal transfer of TEs is a significant factor influencing genome size, structure, and evolution. TE insertions can disrupt genes, alter regulation, create new exons, and facilitate genomic rearrangements.2 The unusual rearranged genome structure of the bdelloid rotifer *Adineta vaga* might be linked to its long-term asexuality and TE activity.34  
* **Impact on Phylogeny:** HGT events complicate the reconstruction of evolutionary history. The incongruence between gene trees and species trees caused by HGT highlights the limitations of representing evolution solely as a bifurcating tree, suggesting that network-like connections are relevant even in eukaryotes, albeit less densely than in prokaryotes.3

### **C. Contrasting Evolutionary Impact: Eukaryotes vs. Prokaryotes**

While HGT is significant in both domains, its overall role and impact differ considerably:

* **Prokaryotes:** HGT is a fundamental, ongoing, and pervasive process that shapes core aspects of prokaryotic biology, including metabolism, antibiotic resistance, virulence, and overall genome content and structure. It affects a large proportion of genes and lineages, acting as arguably a dominant mode of evolutionary innovation alongside vertical descent.2  
* **Eukaryotes:** In contrast, HGT in eukaryotes appears more episodic, lineage-specific, and constrained by numerous barriers. While it can be transformative for particular lineages (e.g., parasitic plants, bdelloids) or specific traits (e.g., carotenoid synthesis, antifreeze), it seems to play a less dominant role in shaping the *overall* gene content and core evolutionary trajectory of most multicellular eukaryotes compared to established mechanisms like vertical inheritance, mutation, gene duplication, and sexual recombination.2 Its impact in many eukaryotic groups might be characterized as more qualitative – introducing key innovations sporadically – rather than quantitative – affecting the sheer number and identity of the majority of genes.

## **VIII. Synthesis: The Role of HGT in the Eukaryotic Tree of Life**

### **A. HGT as a Distinct Evolutionary Force in Eukaryotes**

The accumulated evidence unequivocally establishes HGT as a genuine evolutionary mechanism operating within the eukaryotic domain, including in complex multicellular plants and animals.7 While generally rarer and more complex than in prokaryotes, it is not merely a biological curiosity. Key findings highlight its significance: diverse mechanisms, including mediation by TEs, viruses, and intimate biological associations (parasitism, endosymbiosis), facilitate gene flow across species boundaries. HGT has had a demonstrable impact in specific lineages, such as the massive accumulation of foreign DNA in plant mitochondria (e.g., *Amborella*), the adaptive acquisition of host genes by parasitic plants, and the extensive incorporation of non-metazoan genes in bdelloid rotifers. Functionally, HGT has contributed critical adaptive traits related to metabolism, stress resistance, pathogenicity, and niche specialization.

### **B. Comparison with Other Evolutionary Mechanisms**

HGT represents a distinct pathway for generating evolutionary novelty in eukaryotes, complementing other fundamental mechanisms. Vertical inheritance provides continuity, while mutation generates new allelic variation within existing genes. Gene duplication creates raw material for functional divergence and the evolution of new gene functions. Sexual recombination shuffles existing genetic variation within populations. Polyploidy can lead to rapid genomic changes and reproductive isolation. HGT uniquely allows for the acquisition of entirely novel genes or functional modules from distantly related organisms, potentially enabling rapid adaptation or major evolutionary transitions that might be difficult or slow to achieve through other means.14 While mutation and recombination provide a continuous source of variation across the genome, the impact of HGT in many eukaryotes appears more sporadic but potentially more transformative when it involves the acquisition of a key adaptive trait.

### **C. Concluding Remarks and Future Directions**

Our understanding of the frequency, mechanisms, and impact of HGT in eukaryotes is a rapidly evolving field, continually being reshaped by the influx of genomic data from an ever-wider range of organisms.3 It is clear that HGT is not restricted to microbes but plays a tangible role in the evolution of plants and animals.

Future research must continue to employ rigorous methodologies, including robust contamination control, sophisticated phylogenetic analyses, and experimental validation, to accurately map the extent of HGT across the eukaryotic tree of life.3 Refining bioinformatic pipelines for HGT detection and distinguishing it from confounding factors like contamination and ILS remains crucial.10 Further investigation into the precise mechanisms of transfer, particularly eukaryote-to-eukaryote transfer 26, and the processes governing the functional integration and regulation of horizontally acquired genes is needed.

In conclusion, while vertical inheritance undoubtedly remains the primary mode of gene transmission in eukaryotes, HGT serves as an important supplementary evolutionary force. Although constrained by significant barriers and less pervasive than in prokaryotes, it has undeniably facilitated adaptation, driven innovation, and contributed to the diversification of specific eukaryotic lineages. Recognizing the role of HGT adds crucial complexity and dynamism to our understanding of eukaryotic evolution, revealing a Tree of Life occasionally interwoven with threads of horizontal genetic exchange.
