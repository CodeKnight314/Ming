## **1\. Introduction**

### **1.1. Overview of First-Price Sealed-Bid Auctions**

The first-price sealed-bid auction represents a fundamental mechanism for resource allocation where strategic considerations are paramount. In this format, potential buyers submit their bids simultaneously in sealed envelopes. The auctioneer opens the bids, identifies the highest bidder, and awards the item to that bidder, who then pays the amount they bid.1 This contrasts sharply with other common auction types. For instance, in a second-price sealed-bid (Vickrey) auction, the highest bidder wins but pays the second-highest bid, which incentivizes truthful bidding as a dominant strategy.3 In open ascending-bid (English) auctions, prices rise until only one bidder remains, while in open descending-bid (Dutch) auctions, the price falls until a bidder accepts.3 The first-price mechanism's ruleвЂ”paying one's own bid upon winningвЂ”forces bidders to engage in "bid shading": bidding below their true valuation to secure a positive profit if they win, while balancing this against the risk of losing the auction by bidding too low.1 This inherent strategic complexity makes the first-price auction a rich subject for game-theoretic analysis and explains its continued relevance in various practical settings, including government procurement, resource leasing, and increasingly, online advertising auctions where credibility and speed are crucial.4

### **1.2. The Significance of Asymmetry**

While much of the foundational auction theory focuses on symmetric settings where bidders are statistically identical, real-world scenarios often involve asymmetries.10 In the context of Independent Private Value (IPV) auctions, asymmetry typically means that bidders' private valuations for the item are drawn from different probability distributions.14 One bidder might be perceived as "stronger," drawing values from a distribution that stochastically dominates the "weaker" bidder's distribution, implying a higher likelihood of having a high valuation.10 Asymmetries can arise from various sources: differing information sets, varying cost structures, divergent risk preferences (though the standard model assumes risk neutrality, risk aversion can implicitly affect bidding behavior), or strategic factors like prior collusion among a subset of bidders, which creates an asymmetric entity facing individual bidders.11 Analyzing asymmetric auctions is considerably more complex than the symmetric case but offers a more realistic representation of many competitive bidding environments.10 Understanding equilibrium behavior under asymmetry is crucial for predicting outcomes, assessing efficiency, comparing expected revenues across different auction designs, and analyzing strategic interactions like collusion.10

### **1.3. The Problem Statement**

This report addresses the specific problem of characterizing the equilibrium bidding behavior in a first-price sealed-bid auction involving two bidders with independent private values drawn from potentially different, commonly known distributions. The primary objective is to delineate the methodology for finding the Bayesian Nash Equilibrium (BNE) bidding strategies, denoted by ОІ1вЂ‹(v1вЂ‹) and ОІ2вЂ‹(v2вЂ‹), where viвЂ‹ represents the private valuation of bidder i. ## **3\. Bayesian Nash Equilibrium (BNE) in Asymmetric Auctions**

### **3.1. The BNE Concept**

Given the incomplete information structure inherent in auctions (bidders do not know their rivals' private values), the standard solution concept employed is the Bayesian Nash Equilibrium (BNE).4 A BNE is a profile of strategies, one for each player, such that each player's strategy maximizes their expected payoff given their private information (their type or valuation) and their beliefs about the other players' types and strategies. These beliefs are formed based on the commonly known prior distributions of types (F1вЂ‹ and F2вЂ‹ in our case).4 In essence, each bidder chooses their best course of action, assuming all other bidders are also acting optimally according to the equilibrium strategies.

### **3.3. Properties of Equilibrium Strategies**

Under standard assumptions, particularly the IPV framework and continuous valuation distributions, equilibrium bidding strategies in first-price auctions exhibit several key properties:

* **Monotonicity:** Equilibrium bidding strategies ОІiвЂ‹(viвЂ‹) are typically strictly increasing functions of the bidder's valuation viвЂ‹.3 Intuitively, a bidder with a higher valuation has more "room" to bid higher while still potentially making a profit, and a stronger incentive to increase their probability of winning. This monotonicity is crucial because it ensures that the inverse bid function, П•iвЂ‹(b)=ОІiв€’1вЂ‹(b), is well-defined.6 The function П•iвЂ‹(b) represents the valuation (type) of bidder i who would submit a bid b in equilibrium. * **Continuity and Differentiability:** Equilibrium strategies are often assumed, and under suitable conditions on the distribution functions FiвЂ‹, can be proven to be continuous and differentiable (almost everywhere).14 This smoothness property allows the application of calculus to derive the first-order conditions necessary for characterizing the equilibrium.15  
* **No Atoms (Typically):** When the underlying valuation distributions FiвЂ‹ are continuous (atomless), the equilibrium bidding strategies typically result in bid distributions that are also atomless, meaning no specific bid value occurs with a positive probability.20 An exception can occur at the lower bound of the bidding support if there is a positive probability mass (an atom) in the valuation distribution at the lowest possible value.19

## **6\. Solving the System: Boundary Conditions and Existence/Uniqueness**

### **6.1. The Need for Boundary Conditions**

A system of first-order ODEs, like the one derived in Section 5.3, generally admits an infinite number of solutions. To identify the specific solution corresponding to the auction equilibrium, boundary conditions are required.10 These conditions impose constraints on the values of the inverse bid functions П•iвЂ‹(b) at certain points, typically at the lower and upper ends of the range of possible equilibrium bids. Because conditions are usually specified at both ends of the bid interval, this is often referred to as a two-point boundary value problem (BVP).10 A significant complication, however, is that the upper boundary of the bid interval, the maximum equilibrium bid bЛ‰, is typically not known in advance and must be determined as part of the solution process.33 This makes it a free boundary value problem.

### **6.2. Condition at the Lower Bound**

The condition at the lower end of the bid support reflects the behavior of bidders with the lowest possible valuations. In the absence of reserve prices or other complexities, a bidder with the lowest possible valuation vi,minвЂ‹ anticipates making zero profit and typically submits the lowest possible bid, which is often zero.19

* **Common Lower Bound:** If both bidders share the same minimum valuation, v1,minвЂ‹=v2,minвЂ‹=vminвЂ‹ (often assumed to be 0 for simplicity), the standard boundary condition is that the lowest equilibrium bid bminвЂ‹ (often 0\) corresponds to this minimum valuation for both bidders: П•1вЂ‹(bminвЂ‹)=vminвЂ‹andП•2вЂ‹(bminвЂ‹)=vminвЂ‹ If vminвЂ‹=0 and bminвЂ‹=0, this becomes П•1вЂ‹(0)=0 and П•2вЂ‹(0)=0.33  
* **Different Lower Bounds:** If v1,minвЂ‹=v2,minвЂ‹, the situation is more complex. The bidder with the strictly lower minimum valuation might find it optimal to bid bminвЂ‹ (e.g., 0\) not just at vi,minвЂ‹ but over a range of low valuations, leading to a flat spot in their bidding function at the bottom.27 This requires careful handling of the boundary conditions.  
* **Technical Singularity:** A critical issue arises if П•iвЂ‹(0)=0. In this case, the term (П•jвЂ‹(0)в€’0) in the denominator of the ODE for П•iвЂІвЂ‹(0) becomes zero. This singularity means the ODE system is ill-defined at b=0, and standard numerical methods for solving IVPs starting from b=0 (forward integration) will fail or become unstable.11 This technical difficulty motivates the use of backward integration or other specialized numerical techniques.

### **6.3. Condition at the Upper Bound**

The condition at the upper end of the bid support relates to the behavior of bidders with the highest possible valuations.

* **Common Upper Bid:** Assuming the supports \[v1,minвЂ‹,v1,maxвЂ‹\] and \[v2,minвЂ‹,v2,maxвЂ‹\] overlap sufficiently, rationality implies that in equilibrium, the highest bid submitted by either bidder must be the same. If bidder 1's maximum bid were strictly higher than bidder 2's, bidder 1 could lower their bid slightly without affecting their probability of winning (which would already be 1 against the highest type of bidder 2), thereby increasing their profit. Let this common maximum equilibrium bid be denoted by bЛ‰.6  
* **Valuation at Upper Bound:** This maximum bid bЛ‰ must correspond to the highest possible valuation for each bidder. Therefore, the upper boundary condition is: П•1вЂ‹(bЛ‰)=v1,maxвЂ‹andП•2вЂ‹(bЛ‰)=v2,maxвЂ‹ .6  
* **Unknown Boundary bЛ‰:** As mentioned, the value of bЛ‰ is generally unknown *a priori* and depends on the distributions F1вЂ‹ and F2вЂ‹. Finding bЛ‰ is part of solving the BVP.33

### **6.4. Complications with Different Supports**

The assumption of a common maximum bid bЛ‰ relies implicitly on the supports of the valuations overlapping appropriately. If the supports are disjoint, or one is strictly contained within the other, or they only touch at an endpoint, the logic breaks down. For example, if v1,maxвЂ‹\<v2,minвЂ‹, bidder 2 knows they will always win against bidder 1 if they bid slightly above v1,maxвЂ‹. More generally, significantly different supports can lead to "bid bifurcation," where the maximum bid submitted by the bidder with the lower maximum valuation (vi,maxвЂ‹) is strictly less than the maximum bid bЛ‰ submitted by the bidder with the higher maximum valuation.12 This scenario requires a modification of the upper boundary conditions and significantly complicates the analysis, potentially requiring different ODE systems or conditions over different ranges of bids.12

### **6.5. Existence and Uniqueness**

* **Existence:** Despite the complexities, the existence of a Bayesian Nash Equilibrium for the asymmetric first-price auction is generally well-established under standard assumptions on the distribution functions FiвЂ‹ (e.g., continuity, common support or well-behaved differing supports).4 Proofs often rely on advanced techniques, such as showing the existence of a solution to the BVP for the ODEs (often using continuity arguments related to the unknown boundary bЛ‰ 19), applying general fixed-point theorems to strategy spaces, or constructing equilibria as limits of equilibria in discrete approximations of the game.4 The equilibrium typically involves essentially pure, strictly increasing strategies.  
* **Uniqueness:** Uniqueness of the BNE is not guaranteed in general.24 While the symmetric first-price auction typically has a unique symmetric equilibrium, asymmetry can introduce the possibility of multiple equilibria. However, uniqueness has been proven under certain additional conditions. For example, if one distribution FOSD the other, uniqueness often holds.16 Uniqueness is also established if the distributions have a mass point at the common lower bound of the support 5, or for certain specific families of distributions (e.g., when both distributions are members of a two-distribution set satisfying certain properties, as explored by Lebrun 19). Maskin and Riley have also provided significant contributions to understanding conditions for existence and uniqueness.4 The potential for non-uniqueness means that finding one solution to the ODE system satisfying the boundary conditions might not capture all possible equilibrium outcomes.

The boundary conditions are thus critical for pinning down the relevant solution to the ODEs. They encapsulate how the bidders' behavior is constrained by the limits of their valuations. The fact that the upper boundary bЛ‰ is unknown makes solving the system a non-standard BVP, often requiring iterative numerical methods like shooting or relaxation. The potential for non-uniqueness adds another layer of complexity to the analysis.

## **7\. Analytical vs. Numerical Solutions**

### **7.1. General Intractability of Analytical Solutions**

A central finding in the study of asymmetric first-price auctions is that obtaining general, closed-form analytical solutions for the equilibrium bidding strategies ОІiвЂ‹(viвЂ‹) or their inverses П•iвЂ‹(b) is typically impossible when bidders' valuations are drawn from arbitrary, different continuous distributions F1вЂ‹ and F2вЂ‹.10 The primary reason lies in the complexity of the system of coupled, non-linear ordinary differential equations derived in Section 5\. Standard integration techniques usually fail to yield explicit formulae for П•iвЂ‹(b) for general F1вЂ‹ and F2вЂ‹.

### **7.2. Known Analytical Solutions for Specific Distributions**

Despite the general intractability, analytical solutions have been successfully derived for certain specific and mathematically convenient families of distribution functions. These serve as important benchmarks and provide concrete illustrations of equilibrium behavior. Notable examples include:

* **Uniform Distributions:** This is perhaps the most studied case for analytical solutions. If v1вЂ‹в€јU\[v1,minвЂ‹,v1,maxвЂ‹\] and v2вЂ‹в€јU\[v2,minвЂ‹,v2,maxвЂ‹\], closed-form solutions for П•iвЂ‹(b) can often be found, although the formulae can be complex, involving logarithms and exponentials, and depend on whether there is a minimum bid and how the supports overlap.14 The example in 27 shows a case where the solutions are linear and logarithmic components. Fibich & Gavish (2007) provide detailed solutions for various configurations of uniform supports.25  
* **Power Distributions:** Cases where FiвЂ‹(v)=(v/vmaxвЂ‹)О±iвЂ‹ for vв€€\[0,vmaxвЂ‹\] have also been solved analytically.4 In some instances, particularly when supports are common, the equilibrium strategies are linear, ОІiвЂ‹(v)=ciвЂ‹v, where the constants ciвЂ‹ depend on the exponents О±1вЂ‹,О±2вЂ‹.6  
* **Exponential Distributions:** While less commonly cited in the provided snippets for full analytical solutions in the asymmetric case, specific examples might exist in the broader literature.

These specific solutions are valuable for building intuition but cover only a narrow range of possible asymmetries.

### **7.3. Numerical Solution Techniques**

Given the rarity of analytical solutions, numerical methods become indispensable tools for analyzing asymmetric first-price auctions with more general distribution functions or for applications in empirical research where specific functional forms might not be appropriate.10 Several techniques have been developed to approximate the solution to the BVP defined by the ODEs and boundary conditions:

* **Shooting Methods:** These methods attempt to solve the BVP by treating it as an initial value problem (IVP). A common approach is "backward shooting".4  
  1. Guess a value for the unknown upper bid boundary bЛ‰.  
  2. Use the upper boundary conditions П•1вЂ‹(bЛ‰)=v1,maxвЂ‹,П•2вЂ‹(bЛ‰)=v2,maxвЂ‹ as initial values at b=bЛ‰.  
  3. Integrate the system of ODEs backward from b=bЛ‰ down to b=bminвЂ‹ (usually 0).  
  4. Check if the solution satisfies the lower boundary conditions (e.g., П•1вЂ‹(0)=v1,minвЂ‹,П•2вЂ‹(0)=v2,minвЂ‹).  
  5. If the lower boundary conditions are not met, adjust the initial guess for bЛ‰ and repeat the integration until convergence. While conceptually straightforward, shooting methods are known to be numerically unstable for this problem, particularly due to the singularity near b=0. Solutions can be highly sensitive to the initial guess bЛ‰, and standard algorithms may fail to converge or produce inaccurate results.11 Fibich and Gavish (2011) explicitly proved the inherent numerical instability of shooting methods for asymmetric auctions.17  
* **Polynomial / Spline Approximation Methods:** To overcome the instability of shooting, researchers have developed methods that approximate the unknown functions globally. One approach is to represent the inverse bid functions П•iвЂ‹(b) as polynomials or splines (piecewise polynomials) with unknown coefficients.10  
  1. Define П•iвЂ‹(b)в‰€в€‘k=0KвЂ‹cikвЂ‹PkвЂ‹(b), where PkвЂ‹(b) are basis functions (e.g., standard polynomials bk, or Chebyshev polynomials) and cikвЂ‹ are coefficients to be determined.  
  2. Choose a set of collocation points within the bid interval \[bminвЂ‹,bЛ‰\] (where bЛ‰ might also need to be determined).  
  3. Find the coefficients cikвЂ‹ (and potentially bЛ‰) that minimize some measure of error in satisfying the ODEs and the boundary conditions at the collocation points. This approach transforms the differential equation problem into an algebraic one of solving for the coefficients. Examples include work by Bajari (2001) and Hubbard & Paarsch (2009).13  
* **Taylor Series Expansion Methods:** This is a sophisticated variant of approximation methods that uses local Taylor series expansions to represent the solution.10  
  1. Develop Taylor series expansions for П•iвЂ‹(b) and related auxiliary functions around multiple points in the bid interval.  
  2. Use the ODE system to derive recurrence relations that link the coefficients of the Taylor series at different orders.  
  3. Piece together these local approximations to construct a highly accurate global solution.  
  4. The unknown boundary bЛ‰ is typically found using an outer optimization loop that ensures the boundary conditions are met. The algorithm developed by Perrigne and Vuong (described in 10) exemplifies this approach, offering high accuracy and robustness compared to shooting methods.  
* **Other Methods:** Other numerical strategies include fixed-point iterative methods applied to discretized versions of the problem 13 and perturbation methods that analyze deviations from known solutions (e.g., the symmetric case).26

The choice between these numerical methods involves trade-offs in terms of implementation complexity, computational speed, stability, and accuracy.10 The documented instability of shooting methods has spurred the development and adoption of more robust approximation techniques, particularly Taylor series and polynomial methods, which are crucial for obtaining reliable results in theoretical explorations and especially in empirical applications where the auction model might be nested within larger estimation procedures.10

## **9\. Conclusion**

### **9.1. Summary of Methodology**

The analysis of first-price sealed-bid auctions with two asymmetric, independent private value bidders follows a well-established, albeit mathematically demanding, game-theoretic methodology. The core approach involves:

1. **Modeling:** Formally defining the auction environment, including players, asymmetric valuation distributions (F1вЂ‹,F2вЂ‹), IPV information structure, first-price rules, and risk-neutral payoffs.  
2. **Equilibrium Concept:** Applying the Bayesian Nash Equilibrium (BNE) concept, where each bidder's strategy ОІiвЂ‹(viвЂ‹) maximizes their expected payoff given the opponent's strategy.  
3. **Optimization:** Formulating the expected payoff for each bidder as a function of their valuation and bid, involving the opponent's distribution and inverse bid function.  
4. **Characterization:** Deriving the first-order conditions (FOCs) from the payoff maximization problem.  
5. **Transformation:** Converting the FOCs into a system of coupled, first-order ordinary differential equations (ODEs) governing the inverse equilibrium bid functions П•iвЂ‹(b).  
6. **Solution:** Solving this system of ODEs subject to appropriate boundary conditions derived from bidder behavior at the lowest and highest valuations. This typically constitutes a non-standard boundary value problem, often with an unknown upper bid boundary bЛ‰. The solution yields the inverse bid functions П•iвЂ‹(b), which can be inverted to find the equilibrium strategies ОІiвЂ‹(viвЂ‹).

### **9.2. Key Challenges and Insights**

Several significant challenges and insights emerge from this analysis:

* **Asymmetry Complexity:** Moving from symmetric to asymmetric bidders dramatically increases the analytical complexity. Simple closed-form solutions, common in symmetric models, become exceptionally rare.10  
* **Central Role of ODEs:** The system of coupled ODEs provides the fundamental mathematical characterization of the equilibrium. However, the non-linearity and coupling make these systems difficult to solve analytically.6  
* **Boundary Value Problem:** Solving the ODEs requires careful specification of boundary conditions. The fact that the upper bid boundary bЛ‰ is typically unknown adds complexity, requiring iterative or specialized solution techniques.33 Furthermore, differences in valuation supports can lead to bid bifurcation, further complicating boundary conditions.12  
* **Numerical Methods:** Due to the lack of general analytical solutions, numerical methods are essential for practical analysis and empirical work. However, standard techniques like shooting methods suffer from instability due to singularities in the ODE system, necessitating the use of more robust approximation methods like polynomial or Taylor series expansions.10 The accuracy of these numerical solutions is critical, as poor approximations can lead to incorrect conclusions.17  
* **Existence vs. Uniqueness:** While the existence of an equilibrium is generally guaranteed under standard conditions, uniqueness is not assured and depends on specific properties of the valuation distributions.5

### **9.3. Broader Implications**

Understanding equilibrium behavior in asymmetric first-price auctions has implications beyond the specific model:

* **Revenue Comparisons:** Asymmetry breaks the standard revenue equivalence between different auction formats. Analyzing the ODE solutions allows for comparisons of expected revenue between, for instance, first-price and second-price or English auctions under asymmetry, often showing that the first-price auction can yield higher revenue under certain conditions (e.g., FOSD, risk aversion).10  
* **Efficiency:** Asymmetric auctions are typically inefficient, meaning the bidder with the highest valuation does not always win the item.13 The ODE solutions can be used to quantify the degree of inefficiency.  
* **Collusion and Information:** Asymmetry naturally arises when subsets of bidders collude, forming a stronger entity.11 The framework can also be adapted to study scenarios with asymmetric information beyond differing value distributions, such as when one bidder has superior information about a common value component or when information rents are considered.18

### **9.4. Final Remarks**

The first-price sealed-bid auction with asymmetric bidders serves as a canonical example of strategic interaction under incomplete information. Its analysis demands a rigorous application of game theory, leading to a characterization based on a system of ordinary differential equations. While general analytical solutions remain elusive, the theoretical framework provides the foundation for powerful numerical techniques that allow for the exploration of complex, realistic bidding environments. The interplay between strategic incentives, distributional assumptions, mathematical properties of the resulting differential equations, and the nuances of numerical computation makes this a challenging but rewarding area of microeconomic theory.
