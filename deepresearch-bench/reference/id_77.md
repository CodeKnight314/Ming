# **The Role of Need for Closure in Misinformation Acceptance: A Cognitive-Motivational Analysis**

## **1\. Introduction**

The contemporary information landscape is characterized by an unprecedented volume and velocity of information flow, particularly through digital channels and social media platforms.1 Within this environment, the proliferation of misinformation—defined as false or inaccurate information disseminated regardless of the intent to deceive 2—has emerged as a significant societal challenge.7 Concerns are widespread regarding the potential for misinformation to inflict harm across various domains, undermining public health initiatives (e.g., promoting vaccine hesitancy, hindering pandemic response 10), distorting political processes (e.g., interfering with elections, exacerbating polarization 1), eroding trust in institutions 7, and potentially inciting real-world harms like violence or property destruction.10

Central to the impact of misinformation is the phenomenon of "misinformation acceptance," the psychological process whereby individuals come to believe false claims, incorporating them into their memories and knowledge structures.4 Understanding why some individuals readily accept misinformation while others critically evaluate or reject it is paramount.7 While factors related to the information itself (e.g., repetition, emotional content, source cues 4) and the information environment (e.g., social media algorithms 12) play a role, individual differences in cognitive processing and motivation are also critical determinants of susceptibility.7

One such individual difference that has garnered significant research attention is the "Need for Cognitive Closure" (NFC). NFC refers to a fundamental epistemic motivation—an individual's desire for clear, definite answers and an aversion toward ambiguity and uncertainty.30 It represents a motivated tendency to seek out information specifically to resolve ambiguity.32 Given that misinformation often presents itself in contexts fraught with uncertainty (e.g., public health crises, complex political events) and frequently offers simplistic, definitive narratives, NFC emerges as a potentially crucial factor influencing its acceptance.

The pervasiveness of misinformation and its documented impact necessitate a deep understanding of the psychological factors rendering individuals vulnerable. While widespread concern exists 1, some evidence suggests that the *average* individual's exposure to outright fake news might be relatively limited 9, though exposure can be highly concentrated among certain groups or habitual sharers.12 However, the psychological impact of misinformation can be potent even with limited exposure. Mechanisms like the "illusory truth effect," where mere repetition increases perceived accuracy 5, mean that even brief encounters with falsehoods can foster belief.5 Furthermore, beliefs, even those based on misinformation, can demonstrably influence attitudes, intentions, and behaviors 10, particularly in high-stakes domains like health decisions 10 and political actions.15 This apparent gap between average exposure levels and potential impact underscores the critical importance of investigating individual susceptibility factors like NFC. Understanding *why* certain individuals are more prone to accept misinformation, even if encountered infrequently, is vital because their subsequent beliefs and actions can have disproportionate consequences for themselves and society, especially if these individuals play a role in further disseminating the false information.12

This report aims to provide a comprehensive, evidence-based analysis of the role of the Need for Cognitive Closure in misinformation acceptance. Drawing upon the provided psychological research, it will synthesize findings regarding the conceptualization of NFC and misinformation acceptance, review the empirical evidence linking the two, elucidate the underlying psychological mechanisms, identify key moderating factors that shape this relationship, explore the real-world implications across critical societal domains, and compare the relative influence of NFC against other relevant cognitive predictors. Ultimately, this analysis seeks to provide a nuanced understanding of the complexities and boundary conditions governing NFC's role in the contemporary challenge of misinformation, contributing to the knowledge base required for developing effective countermeasures.14

## **2\. Defining the Need for Closure (NFC)**

The Need for Cognitive Closure (NFC), sometimes referred to interchangeably as Need for Closure 32, is a foundational construct in social psychology representing a fundamental epistemic motivation.37 At its core, NFC is defined as an individual's desire for a clear, firm, unambiguous answer or resolution to a question, problem, or situation, standing in contrast to enduring states of ambiguity or uncertainty.30 It is crucial to understand NFC not merely as a preference but as a *motivation*—a drive to actively seek out information or arrive at judgments that avert the discomfort associated with ambiguity.32 This motivation stems from a fundamental human desire for predictability and the inherent discomfort experienced when faced with uncertainty.31

This core motivation manifests in several key characteristics and cognitive tendencies. Central among these is a pronounced **aversion to ambiguity and uncertainty**. Individuals high in NFC tend to dislike, avoid, or feel uncomfortable in situations that are uncertain, lack clear answers, or are open-ended.44 This psychological "ambiguity intolerance"—the tendency to perceive ambiguous situations as undesirable 47—is closely related to NFC and should be distinguished from the economic concept of "ambiguity aversion," which refers to a preference for known risks over unknown risks in decision-making.47 NFC aligns more directly with the psychological discomfort with ambiguity itself.47

This aversion to ambiguity fuels two general processing tendencies identified by Kruglanski and Webster (1996) 30: the **urgency tendency** and the **permanence tendency**. The urgency tendency reflects the inclination to attain cognitive closure as quickly as possible.31 This drive for speed often leads individuals high in NFC to make decisions and form judgments rapidly, sometimes prematurely.44 The permanence tendency refers to the motivation to maintain closure once it has been achieved, preserving it for as long as possible.32 This leads to a resistance to reopening issues, considering alternatives, or changing one's mind once a judgment has been formed.

These two tendencies give rise to the characteristic cognitive processes of **"seizing" and "freezing"**.31 *Seizing* involves quickly grasping onto early or easily accessible pieces of information that seem to offer a path toward closure, allowing an individual to form an initial judgment or opinion swiftly, especially when faced with ambiguity.31 *Freezing* occurs once a judgment is formed; the individual tends to "freeze" on that conclusion, becoming relatively impervious or closed off to subsequent information, particularly if it challenges the established sense of closure.31 This "motivated closing of the mind" 30 prioritizes the maintenance of certainty over the potential refinement or correction of beliefs.

The NFC construct is often operationalized using scales like the Need for Closure Scale (NFCS) developed by Webster and Kruglanski (1994) 44 and its subsequent revisions.44 These scales typically assess several facets or components reflecting the core motivation 44:

* **Preference for Order/Structure:** A desire for neatness, organization, clear rules, and regular routines.44 Example item: "I think that having clear rules and order at work is essential to success".32  
* **Preference for Predictability:** A need for stable, consistent, and predictable life situations and interactions.44  
* **Decisiveness:** An urge to reach judgments or make decisions quickly, avoiding procrastination and indecision.44 (It is worth noting that some factor analyses suggest Decisiveness may be distinct from a broader Need for Structure factor 32).  
* **Discomfort/Avoidance of Ambiguity:** An explicit intolerance or dislike for uncertain, confusing, or multi-interpretable situations.44 Example items: "I don't like situations that are uncertain," "I dislike questions which could be answered in many different ways".33  
* **Closed-mindedness:** A tendency to resist alternative viewpoints or challenges to one's existing beliefs, seeking to maintain cognitive consistency.44 Example item (reverse scored): "Even after I've made up my mind about something, I am always eager to consider a different opinion".32 While the original NFCS was often treated as unidimensional, subsequent research suggests these components might represent distinct facets, and using a total score could obscure important nuances.32

NFC is conceptualized as both a dispositional trait and a situational state. It is considered a relatively stable individual difference, a personality characteristic that influences how individuals habitually approach information and uncertainty.32 However, the momentary need for closure can also be heightened or induced by situational factors.32 Factors such as time pressure (e.g., needing to make a decision quickly), cognitive load (e.g., mental fatigue, boredom, task difficulty or unattractiveness), environmental stressors (e.g., noise), or situations where lacking closure incurs specific costs (e.g., missing a deadline, requiring extensive effort) can temporarily increase an individual's motivation to seek rapid and stable answers.32

The conceptualization of NFC as an epistemic motivation underscores its fundamental role in shaping how individuals engage with the world of information.32 It goes beyond a simple dislike of uncertainty; it actively influences cognitive functions such as attention, information selection and processing, judgment formation, decision-making, and memory.32 This positions NFC as a critical lens through which individuals perceive and interpret information, particularly in complex, ambiguous domains where misinformation often thrives. The drive for certainty dictates the cognitive strategies employed to navigate such environments.

Furthermore, the cognitive strategies associated with high NFC—rapid seizing, resolute freezing, reliance on heuristics, preference for simplicity—offer a potential trade-off between efficiency and accuracy.31 While these tendencies allow for quick judgments and decisions, potentially conserving cognitive resources 31, this efficiency can come at the expense of thoroughness and accuracy, especially when dealing with complex, novel, or potentially deceptive information.33 In situations where the readily available, simple answer is incorrect—as is often the case with misinformation—the very cognitive style that provides efficiency for high-NFC individuals can become a significant vulnerability. The drive for rapid closure may lead them to accept superficially plausible falsehoods before engaging in deeper, more critical evaluation.

## **3\. Defining Misinformation Acceptance**

Misinformation acceptance refers to the cognitive outcome wherein an individual encounters misleading or false information—typically presented after exposure to an original event or topic—and subsequently incorporates this falsehood into their memory representation, belief system, or general understanding.4 It signifies that the inaccurate information is, at least partially, adopted and treated as factual.4 The core of misinformation acceptance lies in the development or endorsement of a *belief* in the false claim.4 For example, after witnessing an accident, if an individual is later asked a leading question implying high speed, they might incorporate the detail of speeding into their memory, even if it wasn't originally observed.26 Similarly, encountering a fake news report might lead someone to accept its claims as true.4

It is important to distinguish misinformation acceptance from related, yet distinct, cognitive phenomena. One such related concept is **misinformation interference**. Interference occurs when exposure to post-event misinformation hinders or prevents the accurate recall or recognition of the *original* information.25 For instance, if someone saw a hammer in an initial scene but was later exposed to misinformation mentioning a screwdriver, interference would manifest as difficulty remembering the hammer. Misinformation *acceptance*, in contrast, would involve incorrectly recalling or recognizing the screwdriver as having been part of the original scene.25 While both interference and acceptance can stem from exposure to misleading information and contribute to erroneous reports, they represent different underlying processes and outcomes—one involving the impairment of original memory access, the other involving the adoption of the new, false information.27 Research suggests that misinformation acceptance plays a major role in erroneous reporting, potentially involving the creation of new, false memories.27

Misinformation acceptance must also be differentiated from **belief perseverance**. Belief perseverance describes the tendency for individuals to cling to their *initial* or pre-existing beliefs, even after the evidential basis for those beliefs has been thoroughly discredited or refuted.54 It reflects a resistance to updating existing knowledge structures in the face of contradictory evidence.54 Misinformation acceptance, on the other hand, typically involves the formation of a *new* false belief or the modification of an existing memory based on exposure to *newly encountered* false information.26 While both phenomena result in holding incorrect beliefs, belief perseverance centers on the tenacious maintenance of *old* beliefs against new evidence, whereas misinformation acceptance centers on the incorporation of *new* falsehoods into one's cognitive framework. For example, continuing to believe a debunked conspiracy theory one has held for years exemplifies belief perseverance.55 Conversely, starting to believe a novel piece of fake news after seeing it online represents misinformation acceptance.4

Finally, misinformation acceptance is distinct from **confirmation bias**. Confirmation bias refers to the active tendency to seek out, interpret, favor, and recall information in a manner that confirms one's *pre-existing* beliefs, expectations, or hypotheses, while simultaneously neglecting, dismissing, or failing to seek out contradictory evidence.22 It is a biased *process* of information gathering and interpretation driven by the desire to validate existing views.56 Misinformation acceptance, as an *outcome*, does not necessarily require this active, biased search; it can occur more passively through mere exposure to false claims, especially if they are repeated or presented persuasively.4 While confirmation bias can certainly *facilitate* misinformation acceptance—individuals are more likely to accept misinformation that aligns with their pre-existing views 4—the two concepts are not synonymous. One can accept misinformation that is neutral or even contrary to one's prior beliefs under certain conditions (e.g., due to source effects or repetition), whereas confirmation bias specifically involves alignment with existing attitudes.

Understanding misinformation acceptance as involving the cognitive integration of false claims into an individual's knowledge base or memory is crucial.26 This integration suggests that the falsehood becomes part of the person's mental model of the event or topic.59 Consequently, accepted misinformation is not simply a case of ignorance or lacking the correct information; it involves actively holding an incorrect piece of information as true. This makes correction particularly challenging, as it requires not just presenting the factual information but also actively counteracting or overwriting the established false belief, a process known to be difficult due to phenomena like the Continued Influence Effect, where retracted information continues to shape reasoning.58

Furthermore, while misinformation acceptance is conceptually distinct from belief perseverance and confirmation bias, these phenomena often interact in practice.54 An individual's pre-existing beliefs (the focus of belief perseverance) and their tendency to favor confirming evidence (confirmation bias) can significantly increase their vulnerability to accepting specific pieces of misinformation, particularly those that resonate with their existing worldview or come from trusted in-group sources.4 Misinformation that "fits" comfortably within one's established cognitive and affective framework is more likely to be accepted with less critical scrutiny. Therefore, a comprehensive understanding of misinformation acceptance requires acknowledging these dynamic interactions alongside the core definition.

## **4\. The Empirical Link: NFC and Susceptibility to Misinformation**

A growing body of empirical research investigates the relationship between individual differences in the Need for Cognitive Closure (NFC) and susceptibility to various forms of misinformation. The general finding emerging from this literature is that individuals who score higher on measures of dispositional NFC tend to exhibit a greater likelihood of believing and accepting misinformation, including conspiracy theories and fake news.37 This connection often manifests as a positive correlation between NFC scale scores and measures assessing belief in specific false claims or general conspiracy mentality.

This relationship has been observed across several specific domains and types of misinformation:

* **Conspiracy Theories:** Multiple studies explicitly link higher NFC to increased endorsement of conspiracy theories.38 Conspiracy theories often provide simple, comprehensive, and readily available explanations for complex, ambiguous, or threatening events (such as the COVID-19 pandemic 38), thereby potentially satisfying the high-NFC individual's desire for certainty and structure.38 The epistemic motive to reduce uncertainty appears to be a key driver in this context.38  
* **Fake News Belief:** Research also connects NFC with belief in fake news articles or headlines.5 Individuals high in NFC may be less adept at discerning the veracity of news content, potentially accepting false items more readily.7 This aligns with the idea that the drive for quick answers might lead to less critical engagement with news encountered online or elsewhere.  
* **Misinformation Effects in Memory:** Experimental studies, particularly using eyewitness memory paradigms, demonstrate that NFC can amplify the classic misinformation effect.46 High NFC has been shown to augment retrieval-induced forgetting (the tendency to forget non-retrieved information after selectively retrieving related information), which in turn makes individuals more susceptible to incorporating misleading post-event details into their memory reports.46 This suggests NFC can contribute directly to the formation of false memories by motivating the suppression of potentially conflicting original information to achieve closure.46  
* **Online Misinformation Contexts:** NFC has been identified as a relevant factor influencing vulnerability to misinformation encountered in online environments and on social media.14 Studies using crowdsourcing to assess the veracity of online content (e.g., tweets about COVID-19) found that individuals higher in NFC were less aligned with expert judgments regarding misinformation.20 Intriguingly, one study found that higher NFC was associated with *more positive* attitudes towards misinformation warning tags on social media, suggesting a complex interaction with potential interventions where the tag itself might provide a form of closure.14

Despite the consistency of a general link, several nuances and caveats are important to consider:

* **Variable Effect Sizes:** While the relationship between NFC and misinformation acceptance is frequently statistically significant, the magnitude of the effect can vary and is sometimes reported as small.43 This indicates that NFC is likely one contributing factor among a constellation of variables influencing susceptibility. For example, one study examining COVID-19 conspiracy beliefs found that political trust was a substantially stronger predictor than NFC.43  
* **Context Dependency:** The strength of the NFC-misinformation link may depend significantly on the specific context. For instance, the connection might be more pronounced when alternative, official explanations are lacking or when a conspiratorial narrative is particularly accessible and easily processed.43 Similarly, NFC's influence might be amplified in tasks explicitly requiring the resolution of ambiguity.46  
* **Inconsistent Findings and Moderation:** Not all studies find a significant direct link. Some research reported non-significant associations after controlling for potential confounding variables like political orientation.43 Additionally, findings can differ depending on the specific type of misinformation; one study found NFC correlated with general conspiracy mentality but not specifically with belief in COVID-19 conspiracies.43 Furthermore, research on the illusory truth effect (increased belief due to repetition) did not find evidence that NFC moderated this particular phenomenon 46, suggesting NFC's influence might operate through mechanisms other than sensitivity to repetition-based fluency.

The common thread appears to be the resolution of uncertainty, whether that uncertainty pertains to large-scale societal events or the details of a witnessed incident.

## **5\. Psychological Mechanisms: How NFC Facilitates Misinformation Acceptance**

The empirical link between a high Need for Cognitive Closure (NFC) and increased susceptibility to misinformation is underpinned by several interconnected psychological mechanisms. The overarching principle is that the motivation to achieve certainty rapidly and maintain it profoundly shapes how high-NFC individuals select, process, and evaluate information, often in ways that inadvertently lower the threshold for accepting falsehoods.31

Specific mechanisms contributing to this vulnerability include:

* **Rapid "Seizing" on Initial Information:** Driven by the urgency tendency, individuals high in NFC are inclined to quickly grasp the first piece of information encountered that offers a potential answer or reduces ambiguity.31 Misinformation, particularly when presented simply, confidently, or early in the information search process, can effectively fulfill this need for a quick resolution. This can lead to premature acceptance of the misinformation before more comprehensive or critical evaluation occurs.43 This mechanism is closely related to the well-documented primacy effect in impression formation and judgment, where early information exerts a disproportionate influence, a bias shown to be exacerbated by situationally induced NFC like time pressure.40  
* **Resolute "Freezing" on Established Beliefs:** Once cognitive closure is attained, even if based on faulty or incomplete information, the permanence tendency motivates high-NFC individuals to "freeze" on that judgment.31 This manifests as resistance to considering subsequent information that contradicts the initial conclusion, as doing so would necessitate reopening the issue and re-experiencing the discomfort of ambiguity.31 This can lead to closed-mindedness, where alternative perspectives or corrective information are actively avoided, ignored, or dismissed.44 This "freezing" makes high-NFC individuals potentially less receptive to debunking efforts or factual corrections once misinformation has been accepted.  
* **Reliance on Heuristics and Reduced Information Processing:** The drive for quick closure often leads high-NFC individuals to favor less effortful cognitive strategies. This includes relying more heavily on heuristics—mental shortcuts or rules of thumb—and engaging in shallower, less systematic information processing.32 Instead of undertaking extensive information searches or deep analytical evaluation, they may base judgments on peripheral cues (like source likeability or perceived consensus) or readily available information.32 Misinformation often exploits this tendency by employing simple narratives, emotional appeals, or mimicking the appearance of credible sources 19, making it particularly appealing to heuristic-based processing styles favored under high NFC.  
* **Motivated Reasoning (Closure-Driven):** While motivated reasoning is often discussed in the context of protecting one's identity or pre-existing ideological commitments 21, the need for closure itself can act as a distinct motivation shaping reasoning.32 High-NFC individuals may selectively attend to, interpret, or weigh information in a way that facilitates the quickest and most stable path to *any* firm conclusion, irrespective of its accuracy.32 They are motivated to seek information consistent with their current knowledge structure, even if that structure was hastily formed based on initial, potentially misleading, cues.38 This "nonspecific" need for closure 32 means the goal is certainty itself, which can sometimes override accuracy goals.  
* **Enhanced Inhibition and Memory Effects:** In specific cognitive paradigms, such as those involving selective memory retrieval, high NFC has been shown to induce stronger inhibition of non-retrieved information.46 This occurs because suppressing related but potentially conflicting memory traces helps to reduce ambiguity and achieve a clearer, more closed representation of the retrieved information.46 This enhanced inhibition leads to greater retrieval-induced forgetting of the original details, weakening the memory trace.46 Consequently, when later exposed to post-event misinformation that pertains to these forgotten details, high-NFC individuals are more susceptible to accepting it. The misinformation fills the memory gap, providing a coherent (albeit false) narrative that satisfies the need for closure, potentially leading to the creation or strengthening of false memories.27

A key aspect emerging from these mechanisms is that NFC primarily influences the *process* of how information is acquired and handled, rather than dictating a preference for specific informational *content* in the first instance. The core drive is towards achieving certainty and stability ("nonspecific closure" 32), making individuals vulnerable to *any* information that appears to offer a quick and firm answer, regardless of its ideological slant or factual basis. This procedural vulnerability—characterized by speed, superficiality, and resistance to revision—is the primary pathway through which high NFC increases susceptibility. While this procedural vulnerability can certainly interact with content biases (e.g., ideologically congruent misinformation might offer an even faster route to satisfying closure), the fundamental mechanism stems from the way information processing is regulated by the motivation to avoid ambiguity.

Furthermore, these mechanisms highlight a **dual vulnerability** associated with high NFC. The tendencies towards rapid "seizing" and heuristic processing contribute to the *initial acceptance* of misinformation upon first encounter.31 Simultaneously, the "freezing" tendency and the motivation to maintain closure contribute to the *persistence* of these false beliefs once they are formed, making individuals resistant to correction or updating.31 This dual impact—affecting both belief formation and belief maintenance—makes NFC a particularly relevant factor in understanding the challenges posed by misinformation, especially in environments where false claims are pervasive and frequently repeated. It suggests that individuals high in NFC may not only be quicker to adopt misinformation but also significantly harder to correct once they have done so.6

## **6\. Moderating Influences: Context Matters**

The relationship between the Need for Cognitive Closure (NFC) and misinformation acceptance is not static or absolute. Instead, its strength and nature are significantly modulated by a range of interacting factors, including other individual differences, characteristics of the information source and content, and the broader situational context. Understanding these moderators is crucial for developing a nuanced picture of when and why high NFC leads to increased vulnerability.

**Political Ideology and Trust:**

* **Ideological Congruence (Myside/Congeniality Bias):** A robust finding in misinformation research is that individuals are generally more inclined to believe information, whether true or false, that aligns with their existing political identity and ideological commitments.1 This "myside bias" suggests that for individuals high in NFC, misinformation that resonates with their political worldview might be particularly appealing. Such information offers a doubly satisfying route to closure, as it provides a quick answer while simultaneously reinforcing cherished beliefs and group identities. This could potentially amplify the myside bias among high-NFC partisans.  
* **Political Conservatism:** Some research traditions have linked political conservatism with higher average levels of NFC, suggesting conservatives may have a greater preference for certainty, order, and resistance to change.21 Studies have also found correlations between conservatism and susceptibility to certain types of misinformation or lower alignment with expert fact-checkers in specific contexts.20 However, this link is debated, with other research suggesting that cognitive rigidity and strong epistemic motivations might characterize individuals at *both* ends of the political spectrum (i.e., strong liberals and strong conservatives).21 The precise relationship between NFC, conservatism, and misinformation likely depends on the specific issue, the nature of the misinformation, and the cultural context.  
* **Political Trust:** An individual's level of trust in governing institutions, political elites, medical authorities, and the media plays a critical role in determining whether they accept official narratives or turn to alternative explanations, such as conspiracy theories.14 NFC may interact significantly with trust levels. It is plausible that individuals high in NFC and *low* in political trust might readily "seize" upon conspiracy theories or anti-establishment misinformation as these provide clear, albeit alternative, explanations. Conversely, those high in NFC and *high* in political trust might quickly accept official pronouncements without deep scrutiny.43 However, empirical tests of this interaction have yielded mixed results; one large study examining COVID-19 conspiracy beliefs did not find statistical support for this specific interaction, although political trust itself was a very strong predictor.43

**Source Credibility:**

* **Heuristic Cue:** Evaluating the veracity of information can be cognitively demanding. Individuals may therefore rely on source credibility as a heuristic cue, particularly when motivation or capacity for deep processing is low 15—conditions potentially fostered by the high-NFC individual's desire for quick answers. High NFC might lead individuals to rapidly accept information from sources perceived as credible, authoritative, or aligned with their group, and quickly dismiss information from distrusted sources, bypassing careful content analysis.59  
* **Mixed Findings on Source Effects:** Research on the general impact of source cues on misinformation judgment yields mixed results.15 However, some meta-analytic evidence suggests that simply displaying source information alongside news headlines improves overall truth discernment.1 Yet, the specific attributes of the source can have complex effects; one study on fake news rebuttals found that source *authority* negatively impacted acceptance, whereas source *influence* (perhaps related to popularity or peer endorsement) had a positive effect.65 While direct investigation of the NFC x Source Credibility interaction is limited, the theoretical alignment between NFC's promotion of heuristic processing and the use of source cues makes this a plausible moderating pathway.

**Topic Relevance and Familiarity:**

* **Relevance:** The personal relevance or importance of a topic can influence processing depth. When an issue is highly relevant, individuals may be more motivated to engage in careful, systematic processing, potentially overriding the default tendencies associated with high NFC.49 Conversely, for topics perceived as low relevance, the drive for quick closure might exert a stronger influence, leading to more superficial judgments.  
* **Familiarity (Illusory Truth Effect):** Repeated exposure to a statement, even a false one, increases its perceived truthfulness.4 This robust "illusory truth effect" occurs because repetition enhances processing fluency, which is often misattributed as a signal of accuracy.4 While one study found that NFC did *not* moderate the magnitude of the illusory truth effect 46, familiarity itself serves as a potent cognitive cue. It is plausible that high-NFC individuals, seeking quick answers, might readily "seize" upon the feeling of familiarity as evidence of truth. Supporting this, self-reported familiarity with news items has been linked to a general bias towards believing them to be true (a "true-news bias").1

**Cognitive Load and Situational Factors:**

* **Amplification of NFC Effects:** As established in Section 2, NFC is not solely a stable trait but can be situationally induced or amplified. Conditions that increase the desire for closure or tax cognitive resources—such as time pressure, environmental noise, mental fatigue, task unattractiveness, or information overload—can magnify the influence of dispositional NFC.32 Under such demanding conditions, individuals become more reliant on the rapid "seizing" and "freezing" mechanisms and heuristic processing.32 The fast-paced, information-saturated nature of many online environments might constitute precisely such a high-load context, potentially making users generally more susceptible to NFC-driven acceptance of misinformation.2

**Other Individual Differences:**

* **Need for Cognition (NfC):** As distinct from NFC, the Need for Cognition reflects an individual's intrinsic enjoyment of engaging in effortful cognitive activities.7 High NfC generally predicts *less* susceptibility to misinformation, likely because it promotes deeper, more analytical processing that counteracts the superficiality associated with high NFC.7 Low NfC individuals may rely more on heuristics, similar to those high in NFC.26  
* **Analytical Thinking (CRT):** Performance on tasks measuring analytical thinking or cognitive reflection (like the CRT) is a strong predictor of the ability to distinguish true from false information.1 High analytical thinking may buffer against the effects of high NFC by enabling individuals to override intuitive but incorrect responses, though the interplay is complex and discussed further in Section 8\.  
* **Personality Traits:** Other personality dimensions can also play a role. For example, traits like Openness to Experience and Agreeableness have been linked to more positive attitudes towards misinformation interventions like warning tags.14 Dogmatism, which shares conceptual overlap with the closed-mindedness aspect of NFC, is strongly associated with rigid belief systems and resistance to change.34 Narcissism has also been identified as a correlate of misinformation belief.52

The array of moderating factors underscores that NFC rarely, if ever, operates in isolation. Its influence on misinformation acceptance is contingent upon a complex interplay with other cognitive factors (like analytical thinking), motivational states (like political identity and trust), and the specific demands of the situation (like cognitive load). Predicting vulnerability requires moving beyond simple main effects to consider these crucial interactions \[Insight 6.1\]. For instance, the combination of high NFC with low analytical ability and strong partisan bias might represent a particularly high-risk profile for accepting congruent misinformation.

Furthermore, the fact that situational factors like time pressure or cognitive load can induce closure-seeking behaviors and associated biases even in individuals typically low in dispositional NFC is significant.32 This implies that the mechanisms linking NFC to misinformation acceptance—reliance on heuristics, premature judgment—can become temporarily activated in a much broader segment of the population when they are navigating demanding information environments, such as fast-paced social media feeds.2 This broadens the relevance of understanding NFC-related processes beyond just those individuals who consistently score high on trait measures, suggesting that environmental or structural interventions aimed at reducing cognitive load or encouraging deliberation could have widespread benefits in mitigating misinformation acceptance \[Insight 6.2\].

## **7\. Real-World Implications of the NFC-Misinformation Link**

The established connection between a high Need for Cognitive Closure (NFC) and increased susceptibility to misinformation carries significant implications across various real-world domains. By influencing how individuals process information and form beliefs in uncertain situations, NFC can contribute to and exacerbate pressing societal challenges.

**Political Domain:**

* **Distorted Political Decision-Making:** In the political sphere, citizens are constantly required to make judgments about complex issues, evaluate candidates, and decide on policy preferences, often based on incomplete or conflicting information. NFC-driven acceptance of political misinformation—false claims about candidates' records, distorted presentations of policy impacts, or fabricated scandals—can lead to a skewed understanding of the political landscape.15 This can result in voting behavior or policy support that is based on falsehoods rather than factual considerations, potentially undermining the quality of democratic deliberation and outcomes.  
* **Exacerbation of Political Polarization:** Misinformation is widely recognized as a potent fuel for political polarization, particularly affective polarization—the tendency for partisans to view opposing political groups with distrust and animosity.7 Misinformation often employs simplistic "us versus them" narratives, demonizes political opponents, and reinforces group boundaries.23 Individuals high in NFC, especially when combined with strong partisan identification or low trust in out-group sources, may find such polarizing misinformation particularly appealing as it offers clear-cut, unambiguous categorizations of the political world.21 The tendency to "seize" on these narratives and subsequently "freeze" on them makes high-NFC individuals potentially more susceptible to becoming entrenched in polarized viewpoints and resistant to information that might bridge divides.21

**Public Health Crises:**

* **Negative Health Behaviors and Outcomes:** During public health emergencies like the COVID-19 pandemic, uncertainty and anxiety are pervasive, potentially heightening the population's average need for closure.2 In such contexts, health misinformation regarding disease origins, prevention methods (e.g., mask efficacy), treatments, and vaccine safety can spread rapidly.10 Acceptance of this misinformation, potentially facilitated by high NFC seeking definitive answers amidst chaos, can lead individuals to reject evidence-based public health guidance, engage in harmful or ineffective practices (e.g., ingesting dangerous substances purported to be cures 16), and exhibit vaccine hesitancy.10 These behaviors have direct and severe consequences for individual health and contribute to the overall burden on public health systems.16  
* **Erosion of Trust in Health Authorities:** Belief in health-related misinformation and conspiracy theories, often providing simple but incorrect explanations for complex health phenomena, can significantly erode public trust in medical professionals, scientific institutions, and public health agencies.7 High NFC individuals, particularly if they accept misinformation that casts doubt on official sources, may become deeply skeptical of expert advice. This breakdown of trust hinders effective health communication, complicates public health interventions, and undermines collective efforts to manage health crises.16

**Spread of Online Falsehoods:**

* **Amplification via Social Media Dynamics:** The architecture of many social media platforms—characterized by rapid information flow, constant notifications, information overload, and algorithms prioritizing engagement over accuracy—may create an environment particularly conducive to NFC-driven cognitive shortcuts.1 Under these conditions, high-NFC individuals might be more likely to quickly accept and share misinformation without critical reflection, seeking the immediate cognitive closure or social rewards (likes, shares) the platform offers.11 This behavior can contribute significantly to the rapid and widespread dissemination of falsehoods online, as habitual sharers play a disproportionate role in spreading misinformation.12  
* **Reinforcement within Echo Chambers:** While NFC may not directly cause individuals to seek out echo chambers (ideologically homogeneous online environments), the "freezing" tendency associated with high NFC can make individuals more comfortable and resistant to leaving such spaces once inside.15 Within echo chambers, their achieved sense of closure is constantly reinforced, and exposure to challenging or ambiguous information is minimized, further solidifying potentially misinformation-based beliefs.

It is important to recognize that the link between NFC and misinformation acceptance does not imply that NFC *creates* societal problems like polarization or public health crises de novo. Rather, NFC appears to function as a **psychological amplifier** \[Insight 7.1\]. By rendering a portion of the population more susceptible to believing and acting on the false or misleading information that often fuels these issues, high NFC can exacerbate their severity, increase their intractability, and make collective responses more difficult. It represents a cognitive vulnerability that malicious actors or unintentional spreaders of misinformation can exploit, thereby magnifying the negative societal impacts of falsehoods.

This understanding carries significant weight for designing effective interventions \[Insight 7.2\]. The dual nature of NFC's influence—facilitating both initial acceptance ("seizing") and resistance to correction ("freezing") \[Insight 5.2\]—poses a substantial challenge. Simple debunking or fact-checking after misinformation has been accepted may prove ineffective for individuals high in NFC, as they are motivated to avoid the ambiguity that correction entails. This suggests that interventions might need to be more proactive and multifaceted. Strategies like "prebunking" or psychological inoculation—warning individuals about misinformation tactics *before* they encounter false claims—could be more effective by preventing initial acceptance.18 Encouraging accuracy motivations *prior* to information exposure may also help override closure-seeking tendencies.13 Furthermore, structural changes to information environments, such as altering social media algorithms to reward accuracy over engagement or designing platforms to reduce cognitive load, could mitigate the situational factors that amplify NFC-driven vulnerability.12 Finally, exploring interventions aimed at increasing individuals' tolerance for ambiguity might address the root motivation underlying NFC 33, although this likely represents a longer-term educational goal.

## **8\. Comparative Influence: NFC Among Other Cognitive Predictors**

While the Need for Cognitive Closure (NFC) represents a significant motivational factor influencing misinformation acceptance, it is crucial to situate its influence relative to other well-established cognitive predictors. Misinformation susceptibility is a multi-determined phenomenon, and understanding the comparative and interactive roles of different factors provides a more complete picture.

**NFC vs. Analytical Thinking / Cognitive Reflection (CRT):**

* **General Findings:** Analytical thinking, often assessed via self-report measures 13 or performance on tasks like the Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) 67, consistently emerges as a key predictor of misinformation susceptibility. The CRT, comprising questions with intuitively appealing but incorrect answers, measures the propensity to override initial gut feelings and engage in more effortful deliberation.67 Higher levels of analytical thinking and better CRT performance are robustly associated with an improved ability to discriminate between true and false news headlines (higher truth discernment).1 Conversely, a tendency to rely on intuition rather than reflection is linked to falling for fake news.7 Some studies also find that higher analytical thinking correlates with a general skepticism or bias towards judging news as false (false-news bias).1 Interventions aimed at augmenting critical thinking have shown promise in reducing susceptibility.42  
* **Relative Importance:** When directly compared, analytical thinking/CRT often appears to be a *stronger* or more consistent predictor of truth discernment accuracy than NFC or even partisan bias in some studies.13 Large-scale meta-analyses frequently highlight analytical thinking as a primary individual difference factor.1 However, this does not render NFC irrelevant. Studies controlling for CRT still find that NFC predicts unique variance in aspects of misinformation vulnerability, such as alignment with expert fact-checkers or attitudes towards interventions like warning tags.14  
* **Conceptual Distinction:** It is vital to distinguish between NFC as a *motivation* (the drive for certainty and aversion to ambiguity 43) and analytical thinking as a *cognitive style or ability* (the propensity or capacity to engage in effortful reflection and override intuition 67). They capture different facets of cognitive functioning related to belief formation. High NFC might impel an individual towards a quick judgment, while low analytical thinking might represent a failure to detect the flaws in that judgment even if more time were taken.

**NFC vs. Need for Cognition (NfC):**

* **Opposing Effects:** As noted previously (Section 6), NFC and the Need for Cognition (NfC)—the intrinsic enjoyment of effortful thought 51—typically exert opposing influences on information processing. High NFC encourages cognitive shortcuts and shallow processing to achieve rapid closure 38, whereas high NfC promotes deeper engagement with information.7 Individuals low in NfC may rely more on heuristics, exhibiting processing patterns similar to those high in NFC.26 Consequently, high NfC is generally associated with *less* susceptibility to misinformation and better detection of discrepancies between original memories and misleading post-event information.7

**NFC vs. Prior Beliefs / Knowledge / Ideology:**

* **Strong Influence of Beliefs:** An individual's pre-existing knowledge, attitudes, and particularly their political ideology are powerful determinants of which information they accept.1 The tendency to readily accept information that confirms existing beliefs (myside bias or congeniality bias) is one of the most consistent findings in the field.15 Belief in misinformation is strongly predicted by whether the content aligns with one's partisan identity.1  
* **Interaction with NFC:** NFC likely interacts dynamically with prior beliefs and ideology. High NFC could amplify the myside bias, as ideologically congruent misinformation offers the dual satisfaction of achieving cognitive closure *and* affirming one's worldview.37 Conversely, high NFC might lead to particularly rigid rejection ("freezing") of ideologically incongruent information, even if it is factually correct, because challenging it threatens both closure and identity.37

**Synthesis of Predictors:**

Misinformation acceptance is clearly a complex outcome influenced by multiple cognitive and motivational factors operating in concert.1 NFC (the motivation for certainty), analytical thinking (the ability/style of reflection), and prior beliefs/ideology (content-based motivations and identity) all contribute significantly. Their relative importance may vary depending on the individual, the specific misinformation encountered, and the context. While meta-analyses often emphasize the roles of demographics (like age), analytical thinking, and ideological congruency 1, this may partly reflect the wider availability of data on these variables compared to NFC across diverse studies. A comprehensive understanding requires considering the interplay among these factors.

Furthermore, this comparison highlights the **unique contribution of NFC**, rooted specifically in the **affective discomfort with ambiguity** \[Insight 8.2\]. While analytical thinking pertains to the *mechanics* of processing information and ideology pertains to the *content* preferred, NFC introduces a powerful motivational force driven by the desire to alleviate the unpleasant feeling of uncertainty.31 This aversion to ambiguity can shape information processing strategies—driving the quest for rapid closure—even when analytical abilities are intact or when the information itself is ideologically neutral. It underscores that misinformation acceptance is not solely a "cold" cognitive failure of reasoning but can also be a "hot," affectively driven process aimed at achieving psychological comfort and predictability. Recognizing this affective dimension, brought to the fore by the study of NFC, is critical for fully grasping the persuasive allure of misinformation, which often offers simple certainties in a complex and uncertain world.

## **9\. Synthesis and Conclusion**

This report has synthesized psychological research to elucidate the multifaceted role of the Need for Cognitive Closure (NFC) in the acceptance of misinformation. The evidence consistently indicates that NFC, characterized as a fundamental motivation for clear, definitive answers and an aversion to ambiguity and uncertainty, functions as a significant psychological factor increasing individual susceptibility to believing false or misleading information. This link has been observed across various forms of misinformation, including conspiracy theories, fake news headlines, and distortions in eyewitness memory.38

The influence of NFC is mediated through specific cognitive mechanisms. Individuals high in NFC tend to engage in rapid **"seizing"** on initial information that offers potential closure, often leading to premature judgments. Once closure is achieved, they exhibit **"freezing"**, becoming resistant to subsequent contradictory evidence to maintain their state of certainty. These tendencies are often accompanied by a greater reliance on **heuristic processing**, shallower information engagement, and potentially **motivated reasoning** directed not necessarily by ideology, but by the overarching goal of achieving and preserving cognitive closure.31

However, the relationship between NFC and misinformation acceptance is far from deterministic; it is marked by **complexity and interaction** \[Insight 6.1\]. The impact of NFC is significantly moderated by a host of other factors, including the individual's political ideology and trust in institutions, the perceived credibility of the information source, the relevance and familiarity of the topic, situational factors like cognitive load and time pressure, and other cognitive traits such as analytical thinking ability. The interplay between NFC (as a motivation) and analytical thinking (as a cognitive ability/style) appears particularly critical, suggesting distinct pathways—one driven by a rush to judgment, the other by a failure of reflection—can lead to misinformation acceptance \[Insight 8.1\].

The **boundary conditions** for NFC's influence are becoming clearer. Its impact is likely strongest in situations characterized by high ambiguity, significant uncertainty (as often found in crises), time constraints, or when simple, readily available explanations (like those often offered by misinformation) are present.32 Conversely, factors that increase motivation for accuracy, reduce cognitive load, or enhance critical thinking skills may attenuate NFC's effects.13

The **societal relevance** of this psychological construct is substantial. By amplifying vulnerability to misinformation, NFC can contribute to the exacerbation of critical societal problems, including political polarization, the erosion of trust, and difficulties in managing public health crises \[Insight 7.1\]. The dual nature of NFC's influence—affecting both the initial acceptance of falsehoods and subsequent resistance to correction \[Insight 5.2\]—poses significant challenges for effective communication and intervention strategies.

The synthesis of findings points towards the need for **integrative theoretical models** \[Insight 9.1\]. Understanding misinformation susceptibility requires moving beyond single-factor explanations to frameworks that explicitly account for the dynamic interplay between epistemic motivations (like NFC), identity-based motivations (like partisanship), cognitive abilities (like analytical thinking), and the characteristics of the information environment itself. Furthermore, the study of NFC uniquely highlights the **affective dimension of misinformation acceptance** \[Insight 9.2\]. The drive to avoid the negative feeling of uncertainty provides a powerful, affect-based explanation for why simple, clear, albeit false, narratives can be so compelling, complementing explanations based purely on cognitive deficits or biases.

Looking forward, the insights gleaned from research on NFC and misinformation have implications for **intervention design**. Given the challenges posed by the "freezing" mechanism, proactive strategies like psychological inoculation or "prebunking" 18 may be particularly valuable. Efforts to promote accuracy motivation 13, enhance media literacy and critical thinking skills 2, and modify information environments to reduce cognitive load and discourage habitual, uncritical sharing 12 also represent promising avenues. Addressing the underlying aversion to ambiguity through education or training aimed at increasing ambiguity tolerance 33 could offer a more fundamental, though challenging, approach.

In conclusion, the Need for Cognitive Closure stands as a crucial individual difference variable that shapes how people navigate the complexities and uncertainties of the modern information environment. Its influence on information processing—driving individuals towards rapid and stable conclusions—renders those high in this motivation more vulnerable to accepting misinformation. Recognizing the mechanisms, moderators, and implications of this cognitive-motivational factor is essential for developing a comprehensive understanding of why misinformation persists and for devising effective strategies to foster a more discerning and resilient public. Continued research into the intricate interplay of NFC with other cognitive, motivational, and contextual factors will be vital in the ongoing effort to mitigate the societal harms posed by the spread of falsehoods.
