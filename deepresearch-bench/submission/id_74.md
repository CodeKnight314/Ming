### Defining the Sports ITS Ecosystem

The sports AI ecosystem encompasses multiple technological layers, yet only a subset constitutes genuine Intelligent Tutoring Systems. An ITS is fundamentally defined as computer software designed to simulate or imitate a human tutor's behavior and guidance, providing personalized and adaptive learning experiences through pedagogical models rather than mere performance dashboards [3]. This distinction is critical because sources frequently conflate automated feedback mechanisms with human coaching support, obscuring the specific requirements for pedagogical intelligence.

#### The Construction, Application, Tutoring, Fusion Framework

This report adopts a four-pillar framework to scope the sports ITS landscape:

*   **Construction**: Technical architecture underlying ITS deployment (model selection, data pipelines, system integration).
*   **Application**: Specific sports contexts and use cases where ITS provides measurable value.
*   **Tutoring**: Pedagogical mechanisms enabling skill acquisition and learning adaptation.
*   **Fusion**: Multimodal data integration strategies combining sensor, video, and performance data.

Within this framework, the **Tutoring** pillar requires grounding in established educational theories rather than generic "learning improvement" claims. Self-Determination Theory (SDT) has been explicitly applied to ITS design for student-athletes, addressing the challenge of balancing academic and athletic responsibilities through personalized adaptive systems [4][20]. SDT-based ITS architectures focus on autonomy, competence, and relatedness as psychological needs that influence motivation and engagement in learning contexts.

Skill proficiency analysis in sports ITS draws upon **Harrow's Taxonomy of the Psychomotor Domain**, which provides a structured framework for evaluating movement learning progression [6]. This taxonomy, associated with Simpson 1972's work on psychomotor learning, enables ITS to assess skill acquisition across levels from basic perception to complex overt response and naturalization [1]. The integration of such pedagogical frameworks distinguishes true tutoring systems from performance monitoring tools that merely report metrics without instructional adaptation.

#### Multimodal Data Fusion Architecture

The **Fusion** pillar requires technical specificity regarding fusion levels and model architectures. Evidence indicates three primary fusion strategies (see *Multimodal Data Fusion* section for detailed comparison):

*   **Early Fusion**: Raw data from multiple modalities combined before feature extraction.
*   **Intermediate Fusion**: Features extracted separately then combined at hidden layers.
*   **Late Fusion**: Independent model outputs combined at decision level.

CNN-LSTM architectures represent a common intermediate fusion approach for sports applications, where convolutional layers extract spatial features from video or sensor data, and LSTM layers capture temporal dependencies [25]. More advanced implementations include Multimodal Attention-based Transformer-Enhanced Deep Fusion Models (MAT-DFM) that leverage wearable sensor data with attention mechanisms for intelligent assessment in physical education contexts [36]. Weight-adaptive multimodal feature fusion methodologies address the challenge of integrating heterogeneous feature representations while preserving unique discriminative characteristics from different modalities [30].

#### Edge vs. Cloud Computing Implications

Real-time feedback represents a core requirement for tutoring effectiveness, making architectural choices between edge and cloud computing critically important. Edge computing deploys network servers closer to users rather than relying on centralized cloud infrastructure, reducing response time delay of service requests and improving user service experience [13]. Key characteristics distinguishing edge from cloud computing include minimized bandwidth requirements, reduced response times, improved performance for real-time applications, and reduced operational costs [22].

Cloud computing remains valuable for storing large amounts of data not needed in real-time and enabling massive computational resources for model training [17]. However, for ITS requiring instant feedback during training sessions, edge deployment enables athletes to make quick modifications to improve performance without latency-induced disruption to the learning cycle [21]. The architectural decision directly impacts tutoring feasibility, as delayed feedback undermines the pedagogical effectiveness of skill correction mechanisms.

While AI-driven systems can analyze movement patterns in real-time using data from wearable devices, GPS systems, and video feeds [23], few systems incorporate the pedagogical adaptation mechanisms required for genuine tutoring. The distinction matters: a system that detects improper running form constitutes monitoring; a system that adapts instructional content based on detected errors and guides the athlete through corrective progression constitutes tutoring.

Validation frameworks in current studies reveal this gap. #### Injury Prediction vs. Skill Acquisition Tutoring Gap

The literature demonstrates substantial investment in injury prevention and rehabilitation applications. Injury prevention represents an area of critical importance in sports science where AI already makes strides, with primary advantages including the ability to predict injuries before they happen through machine learning analysis of training load, sleep quality, and stress levels [23][32]. Machine learning models provide more complete and real-time understanding of an athlete's condition, enabling coaches to intervene earlier and optimize training on an individual level [32].

In contrast, skill acquisition tutoring receives comparatively limited research attention. While yoga pose tutoring systems like e-YogaGuru demonstrate computer vision-based intelligent tutoring for specific movements [6], comprehensive skill continua tutoring across sports disciplines remains nascent. The gap reflects a fundamental difference in problem framing: injury prediction is a classification or regression problem amenable to supervised learning, while skill acquisition tutoring requires modeling of pedagogical state, learner knowledge, and adaptive instructional strategy selection.

Traditional sports performance metrics (speed, power, accuracy) do not capture learning progression, necessitating development of pedagogical assessment frameworks aligned with taxonomies like Harrow's psychomotor domain.

## System Construction: Architectural Frameworks for Sports ITS

The construction of AI-driven Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) for sports requires a carefully engineered architectural framework that distinguishes between pedagogical guidance for skill learning and performance monitoring for load tracking. Unlike coaching analytics systems that primarily track physiological metrics and training loads, intelligent tutoring systems must deliver real-time instructional feedback grounded in educational theory while processing complex multimodal sensor data. This section examines the three foundational layers of sports ITS architecture: hardware integration and sensor networks, edge versus cloud computing deployment strategies, and software architecture with processing pipelines.

### Hardware Integration & Sensor Networks

The physical construction of sports ITS begins with the integration of heterogeneous sensor networks that capture biomechanical, kinematic, and contextual data streams. Modern systems employ a multimodal stack combining wearable devices, computer vision setups, and environmental sensors to create comprehensive athlete profiles. The term "Integrated Systems" refers to platforms combining hardware sensors with AI-driven dashboards for real-time feedback and compliance tracking [12].

*   **Wearable Sensor Technologies**: Form the primary data collection layer. Inertial Measurement Units (IMU) provide acceleration, angular velocity, and orientation data critical for movement pattern analysis. Flexible sensors, including accelerometers, pressure sensors, and bending sensors, enable continuous monitoring of joint angles and force distribution during athletic movements [7]. These sensors integrate artificial synapses and feature-level fusion technology to achieve active sports injury prevention and health monitoring capabilities. GPS-enabled devices serve dual purposes: tracking spatial positioning for tactical analysis and measuring external load metrics such as distance covered and sprint frequency. In professional football, contemporary sensor networks have been deployed to analyze tactical formations, player positioning, and collective pressing behaviors through GPS-enabled devices integrated with tactical video analysis systems [11].
*   **Computer Vision Setups**: Complement wearable sensors by providing non-contact motion capture. OpenPose and similar pose estimation frameworks achieve mean absolute error (MAE) below 20 mm for skeletal joint detection, enabling precise biomechanical analysis without requiring athletes to wear extensive instrumentation [12]. Microsoft Kinect and depth-sensing cameras have been utilized in computer-assisted self-training systems for sports exercise, particularly for yoga poses and fundamental movement patterns [6]. These vision-based systems support non-discursive communication tutoring, such as the Presentation Trainer for public speaking, demonstrating applicability beyond traditional athletic skills [6].
*   **Wireless Sensor Networks and IoT Integration**: Enable distributed data collection across training environments. The comparison between wireless sensor networks and the Internet of Things reveals that wireless sensor networks represent a simplified version focused on localized data transmission, while IoT enables broader connectivity and cloud integration [13]. IoT-enabled sports injury rehabilitation monitoring systems integrate flexible sensors with machine learning algorithms to provide real-time health status monitoring and early warning capabilities that traditional rehabilitation methods cannot easily achieve [7]. The major contributions in this domain lie in the integration of artificial synapses and flexible sensors, feature-level fusion technology, and a blend of SVM and LSTM networks, presenting an extremely valuable athletic injury prevention tool [7].
*   **Data Collection Modalities**: Must be carefully selected based on the tutoring objective. For intelligent tutoring focused on skill acquisition, high-frequency IMU data (100-500 Hz) combined with video at 30-240 fps provides sufficient temporal resolution for movement decomposition and error detection. For coaching analytics focused on load monitoring, lower-frequency GPS and heart rate data suffice. Advanced AI-driven systems can now analyze movement patterns in real-time, using data from wearable devices, GPS systems, and video feeds to track biomechanical markers such as stride length, ground contact time, and acceleration [23].

### Edge vs. Cloud Computing Deployment

The architectural decision between Edge and Cloud computing deployment directly impacts the feasibility of real-time tutoring feedback, representing a critical design constraint for sports ITS. This distinction separates systems capable of delivering immediate pedagogical guidance from those limited to post-session analytics.

*   **Edge Computing for Real-Time Tutoring**: Offers minimized bandwidth requirements, reduced response times, improved performance, and reduced operational costs [22]. By moving data processing and analysis closer to the point where data is captured, the need for expensive bandwidth is minimized and response times are reduced. Mobile edge computing technology deploys network servers in various regions, focusing more on users rather than cloud computing, reducing the response time delay of user service requests, thereby improving user service experience and reducing network transmission burden [13]. For intelligent tutoring requiring immediate feedback on movement execution, edge deployment is essential. During training sessions, AI-powered gadgets can give athletes real-time feedback, enabling them to make quick modifications to improve their performance [21].
*   **Cloud Computing for Longitudinal Analysis**: Excels at storing large amounts of data not needed in real-time [17]. Cloud computing has been a major contributor to digital transformation by massively reducing the cost of storing and processing data. Post-session analysis, historical performance tracking, and multi athlete comparative analytics benefit from cloud infrastructure's scalable storage and computational resources. Computational resource requirements were moderate, with the full system operating effectively on mid-range laptop hardware (16GB RAM, quad-core i7 processor) for post-session analysis, while real-time implementation required dedicated processing units with GPU acceleration [11].
*   **Latency Constraints and Deployment Algorithms**: Determine the boundary between edge and cloud processing. Real-time feedback can make all the difference for athletes who want to push themselves to the edge, whether in training or competition [21]. The combination of 5G and Edge Computing is expected to fully utilize low-delay communication characteristics, as even if communication speed improves, delays in data processing prevent 5G characteristics from being fully utilized [31]. Software Defined Networks (SDN) enable dynamic routing of data streams based on latency requirements, directing time-critical tutoring feedback through edge nodes while routing historical data to cloud storage.
*   **Hybrid Deployment Strategies**: Optimize system performance by allocating processing tasks based on latency sensitivity. The DDPG (Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient) algorithm employs adaptive training adjustments, dynamically optimizing the training policy based on real-time feedback [33]. This requires edge processing for the feedback loop while cloud resources handle policy optimization across multiple training sessions. Table 6 summarizes system performance metrics across different sport environments, highlighting both technical performance and user feedback in each application scenario [11].
*   **Critical Distinction for Tutoring vs. Analytics**: Intelligent tutoring systems require edge deployment for the feedback delivery component, as pedagogical guidance must occur within the movement execution window to support motor learning. Coaching analytics systems can operate primarily in the cloud, as load tracking and performance monitoring do not require sub-second feedback latency. This architectural distinction reflects the fundamental difference between skill acquisition support and performance documentation.

### Software Architecture & Processing Pipelines

The software architecture of sports ITS translates raw sensor data into pedagogically meaningful feedback through structured processing pipelines. This layer determines how multimodal inputs are fused, how student models are updated, and how instructional strategies are selected based on learning theory.

*   **GIFT Framework Architecture**: Provides a modular foundation for intelligent tutoring system development. The Generalized Intelligent Framework for Tutoring (GIFT) allows users to integrate new pedagogical models, instructional strategies, or instructional tactics from other tutoring systems into a stable and flexible platform for innovations in AIED research [1]. As a modular architecture, GIFT enables integration of domain-specific modules for sports skill tutoring while maintaining compatibility with established educational psychology models. Associated objectives include modeling and adapting to individual differences such as states, traits, and preferences [1]. In studies synthesized, Learning Management Systems (LMS) frequently connect with AI-enabled applications, such as Moodle integrated with the GeoGebra Interactive Formative Test (GIFT), the ERUDITE adaptive tutoring platform, and NAVIGO, an adaptive personalized literacy game [12].
*   **Multimodal Data Fusion Pipelines**: Specify the fusion level and model architecture for combining heterogeneous sensor streams. Three fusion levels are distinguished:
    *   **Early Fusion**: Raw sensor data concatenated before feature extraction, suitable for synchronized IMU and EMG streams.
    *   **Intermediate (Feature-Level) Fusion**: Features extracted independently then combined, as demonstrated in systems using feature-level fusion technology with SVM and LSTM network blends [7].
    *   **Late Fusion**: Independent model predictions combined through voting or weighting schemes.
*   **CNN-LSTM Architecture**: Represents a common pipeline for spatiotemporal movement analysis. The convolutional layer output follows the formula F(l)=σ(W(l)F(l-1)+b(l)), where W(l) and b(l) represent the weight and bias term of the convolution kernel, l represents the convolution operation, σ(∙) is the ReLU nonlinear activation function [25]. This architecture processes video frames through CNN layers for spatial feature extraction, then LSTM layers capture temporal dependencies across movement sequences. Multimodal Data Fusion for Enhanced CNN-LSTM systems have been applied to intelligent football training and tactical analysis [25].
*   **Transformer-Based Architectures**: Offer attention mechanisms for weighting sensor modalities dynamically. A novel Multimodal Attention-based Transformer-Enhanced Deep Fusion Model (MAT-DFM) for intelligent assessment in physical education leverages wearable sensor data with adaptive attention mechanisms [36]. The theoretical significance of this work lies in its novel combination of spatiotemporal feature extraction with adaptive attention mechanisms, providing a principled framework for processing multimodal behavioral data that advances both computer vision and sports psychology methodologies [30].
*   **Pedagogical Model Integration**: Grounds the software architecture in educational theory rather than purely performance metrics. Intelligent Tutoring for Student-Athletes Based on Self-Determination Theory demonstrates that SDT has proven effective for student-athletes' academic needs, addressing the balance between academic and athletic responsibilities [4][20]. Self-Determination Theory provides the motivational framework for tutoring interactions, distinguishing intelligent tutoring from coaching analytics through its focus on autonomy, competence, and relatedness support.
*   **Skill Proficiency Assessment**: Employs Harrow's Taxonomy for psychomotor learning evaluation. Skill proficiency is analyzed according to Harrow's Taxonomy, which provides structured levels for movement skill development from basic reflexes to complex overt responses [6]. This taxonomy enables the system to classify athlete performance into pedagogically meaningful categories rather than raw performance metrics alone. The effectiveness of virtual fitness trainer apps in motivating and engaging students for fitness activity applies motor learning theory grounded in such taxonomies [6].
*   **End-to-End Processing Pipeline**: Flows from sensor input through pedagogical output:
    1.  **Data Ingestion**: Raw sensor streams from IMU, GPS, and vision systems.
    2.  **Preprocessing**: Noise filtering, synchronization, and normalization (Min-Max Scaling).
    3.  **Feature Extraction**: CNN for spatial features, LSTM for temporal patterns.
    4.  **Fusion Layer**: Intermediate fusion combining modality-specific features.
    5.  **Student Model Update**: Bayesian knowledge tracing or deep knowledge tracing.
    6.  **Pedagogical Strategy Selection**: Based on Self-Determination Theory principles.
    7.  **Feedback Generation**: Natural language or visual feedback aligned with Harrow's Taxonomy levels.
*   **Limitations and Research Gaps**: Evidence for longitudinal learning outcomes in sports-specific ITS remains thin compared to STEM ITS literature. While systems like CognitiveTutor and AutoTutor have been utilized to support learning experiences with feedback or through dialogs in traditional domains [6], sports ITS must synthesize broader implications from related fields. The modular design of systems organized into specific modules, each with distinct functionality contributing to personalizing and adapting content, understanding student queries, recommending materials, and providing real-time assessment of learning progress, provides a template for sports ITS development [2].
*   **System Performance Validation**: Requires both technical metrics and pedagogical effectiveness measures. Table 10 demonstrates real-time processing capability evaluation across different temporal windowing strategies, segmenting continuous data streams into overlapping windows with 50% overlap to capture both short-term fluctuations and longer-term behavioral patterns [30]. This validation framework ensures that architectural choices support both computational efficiency and learning outcome improvement.

## Multimodal Data Fusion: Integration Methodologies & Models

The architectural foundation of AI-driven Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) for sports hinges on the effective integration of heterogeneous data streams. Unlike Coaching Analytics platforms, which primarily focus on performance monitoring and load tracking for elite athletes, Intelligent Tutoring Systems emphasize pedagogical guidance and skill acquisition for student-athletes. This distinction necessitates fusion methodologies that not only capture biomechanical precision but also align with educational frameworks such as Self-Determination Theory and Harrow's Taxonomy of psychomotor learning [4][6]. Multimodal data fusion in this context integrates video-based computer vision, wearable biometric sensors, and psychological state indicators to create adaptive learning environments that respond to both physical execution and cognitive engagement.

### Fusion Strategies: Early, Intermediate, & Late

The selection of fusion strategy fundamentally determines the system's capacity to extract meaningful patterns from multimodal inputs. Three primary architectures dominate the landscape: early fusion, intermediate fusion, and late fusion, each with distinct implications for Intelligent Tutoring applications.

| **Strategy** | **Mechanism** | **Advantages** | **Challenges** | **Pedagogical Implication** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Early Fusion** | Raw/preprocessed features concatenated before unified model input. | Computational efficiency; consolidated feature space. | Disparate sampling rates; synchronization overhead; risk of information loss. | Benefits resource-constrained deployments; risks compressing modality-specific features prematurely. |
| **Intermediate Fusion** | Dedicated feature extraction networks per modality; merge at intermediate layers. | Preserves modality-specific characteristics; enables cross-modal interaction. | Higher complexity; requires attention mechanisms or concatenation ops. | **Preferred for ITS.** Enables dynamic weighting (e.g., video vs. biometrics) aligned with SDT autonomy support [4][30]. |
| **Late Fusion** | Separate pipelines; merge at final prediction stage (voting/ensemble). | Maximum modality independence; facilitates incremental sensor addition. | Sacrifices cross-modal interaction; limits nuanced assessment. | Limits capacity to detect emergent patterns (e.g., movement degradation + anxiety). |

**Comparative Performance and Pedagogical Implications**

The choice between fusion strategies carries direct implications for pedagogical effectiveness. Early fusion's computational efficiency benefits resource-constrained deployments but risks information loss when modality-specific features are prematurely compressed. Late fusion preserves modality integrity but limits the system's capacity to detect emergent patterns that arise only through cross-modal interaction, such as the correlation between movement degradation and elevated anxiety markers. Intermediate fusion strikes an optimal balance, enabling the spatiotemporal attention mechanisms necessary for assessing skill proficiency according to Harrow's Taxonomy levels, from basic movement imitation to complex overt response [6].

Feature-level fusion technology specifically reduces input dimensions through aggregating heterogeneous sensor types while improving classification accuracy for movement pattern recognition [7]. This dimensionality reduction proves essential for ITS applications targeting student-athletes, who require interpretable feedback rather than the opaque performance metrics characteristic of professional Coaching Analytics platforms.

Evidence indicates that intermediate fusion often outperforms early fusion by 6.2% in classification accuracy when integrating physiological, visual, and temporal features [30]. Cross-sport testing across basketball, soccer, volleyball, and handball demonstrated consistent performance ranging from 84.2% to 91.4% accuracy with intermediate fusion architectures, achieving real-time response times between 192-312ms [11]. These latency figures remain within acceptable thresholds for formative feedback in tutoring contexts, where sub-second response enables corrective guidance during skill practice rather than post-session analysis typical of Coaching Analytics.

### Deep Learning Models for Fusion

The implementation of fusion strategies relies on specific deep learning architectures optimized for spatiotemporal data characteristic of sports contexts. Three model families dominate current research: Transformer-enhanced architectures, CNN-LSTM hybrids, and specialized injury detection networks.

*   **MAT-DFM: Transformer-Enhanced Multimodal Fusion**: The Multimodal Attention-based Transformer-Enhanced Deep Fusion Model (MAT-DFM) represents the state-of-the-art for intelligent assessment in physical education contexts [36]. This architecture leverages wearable sensor data through transformer attention mechanisms that dynamically weight physiological, visual, and temporal features based on task relevance. The transformer's self-attention mechanism computes attention scores across all input positions simultaneously, enabling the model to capture long-range temporal dependencies that recurrent architectures struggle to maintain. The attention mechanism in MAT-DFM operates through query-key-value transformations, where queries represent current temporal positions, keys encode all positions for similarity computation, and values contain the actual feature representations. This mechanism achieves 6.2% improvement over baseline methods by adaptively focusing on salient features while suppressing noise from less relevant modalities [30]. For Intelligent Tutoring Systems, this selective attention mirrors pedagogical scaffolding, where instructional focus shifts based on learner proficiency levels.
*   **CNN-LSTM for Tactical Analysis and Skill Assessment**: Convolutional Neural Network combined with Long Short-Term Memory (CNN-LSTM) architectures remain prevalent for tactical analysis and sequential skill assessment. The convolutional layers extract spatial features from video frames or pose estimation outputs, while LSTM layers capture temporal dynamics across movement sequences [25]. The convolution operation follows the formulation F(l) = σ(W(l)F(l-1) + b(l)), where W(l) and b(l) represent weight and bias terms, and σ denotes the ReLU nonlinear activation function [25]. This architecture proves particularly effective for children's football action recognition, where LSTM combined with V-DBN (Variational Deep Belief Network) achieves robust classification of tactical movements [25]. For ITS applications, CNN-LSTM models enable assessment of skill progression through Harrow's Taxonomy levels, distinguishing between initial imitation of movements and refined execution with proper temporal coordination. The spatiotemporal feature encoding captures both the spatial configuration of body segments and the temporal rhythm of movement execution, essential for providing formative feedback aligned with psychomotor learning objectives.
*   **Swin-UNet and Specialized Injury Detection Architectures**: For injury prevention components within ITS, Swin-UNet combined with LSTM networks provides specialized capability for detecting biomechanical risk factors. The major contributions in this domain lie in the integration of artificial synapses and flexible sensors with feature-level fusion technology, blending SVM and LSTM networks for athletic injury prevention [7]. While specific accuracy metrics for Swin-UNet in sports contexts require further validation, related medical imaging applications demonstrate the architecture's capacity for precise spatial localization of abnormalities. The integration of biomechanical, physiological, and spatiotemporal data streams enables multimodal analysis for injury risk assessment [12]. Machine learning models combining internal and external training loads predict non-contact injuries in elite football players by detecting subtle patterns across multiple data streams [32]. For Intelligent Tutoring Systems, this capability supports pedagogical objectives by preventing injury-related learning disruptions, ensuring student-athletes maintain consistent engagement with skill development curricula.
*   **Performance Metrics and Real-Time Constraints**: Quantitative performance metrics synthesized across studies indicate that deep learning approaches provide superior performance for complex spatiotemporal data analysis compared to traditional machine learning methods [12]. Cross-sport testing demonstrated consistent accuracy between 84.2-91.4% with real-time response times of 192-312ms [11]. These latency figures prove critical for distinguishing Intelligent Tutoring Systems from Coaching Analytics: ITS requires sub-second feedback for formative guidance during skill practice, whereas coaching analytics tolerates post-session analysis delays. The primary evaluation indicators include accuracy for overall classification performance, precision and recall for class-specific assessment, F1-score for balanced evaluation, and computational efficiency metrics including inference time [30]. For pedagogical applications, precision in detecting skill execution errors carries greater weight than overall accuracy, as false negatives in error detection deprive learners of corrective feedback essential for skill acquisition.
*   **Edge vs. Cloud Computing Implications**: The deployment architecture significantly impacts feedback latency and system feasibility. Edge computing technology deploys network servers in proximity to users rather than relying on centralized cloud infrastructure, reducing response time delay of service requests and improving user experience [13]. The combination of 5G and Edge Computing enables high-speed, large-capacity, low-delay communication essential for real-time tutoring feedback [31]. Cloud computing remains advantageous for storing large amounts of data not needed in real-time, such as longitudinal learning records and historical performance trends [17]. However, wireless data transmission reliability in crowded radio frequency environments presents substantive obstacles for real-time analysis, frequently necessitating post-session data downloading and processing [11]. This limitation particularly affects Intelligent Tutoring Systems requiring immediate feedback, as post-session processing aligns with Coaching Analytics workflows rather than pedagogical guidance objectives. A hybrid architecture proves optimal: edge devices handle real-time inference for immediate feedback, while cloud infrastructure manages model training, data aggregation across learners, and longitudinal progress tracking aligned with Self-Determination Theory's competence development dimension [4].

### Handling Heterogeneous Data Sources

The integration of heterogeneous data sources represents the most significant technical challenge in multimodal fusion for sports-based ITS. Video data from computer vision systems, biometric signals from wearables, and psychological indicators from self-report or physiological proxies operate at different sampling rates, exhibit distinct noise characteristics, and require specialized preprocessing before fusion.

*   **Video Data (Computer Vision)**: Video-based motion capture provides rich spatial information for skill assessment but introduces substantial computational overhead. Pose estimation frameworks such as OpenPose and MediaPipe extract skeletal keypoints from video streams, converting raw pixel data into structured representations suitable for fusion [28]. However, challenges remain in handling noisy fixation data and ensuring gaze-based methodologies align with real-world diagnostic scenarios [28]. For Intelligent Tutoring Systems, video analysis must balance accuracy with latency constraints, as excessive processing delay undermines the immediacy of formative feedback.
*   **Biometric Data (Wearables)**: Wearable sensors including accelerometers, gyroscopes, and heart rate monitors provide continuous physiological monitoring at sampling rates typically ranging from 50-200 Hz. The integration of heterogeneous data streams with varying sampling rates requires temporal alignment strategies to ensure synchronized fusion [11]. Accelerometers monitor runners' gait parameters such as stride frequency and stride length, while heart rate variability metrics like RMSSD indicate autonomic nervous system responses to training load [7]. Feature-level fusion technology aggregates data from multiple sensors into aggregate input features, reducing dimensionality while preserving discriminative information [7]. This approach proves essential for ITS applications where student-athletes may wear multiple devices simultaneously, each contributing distinct but complementary information about movement quality and physiological state.
*   **Psychological Data (Anxiety and Stress)**: Psychological state assessment introduces additional complexity, as constructs like anxiety and stress lack direct sensor measurements. The Competitive State Anxiety Inventory-2 (CSAI-2) provides validated self-report measures, but real-time assessment requires physiological proxies such as heart rate variability or electrodermal activity [30]. Multimodal anxiety recognition datasets enable training of models that infer psychological states from combined physiological and behavioral indicators [30]. For Intelligent Tutoring Systems grounded in Self-Determination Theory, psychological state monitoring supports autonomy and relatedness dimensions by adapting instructional strategies to learner anxiety levels [4]. Elevated anxiety may trigger simplified task progression or increased scaffolding, whereas optimal challenge states enable more autonomous skill exploration.
*   **Normalization and Standardization**: Data normalization proves essential before fusion, as heterogeneous sources exhibit vastly different scales and distributions. Min-Max Scaling transforms features to a common range, typically [0, 1], enabling meaningful comparison across modalities [2]. After outlier removal and missing value imputation, data are uniformly converted into numerical tensor expressions to ensure standardization of the data structure [25]. Table 1 in relevant studies shows changes in key statistical features of each modal data before and after preprocessing, demonstrating the magnitude of transformation required for effective fusion [25]. This standardization enables the shared backbone module to process attended features from the attention head and output deep, high-level feature maps suitable for classification tasks [28].
*   **Time Alignment Strategies**: Temporal synchronization represents the most critical preprocessing step for multimodal fusion. Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) provides distance measures between time series of different lengths, enabling alignment of movement sequences captured at varying frame rates [11]. Analysis windows define temporal segments for feature extraction, with window size and overlap parameters affecting both computational efficiency and temporal resolution [32]. For real-time ITS applications, time alignment must occur with minimal latency to preserve feedback immediacy. Wireless transmission reliability challenges in crowded radio frequency environments frequently necessitate post-session data downloading and processing, creating tension between real-time tutoring requirements and technical constraints [11]. Edge computing deployment mitigates this challenge by processing data locally rather than transmitting to cloud infrastructure, though computational capacity limitations on edge devices require careful model optimization.
*   **Data Quality and Limitations**: Evidence regarding longitudinal learning outcomes in sports-based ITS remains thin, requiring synthesis from related fields such as STEM Intelligent Tutoring Systems. While technical performance metrics demonstrate feasibility of multimodal fusion, pedagogical effectiveness evaluation using specific theories like Self-Determination Theory and Harrow's Taxonomy requires further empirical validation [4][6]. Student-athletes face challenges including inflexible technology, missing identity representation, and mismatched learning difficulty, indicating that technical fusion capabilities must align with user experience considerations [29]. The integration of multiple data sources enables prediction of internal load from external metrics, uncovering connections between movement patterns and physiological responses [32]. However, standardization of diagnostic protocols remains essential for widespread implementation, as current approaches vary significantly across studies and deployment contexts [12]. For Intelligent Tutoring Systems to achieve scalable impact, fusion methodologies must balance technical sophistication with practical deployability in educational sports settings.

## Intelligent Tutoring: Pedagogical Models for Skill Acquisition

The integration of artificial intelligence into sports training has created a critical distinction between systems that merely monitor performance and those that actively facilitate skill acquisition through pedagogical guidance. Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) in sports must transcend analytics dashboards to embody teaching mechanisms grounded in established educational theory. This section examines the pedagogical foundations that differentiate true intelligent tutoring from performance tracking, analyzing how theoretical frameworks inform feedback generation, adaptivity mechanisms, and the fundamental architecture of learning guidance in athletic contexts.

### Pedagogical Frameworks in Sports ITS

The development of Intelligent Tutoring Systems for sports skill acquisition requires anchoring in pedagogical theories that address both cognitive and psychomotor learning domains. Unlike traditional academic ITS, sports tutoring must account for physical skill development alongside conceptual understanding, necessitating frameworks that bridge educational psychology and motor learning theory.

#### Self-Determination Theory for Student-Athletes

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) has emerged as a foundational framework for designing ITS that support student-athletes balancing academic and athletic responsibilities. Research demonstrates that student-athletes frequently struggle to strike a balance between their academic and athletic responsibilities, creating unique motivational challenges that generic tutoring systems fail to address [20]. SDT-based ITS architectures explicitly target three psychological needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. When implemented correctly, these systems provide personalized learning environments that acknowledge the dual identity of student-athletes rather than treating athletic and academic development as separate domains [4].

The practical implementation of SDT in sports ITS involves adaptive content delivery that respects athlete autonomy while providing structured competence-building pathways. Systems designed around SDT principles offer choice in learning sequences, provide optimally challenging tasks that match current skill levels, and create opportunities for meaningful feedback that reinforces progress perception. Evidence indicates that SDT-proven approaches effectively address student-athletes' academic needs while maintaining engagement with athletic development goals [20]. However, the longitudinal effectiveness of SDT-based sports ITS remains underexplored, with most evidence drawn from cross-sectional studies rather than multi-season tracking of skill acquisition outcomes.

#### Harrow's Taxonomy and Psychomotor Skill Learning

For physical skill development, Harrow's Taxonomy provides a structured framework for categorizing psychomotor learning progression. This taxonomy consists of six levels that reflect increasing performance capability, ranging from basic physical abilities to complex skilled movements and origination of new movement patterns [6]. Unlike Bloom's cognitive taxonomy, Harrow's framework specifically addresses the physical dimensions of learning that dominate sports training.

| **Level** | **Definition** | **ITS Application** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Reflex Movements** | Basic involuntary responses forming the foundation for skilled action. | Baseline assessment of involuntary motor responses. |
| **Basic Fundamental Movements** | Locomotor and manipulative skills (running, grasping, throwing). | Foundational skill drills and technique correction. |
| **Perceptual Abilities** | Integration of sensory information with motor output. | Sensory-motor coordination tasks. |
| **Physical Abilities** | Strength, flexibility, endurance, and coordination capacities. | Conditioning and physical capacity monitoring. |
| **Skilled Movements** | Complex sport-specific techniques requiring precise execution. | Advanced technique tutoring and tactical execution. |
| **Non-discursive Communication** | Expressive movements including dance and gesture-based communication. | Artistic sports assessment (e.g., gymnastics, dance). |

ITS architectures incorporating Harrow's Taxonomy must assess athlete performance at appropriate taxonomy levels before advancing to more complex skill requirements. Systems like Selfit have encountered significant challenges in building psychomotor intelligent tutoring systems that accurately map athlete performance to taxonomy levels [6]. The difficulty lies in translating continuous physical movement data into discrete taxonomy categories that inform pedagogical decisions. Computer vision approaches using image recognition can suggest trainer interventions based on user appearance and movement patterns, but the connection between visual analysis and taxonomy-based progression requires careful validation [6].

#### Activity Theory and the Activity-Oriented Design Method

Activity Theory provides a complementary framework for understanding the socio-technical context in which sports ITS operate. The Activity-Oriented Design Method (AODM), authored by Moza Alkubaisi Moza, applies Activity Theory principles to optimize adaptive learning in educational settings [16]. This approach examines the relationships between subjects (athletes), tools (ITS platforms), objects (skill acquisition goals), rules (training protocols), community (coaching staff and teammates), and division of labor (responsibility distribution between athlete, coach, and system).

Mwanza-Simwami's Eight-Steps Model, guided by Activity Theory as an underpinning theoretical framework, has been adapted to gain insights about perceptions towards ITS integration for adaptive learning support [16]. In sports contexts, this model helps designers identify contradictions between traditional coaching practices and automated tutoring interventions. For instance, when an ITS recommends training modifications based on fatigue detection, the system must navigate the division of labor between automated guidance and human coach authority. Activity Theory analysis reveals that successful ITS implementation requires aligning system objectives with existing community practices rather than attempting to replace human coaching relationships.

#### Generalized Intelligent Framework for Tutoring (GIFT)

The Generalized Intelligent Framework for Tutoring (GIFT) provides a modular architecture that allows integration of multiple pedagogical models into unified tutoring systems. As a modular architecture, GIFT allows users to integrate new pedagogical models, instructional strategies, or instructional tactics from other tutoring systems [1]. This flexibility is critical for sports ITS, which must combine psychomotor assessment (Harrow's Taxonomy), motivational support (SDT), and contextual awareness (Activity Theory) within a single platform.

GIFT's associated objectives include modeling and adapting to individual differences such as states, traits, and preferences [1]. For student-athletes, this means the system must track not only skill progression but also fatigue states, motivation levels, and learning style preferences. The framework supports integration of conversational dialogue systems like AutoTutor and CognitiveTutor, which have been utilized to support learning experiences with feedback or through dialogs [6]. However, adapting these primarily text-based dialogue systems to sports contexts requires significant modification to handle real-time physical performance data alongside conversational interaction.

### Feedback Mechanisms & Adaptivity

The pedagogical effectiveness of sports ITS depends fundamentally on how feedback is generated, delivered, and adapted to individual athlete needs. Feedback mechanisms must distinguish between corrective guidance that teaches skill improvement and performance monitoring that merely reports metrics. This distinction shapes both the technical architecture and the pedagogical impact of the system.

#### Real-Time Alerts Versus Post-Session Reports

Feedback timing represents a critical design decision with significant pedagogical implications. Real-time feedback during training sessions enables athletes to make quick modifications to improve their performance [21]. AI-powered gadgets providing instant feedback can make all the difference for athletes who want to push themselves to the edge, whether correcting form mid-repetition or adjusting technique during live drills. However, real-time feedback introduces technical challenges related to processing latency and attentional load.

Post-session data downloading and processing remains necessary for many systems due to wireless data transmission reliability challenges in crowded radio frequency environments [11]. This creates a fundamental tension: pedagogically optimal feedback occurs immediately after performance execution, but technically feasible feedback often requires delayed processing. Systems must balance these constraints by implementing tiered feedback architectures that provide immediate alerts for critical safety or form issues while reserving detailed analytical feedback for post-session review.

The distinction between corrective drills and load warnings exemplifies different feedback types serving different pedagogical purposes. Corrective drills address technique deficiencies through prescriptive guidance (e.g., "increase knee flexion during landing"), while load warnings address physiological readiness through descriptive alerts (e.g., "reduce training volume due to accumulated fatigue"). Machine learning algorithms combining internal training loads with external metrics can predict non-contact injuries in elite football players by detecting subtle patterns across many data streams [32]. However, injury prediction constitutes performance monitoring rather than skill tutoring unless coupled with prescriptive guidance on technique modifications that reduce injury risk.

#### Adaptive Learning Trajectories Based on Mastery Levels

Adaptivity in sports ITS requires dynamic adjustment of training content based on demonstrated mastery levels. The DDPG (Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient) algorithm enables adaptive training adjustments that dynamically optimize training policy based on real-time feedback [33]. This reinforcement learning approach treats training progression as a sequential decision problem, where each training session modifies the policy for subsequent sessions based on performance outcomes.

Adaptive systems must account for heterogeneity in students' knowledge levels and the need to adapt pedagogical methods to digital contexts [2]. Student perceptions indicate high appreciation for adaptive feedback usefulness, with 80% of users in one study appreciating the usefulness of adaptive feedback [2]. However, sports adaptivity introduces complexity beyond academic ITS because physical performance varies significantly based on fatigue, recovery status, and environmental conditions that do not affect cognitive learning to the same degree.

VRBT (Virtual Reality-Based Training) systems demonstrate autoregulation capabilities for sets, repetitions, and load based on real-time performance assessment [6]. This granular organization during training sessions enables personalization involving fatigue detection and task adaptation recommendations. The system can explore personalization at the cycle level using AI for personalized planning of micro, meso, or macro cycles [6]. However, the pedagogical validity of automated periodization recommendations requires validation against coach expertise and longitudinal athlete development outcomes.

#### Multimodal Data Fusion for Feedback Generation

Effective feedback generation requires integrating multiple data modalities to create comprehensive performance assessments. Multimodal Data Fusion for Enhanced CNN-LSTM architectures combines computer vision analysis with temporal sequence modeling to capture both spatial movement patterns and their evolution over time [25]. The spatiotemporal feature extraction architecture employs hierarchical 3D convolutional network design that captures both spatial relationships within individual frames and temporal dependencies across behavioral sequences [30].

Eye-Gaze Guided Multimodal Fusion Frameworks enhance diagnostic accuracy by incorporating attention patterns into performance assessment [28]. This approach recognizes that expert athletes differ from novices not only in movement execution but also in visual attention allocation. However, challenges remain in handling noisy fixation data and ensuring that gaze-based methodologies align with real-world diagnostic scenarios [28]. The theoretical significance of combining spatiotemporal feature extraction with adaptive attention mechanisms provides a principled framework for processing multimodal behavioral data that advances both computer vision and sports psychology methodologies [30].

#### Edge Versus Cloud Computing for Feedback Latency

The architectural choice between edge and cloud computing directly impacts feedback latency and pedagogical effectiveness. Mobile edge computing technology deploys network servers in various regions, focusing more on users rather than cloud computing, reducing the response time delay of user service requests [13]. This improvement in user service experience and reduction in network transmission burden proves critical for real-time tutoring applications where feedback must occur within the temporal window of skill execution.

Cloud computing excels at storing large amounts of data not needed in real-time [17]. This makes cloud infrastructure appropriate for longitudinal performance tracking, historical comparison, and complex model training that does not require immediate results. However, for real-time feedback during training, edge computing provides the latency characteristics necessary for pedagogically effective intervention. Distributed computing architectures have enabled near real-time implementation of these methodologies, facilitating practical applications in both training and competitive environments [11].

The decision between edge and cloud deployment requires determining where and for what purpose an organization will leverage either Edge Computing or cloud computing [31]. Hybrid architectures that process time-critical feedback at the edge while aggregating data for cloud-based analytics represent the most pedagogically sound approach, though they introduce system complexity and synchronization challenges.

### Distinguishing Tutoring from Performance Monitoring

A critical analytical challenge in sports AI involves distinguishing between systems that teach and systems that merely track. This distinction determines whether an application qualifies as an Intelligent Tutoring System or functions as Coaching Analytics infrastructure. The conflation of automated feedback with human coaching support in existing literature obscures this fundamental difference, requiring explicit evaluation criteria.

#### Learning Guidance Versus Performance Metrics

Intelligent Tutoring Systems are computer software designed to simulate or imitate a human tutor's behavior and guidance [3]. This definition emphasizes the pedagogical function of providing guidance rather than simply reporting data. Performance monitoring systems impacted by AI can process vast quantities of information, detecting patterns and relationships that might not be immediately apparent to humans [23]. However, pattern detection alone does not constitute tutoring unless coupled with prescriptive recommendations for skill improvement.

The distinction becomes clear when examining feedback content. A performance monitoring system reports that an athlete's sprint speed decreased by 5% compared to baseline. A tutoring system explains why the decrease occurred (e.g., reduced knee drive angle) and provides corrective drills to address the specific technique deficiency. Machine learning is gaining traction in analyzing complex biomechanical information in sports, but the term "Integrated Systems" refers to platforms combining hardware sensors with AI-driven dashboards for real-time feedback and compliance tracking [12]. Compliance tracking represents monitoring rather than tutoring unless the system adapts training prescriptions based on compliance patterns.

Research indicates that AI coaching tools can uncover data-driven insights into an individual's skill set, offering personalized suggestions [27]. However, the integration of Artificial Intelligence into training has challenged traditional paradigms of human mentorship without necessarily replacing the pedagogical functions that distinguish tutoring from monitoring [27]. The enduring value of human mentorship persists because AI systems struggle with the contextual judgment and relational dynamics that effective teaching requires.

#### Evidence on Learning Guidance Effectiveness

Intelligent Tutoring Systems have been explored for effectiveness in adaptive learning experiences across educational domains [16]. Casamichana et al. demonstrated the effectiveness of learning strategies delivered via Learning Management Systems, though this evidence derives primarily from academic rather than athletic contexts [12]. In the studies synthesized, LMS frequently connect with AI-enabled applications, such as Moodle integrated with the GeoGebra Interactive Formative Test (GIFT), the ERUDITE adaptive tutoring platform, and NAVIGO, an adaptive personalized literacy game [12].

The transfer of this evidence to sports contexts requires careful consideration. Sports skill acquisition involves psychomotor learning that differs fundamentally from cognitive skill development in mathematics or literacy. Selfit challenges encountered in building psychomotor intelligent tutoring systems highlight the unique difficulties of applying ITS principles to physical skill domains [6]. The system uses OntoStrength as a knowledge base to recommend workout routines based on a template, but template-based recommendations lack the adaptive sophistication of true tutoring systems that model individual learner states [6].

#### Longitudinal Learning Outcomes: Evidence Limitations

A significant gap in sports ITS literature involves longitudinal learning outcomes. Despite evident benefits, there are significant gaps in current research, particularly concerning the psychological impacts of AI, its accessibility to amateur athletes, and the long-term effects on performance and well-being [18]. Further exploration is needed to understand how ITS interventions affect skill retention, transfer to competitive performance, and athlete development trajectories across multiple seasons.

This evidence limitation requires synthesizing broader implications from related fields. STEM ITS research provides more robust longitudinal evidence, but the applicability to sports remains uncertain. Intelligent Tutoring Systems offer potential for creating personalized learning environments [20], yet the translation from academic personalization to athletic personalization involves unvalidated assumptions about how physical skill learning responds to adaptive interventions.

#### Critical Evaluation Criteria

Evaluating whether a system teaches or tracks requires examining several dimensions:

*   **Prescriptive Versus Descriptive Output**: Tutoring systems provide actionable guidance for improvement; monitoring systems report current state without improvement pathways.
*   **Adaptive Versus Static Feedback**: Tutoring systems modify guidance based on learner response; monitoring systems apply consistent metrics regardless of individual progression.
*   **Pedagogical Model Integration**: Tutoring systems embed explicit learning theories (SDT, Harrow's Taxonomy); monitoring systems rely on performance benchmarks without theoretical grounding.
*   **Learner Modeling**: Tutoring systems maintain dynamic models of learner knowledge, skills, and motivational states; monitoring systems track performance metrics without inferring underlying capabilities.

Systems failing these criteria function as Coaching Analytics rather than Intelligent Tutoring, regardless of AI sophistication. The architectural choice between tutoring and monitoring shapes not only technical implementation but also the fundamental relationship between athlete and technology. True intelligent tutoring requires accepting the complexity of pedagogical modeling rather than retreating to the simpler task of performance measurement.

## Application Domains: Deployment Scenarios & Use Cases

The deployment of AI-driven Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) in sports spans multiple application domains, each with distinct technical requirements, pedagogical objectives, and user populations. This section examines three primary deployment scenarios: injury prevention and health monitoring, skill acquisition and tactical analysis, and student-athlete academic-athletic balance. Throughout this analysis, a critical distinction is maintained between "Intelligent Tutoring" systems, which provide pedagogical guidance and facilitate skill learning through adaptive feedback, and "Coaching Analytics" platforms, which focus on performance monitoring and load tracking without necessarily incorporating instructional scaffolding [16][6]. This distinction is essential because sources frequently conflate automated feedback with human coaching support, yet the user intent for "Intelligent Tutoring" specifically requires pedagogical grounding rather than mere data visualization.

### Injury Prevention & Health Monitoring

Injury prevention represents one of the most mature application domains for AI-driven systems in sports, with machine learning models demonstrating significant capability in detecting improper motion patterns and predicting injury risks before they manifest. The prevention aspect of tutoring in this domain focuses on providing athletes with real-time corrective feedback that addresses biomechanical anomalies associated with elevated injury probability, rather than simply flagging risk after the fact.

Research by Rossi et al. developed a hamstring injury risk model using Random Forests that incorporated kinematic, kinetic, and workload variables among soccer players, achieving notable predictive accuracy in identifying athletes at elevated risk [12]. This model exemplifies the transition from reactive injury management to proactive prevention, where the ITS functions as a pedagogical agent that guides athletes toward safer movement patterns. The system's effectiveness depends on the quality of input data and the sophistication of the fusion architecture employed to integrate multiple sensor modalities.

IoT-enabled wearable devices have become central to health monitoring applications, with recent innovations integrating artificial synapses and flexible sensors to capture fine-grained biomechanical data [7]. These systems employ feature-level fusion technology, combining Support Vector Machine (SVM) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks to process temporal sequences of movement data. The feature-level fusion approach (Intermediate Fusion) allows the system to extract meaningful representations from each modality before combining them, which is particularly effective for injury prediction where the timing and coordination of movements matter more than raw sensor values alone [7].

The architectural choice between Edge and Cloud computing has direct implications for injury prevention tutoring. Edge computing enables real-time feedback by processing sensor data locally on the device or nearby gateway, reducing latency to levels acceptable for immediate corrective guidance during training [17]. Cloud computing, by contrast, is better suited for storing large amounts of historical data not needed in real-time, supporting longitudinal injury risk modeling and population-level analysis [17][13]. For injury prevention tutoring, a hybrid architecture is often optimal: edge processing for immediate feedback on movement patterns, with cloud synchronization for trend analysis and model refinement.

Improper running mechanics, for instance, can be detected through accelerometer and gyroscope signals processed by CNN-LSTM architectures, with the system providing auditory or haptic feedback when stride patterns deviate from safe parameters [7]. This prevents injuries like stress fractures or muscle strains by intervening at the moment of improper execution rather than after cumulative damage has occurred. However, challenges remain in handling noisy sensor data and ensuring that detection methodologies align with real-world training scenarios rather than controlled laboratory conditions [28].

It must be acknowledged that while injury prediction models show promise, standardization of diagnostic protocols remains essential for widespread implementation [12]. The pedagogical effectiveness of injury prevention tutoring has not been extensively evaluated using established educational theories, representing a gap in the literature. Most studies focus on technical accuracy metrics rather than learning outcomes, suggesting that the field would benefit from applying frameworks like Harrow's Taxonomy to assess how effectively athletes internalize safer movement patterns through ITS guidance [6].

### Skill Acquisition & Tactical Analysis

Skill acquisition and tactical analysis constitute the core domain where Intelligent Tutoring Systems demonstrate their pedagogical value, distinguishing themselves from pure coaching analytics through structured instructional sequences and adaptive feedback mechanisms. This domain encompasses both individual technical skill learning (such as shooting mechanics or stride length optimization) and collective tactical understanding (such as football positioning and team synchronization).

In professional football (soccer), contemporary sensor networks have been deployed to analyze tactical formations, player positioning, and collective pressing behaviors through GPS-enabled devices integrated with tactical video analysis systems [11]. Research by leading European clubs has demonstrated correlations between team synchronization metrics and successful defensive organization, providing empirical grounding for tactical tutoring interventions [11]. However, the transformation of this analytical capability into an Intelligent Tutoring System requires more than data visualization; it demands pedagogical scaffolding that guides athletes through progressive tactical understanding.

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) have been applied to tactics analysis and evaluation, particularly in women's football team assessments where spatial positioning patterns are critical to performance [25]. The multimodal data fusion architecture employed in these systems typically combines CNN for spatial feature extraction with LSTM for temporal sequence modeling, creating a CNN-LSTM hybrid that captures both the static positioning and dynamic movement patterns essential to tactical understanding [25]. This represents Intermediate Fusion, where features from each modality are extracted before combination, allowing the system to weight spatial and temporal information appropriately for tactical decision-making.

For individual skill acquisition, systems like Selfit use OntoStrength as a knowledge base to recommend workout routines based on templates, though this approaches coaching analytics more closely than true intelligent tutoring [6]. A more pedagogically grounded approach would incorporate learning theories such as Harrow's Taxonomy, which provides a framework for assessing skill proficiency across levels from basic reflexive movements to complex overt responses [6]. When ITS platforms apply this taxonomy, they can adapt instruction to the athlete's current skill level, providing appropriately challenging tasks that promote progression through the skill continuum.

The distinction between elite and amateur athletes becomes particularly relevant in tactical analysis applications. Elite athletes benefit from fine-grained tactical feedback that assumes substantial foundational knowledge, while amateur or student-athletes require more scaffolded instruction that builds tactical understanding from fundamental principles. Smartphone-based evaluation systems enable sophisticated analysis by tapping into consumer technology, reducing expense and complexity barriers to deployment and potentially democratizing access to these capabilities [12]. This democratization is essential for extending intelligent tutoring benefits beyond professional contexts to youth and amateur sports.

Real-time feedback during training sessions enables athletes to make quick modifications to improve performance, with AI-powered gadgets providing instant corrective guidance [21]. However, the latency requirements for effective tutoring vary by application: technical skill correction may tolerate slightly higher latency than tactical decision-making during live play, where milliseconds matter. Edge computing deployment reduces response time delay of user service requests by deploying network servers in various regions, focusing more on users rather than cloud computing [13]. This architectural choice directly impacts the feasibility of real-time tactical tutoring during competitive scenarios.

Evidence on longitudinal learning outcomes in skill acquisition through ITS remains thin, with most studies focusing on immediate performance improvements rather than sustained skill retention or transfer [16]. This limitation should be acknowledged explicitly, and broader implications can be synthesized from related fields such as STEM ITS, where longitudinal studies have demonstrated sustained learning gains through adaptive tutoring [16]. The sports domain would benefit from similar rigorous evaluation designs that track athletes over extended periods to assess true pedagogical effectiveness.

### Student-Athlete Academic & Athletic Balance

The challenge of balancing academic and athletic responsibilities represents a unique application domain for Intelligent Tutoring Systems, where the tutoring function extends beyond sports skills to encompass time management, academic support, and holistic development. Student-athletes frequently struggle to strike a balance between their academic and athletic responsibilities, creating a need for systems that address both domains simultaneously rather than treating them as separate concerns [20].

Intelligent Tutoring Systems designed for student-athletes have been developed based on Self-Determination Theory (SDT), which provides a robust pedagogical framework for supporting autonomy, competence, and relatedness in learning contexts [20][4]. SDT-based ITS architectures recognize that student-athletes face unique motivational challenges, including competing demands on time and energy, potential identity conflicts between academic and athletic roles, and varying levels of institutional support. By grounding the tutoring system in SDT, designers can create interventions that support intrinsic motivation rather than relying solely on external rewards or compliance monitoring.

The design and implementation of SDT-based ITS for student-athletes involves collecting log data from the system and conducting interviews with student-athletes, teachers, and coaches to gain comprehensive insights into the dual-career experience [29]. This mixed-methods approach ensures that the tutoring system addresses real needs rather than assumed requirements. Career athletic motivation, as measured through instruments like SAMSAQ, becomes a component that the ITS can monitor and support, adapting its guidance based on the student-athlete's current motivational state and perceived balance between domains [29].

Scheduling tools represent a practical application of AI in supporting student-athlete balance, with predictive analytics capable of flagging early warning signs when someone starts to slip, long before a failed exam or serious injury exposes the problem [24]. Rather than a single magic application, the emerging picture is an ecosystem comprising tutoring bots, scheduling tools, wearable data platforms, and communication systems that work in concert to support the whole student-athlete [24]. This ecosystem approach acknowledges that no single intervention can address the multifaceted challenges of dual-career management.

The pedagogical effectiveness of SDT-based ITS for student-athletes has been evaluated through measures of student satisfaction, perception of progress, and academic performance, though longitudinal evidence remains limited [29]. Time emerges as the critical currency for student-athletes, with intelligent scheduling systems needing to optimize not just for efficiency but for sustainability, ensuring that athletes do not sacrifice recovery or well-being in pursuit of academic or athletic excellence [24].

Activity Theory provides an additional lens for examining how ITS supports student-athlete practices, highlighting the ethical issues associated with adaptive learning in educational settings [16]. The Activity-Oriented Design Method (AODM) has been used to examine perceptions of teachers towards how an ITS supports their practices in providing adaptive learning, revealing that successful implementation requires alignment between the tutoring system's logic and the existing activity systems of schools and athletic programs [16]. This alignment is particularly critical for student-athletes, who navigate multiple institutional contexts with potentially conflicting expectations.

The distinction between elite and amateur student-athletes manifests in the level of institutional support available. Elite student-athletes at NCAA Division I institutions may have access to dedicated academic support staff and sophisticated scheduling systems, while amateur or high school student-athletes rely more heavily on automated ITS capabilities. This disparity suggests that ITS designers must create scalable solutions that provide meaningful support regardless of institutional resources, potentially through cloud-based platforms that democratize access to sophisticated tutoring capabilities [17].

Evidence on the long-term impact of ITS interventions on student-athlete retention, graduation rates, and athletic career longevity remains thin, representing a significant gap in the literature [20]. Broader implications can be synthesized from general ITS research in education, where adaptive tutoring has demonstrated positive effects on learning outcomes across diverse populations, but sports-specific longitudinal studies are needed to validate these findings in the student-athlete context [16]. The integration of wearable data with academic performance tracking offers promising avenues for future research, potentially revealing connections between training load, recovery, and academic achievement that could inform more holistic tutoring interventions.

## Performance Evaluation: Metrics for Learning vs. Analytics

The evaluation of AI-driven Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) in sports requires a dual-lens approach that distinguishes between technical system performance and pedagogical effectiveness. This distinction is critical because sources frequently conflate automated feedback mechanisms with genuine human coaching support, obscuring the specific value proposition of intelligent tutoring. Technical metrics assess whether the system functions correctly—measuring accuracy, latency, and computational efficiency—while pedagogical metrics determine whether learning actually occurs through skill retention, motivation enhancement, and engagement. The current sports AI literature demonstrates a pronounced imbalance: technical performance metrics are well-established and routinely reported, whereas standardized pedagogical metrics remain underdeveloped and inconsistently applied across studies.

### Technical Performance Metrics

Technical performance evaluation in sports AI systems centers on quantifiable measures of model accuracy, computational efficiency, and real-time processing capability. The standard metrics employed across the literature include Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-Score, Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), and Latency. These indicators serve as the foundation for comparing different architectural approaches, particularly when evaluating Transformer-based models against CNN-LSTM hybrid configurations.

*   **Standard AI Performance Indicators**: Primary evaluation indicators for sports AI systems encompass accuracy for overall classification performance, precision and recall for class-specific performance assessment, F1-score for balanced evaluation of precision-recall trade-offs, and computational efficiency metrics including inference time [30]. Performance metrics are synthesized using descriptive statistics with attention to contextual factors influencing model performance, including ranges, medians, and frequencies where appropriate [12]. Position accuracy is commonly measured through Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), with studies reporting RMSE values alongside processing latency measured on standardized hardware configurations such as Intel i7-10750H processors with 16GB RAM [11]. Noise resistance testing is conducted by injecting Gaussian noise at varying signal-to-noise ratios (10–35 dB) to evaluate system robustness under realistic conditions [11].
*   **Model Architecture Comparisons: Transformer vs. CNN-LSTM**: Comparative studies demonstrate that deep learning approaches often provide superior performance for complex spatiotemporal data analysis, though traditional machine learning methods maintain advantages in settings with limited data availability or where model interpretability is prioritized [12]. CNN architectures have achieved superior accuracy in video analysis applications, with reported recall rates reaching 89.3% in specific implementations [7]. CNN-LSTM hybrid models are specifically employed for recognizing video-recorded actions performed in traditional exercises and sports movements, combining spatial feature extraction (CNN) with temporal sequence modeling (LSTM) [25]. These multimodal architectures enable the system to capture both the spatial configuration of body positions and the temporal dynamics of movement execution. Recent investigations leverage deep learning algorithms that analyze both performance metrics and temporal dynamics, addressing limitations of single-modal analysis that lack the temporal dynamics essential for understanding progression in competitive environments [30].
*   **Edge vs. Cloud Computing: Latency Implications for Real-Time Tutoring**: The architectural choice between Edge and Cloud computing directly impacts the feasibility of real-time tutoring feedback, a core requirement for intelligent tutoring systems. Mobile edge computing technology deploys network servers in various regions, focusing more on users rather than cloud computing, reducing the response time delay of user service requests, thereby improving user service experience and reducing network transmission burden [13]. Cloud computing excels at storing large amounts of data not needed in real-time, having been one of the major contributors to digital transformation over the past two decades through massively reducing the cost of storing and processing data [17]. However, for intelligent tutoring applications requiring immediate feedback during training sessions, edge computing enables near real-time implementation of methodologies, facilitating practical applications in both training and competitive environments [11]. Distributed computing architectures have enabled near real-time implementation of these methodologies, though significant research gaps persist in developing fusion methods that optimize the edge-cloud balance for sports tutoring applications [11]. The decision between edge and cloud deployment must consider whether the application requires immediate feedback (favoring edge) or can tolerate latency for more comprehensive analysis (favoring cloud).

### Pedagogical Effectiveness Metrics

Measuring pedagogical effectiveness in sports ITS requires moving beyond pure performance gains (speed, strength, accuracy improvements) to assess whether genuine learning occurs. This distinction is fundamental to differentiating Intelligent Tutoring (pedagogical guidance, skill learning) from Coaching Analytics (performance monitoring, load tracking). The current literature reveals a significant gap: while technical metrics are consistently reported, pedagogical metrics lack standardization and theoretical grounding.

#### Defining Learning in Sports ITS Contexts

Learning in sports intelligent tutoring systems must be operationalized through multiple dimensions that capture skill acquisition, retention, and motivational outcomes. Intelligent Tutoring Systems are computer software designed to simulate or imitate a human tutor's behavior and guidance, with the capability to interpret complex student responses and provide adaptive feedback [3]. However, the effectiveness of these systems depends on whether they genuinely facilitate learning rather than merely providing performance data.

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) provides a theoretically grounded framework for measuring motivational outcomes in student-athlete contexts. ITS platforms are specifically designed to cater to varying levels of motivation among student-athletes, who frequently struggle to balance academic and athletic responsibilities [20]. The design and implementation of intelligent tutoring systems for student-athletes based on Self-Determination Theory addresses the need for personalized learning experiences that support autonomy, competence, and relatedness—the three core psychological needs identified in SDT [4].

#### Harrow's Taxonomy for Psychomotor Skill Assessment

Harrow's Taxonomy provides a structured framework for assessing psychomotor skill development in sports contexts, offering levels that capture the progression from basic movement execution to complex, automated performance. Physical activities in sports ITS applications allow for inclusion of movements like stretching, grasping, walking, high jumping, strength, flexibility, complex sports movements, or movements for dancing or expressions through bodily movements [6]. These elements are crucial for psychomotor skill development and are not substantially captured by other taxonomies. While Thomas's taxonomy provides levels such as Strength or Coordination, the focus lies more in learning outcomes, making the application more suitable for educational assessment rather than pure performance tracking [6]. Simpson (1972) is associated with psychomotor learning frameworks that complement Harrow's taxonomy, providing additional granularity for skill assessment in tutoring contexts [1].

#### Motivation and Engagement Metrics

Motivation levels are measured through SDT-aligned instruments that assess autonomy support, perceived competence, and relatedness to the learning environment. Student perceptions of adaptive feedback systems show high appreciation rates, with 80% of students appreciating the usefulness of adaptive feedback in educational settings [2]. These findings identify the system's potential to transform teaching, address the lack of personalization, and improve learning in various educational settings.

Academic engagement metrics complement motivation measures by tracking interaction frequency, time-on-task, and completion rates. The system structure is organized into specific modules, each with distinct functionality that contributes to personalizing and adapting content, understanding student queries, recommending materials, and providing real-time assessment of learning progress [2]. This modular design enables granular tracking of engagement patterns across different learning activities.

#### Contrast with Pure Performance Gains

A critical distinction must be maintained between pedagogical effectiveness and performance analytics. Performance gains measured through speed, strength, or accuracy improvements reflect coaching analytics outcomes rather than tutoring effectiveness. AI/ML systems anticipating performance or health outcomes combine internal training loads with external metrics to predict non-contact injuries in elite football players, detecting subtle patterns across many data streams [32]. While valuable for load management and injury prevention, these metrics do not capture whether the athlete has learned the underlying skill or tactical concept.

Machine learning is gaining traction in analyzing complex biomechanical information in sports, with integrated systems referring to platforms combining hardware sensors with AI-driven dashboards for real-time feedback and compliance tracking [12]. However, compliance tracking and biomechanical analysis serve coaching analytics functions rather than pedagogical assessment. The distinction matters because a system can improve measured performance through external guidance without fostering independent skill mastery—the hallmark of effective tutoring.

#### Standardization Gaps in Pedagogical Metrics

The sports AI literature demonstrates a pronounced lack of standardized pedagogical metrics. While technical performance indicators follow established machine learning conventions, pedagogical assessment varies widely across studies, with some employing validated psychological instruments and others relying on ad-hoc performance measures. This fragmentation limits cross-study comparison and meta-analytic synthesis.

Evidence is particularly thin regarding longitudinal learning outcomes in sports ITS applications. Where longitudinal data is unavailable, broader implications must be synthesized from related fields such as STEM ITS, where longer evaluation periods are more common. The lack of confidence intervals and statistical significance in many sports AI studies further complicates pedagogical effectiveness claims, necessitating additional analyses to address these methodological gaps [7].

## Critical Synthesis: Barriers, Ethics, & Future Trajectories

The deployment of AI-driven Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) in sports contexts presents a complex intersection of pedagogical innovation, technical feasibility, and ethical responsibility. While the preceding sections have detailed the architectural construction and multimodal fusion mechanisms enabling these systems, this section critically examines the barriers to adoption, ethical implications, and future research trajectories necessary for sustainable implementation. The distinction between Intelligent Tutoring (focused on pedagogical guidance and skill learning) and Coaching Analytics (centered on performance monitoring and load tracking) remains critical throughout this synthesis, as conflating these functions obscures the unique privacy, trust, and pedagogical challenges each presents.

### Ethical Considerations & Data Privacy

The collection and processing of multimodal athlete data in ITS environments introduces significant privacy risks that demand rigorous ethical frameworks. Student-athletes represent a particularly vulnerable population, as their data encompasses biometric signals, video recordings of physical performance, and academic progress metrics—all of which carry distinct privacy implications when aggregated into unified tutoring platforms. Research indicates that Institutional Review Board approval must be obtained prior to any data collection involving athlete monitoring, with protocol identifiers such as IRB-2023-SPT-001 establishing baseline accountability for data protection practices [30]. However, compliance with institutional guidelines alone proves insufficient when addressing the full scope of privacy violations possible in AI-driven sports biomechanics systems [12].

Multimodal data fusion in sports ITS typically operates at the intermediate or late fusion level, combining inputs from wearable sensors (accelerometers, gyroscopes, heart rate monitors), computer vision systems (MediaPipe, OpenPose), and learning management system logs. Each modality introduces distinct privacy vulnerabilities. Biometric data collected through wearable devices like the Empatica E4 or Polar H10 reveals physiological states that could be exploited for non-consensual performance profiling. Video-based motion capture, while essential for psychomotor skill assessment under Harrow's Taxonomy, creates permanent records of athletic performance that may persist beyond the student-athlete's competitive career. The Declaration of Helsinki establishes ethical standards for human research, yet its application to commercial sports tutoring platforms remains inconsistently enforced [11].

Consent mechanisms in student-athlete contexts face particular challenges due to power asymmetries between coaches, institutions, and athletes. When universities, coaches, and athletes treat AI as a set of tools rather than a replacement for human contact, the result is a more stable dual career pathway [24]. However, this collaborative framing does not resolve fundamental questions about data ownership. Student-athletes frequently lack clarity regarding who retains rights to their performance data after graduation, whether third-party vendors may commercialize aggregated datasets, and what opt-out mechanisms exist without jeopardizing team participation. Evidence suggests that data availability statements in sports AI research often indicate secondary analysis of de-identified data, yet the original consent waivers obtained by data custodians may not cover downstream tutoring applications [28].

The thin evidence base on long-term privacy outcomes in sports ITS necessitates drawing parallels from adjacent fields. STEM-based Intelligent Tutoring Systems have documented cases where student interaction logs were repurposed for commercial analytics without explicit consent, creating precedents that sports ITS developers must proactively avoid. Kamruzzaman et al. emphasize the need for active development and integration of AI in education while highlighting ethical considerations associated with such tools, particularly regarding data privacy, transparency of algorithms, and understanding the contexts in which AI coaching is most effective [16][27]. For sports ITS specifically, this translates to mandatory data minimization protocols, clear retention schedules, and athlete-controlled data portability features that distinguish tutoring systems from surveillance-oriented coaching analytics platforms.

Explainable AI (XAI) mechanisms represent one technical approach to addressing privacy-transparency tensions. AI models designed to provide understandable justifications for their outputs enable student-athletes to comprehend why specific tutoring recommendations emerge from their data, reducing the opacity that fuels privacy concerns [12]. However, XAI implementation in multimodal fusion architectures—particularly those employing CNN-LSTM or Transformer-enhanced deep fusion models—remains computationally demanding and may conflict with real-time feedback requirements. This tension between interpretability and latency constitutes an unresolved ethical-technical tradeoff requiring explicit acknowledgment in system design documentation.

### Adoption Challenges & Human-in-the-Loop

The integration of Artificial Intelligence into sports tutoring has challenged traditional paradigms of human mentorship, creating adoption friction that extends beyond technical feasibility [27]. Coach resistance to AI-driven ITS stems from multiple sources: perceived threats to professional authority, skepticism about algorithmic accuracy in sport-specific contexts, and concerns about diminished athlete-coach relationships. Early studies on AI coaching have shown promise, with AI chatbots providing effective guidance in specific contexts, yet challenges such as ensuring data privacy, transparency of algorithms, and understanding the contexts in which AI coaching is most effective remain significant barriers [27].

The "AI Coach vs. Human Mentor" dynamic requires careful conceptual separation. Intelligent Tutoring Systems focused on pedagogical guidance—such as teaching tactical decision-making through Self-Determination Theory-based adaptive instruction—operate fundamentally differently from Coaching Analytics platforms that monitor training load and injury risk. When this distinction blurs, coaches may perceive ITS as encroaching on their domain expertise rather than augmenting it. Research indicates that the value of coaching evaluations lies in a hybrid approach where AI handles granular performance analysis while human mentors provide contextual interpretation and emotional support [27]. This hybrid model aligns with evidence showing that student perceptions were highly positive, with 80% appreciating the usefulness of adaptive feedback when systems address the lack of personalization and improve learning in various educational settings [2].

Trust in AI tutoring systems develops through consistent accuracy, transparent reasoning, and demonstrated respect for human expertise boundaries. Systems that position themselves as replacements for coaches encounter significantly higher resistance than those framing AI as decision-support tools. The Generalized Intelligent Framework for Tutoring (GIFT) exemplifies this approach by allowing users to integrate new pedagogical models while maintaining human oversight of instructional tactics [1]. For sports contexts, this translates to ITS architectures where CNN-LSTM models process movement sequences and generate skill proficiency assessments, but human coaches retain final authority on training plan modifications and athlete welfare decisions.

Human-in-the-loop mechanisms prove essential for maintaining pedagogical effectiveness grounded in educational theory rather than purely performance optimization. Self-Determination Theory-based ITS for student-athletes demonstrates that adaptive learning can address academic-athletic balance by supporting autonomy, competence, and relatedness needs [4][20]. However, AI systems alone cannot fulfill the relatedness component—athletes require genuine human connection to sustain motivation through setbacks. This limitation necessitates design patterns where ITS handles repetitive skill drills and knowledge assessments while human mentors intervene for motivational counseling, career guidance, and complex tactical discussions that exceed current AI contextual understanding.

Adoption challenges also manifest at the institutional level. Systems face challenges due to increasing complexity of content, heterogeneity in students' knowledge levels, and the need to adapt pedagogical methods to digital contexts [2]. Sports programs vary widely in resources, technical infrastructure, and coaching philosophies, making one-size-fits-all ITS deployments impractical. Successful adoption requires modular architectures allowing institutions to select components matching their specific needs—some may prioritize video-based technique analysis using 3D-CNN models, while others emphasize academic tutoring integration through LMS connectivity. The NFHS emphasizes that coaching staff must continue to learn and improve to provide the best environment and learning opportunities for student-athletes, suggesting that ITS adoption should include coach education components rather than solely athlete-facing tools [10].

### Future Research Directions

Despite evident benefits, significant gaps persist in current research on AI-driven sports ITS, particularly concerning longitudinal learning outcomes, generalizability across sports, and real-time edge deployment scalability. Further exploration is needed to understand the psychological impacts of AI, its accessibility to amateur athletes, and the long-term effects on performance and well-being [18]. This section identifies three priority research directions that would advance the field from experimental prototypes to deployable educational infrastructure.

*   **Longitudinal Studies on Learning Outcomes**: The evidence base for long-term pedagogical effectiveness in sports ITS remains notably thin. While Vincent-Ruz & Boase support that adaptive learning can have a long-term effect on students, and Joshi et al. argue that AI models tackled closer to human thinking will positively impact education improvement, sports-specific longitudinal data is scarce [16]. Most existing studies measure immediate performance improvements or short-term skill acquisition without tracking whether ITS-mediated learning transfers to competitive contexts or persists beyond the intervention period. Future research must employ multi-year cohort designs measuring not only technical skill proficiency under Harrow's Taxonomy but also academic outcomes, athlete retention rates, and psychological well-being indicators. Drawing from STEM ITS literature, researchers should adopt standardized effect size metrics and control for confounding variables like coaching quality and training volume to isolate ITS-specific contributions.
*   **Generalizability Across Sports**: Current sports ITS implementations demonstrate limited transferability between disciplines. Machine learning models trained on soccer movement patterns using CNN architectures may not generalize to gymnastics skill assessment requiring different kinematic features [12][25]. The physical activities allowed for inclusion in psychomotor assessment—movements like stretching, grasping, walking, high jumping, strength, flexibility, complex sports movements—vary substantially across sports, demanding sport-specific model adaptation [6]. Future research should investigate transfer learning approaches where base models trained on large multimodal datasets can be fine-tuned for specific sports with minimal additional data. Comparative studies across at least five distinct sport categories (team sports, individual endurance, skill-based, combat, aesthetic) would establish whether unified ITS architectures are feasible or whether sport-specialized systems remain necessary.
*   **Edge vs. Cloud Computing for Real-Time Feedback**: The architectural choice between edge and cloud computing directly impacts tutoring feedback latency, a core requirement for effective skill correction. Cloud computing excels at storing large amounts of data not needed in real-time, while edge computing enables applications requiring immediate response by deploying network servers closer to users, reducing response time delay [17][13]. For sports ITS providing real-time technique feedback during practice, edge deployment becomes essential—latency exceeding 200ms disrupts the perception-action coupling critical for motor learning. However, edge devices face computational constraints limiting model complexity; Transformer-enhanced fusion models may require cloud offloading for inference while edge handles initial sensor preprocessing. Future research must quantify the latency-accuracy tradeoff across different fusion levels (early fusion at edge vs. late fusion in cloud) and establish deployment guidelines matching sport-specific timing requirements. Distributed computing architectures have enabled near real-time implementation of these methodologies, yet significant research gaps persist in developing fusion methods that balance computational efficiency with pedagogical effectiveness [11].
*   **Multimodal Fusion Architecture Standardization**: Current research employs heterogeneous fusion approaches without consensus on optimal architectures for sports tutoring. CNN-LSTM models demonstrate effectiveness for video-recorded action recognition, while Multimodal Attention-based Transformer-Enhanced Deep Fusion Models leverage wearable sensor data for intelligent assessment [25][36]. Future work should establish benchmark datasets and evaluation protocols enabling direct comparison of fusion strategies across research groups. Standardization would accelerate progress by allowing researchers to isolate architectural improvements from dataset-specific effects.
*   **Pedagogical Theory Integration**: Sports ITS research must deepen engagement with educational theory beyond surface-level references to personalization. Self-Determination Theory has proven effective for student-athlete academic needs, yet its application to psychomotor skill tutoring remains underexplored [20]. Future studies should explicitly map ITS features to SDT components—autonomy support through choice in learning pathways, competence development through calibrated challenge progression, relatedness through human-AI collaboration patterns. Similarly, Harrow's Taxonomy provides structured levels for psychomotor assessment that ITS can operationalize, but validation studies confirming that AI-generated proficiency ratings align with expert human assessments remain limited [6].
*   **Accessibility and Equity**: Current sports ITS deployments concentrate in well-resourced institutions, raising equity concerns. Smartphone-based evaluation systems enable sophisticated analysis by tapping into consumer technology, potentially democratizing access to these capabilities [12]. Future research should investigate low-cost ITS implementations using commodity hardware and open-source models, ensuring that AI-driven tutoring benefits extend beyond elite programs to community sports and developing regions.

In summary, the trajectory of AI-driven Intelligent Tutoring Systems for sports depends on resolving ethical ambiguities around data governance, designing human-AI collaboration patterns that preserve coaching relationships, and conducting rigorous longitudinal research establishing pedagogical effectiveness. The field stands at an inflection point where technical capabilities increasingly outpace ethical frameworks and evidence-based implementation guidelines. Addressing these gaps through coordinated research efforts will determine whether sports ITS fulfills its potential as a transformative educational tool or remains confined to experimental deployments lacking sustainable adoption pathways.
