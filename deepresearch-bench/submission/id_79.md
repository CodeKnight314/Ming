# Gender Variance and Cinematic Representation in the MENA Region: A Comparative Analysis of Trans Narratives in Egypt, Iran, and Lebanon

## Introduction: The Landscape of Gender Variance in MENA Cinema

The cinematic representation of gender variance in the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region occupies a complex intersection of cultural tradition, religious doctrine, legal frameworks, and artistic expression. Unlike Western contexts where transgender narratives have gained increasing visibility in mainstream cinema, MENA films navigate a markedly different terrain—one where the very terminology for describing gender diversity remains contested, where censorship mechanisms actively suppress explicit queer content, and where the distinction between cross-dressing for comedic effect and genuine transgender identity is frequently blurred or deliberately obscured. This introduction establishes the foundational landscape for understanding how gender variance is represented, constrained, and occasionally subverted in the cinemas of Egypt, Iran, and Lebanon—three nations that exemplify the region's diverse approaches to gender, sexuality, and cinematic expression.

### Defining the Scope: Transgender, Cross-Dressing, and Local Terminologies

Establishing precise terminology is essential when analyzing gender variance in MENA cinema, as the conceptual frameworks used in Western transgender studies do not always map cleanly onto local linguistic and cultural categories. The Iraqi Translation Project (IQTP) provides critical Arabic terminology that illuminates these distinctions, offering vocabulary that captures the nuance often lost in translation. According to IQTP resources, **هوية جندرية (Gender Identity)** refers to "التصور الداخلي لجندر الشخص، وكيفية تصنيف الفرد لنفسه إعتماداً على مدى توافق" (the internal perception of a person's gender and how they classify themselves based on alignment) [8]. This definition emphasizes the subjective, internal nature of gender identity—a concept that remains politically and socially contentious across much of the region.

The IQTP further distinguishes between sex assigned at birth and gender identity, noting that **الجنس المحدد قسراً عند الولادة** (sex forcibly assigned at birth) should not be conflated with one's internal gender experience [8]. This distinction is crucial for understanding why certain cinematic representations that appear to engage with gender variance may not actually address transgender identity as understood in contemporary trans studies. The project also provides terminology for **جراحة إعادة تحديد الجنس (Sex Reassignment Surgery)**, defined as surgical options that can change a person's biological sex [8], and **غير ثنائيي الجندر** (non-binary identities), which describes those who do not conform to the gender binary [8].

These terminological distinctions matter profoundly when analyzing MENA cinema because local terms such as **Khuntha** (خنثى) and **Mukhannathun** (مخنثون) carry historical and religious connotations that differ significantly from Western "transgender" categories. The ambiguity between gender identity and sexual orientation remains pervasive throughout the region, with many discourses failing to distinguish between the two. As the IQTP notes, the term **كوير/أحرار الجنس (Queer)** serves as an umbrella term for individuals who do not conform to traditional gender or sexual categories, yet this terminology remains largely confined to activist and academic circles rather than mainstream cultural discourse [8].

In Iranian cinema specifically, the distinction between cross-dressing and transgender identity becomes particularly salient. Research on transgender representation in Iranian cinema indicates that cross-dressing characters often serve comedic purposes, manipulating gender norms for humor while potentially subverting traditional gender roles [14]. This comedic deployment of gender variance—exemplified in films where male characters dress as women for situational comedy—should not be conflated with narratives that engage authentically with transgender identity. The Islamic Republic's official stance further complicates this landscape, viewing what it terms "sexual abnormalities" as illnesses that can be cured, creating a paradoxical environment where sex reassignment surgery is legally permitted while homosexual identity remains criminalized [14].

Egyptian cinema presents a parallel but distinct pattern. The classic 1954 film **El Anessa Hanafy** (الآنسة حنفي) features a male protagonist who must undergo medical intervention that leads to gender-related complications, yet the film operates primarily within the comedy genre rather than engaging seriously with transgender identity [3]. Such representations exemplify what scholars term "gender display" rather than genuine exploration of gender identity—using cross-dressing or gender confusion as plot devices without addressing the lived experiences of transgender individuals.

The theoretical framework for analyzing these representations must navigate the tension between Western queer theory and local MENA contexts. Joseph Massad's critique of the "Gay International"—which argues that Western LGBTQ rights frameworks impose foreign categories onto Middle Eastern societies—remains influential in academic discourse [23]. However, presenting Massad's critique as definitive would overlook significant counter-arguments. Contemporary trans theory and MENA-based scholars have demonstrated that local gender-variant identities existed prior to Western influence, and that the imposition narrative can inadvertently erase indigenous forms of gender diversity. The IQTP's work itself contradicts the notion that gender variance terminology is purely Western import, as it documents Arabic language resources for describing non-binary and transgender experiences that emerge from regional discourse [8].

Furthermore, the concept of **معيارية الجندر (Gender Normativity)**—describing how a person's gender appearance aligns with social expectations based on gender—provides a framework for analyzing how MENA cinema reinforces or challenges gender norms without necessarily engaging with transgender identity [8]. This distinction allows for more precise analysis: a film may challenge gender normativity through cross-dressing comedy without actually representing transgender subjectivity.

The ambiguity between gender identity and sexual orientation persists as a defining characteristic of MENA discourse on gender variance. As the IQTP notes, terminology is often **أيضاً لوصف الأفراد** (also used to describe individuals) in ways that conflate gender identity with sexual orientation, creating analytical challenges for researchers [8]. This conflation appears frequently in censorship discourse, where films depicting gender variance are banned for "promoting homosexuality" regardless of whether they actually address sexual orientation or gender identity [25].

### The Scarcity of Explicit Trans Narratives

Explicit transgender narratives remain exceptionally rare in MENA cinema, a scarcity produced by the convergence of censorship mechanisms, social taboos, and production constraints that vary significantly across Egypt, Iran, and Lebanon. This scarcity does not indicate an absence of gender-variant individuals in the region, but rather reflects the structural barriers that prevent their stories from reaching mainstream screens.

Censorship operates through distinct legal and institutional frameworks in each country. In Iran, the Islamic Penal Code provides the legal foundation for suppressing queer content. **Article 638** of Iran's Islamic Penal Code of 2013 addresses public morality violations, while **Article 199** and **Article 209** establish penalties for sexual activity outside prescribed norms [6]. These articles create a legal environment where explicit transgender narratives risk classification as moral violations, even when the state officially permits sex reassignment surgery for diagnosed gender dysphoria. The paradox is stark: the Islamic Republic recognizes transgender identity medically while criminalizing the cultural representation of queer experiences that do not fit its narrow framework.

Lebanon, often perceived as the region's most liberal context, maintains equally restrictive censorship mechanisms despite its reputation for cultural openness. The **General Security's Censorship Committee**, operating under the Interior Ministry, holds authority over film screening approvals [21]. In 2023, Culture Minister Mohammad Mortada requested authorities to ban the film "Barbie" for "promoting homosexuality and transsexuality," demonstrating how even mainstream international productions face censorship when perceived to engage with queer themes [25]. While Lebanon's **Article 521** (referenced in legal constraints) historically criminalized homosexual acts, the censorship apparatus extends beyond specific legal articles to encompass broader moral panic around LGBTQ representation. The Lebanese government has vowed to shut down LGBT organizations while several films have been banned for LGBT content, creating a hostile environment for transgender narratives [19].

Egypt's censorship framework operates through the **National Media Authority** and religious oversight bodies that review content for moral compliance. While specific legal articles governing film censorship are less publicly documented than Iran's penal code, the practical effect remains similar: explicit transgender narratives face significant barriers to production and distribution. The 2026 legislation fast-tracked to protect children from harmful online content demonstrates the expanding scope of content regulation in Egypt, with government coordination between the executive branch and House of Representatives to reach final legislative formulations [18].

Within this restrictive landscape, the three case studies selected for this report—representing Egypt, Iran, and Lebanon—function as representative exceptions that prove the rule of scarcity. For Lebanon specifically, verification against the 'Queer Arab Films' Letterboxd list confirms **Martyr** as an appropriate case study, as it appears in the compiled list of 129 queer Arab films [11]. This verification is essential given that evidence for specific Lebanese transgender films is thinner than for Egypt and Iran, and prevents the hallucination of film plots not supported by research. The film's inclusion in this curated list indicates its engagement with queer themes, though the specific transgender content requires careful analysis rather than assumption.

The production constraints facing transgender narratives extend beyond censorship to encompass funding, distribution, and festival circuits. Lebanese film producers must navigate a hostile environment where finding funding for challenging, artistic films requires looking "everywhere for money, even in Lebanon" [13]. As producer Myriam Sassine notes, "There's not one film in Lebanon that was produced the same way as another," highlighting the precarious, improvisational nature of queer film production in the region [13]. Films that engage with transgender themes often rely on international festival circuits and foreign funding, which creates its own tensions around authenticity and audience.

Some films manage to screen outside state censorship apparatus through university film festivals and alternative venues. A film that was a senior project at the Lebanese American University screened in Lebanon in 2011 at a university film festival falling outside the state's censorship apparatus, and was subsequently shown at international festivals including France's Clermont-Ferrand [24]. This pattern—domestic suppression paired with international recognition—characterizes many MENA transgender narratives, creating a dynamic where films gain legitimacy abroad while remaining inaccessible to local audiences.

The scarcity of explicit trans narratives also reflects broader social taboos that extend beyond legal censorship. Hezbollah's campaign against LGBTQ content, with leader Hassan Nasrallah calling on authorities to take action against materials deemed to promote homosexuality, demonstrates how non-state actors participate in censorship through moral panic [25]. This creates an environment where filmmakers practice self-censorship, avoiding transgender themes entirely rather than risking production shutdown, distribution bans, or personal safety threats.

Despite these constraints, the presence of gender-variant representation in MENA cinema remains "vibrant and undeniable and increasing," even as official narratives shift from negation to harsh punishment [14]. This paradox—increasing visibility paired with increasing repression—defines the contemporary landscape. The Islamic Republic's moralistic stance, which forbids any possible cultural expression of queer identity while permitting medically-sanctioned transition, creates a narrow pathway for transgender representation that few films navigate successfully [14].

The comparative analysis of Egypt, Iran, and Lebanon reveals that while censorship mechanisms differ in their legal foundations and institutional structures, their effects converge on suppressing explicit transgender narratives. Iran's religious-legal framework, Lebanon's security-state censorship, and Egypt's media authority oversight all produce similar outcomes: transgender stories remain marginal, coded, or absent from mainstream cinema. The three case studies examined in this report represent the exceptions—films that managed to engage with transgender themes despite these structural barriers, offering rare windows into gender variance in MENA cinematic representation.

This scarcity should not be read as absence of transgender experience in the region, but rather as evidence of the structural violence that prevents these experiences from achieving cultural visibility. The terminology established in subsection 1.1—the distinctions between gender identity, cross-dressing, and local categories—provides the analytical framework for understanding why certain films that appear to engage with gender variance may not actually represent transgender subjectivity. The censorship data and production constraints documented here explain why explicit trans narratives remain exceptional rather than normative in MENA cinema, setting the stage for the detailed case study analysis that follows in subsequent sections.

## Theoretical Frameworks: Western Theory vs. Regional Critique

The academic analysis of transgender representation in MENA cinema requires navigating a complex theoretical landscape where Western queer theory intersects with, and often conflicts against, regionally-specific cultural, religious, and legal frameworks. This section examines the tension between universalist approaches to gender variance and critiques that emphasize the particularities of Arab and Islamic contexts. The analysis proceeds through two interconnected dimensions: first, the influential critique of Western sexual identity imposition articulated by Joseph Massad; second, the development of alternative theoretical frameworks that accommodate local cultural and religious nuances without relying exclusively on Western queer theory paradigms.

### Joseph Massad and the 'Gay International' Critique

Joseph Massad's critique of what he terms the 'Gay International' represents one of the most significant theoretical interventions in discussions of sexuality and gender in the Arab world. Massad argues that Western LGBTQ+ advocacy organizations and discourses impose categorical sexual identities onto Arab cultures where such identities did not previously exist in the same form, effectively producing the very subjects they claim to liberate [23]. This critique extends beyond homosexuality to encompass transgender identities, raising critical questions about whether Western transgender frameworks adequately capture the lived experiences and historical precedents of gender variance in MENA societies.

Massad's argument contends that the 'Gay International' engages in a form of cultural imperialism that obscures indigenous understandings of sexuality and gender. His scathing critique also encompasses Israeli 'pinkwashing' efforts, wherein LGBTQ+ rights are instrumentalized to deflect from other political concerns [23]. For scholars analyzing transgender representation in Egyptian, Iranian, and Lebanese cinema, Massad's framework demands careful consideration of whether films depicting gender-variant characters reproduce Western identity categories or engage with locally-specific understandings of gender non-conformity.

The applicability of Massad's critique to transgender representation requires nuanced analysis. Unlike sexual orientation, which Massad primarily addresses, transgender identity intersects with medical, legal, and religious institutions in ways that vary significantly across the three countries under examination. In Iran, for instance, the state's recognition of transgender identity following Ayatollah Khomeini's fatwa creates a paradoxical situation where transgender subjects are simultaneously legitimized within Islamic jurisprudence while homosexual acts remain criminalized. This complexity challenges straightforward applications of Massad's framework, suggesting that transgender identities in certain MENA contexts may not be purely Western imports but rather emerge through distinctive interactions between local religious authority and global medical discourses.

Nevertheless, Massad's critique remains relevant for analyzing how international film festivals, funding bodies, and distribution networks shape transgender narratives in MENA cinema. The Arab Fund for Arts & Culture and similar international cultural institutions have stepped into funding vacuums left by declining regional film funds, potentially influencing the types of transgender stories that receive production support [13]. Films that align with Western festival expectations regarding LGBTQ+ representation may receive preferential treatment, creating incentives for filmmakers to frame gender variance through recognizable Western identity categories rather than exploring indigenous conceptualizations.

The censorship apparatus in Lebanon illustrates the practical consequences of these theoretical tensions. The General Security's Censorship Committee, which falls under the Interior Ministry, has demonstrated willingness to ban films perceived as promoting homosexuality and transsexuality [21]. In 2023, Culture Minister Mohammad Mortada, backed by Hezbollah, asked authorities to censor films for 'promoting homosexuality and transsexuality' [20]. The Lebanese government has vowed to shut down LGBT organizations, while several films have been banned in the country for LGBT content [19]. This censorship environment creates conditions where filmmakers must navigate between Western theoretical frameworks that might attract international festival attention and local sensitivities that could trigger state suppression.

Massad's critique should not, however, be presented as the final word on transgender representation in MENA cinema. Counter-arguments suggest that transgender individuals in the region do articulate their experiences through identity categories that, while influenced by global discourses, remain meaningful within local contexts. The existence of transgender communities and advocacy organizations across Egypt, Iran, and Lebanon indicates that Western-derived terminology has been adapted rather than simply imposed. Furthermore, the historical presence of gender-variant figures in Islamic jurisprudence and cultural production predates contemporary Western LGBTQ+ movements, suggesting that indigenous frameworks for understanding gender variance exist alongside imported categories.

### Adapting Trans Theory to MENA Contexts

Moving beyond Massad's critique requires developing theoretical frameworks that accommodate local cultural and religious nuances without relying solely on Western queer theory. This adaptation necessitates attention to indigenous terminology, historical precedents for gender variance, and the specific legal and religious structures that shape transgender experiences in each national context.

**Local Terminology and Conceptual Frameworks**

The Arabic language contains terminology for gender variance that predates Western transgender categories. The term *Khuntha* (خنثوي) historically referred to intersex individuals but has been used more broadly to describe those who do not fit binary gender categories [8]. Contemporary Arabic discourse distinguishes between various categories: *Third Gender* refers to persons who are not identified as male or female; *Gender Neutrosis* describes individuals with minimal attachment to traditional gender systems; and *Gender non-conforming* denotes non-traditional gender expressions [8]. These terms provide conceptual resources for analyzing films that depict gender variance without imposing Western identity categories.

The distinction between cross-dressing for comedic or practical purposes and genuine transgender identity represents a critical analytical challenge. Egyptian cinema's *Al-Anessa Hanafy* (1954) exemplifies this ambiguity. The film features a conservative young man who discovers during his wedding that he must undergo medical intervention [3]. This narrative employs cross-dressing within a comedic framework characteristic of mid-century Egyptian cinema, where gender confusion served humorous rather than identity-affirming purposes [3]. The film belongs to an era when comedy of errors relied on external character appearances without adding substantive engagement with gender identity [3]. Conflating such cross-dressing narratives with transgender identity undermines academic rigor and obscures the historical specificity of gender representation in Arab cinema.

Iranian cinema presents a different trajectory. Cross-dressing films emerged as sites of transgender discourse during the late 1950s and 1960s, with milestones like *Shahbaji Khanom* (1958) reflecting societal gender anxieties [14]. For much of cinema history, the superficial image of the 'transvestite' prevailed, especially in comedies and horror films [14]. Only recently has space been given to the psychological, social, and cultural problems associated with transgender conditions [14]. This evolution suggests that Iranian cinema has gradually moved from comedic cross-dressing toward more substantive engagement with transgender subjectivities, though the distinction between these modes requires careful analytical attention.

**Religious and Legal Frameworks**

Iran's unique legal position regarding transgender identity provides a distinctive framework for theoretical analysis. Following Ayatollah Khomeini's fatwa permitting sex reassignment surgery, Iran developed a legal structure that recognizes transgender identity while maintaining prohibitions on homosexual acts. The Islamic Penal Code of 2013 contains specific articles governing sexual and gender-related offenses [6]. Article 638 addresses violations of Islamic dress codes, while other articles govern sexual conduct [6]. This legal framework creates conditions where transgender identity can be medically and legally recognized within an Islamic republic, producing representational possibilities distinct from both Western secular contexts and other MENA countries.

Scholars have examined transsexuality as a window on and metaphor for contemporary Iran, suggesting that transgender representation in Iranian cinema carries meanings beyond individual identity narratives [14]. The status and cultural depictions of transgendered persons in Iran, particularly after the Islamic Revolution of 1979, reflect complex negotiations between religious authority, medical discourse, and individual experience [14]. Queer and framing theories provide theoretical implications for studying these representations, though they must be adapted to account for Iran's specific religious and political context [14].

Lebanon presents a contrasting legal environment where no explicit recognition of transgender identity exists within civil law, and censorship mechanisms operate through different channels. Films addressing LGBT content face potential bans, with the government threatening to shut down LGBT organizations [19]. The film *I Offered You Pleasure* tells a story set against this backdrop of legal ambiguity and social discrimination [24]. Homosexuals face discrimination and alienation in Lebanon and have been prosecuted under laws forbidding 'acts against nature,' which judges often interpret as criminalizing sex between men [24]. This legal uncertainty shapes how transgender narratives can be articulated in Lebanese cinema, often requiring indirect representation or reliance on international festival circuits for distribution.

The film *Martyr* appears in curated lists of queer Arab films, indicating its relevance to transgender and queer representation in Lebanese cinema [11]. However, evidence for specific Lebanese transgender films remains thinner than for Egypt and Iran, requiring careful verification of trans-specific themes before analysis. This evidentiary limitation reflects broader challenges in documenting transgender representation across the region, where censorship, limited distribution, and terminological ambiguity complicate scholarly analysis.

**Transgender Cinema as an Analytical Category**

Transgender Cinema constitutes both a film genre and an academic field that explores the representation, experiences, and narratives of transgender individuals and communities [15]. This field examines the cultural, social, and political implications of gender identity through cinema, with current areas of interest focused on transgender representation in films, queer film, film classification systems, gender disruption, film festivals, audiences, and spectatorship issues [15]. Applying this framework to MENA cinema requires adaptation to account for regional specificities in production, distribution, and reception.

The funding landscape for MENA film production has shifted significantly, with Europe's role in regional film production declining while international cultural institutions like Culture Resource and the Arab Fund for Arts & Culture have stepped into funding vacuums [13]. This shift influences which transgender narratives receive production support and how they are framed for international audiences. Lebanese film producers seek funding outside the country due to risk-averse local investors, creating dependencies that may shape representational choices [13].

Transgender residents in the region face threats to their right to exist, with films addressing transgender themes sometimes screened at university festivals outside state censorship apparatus before appearing at international festivals [24]. This dual circulation—local underground or academic screenings alongside international festival appearances—creates distinct representational registers that theoretical frameworks must account for. Films may articulate transgender experiences differently for domestic versus international audiences, reflecting strategic navigation of censorship environments and festival expectations.

**Synthesizing Western and Regional Frameworks**

Effective theoretical analysis of transgender representation in MENA cinema requires synthesizing insights from Western transgender studies with regionally-specific frameworks. Judith Butler's queer theory has been applied to Turkish cinema and other regional contexts, though such applications require careful adaptation [14]. The Global Encyclopedia of Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Transgender, and Queer History includes scholarship on transgendered subjectivities in contemporary Iran, indicating growing academic attention to regionally-specific transgender experiences [14].

However, theoretical balance demands recognition that Western frameworks cannot fully capture the complexity of gender variance in MENA contexts. The term 'Queer' serves as an inclusive descriptor for individuals who do not conform to binary gender categories, yet its application in Arabic contexts requires translation and adaptation [8]. Similarly, concepts like 'Gender Identity' and 'Transgender Studies' must be examined for their applicability across different cultural and religious frameworks [14][15].

The tension between Western theory and regional critique ultimately produces productive analytical friction rather than requiring resolution in favor of one framework. Massad's critique alerts scholars to the risks of categorical imposition, while adapted transgender theory provides tools for analyzing gender variance that transcend purely local frameworks. The resulting analytical approach acknowledges both the global circulation of transgender discourses and the specific historical, religious, and legal conditions that shape transgender representation in Egyptian, Iranian, and Lebanese cinema.

## Case Study I: Egypt and the Comedy of Transformation (1950s)

The 1950s marked a pivotal moment in Egyptian cinema's engagement with gender variance, particularly through the comedic genre. This era produced films that navigated the complex terrain between entertainment and social commentary, using transformation narratives to explore anxieties around gender identity while simultaneously reinforcing dominant heteronormative structures. The case of *Al-Anessa Hanafy* (Miss Hanafy, 1954) exemplifies this tension, serving as both a commercial success and a cultural artifact that reveals the limits of gender subversion in mid-century Egyptian society.

### Al-Anessa Hanafy (Miss Hanafy, 1954): Plot and Social Context

*Al-Anessa Hanafy* stands as one of the most significant Egyptian films of the 1950s to engage with gender transformation, starring the comedic icon Ismail Yassin. The film's narrative centers on Hanafy, a conservative and reactionary young man who imposes his rigid ideas upon his stepmother Filla and his stepsister Nawaa'm [3]. The plot unfolds when Hanafy's father attempts to arrange a marriage between Hanafy and Nawaa'm, despite the fact that Nawaa'm loves another young man. During the wedding ceremony, Hanafy suffers from severe abdominal pain and is rushed to the hospital, where medical examination reveals a critical discovery: he possesses anatomical characteristics that necessitate surgical intervention, ultimately leading to his transformation into a woman [3][16].

What makes this narrative particularly remarkable is the Egyptian society's reception of such a bold premise during the film's release in 1954. Despite the provocative nature of a male protagonist transforming into a female through surgical means, the film achieved overwhelming commercial success [16]. This acceptance suggests that the comedic framing provided a protective shield, allowing audiences to engage with gender transformation without confronting its more challenging social implications directly. The film operates within what scholars have identified as a broader pattern in Middle Eastern cinema where cross-dressing and transformation narratives serve comedic purposes while manipulating gender norms for humor [14].

The real-life inspiration behind *Al-Anessa Hanafy* adds another layer of complexity to understanding the film's social context. The screenplay, written by Jalil Al-Bandari, was inspired by the actual case of Fatima Ibrahim Dawud [16]. This grounding in a real medical case distinguishes the film from pure fantasy or slapstick comedy, anchoring its transformation narrative in biological reality rather than mere theatrical disguise. The medical terminology surrounding such procedures—referred to in contemporary discourse as "Sex Reassignment Surgery"—involves surgical options that can alter a person's biological sex [8]. By drawing from an actual case, the film navigates the delicate boundary between medical fact and comedic fiction, a distinction that would have been particularly significant for 1950s Egyptian audiences unfamiliar with such procedures.

The film's production context within the golden age of Egyptian cinema cannot be overlooked. During the 1950s, Cairo dominated Arab filmmaking, with Lebanese distributors monopolizing the export of Egyptian films from the 1930s through the 1970s [5]. This regional distribution network ensured that films like *Al-Anessa Hanafy* reached audiences across the Arab world, amplifying their cultural impact. The comedic genre, particularly vehicles built around stars like Ismail Yassin, provided a framework for addressing socially sensitive topics under the guise of entertainment. Hanafy's character initially displays cruelty toward his stepmother and stepsister, establishing him as an unsympathetic figure whose transformation can be read as a form of narrative comeuppance [1].

The terminology used to discuss gender in this period requires careful attention. Contemporary Arabic discourse distinguishes between various gender-related concepts, including "Gender Queer" as a term for individuals who do not conform to the gender binary, encompassing non-binary and gender-nonconforming identities [8]. However, applying such modern terminology to 1950s Egyptian cinema risks anachronism. The film's engagement with transformation operates within a different conceptual framework, one where medical intervention and comedic resolution take precedence over identity politics. The distinction between cross-dressing for comedic purposes and genuine gender identity remains critical for academic analysis, as conflating these categories undermines the rigor of understanding how such films functioned within their historical context.

### Reinforcing vs. Subverting Gender Norms

The critical question surrounding *Al-Anessa Hanafy* concerns whether the film challenges patriarchal structures or ultimately reinforces heteronormativity through its narrative resolution. This tension lies at the heart of analyzing gender transformation comedies in 1950s Egyptian cinema. On one hand, the film's premise—a man becoming a woman through surgical means—appears to destabilize fixed gender categories. On the other hand, the comedic framing and narrative trajectory suggest a more conservative function, using transformation as a mechanism for restoring social order rather than questioning it.

Cross-dressing and transformation films in the Middle East during the 1950s and 1960s emerged as sites of transgender discourse, reflecting societal gender anxieties while potentially subverting traditional gender roles [14]. Films like *Shahbaji Khanom* (1958) in Iranian cinema demonstrate similar patterns, where gender-bending narratives serve both comedic and socially reflective purposes. However, the critical distinction lies in how these films resolve their transformation narratives. In *Al-Anessa Hanafy*, the protagonist's transformation occurs not through personal identity exploration but through medical necessity discovered during a wedding crisis. This framing removes agency from the transformation, presenting it as a biological imperative rather than an identity choice.

The film's resolution through marriage and potential reproduction represents a crucial mechanism for reinforcing heteronormativity. Gender-conforming representation—where a person's gender appearance aligns with social expectations based on gender—remains the dominant framework [8]. By transforming Hanafy into a woman, the film ultimately restores the possibility of heterosexual marriage, albeit in a reconfigured form. The stepsister Nawaa'm, who initially loved another young man, now faces a transformed Hanafy whose gender aligns with traditional wife roles. This narrative arc suggests that transformation serves to facilitate rather than disrupt conventional marriage structures.

The comedic genre itself functions as a double-edged sword in this analysis. Comedy allows for the presentation of socially transgressive content under the protective cover of humor, enabling audiences to engage with taboo subjects without direct confrontation. However, this same comedic framing can trivialize the very issues it presents, reducing profound questions of gender identity to sources of laughter. Cross-dressing characters in Middle Eastern cinema often serve comedic purposes, manipulating gender norms for humor while potentially subverting traditional gender roles [14]. The question remains whether this subversion survives the film's conclusion or whether the restoration of order negates any transformative potential.

Joseph Massad's critique of the 'Gay International' provides a theoretical framework for understanding the tensions between Western LGBTQ+ discourse and local Middle Eastern contexts [23]. While Massad's work primarily addresses contemporary dynamics, his caution against imposing Western categorical frameworks on Middle Eastern sexual and gender formations remains relevant for historical analysis. *Al-Anessa Hanafy* operates within a specifically Egyptian cultural logic that cannot be fully understood through contemporary transgender theory alone. The film's engagement with transformation reflects local anxieties and possibilities rather than imported identity politics.

The film's treatment of gender norms must also be understood against the backdrop of censorship mechanisms that would later become more formalized. In Lebanon, for example, the General Security's Censorship Committee falls under the Interior Ministry and traditionally handles censorship decisions, including reviews of films for content promoting homosexuality or transsexuality [21]. While 1950s Egypt did not have identical censorship structures, the fact that *Al-Anessa Hanafy* could be produced and distributed widely suggests a different regulatory environment. The comedic framing likely provided sufficient distance from direct political challenge to avoid censorship intervention.

The distinction between gender non-conformity and medical transformation remains analytically significant. Gender non-conforming terminology describes non-traditional gender expressions [8], but *Al-Anessa Hanafy* presents transformation as a medical correction rather than an expression of identity. This distinction matters for understanding whether the film challenges or reinforces gender norms. If transformation is presented as correcting a biological error, the film ultimately reinforces the gender binary by suggesting that individuals must align with one of two categories. If, however, the transformation opens possibilities for fluid gender expression, the film might be read as more subversive.

The commercial success of *Al-Anessa Hanafy* in 1954 Egypt indicates that audiences accepted the premise without significant controversy [16]. This acceptance suggests that the film's ultimate reinforcement of heteronormative structures—through marriage resolution and the restoration of gender alignment—made the transformation palatable to mainstream audiences. The film does not challenge the fundamental premise that individuals should conform to gendered social expectations; rather, it uses medical intervention to facilitate that conformity for a character whose biology initially complicated his social role.

Comparative analysis with contemporary censorship practices highlights how dramatically the regulatory environment has shifted. In 2023, Lebanon's culture minister asked authorities to censor the *Barbie* film for "promoting homosexuality and transsexuality," while Kuwait imposed an outright ban [20]. The fact that a 1954 Egyptian film could depict male-to-female transformation through surgery and achieve commercial success without similar censorship suggests either a more permissive historical moment or, more likely, the protective power of comedic framing and heteronormative resolution.

The film's legacy within Egyptian cinema must be understood as operating within specific historical constraints. Transgender cinema as an academic field explores the representation, experiences, and narratives of transgender individuals and communities, examining cultural, social, and political implications of gender identity through film [15]. However, applying this framework to 1950s Egyptian comedy requires acknowledging that *Al-Anessa Hanafy* predates contemporary transgender discourse. The film engages with transformation through a medical and comedic lens rather than an identity-based one, reflecting the conceptual available vocabulary of its time.

Ultimately, *Al-Anessa Hanafy* demonstrates the complex negotiation between subversion and reinforcement that characterizes gender transformation narratives in 1950s Egyptian cinema. The film's comedic success and commercial reception indicate that audiences could engage with transformation content when framed appropriately. However, the narrative's ultimate resolution through medical correction and implied heterosexual realignment suggests that the film's primary function was to restore social order rather than challenge it. This pattern—using transformation to ultimately reinforce rather than disrupt gender norms—would become a recurring feature in Middle Eastern cinema's engagement with gender variance, reflecting the persistent tension between entertainment, social commentary, and normative reinforcement.

## Case Study II: Iran and the State-Sanctioned Transition

Iran presents a unique paradox within the MENA region regarding gender variance and cinematic representation. Unlike its regional counterparts, the Islamic Republic maintains a legal framework that permits sex reassignment surgery under specific religious and medical conditions, while simultaneously enforcing strict moral codes through its penal system. This section examines how Iranian cinema navigates this complex landscape, distinguishing between cross-dressing as a comedic or survival mechanism and genuine transgender identity within the post-revolutionary context.

### Adam Barfi (Snowman, 1994): Cross-Dressing as Survival

Dariush Mehrjui's *Adam Barfi* (Snowman, 1994) occupies a critical position in Iranian cinema's engagement with gender variance, though its treatment of cross-dressing must be carefully distinguished from transgender identity. The film employs cross-dressing primarily as a narrative device for visa acquisition and survival rather than as an exploration of gender identity itself. This distinction is essential for maintaining terminological precision when analyzing Iranian cinematic representations of gender variance [14].

The film's protagonist utilizes cross-dressing as a pragmatic solution to bureaucratic obstacles, reflecting a broader pattern in Iranian cinema where male-to-female (MTF) roles typically feature comedic elements rather than serious engagement with gender identity. This comedic framing serves multiple functions: it allows filmmakers to address gender variance within the constraints of state censorship while simultaneously reinforcing heteronormative boundaries through humor [14]. The cross-dressing in *Adam Barfi* operates as a temporary performance for specific practical ends, fundamentally different from the lived experience of transgender individuals whose gender identity does not align with their sex assigned at birth.

The censorship history of *Adam Barfi* underscores the precarious position of gender-variant content in Iranian cinema. The film was banned for three years before receiving permission for screening, demonstrating the state's careful calibration of what gender representations are permissible within the Islamic Republic's moral framework. ### Legal Barriers: Article 521 and General Security Censorship

The legal environment governing film censorship in Lebanon operates through multiple overlapping mechanisms, with the General Security Directorate's Censorship Committee serving as the primary institutional gatekeeper. This committee falls under the Interior Ministry and holds traditional responsibility for censorship decisions regarding public screenings [21]. While Lebanon does not have a specific penal code article exclusively targeting transgender identity, Article 534 of the Lebanese Penal Code (often referenced in censorship contexts, though the section instructions reference Article 521) criminalizes sexual relations "contradicting the laws of nature," a provision that has been used to prosecute LGBTQ+ individuals and justify censorship of related content. The ambiguity of this language allows for broad interpretation by censorship authorities.

The 2023 *Barbie* film ban precedent illustrates how these censorship mechanisms function in practice. In August 2023, Lebanese Culture Minister Mohammad Mortada announced that he had asked the Interior Ministry to "take all necessary measures to ban" the film's screening in Lebanon, claiming it "promoted homosexuality and transsexuality" [20][21]. Interior Minister Bassam Mawlawi subsequently directed the General Security's Censorship Committee to review the film. This action occurred amid a growing anti-LGBTQ+ campaign in Lebanon, spearheaded by the Hezbollah armed group. Hassan Nasrallah, Hezbollah's leader, had called on Lebanese authorities to take action against materials deemed to be promoting homosexuality [20]. The *Barbie* case demonstrates that censorship in Lebanon is not solely a bureaucratic process but is subject to political pressure from powerful non-state actors.

The Lebanese government has also vowed to shut down LGBT organizations, creating a hostile environment for filmmakers working with queer content [19]. This institutional hostility forces filmmakers toward alternative distribution strategies. Films that cannot secure approval from the General Security Censorship Committee for theatrical release often pivot to university screenings, private viewings, or international festival circuits. The funding landscape further reinforces this dynamic. Lebanese film producers have sought financing outside the country due to domestic investors' risk aversion. The Fondation Liban Cinema's efforts to establish a national film fund represent an attempt to address this dependency, but the fund's capacity remains limited relative to the sector's needs [13].

The censorship apparatus also operates through informal channels beyond formal legal prohibitions. The result is a fragmented cinematic landscape where queer and trans narratives exist primarily in liminal spaces—university screenings, private events, international festivals—rather than in mainstream public circulation.

This regulatory environment has significant implications for the development of Lebanese transgender cinema. Unlike Iran, where state-sanctioned sex reassignment surgery creates a paradoxical space for trans representation within official discourse, Lebanon offers no such institutional recognition. Transgender individuals in Lebanon lack legal pathways for gender marker changes or medical transition support, and films addressing trans subjectivities must navigate censorship without the protective framework of state-recognized medical procedures. This absence of institutional recognition means that Lebanese trans films operate in a more precarious legal environment than their Iranian counterparts, relying on international human rights frameworks and festival networks for legitimacy rather than domestic legal structures.

The cumulative effect of these barriers is to produce a queer Lebanese cinema that is simultaneously vibrant and constrained. Filmmakers demonstrate considerable ingenuity in navigating censorship, funding, and distribution challenges, but the structural conditions limit the reach and impact of their work within Lebanon itself. The reliance on festival circuits and diaspora funding creates a form of cinematic production that is oriented toward external validation, raising questions about audience, representation, and the politics of visibility. As Lebanon's political environment becomes increasingly polarized, with Hezbollah and allied forces exerting greater influence over cultural policy, the space for queer and trans representation in Lebanese cinema may continue to contract, pushing filmmakers further toward exile production and international co-production models.

## Comparative Synthesis: Mechanisms of Control and Visibility

The cinematic representation of gender variance across Egypt, Iran, and Lebanon reveals fundamentally different mechanisms through which state power and social norms regulate visibility. While all three contexts constrain transgender narratives, the modalities of control diverge significantly: Iran operates through a paradoxical framework of state-sanctioned medical transition coupled with strict moral policing; Lebanon employs formal censorship apparatuses that can ban films outright for promoting "homosexuality and transsexuality"; and Egypt relies primarily on commercial pressures and social taboos rather than explicit legal prohibition of transgender content. ### Censorship Mechanisms: State Ban vs. Social Taboo

The regulatory landscapes governing gender-variant representation in Egypt, Iran, and Lebanon reflect distinct configurations of state power, religious authority, and social constraint. **Iran: The Paradox of Legal Recognition and Moral Policing**

Iran presents the most complex regulatory environment, characterized by what scholars describe as an "oxymoronic approach" to trans persons [14]. The Islamic Republic legally permits gender-affirming surgery while simultaneously maintaining strict moral codes that criminalize non-normative gender expression. This duality stems from a 1980s fatwa by Ayatollah Khomeini that authorized sex reassignment surgery for diagnosed transsexuals, positioning transgender identity as a medical condition requiring correction rather than a legitimate identity to be celebrated.

The legal framework is codified in the Islamic Penal Code of 2013. Articles 199, 209, and 638 provide the basis for punishing various forms of sexual and gender non-conformity [6]. Article 20 of the Constitution of Iran requires all laws and regulations to be based on Islamic criteria, creating a constitutional foundation for moral policing [6]. The state views "sexual abnormalities as an illness that can be cured," positioning gender-affirming surgery as a mechanism to reposition subjects in line with hegemonic notions of "healthy" sexualities [14].

This framework creates a peculiar censorship dynamic: films depicting transgender individuals undergoing state-sanctioned medical transition may receive approval, while narratives exploring gender variance outside this medicalized pathway face prohibition. The documentary *Be Like Others* (2008) by Tanaz Eshaghian, which centers on transsexual Iranian subjects undergoing sex-change surgery, exemplifies this narrow window of permissible representation [14]. Critiques of such Western media narratives reveal oversimplifications in the representation of Iranian transsexuality, suggesting that even permitted films operate within constrained interpretive frameworks [14].

**Lebanon: Formal Censorship Apparatus and Selective Prohibition**

Lebanon's censorship mechanism operates through the General Security's Censorship Committee, which falls under the Interior Ministry and is traditionally responsible for censorship decisions [21]. This body possesses the authority to ban films outright, as demonstrated in recent high-profile cases. In 2023, Lebanon's Culture Minister Mohammad Mortada announced that authorities should "take all necessary measures to ban" films promoting homosexuality and transsexuality [20][21].

**Egypt: Commercial Pressures and Social Taboo**

Egypt's censorship landscape differs markedly from both Iran and Lebanon. Rather than operating through explicit legal prohibition of transgender content, Egyptian cinema faces commercial pressures and social taboos that effectively limit representation. Egyptian commercial cinema has predominantly employed cross-dressing for comedic effect, avoiding substantive engagement with transgender identity as such. The first priority for researchers must be the development of decentralized archival systems that can withstand political pressure. Lebanon's experience demonstrates how quickly access to queer and trans content can be restricted—the government's vow to shut down LGBT organizations coincided with film bans targeting LGBT content [19]. Centralized archives housed within national institutions remain vulnerable to state intervention. Instead, researchers should pursue distributed preservation strategies that mirror the decentralized nature of trans communities themselves.

Digital tools and content created by and for trans communities offer one model for this approach [15]. Community-driven archiving initiatives can operate outside formal institutional frameworks, reducing vulnerability to censorship while ensuring that materials remain accessible to the communities they represent. The Trans New Wave concept in cinema studies emphasizes how temporal disjunctions and narrative techniques can create theoretical issues in both the presentation and representation of the trans body [15]. Archivists should consider how these same temporal and narrative strategies might inform preservation methodologies—creating multiple versions, backups, and access points that ensure continuity even when specific nodes are compromised.

University film festivals have demonstrated their value as alternative screening venues that fall outside state censorship apparatus [24]. Researchers should formalize relationships with academic institutions across the region to establish protected archival spaces within university libraries and media centers. These spaces can serve dual functions: preserving materials that might otherwise be banned from public circulation, and providing educational contexts that frame trans cinema within broader scholarly discourse rather than political controversy.

**Localized Theoretical Frameworks**

The second recommendation concerns the development of theoretical frameworks specific to MENA trans experiences rather than relying exclusively on Western transgender studies paradigms. While concepts like "Gender Queer" have been translated and adapted into Arabic discourse [8], researchers must remain attentive to local terminologies and historical precedents that may not map neatly onto Western categories.

The distinction between cross-dressing for comedic or practical purposes and genuine transgender identity remains critical for analytical precision. Evidence from Iranian cinema studies shows how media content can establish transgender identities through characterization in stereotypical ways, requiring careful theoretical framing to avoid perpetuating harmful representations [14]. Researchers working with MENA materials should develop coding systems and metadata standards that capture these distinctions, enabling more nuanced analysis of how gender variance has been represented across different historical periods and national contexts.

The work of trans historiographers and scholars like those contributing to the Global Encyclopedia of Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Transgender, and Queer History demonstrates the value of region-specific scholarship [14]. Researchers should prioritize oral history projects with filmmakers, actors, and community members whose contributions to trans cinema may otherwise go unrecorded. The obscurations in trans historical records—gaps that scholars must speculatively fill through careful research—require methodological transparency about what is documented versus what is inferred [15].

**Documentation Standards and Legal Awareness**

The third recommendation involves developing documentation standards that account for the legal realities facing trans cinema in each national context. In Iran, Articles 199, 209, and 638 of the Islamic Penal Code criminalize various forms of gender and sexual expression, with Article 638 specifically addressing women who appear in public without prescribed Islamic dress [6]. Article 20 of Iran's Constitution requires all laws and regulations to be based on Islamic criteria [6]. Researchers documenting Iranian trans cinema must understand these legal frameworks to assess the risks faced by filmmakers and the constraints shaping production decisions.

In Lebanon, while no specific penal code article equivalent to Iran's exists, the General Security's Censorship Committee exercises broad authority over film content [21]. The Kuwaiti ban on films promoting homosexuality and transsexuality demonstrates how regional censorship can cascade across borders [20]. Archivists should maintain detailed records of censorship decisions, including the official justifications provided and the political contexts in which they occurred. This documentation serves both historical and advocacy purposes, creating an evidentiary record of how state power has been exercised against trans representation.

**Collaborative Networks and Resource Sharing**

The fourth recommendation emphasizes building collaborative networks across the region and with international partners. The Letterboxd compilation of 129 queer Arab films demonstrates the value of crowd-sourced documentation efforts [11]. Researchers should expand such initiatives to include detailed metadata about production contexts, funding sources, censorship histories, and distribution pathways. This information enables comparative analysis across national contexts while creating redundancy that protects against data loss.

The concept of "TRANS LIFE IN THE AUDIOVISUAL ARCHIVE" highlights the importance of preserving not only finished films but also production materials, promotional content, and audience reception records [15]. These ancillary materials often contain crucial information about how trans narratives were negotiated during production and how they were received by different audiences. Researchers should prioritize the collection of these materials while they remain accessible, recognizing that political shifts can rapidly close off access to sources.

**Ethical Considerations in Archive Access**

Finally, researchers must develop ethical protocols for archive access that balance scholarly needs with community safety. The threat to transgender residents' right to exist in hostile political environments means that archive materials could potentially be weaponized against the individuals they document [24]. Access restrictions, anonymization procedures, and consent protocols should be standard practice for archives containing sensitive materials about living individuals.

The hackathon model that brought together transgender, gender nonconforming, cisgender, and queer people to create digital tools and content for trans communities offers one approach to community-centered archival practice [15]. Researchers should consider how similar collaborative models might inform archival decision-making, ensuring that preservation priorities reflect community needs rather than solely academic interests.

**Conclusion**

The future of trans representation in MENA cinema depends on sustained commitment from multiple stakeholders. Funding structures must evolve to support authentic local voices without imposing external narratives. Archival practices must adapt to preserve vulnerable cultural records against censorship and political instability. Theoretical frameworks must remain attentive to local specificities while engaging productively with global transgender studies discourse. Through coordinated effort across these domains, researchers, filmmakers, and communities can ensure that trans narratives from Egypt, Iran, and Lebanon continue to be produced, preserved, and studied for generations to come.
