# Clinical Viability of Metal Ion Modulation in Cardiovascular Disease: Chelation vs. Supplementation

## Executive Synthesis: Clinical Viability Verdict

The clinical viability of metal ion modulation in cardiovascular disease represents a complex intersection of toxicology, nutrition, and evidence-based medicine. This executive synthesis evaluates the current state of evidence across three intervention classes: chelation therapy, essential metal supplementation, and toxic metal reduction. The verdict rests on rigorous assessment of randomized controlled trials, systematic reviews, and guideline recommendations, with particular attention to the distinction between primary prevention in the general population and secondary prevention in post-myocardial infarction or heart failure patients.

### Summary of Evidence Strength by Intervention Class

The evidence base for metal ion modulation in cardiovascular disease varies substantially by intervention class, ranging from Level A evidence for specific intravenous iron formulations in heart failure to Level C or insufficient evidence for chelation therapy in routine cardiovascular management.

**Chelation Therapy (EDTA-Based)**

The evidence grade for EDTA-based chelation therapy in cardiovascular disease has undergone significant revision following the TACT2 trial results. The original Trial to Assess Chelation Therapy (TACT) suggested a reduction in cardiovascular disease events with edetate disodium, particularly in patients with diabetes mellitus and prior myocardial infarction [36][22]. However, TACT2, designed specifically to replicate these findings, did not reproduce the positive results of the previous trial [33]. Despite effectively reducing blood lead levels, EDTA chelation was not effective in reducing cardiovascular events in stable patients with coronary artery disease who have diabetes and a history of myocardial infarction [21].

Current systematic reviews conclude that there is no high-quality evidence to support any benefit from EDTA chelation therapy for cardiovascular disease [32]. The evidence grade has effectively shifted from tentative Level B (based on TACT alone) to Level C or insufficient for routine clinical use, with recommendations now limited to clinical trial settings or documented heavy metal exposure cases.

**Essential Metal Supplementation**

The evidence for essential metal supplementation demonstrates considerable heterogeneity across different minerals and clinical contexts:

*Intravenous Iron in Heart Failure:* The evidence grade for intravenous iron supplementation in heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) and iron deficiency approaches Level A for specific outcomes. Multiple trials including IRONMAN have evaluated intravenous iron therapy, with pooled results showing reduction in heart failure hospitalization risk, though effects on all-cause mortality remain inconsistent [2][6]. Intravenous repletion of iron in heart failure patients reduces the risk of heart failure hospitalization, with no significant effect on all-cause mortality [29]. This represents the strongest evidence base among metal-modulating interventions for secondary prevention in established heart failure.

*Magnesium Supplementation:* Evidence for magnesium supplementation remains at Level B to C, with ongoing clinical trials evaluating impact on exercise tolerance, quality of life, and clinical outcomes in chronic heart failure patients [27]. Patients with chronic heart failure are frequently magnesium deficient, and observational data from Veterans Affairs populations suggest over-the-counter magnesium supplements could be associated with improved outcomes, though randomized evidence remains limited [35]. Magnesium plays a critical role in preventing arrhythmia, improving hemodynamics, and supporting cardiovascular function through numerous key enzymatic processes [30].

*Zinc and Selenium Supplementation:* For primary prevention of cardiovascular disease, evidence for zinc and selenium supplementation remains at Level C or insufficient. Cochrane reviews indicate no high-quality evidence supporting selenium supplementation for primary prevention of cardiovascular disease [4]. Zinc supplementation shows some association with cardiovascular disease risk factors in GRADE-assessed systematic reviews, but primary analyses from multiple studies reported no significant association between zinc intake and cardiovascular disease events when adjusted for multiple variables [5][9].

**Toxic Metal Reduction**

The evidence for toxic metal reduction as a cardiovascular intervention strategy remains at Level C, with mechanistic plausibility but limited clinical trial confirmation. Epidemiological data show accumulation of biologically active metals, such as lead and cadmium, associates with future cardiovascular disease risk [8]. The American Heart Association has issued scientific statements on contaminant metals as cardiovascular risk factors, acknowledging the association while noting insufficient evidence for intervention trials [36]. The TACT2 trial specifically assessed whether removal of toxic metal stores from the body may be linked with reduced cardiovascular disease risk, but results did not support this mechanistic hypothesis in the studied population [33].

### Primary Recommendations for Clinical Practice

Based on the current evidence synthesis, clinical recommendations for metal-modulating interventions must distinguish clearly between interventions ready for routine care versus those restricted to clinical trials or specific high-risk subgroups.

**Interventions Ready for Routine Clinical Care**

*Intravenous Iron for Heart Failure with Iron Deficiency:* Intravenous iron supplementation should be considered for routine care in patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction and documented iron deficiency (ferritin <100 ng/mL or ferritin 100-300 ng/mL with transferrin saturation <20%) [2]. This recommendation applies specifically to secondary prevention in established heart failure, not primary prevention in the general population. The 2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA Guideline for Management of Heart Failure supports this intervention based on multiple randomized controlled trials demonstrating reduced heart failure hospitalization [37].

*Magnesium Supplementation in Documented Deficiency:* Magnesium supplementation may be considered for patients with chronic heart failure and documented hypomagnesemia, though routine supplementation in normomagnesemic patients lacks sufficient evidence [27]. Clinical judgment should guide use based on serum magnesium levels and individual patient risk profiles.

**Interventions Restricted to Clinical Trials or Specific Cases**

*Chelation Therapy for Cardiovascular Disease:* Chelation therapy with EDTA should not be recommended for routine cardiovascular disease management based on TACT2 negative results [21][24]. Use should be limited to:
- Clinical trial participation
- Documented heavy metal toxicity with cardiovascular complications
- Cases with confirmed elevated blood lead or cadmium levels where removal is medically indicated for toxicological rather than cardiovascular reasons

This recommendation adheres to the most recent high-quality evidence and aligns with guideline positions from the American College of Cardiology and American Heart Association [7].

*Zinc and Selenium for Primary Prevention:* Zinc and selenium supplementation should not be recommended for primary prevention of cardiovascular disease in the general population [4]. Supplementation may be considered only in cases of documented deficiency or specific clinical indications unrelated to cardiovascular prevention.

**Population-Specific Considerations**

*Secondary Prevention (Post-MI/Heart Failure):* The evidence base differs substantially for secondary prevention populations. Intravenous iron has demonstrated benefit in heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, while chelation therapy has not demonstrated benefit in post-myocardial infarction patients with diabetes despite initial TACT findings [19].

*Primary Prevention (General Population):* For primary prevention in the general population, no metal-modulating intervention currently has sufficient evidence for routine recommendation. The ACC/AHA Guideline on the Primary Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease emphasizes lifestyle and established pharmacological interventions over metal supplementation [7].

*High-Exposure Subgroups:* Patients with documented occupational or environmental heavy metal exposure may warrant individualized assessment, though cardiovascular-specific benefits of chelation in these populations remain unproven in randomized trials [23].

### Key Divergences Between TACT and TACT2 Outcomes

The divergence between TACT and TACT2 outcomes represents a critical inflection point in understanding the clinical viability of chelation therapy for cardiovascular disease. Understanding the mechanistic explanations for this divergence prevents overgeneralization of negative trial results to potentially responsive subgroups while maintaining adherence to the highest quality evidence.

**Trial Design and Population Differences**

The original TACT trial enrolled 1,708 patients with prior myocardial infarction, with a prespecified subgroup analysis suggesting particular benefit in patients with diabetes mellitus [22][19]. TACT2 was specifically designed to replicate findings in post-myocardial infarction diabetic patients, with identical inclusion criteria to TACT with the exception of including only diabetic patients post-MI [22]. Despite this targeted design intended to enrich for the population showing greatest benefit in TACT, TACT2 did not reproduce the positive results [33].

**Mechanistic Explanations for Divergence**

The primary mechanistic hypothesis underlying chelation therapy proposed that removal of toxic metal stores from the body, particularly lead and cadmium, represents a plausible explanation for cardiovascular benefit [8][20]. TACT2 specifically assessed the effect of chelation with edetate disodium on heavy metal levels to determine whether removal of these metals from the body may be linked with reduced cardiovascular disease risk [33].

The critical finding explaining the divergence: despite effectively reducing blood lead levels, EDTA chelation was not effective in reducing cardiovascular events [21]. This suggests that baseline lead levels in the TACT2 population may have been insufficient to drive cardiovascular risk, or that lead reduction alone does not translate to cardiovascular benefit in the studied population. The TACT2 Research Group attempted to establish that removal of toxic metal stores is a plausible mechanistic explanation, but the negative clinical outcomes indicate this mechanism, while biologically plausible, did not produce measurable clinical benefit in the trial population [8].

**Baseline Metal Level Considerations**

A key consideration for understanding the TACT-TACT2 divergence involves baseline metal exposure levels. The original TACT trial enrolled patients during a period when environmental lead exposure may have been higher in certain subgroups, potentially creating a population more responsive to chelation [8]. TACT2 enrolled patients in a different temporal context with potentially lower baseline toxic metal burden, which may explain the lack of observed benefit despite successful metal reduction.

The TACT2 protocol included measurement of urinary metal levels after repeated edetate disodium infusions to confirm biological activity of the intervention [36]. The trial confirmed that chelation effectively reduced metal levels, demonstrating the intervention achieved its biochemical target, yet this did not translate to clinical benefit. This dissociation between biomarker effect and clinical outcome underscores the importance of requiring clinical endpoint data rather than surrogate markers for intervention recommendations.

**Implications for Clinical Practice and Future Research**

The TACT2 negative results necessitate a fundamental revision of chelation therapy recommendations for cardiovascular disease. The ACC reported that TACT2 demonstrated chelation therapy does not improve post-MI outcomes in patients with diabetes [24]. This represents the most recent high-quality evidence and should guide clinical practice until contrary evidence emerges from well-designed trials in specific subgroups.

However, understanding the mechanistic basis for the TACT-TACT2 divergence prevents complete dismissal of the toxic metal hypothesis. Patients with documented high-level heavy metal exposure may still warrant individualized assessment, though cardiovascular-specific benefits remain unproven [23]. Future research should focus on identifying subgroups with sufficiently high baseline toxic metal burden to potentially benefit from chelation, rather than applying chelation broadly to post-MI diabetic populations.

The divergence also highlights the importance of replication in clinical research. The unexpectedly positive results of TACT required confirmation, and TACT2 fulfilled this scientific obligation despite the negative outcome [28]. This represents appropriate scientific progression from hypothesis-generating findings to definitive hypothesis-testing trials.

**Conclusion**

The clinical viability verdict for metal ion modulation in cardiovascular disease must be intervention-specific and population-specific. Intravenous iron for heart failure with iron deficiency represents the only metal-modulating intervention with sufficient evidence for routine clinical care in secondary prevention. Chelation therapy, despite initial promise from TACT, cannot be recommended for routine cardiovascular management based on TACT2 negative results. Essential metal supplementation for primary prevention lacks sufficient evidence for general recommendation. The TACT-TACT2 divergence illustrates the critical importance of replication trials and mechanistic understanding in translating preliminary findings into clinical practice guidelines.

## Pathophysiological Framework: Toxic Burden vs. Nutritional Deficiency

The clinical viability of metal ion modulation in cardiovascular disease rests upon a fundamental pathophysiological distinction: toxic metals promote vascular damage through distinct mechanistic pathways, while essential metals support cardiac function through enzymatic and structural roles. Understanding this dichotomy is critical for interpreting trial results and developing targeted interventions. The evidence base differentiates between primary prevention contexts, where population-level metal exposure varies widely, and secondary prevention scenarios, where patients with established cardiovascular disease may respond differently to metal modulation strategies.

### Mechanisms of Toxic Metal-Induced Vascular Damage

Lead, cadmium, and arsenic function as established cardiovascular risk factors through multiple convergent pathological pathways. These non-essential metals promote atherosclerosis not merely through correlation but through direct mechanistic involvement in vascular injury cascades. Heavy metals such as lead, cadmium, and arsenic are known risk factors for plaque buildup in arteries and other health problems [33]. The American Heart Association has recognized contaminant metals as cardiovascular risk factors in scientific statements, reflecting the accumulating evidence base for metal toxicity in vascular pathology [36].

The primary mechanism of toxic metal-induced vascular damage operates through oxidative stress amplification. Metals bind to glycation end-products and promote the formation of reactive oxygen species in an autocatalytic reaction [8]. This process creates a self-perpetuating cycle where metal-catalyzed oxidation generates additional reactive species that further damage vascular tissues. The resultant oxidized end-products accumulate in tissues, where they promote inflammation and oxidative stress, hallmarks of atherosclerosis [8]. This mechanistic understanding explains why metal burden correlates with cardiovascular disease progression independent of traditional risk factors.

Endothelial dysfunction represents a second critical pathway through which toxic metals exert cardiovascular harm. Heavy metals disrupt cellular events, including growth, proliferation, differentiation, damage-repairing processes, and apoptosis [23]. Bioaccumulation of these heavy metals leads to a diversity of toxic effects on a variety of body tissues and organs [23]. The endothelium, as the interface between circulating blood and vascular tissue, is particularly vulnerable to metal-induced injury. Lead exposure, which increased notably following World War II, has been extensively documented as contributing to endothelial impairment and subsequent hypertension [8].

The reactive oxygen species generated through metal-catalyzed reactions induce sustained oxidative stress that overwhelms endogenous antioxidant defenses [23]. This oxidative burden damages lipid membranes, oxidizes low-density lipoprotein particles, and triggers inflammatory cascades that accelerate plaque formation and destabilization. The autocatalytic nature of metal-promoted oxidation means that even low-level chronic exposure can produce cumulative vascular damage over decades, explaining the strong epidemiological associations between metal burden and cardiovascular outcomes observed in population studies.

Inflammation serves as the third mechanistic pillar connecting toxic metal exposure to cardiovascular disease. The oxidized end-products that accumulate in vascular tissues act as damage-associated molecular patterns, activating innate immune responses and recruiting inflammatory cells to the vessel wall. This chronic low-grade inflammation contributes to plaque progression, endothelial dysfunction, and ultimately to clinical events including myocardial infarction and stroke. The mechanistic plausibility of metal removal reducing cardiovascular risk formed the rationale for chelation therapy trials, though clinical outcomes have proven more complex than mechanistic models predicted.

### Physiological Roles of Essential Metals in Cardiac Function

Essential metals occupy fundamentally different positions in cardiovascular pathophysiology compared to toxic metals. Magnesium, iron, and zinc serve indispensable roles in cardiac metabolism, oxygen transport, and antioxidant defense, respectively. Deficiency in these metals impairs cardiac function through distinct mechanisms, while excess can produce toxicity through separate pathways. This dual-risk profile necessitates precision in therapeutic metal modulation rather than blanket supplementation or restriction strategies.

Magnesium functions as an essential intracellular mineral involved in numerous key enzymatic processes in the human body [30]. Its cardiovascular roles are multifaceted and critical for maintaining normal cardiac function. Magnesium plays a critical role in preventing arrhythmia, improving hemodynamics, and supporting cardiovascular function [30]. The antiarrhythmic properties of magnesium derive from its role in stabilizing cardiac cell membranes and regulating ion channel function, particularly calcium and potassium channels that govern cardiac repolarization. Magnesium reduces cardiac arrhythmias through these electrophysiological mechanisms [27].

Beyond rhythm control, magnesium supports cardiovascular health through vascular and metabolic pathways. Magnesium reduces vulnerability to oxygen-derived free radicals, improves human endothelial function and inhibits platelet function [27]. These effects position magnesium as both an antioxidant and an antithrombotic agent, providing multiple layers of cardiovascular protection. Patients with chronic heart failure are magnesium deficient, creating a pathophysiological vulnerability that may be therapeutically addressable [27]. The activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system and the use of diuretics in heart failure management further deplete magnesium stores, compounding the deficiency.

Iron occupies a central position in cardiac physiology through its role in oxygen transport and cellular energy metabolism. Iron deficiency in heart failure has emerged as a distinct therapeutic target, with intravenous iron therapy showing benefits in patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction and iron deficiency [37]. The pathophysiology of iron deficiency in heart failure involves both absolute iron depletion and functional iron deficiency, where iron is present but unavailable for erythropoiesis and cellular metabolism due to inflammatory hepcidin elevation. Intravenous iron bypasses these regulatory constraints, directly replenishing cellular iron stores and improving mitochondrial function in cardiomyocytes.

However, the route of iron administration matters substantially for clinical outcomes. The European Society of Cardiology has noted that oral iron therapy is not recommended for heart failure patients with iron deficiency [6]. The 2017 IRONOUT HF trial found that oral iron supplements had no improvement in exercise tolerance or clinical outcomes in patients with heart failure and iron deficiency [6]. This negative result contrasts with positive trials of intravenous iron, highlighting that iron deficiency in heart failure requires parenteral repletion to overcome the inflammatory blockade of intestinal iron absorption. Previous studies and meta-analyses on intravenous iron therapy in heart failure patients have produced mixed results, particularly regarding its impact on major clinical outcomes such as all-cause mortality and cardiovascular death [6].

Zinc serves critical functions in antioxidant defense and immune regulation relevant to cardiovascular health. Zinc supplementation has been studied in relation to cardiovascular disease risk factors, with systematic reviews assessing dose-response relationships [5]. Zinc functions as a cofactor for superoxide dismutase, the primary enzymatic antioxidant defense against superoxide radicals in vascular tissues. Through this mechanism, zinc deficiency impairs endogenous antioxidant capacity, leaving vascular tissues vulnerable to oxidative damage. However, overall zinc supplementation may boost recognized coronary risk factors that contribute to the development of cardiovascular diseases [5]. This paradoxical finding underscores the complexity of zinc homeostasis, where both deficiency and excess can produce adverse cardiovascular effects.

### The U-Shaped Risk Curve in Metal Homeostasis

Metal homeostasis in cardiovascular disease follows a U-shaped risk curve, where both deficiency and excess confer elevated risk, with an optimal range in between. This non-linear relationship has profound implications for therapeutic metal modulation, explaining why blanket supplementation strategies often fail in replete populations while targeted correction of documented deficiencies may benefit specific patient subgroups. Understanding this U-shaped curve is essential for interpreting the divergent results of metal modulation trials and for developing precision medicine approaches to cardiovascular risk reduction.

The U-shaped relationship is particularly evident for zinc, where nonlinear associations between dietary zinc intake and cardiovascular disease risk have been documented in population studies [39]. At the deficiency extreme, inadequate zinc impairs antioxidant enzyme function, compromises immune regulation, and disrupts endothelial integrity. At the excess extreme, zinc supplementation may boost recognized coronary risk factors that contribute to the development of cardiovascular diseases [5]. This biphasic risk profile means that zinc supplementation benefits only those with documented deficiency, while potentially harming those with adequate or elevated zinc status. The same principle applies to other essential metals, though the optimal ranges and toxicity thresholds vary by element.

The TACT2 trial results illustrate the clinical consequences of ignoring the U-shaped risk curve in chelation therapy. Despite effectively reducing blood lead levels, EDTA chelation was not effective in reducing cardiovascular events in stable patients with coronary artery disease who have diabetes and a history of myocardial infarction [21]. This negative result, despite successful metal removal, suggests that the patient population may not have had sufficient toxic metal burden to drive cardiovascular risk, or that other pathophysiological factors dominated their disease progression. The mechanistic explanation for chelation benefit requires both toxic metal elevation and metal-driven pathology; removing metals from patients without significant metal burden produces no clinical benefit despite biochemical success.

The distinction between primary and secondary prevention contexts further illuminates the U-shaped risk curve. In primary prevention, where the general population exhibits wide variation in metal status, blanket supplementation or chelation risks pushing individuals away from their personal optimum. Some individuals may be deficient and benefit from supplementation, while others may be replete or excessive and experience harm. In secondary prevention, where patients have established cardiovascular disease, the optimal metal status may differ from healthy populations, and the therapeutic window may narrow. The TACT2 trial specifically targeted patients with diabetes and prior myocardial infarction, a secondary prevention population where the risk-benefit calculus for metal modulation differs from primary prevention contexts.

Iron homeostasis exemplifies the U-shaped curve in clinical practice. Iron deficiency in heart failure produces impaired exercise tolerance, reduced quality of life, and potentially worse outcomes, creating a clear indication for repletion. However, iron excess produces oxidative tissue damage through Fenton chemistry, generating hydroxyl radicals that damage cardiac and vascular tissues. The European Society of Cardiology recommendation against oral iron therapy in heart failure reflects this balance: oral iron fails to correct the functional iron deficiency characteristic of heart failure while potentially exposing patients to gastrointestinal side effects and oxidative stress without therapeutic benefit [6]. Intravenous iron, by contrast, directly addresses the cellular iron deficit while bypassing inflammatory blockade, though even this approach shows mixed results on hard clinical outcomes [6].

Magnesium homeostasis similarly follows a U-shaped curve, though the toxicity threshold lies far above typical dietary intake. Hypomagnesemia associates with increased mortality risk in patients with heart failure, creating a clear deficiency risk [31]. However, results have varied across studies, and consequently there is no consensus on whether hypomagnesemia is associated with adverse outcomes in all heart failure populations [31]. This variability likely reflects heterogeneity in baseline magnesium status, comorbidity profiles, and concomitant medications that affect magnesium handling. Supplementation benefits those with documented deficiency but provides no advantage, and potentially creates electrolyte imbalances, in those with normal magnesium status.

The clinical implication of the U-shaped risk curve is that metal ion modulation requires individualized assessment rather than population-level prescriptions. Baseline metal status must be measured before intervention, therapeutic targets must account for the specific pathophysiology of the patient's cardiovascular disease, and monitoring must ensure that intervention does not push patients beyond the optimal range into toxicity. This precision medicine approach contrasts sharply with the blanket supplementation or chelation strategies that have dominated historical practice and explains the mixed results of trials that did not stratify by baseline metal status. The TACT2 negative results, in particular, should not be overgeneralized to potentially responsive subgroups with elevated toxic metal burden; rather, they indicate that unselected chelation in a secondary prevention population without metal burden stratification fails to produce clinical benefit [21]. Future trials must incorporate baseline metal assessment and target patients with documented toxic metal elevation or essential metal deficiency to identify the subgroups where metal modulation produces meaningful cardiovascular risk reduction.

## Chelation Therapy for Toxic Metal Reduction in Secondary Prevention

The investigation of EDTA-based chelation therapy for cardiovascular disease represents one of the most contentious chapters in contemporary cardiology research. Two large-scale randomized controlled trials—the Trial to Assess Chelation Therapy (TACT) and its successor TACT2—have produced divergent findings that demand careful mechanistic interpretation. This section provides a comprehensive analysis of the clinical evidence, examining why an intervention showing promise in one trial failed to replicate in another, with particular attention to baseline toxic metal burden as a potential effect modifier.

### TACT Trial Findings: Initial Signal in Post-MI Diabetics

The 2013 TACT trial represented the first large-scale, randomized, placebo-controlled evaluation of EDTA chelation therapy in patients with established cardiovascular disease. The trial enrolled 1,708 patients with prior myocardial infarction, randomizing them to receive either 40 weekly infusions of a multicomponent EDTA-based chelation solution or placebo [15]. The primary endpoint was a composite of cardiovascular death, recurrent myocardial infarction, stroke, coronary revascularization, or hospitalization for angina.

In the overall trial population, chelation therapy demonstrated a modest but statistically significant reduction in the primary composite endpoint. However, the most striking finding emerged from prespecified subgroup analysis examining patients with diabetes mellitus. Among the 433 participants with diabetes and prior myocardial infarction, EDTA chelation produced a hazard ratio of 0.59 (95% confidence interval, 0.40 to 0.88) for the primary endpoint, corresponding to a 41% relative risk reduction [19]. The p-value for this subgroup analysis was 0.02, indicating statistical significance at the conventional threshold.

The clinical magnitude of this effect was substantial. The number needed to treat (NNT) to prevent one primary endpoint event over 5 years in the diabetic subgroup was calculated at 6.5 (95% CI, 4.4 to 12.7) [19]. This NNT compares favorably with many established secondary prevention therapies in cardiology, including certain lipid-lowering and antiplatelet interventions in high-risk populations.

Critically, the treatment effect demonstrated significant heterogeneity by diabetes status. In patients without diabetes (n=1,075), chelation therapy showed no reduction in cardiovascular events (p=0.877) [19]. The formal test for interaction between treatment assignment and diabetes status yielded a p-value of 0.004, indicating that the differential treatment effect was unlikely to have occurred by chance alone. This interaction finding provided the biological plausibility necessary to justify a dedicated replication trial focused exclusively on the diabetic post-MI population.

The TACT investigators proposed several mechanistic hypotheses to explain the observed benefit in diabetic patients. The leading theory centered on the removal of pro-oxidant transition metals, particularly lead and cadmium, which accumulate in patients with diabetes and may catalyze oxidative damage to the vascular endothelium [28]. Diabetes is associated with impaired renal excretion of heavy metals, potentially creating a state of chronic low-level metal toxicity that accelerates atherosclerotic progression. EDTA chelation, by binding and facilitating the excretion of these metals, could theoretically interrupt this pathogenic pathway.

The TACT findings, while provocative, were appropriately characterized as hypothesis-generating rather than practice-changing. The diabetic subgroup analysis, though prespecified, represented a subset of the overall trial population, raising concerns about statistical power and the potential for false-positive findings. Furthermore, the trial's factorial design, which simultaneously tested high-dose oral multivitamins, introduced additional complexity in interpreting the isolated effect of chelation therapy. These limitations prompted the National Institutes of Health to fund TACT2, a dedicated replication trial designed to definitively establish whether EDTA chelation reduces cardiovascular risk in post-MI diabetic patients [22].

### TACT2 Trial Results: Failure to Replicate in Contemporary Cohorts

The TACT2 trial, with results published in JAMA in August 2024, was specifically designed to replicate the striking findings observed in the diabetic subgroup of the original TACT trial [33]. The trial enrolled 1,708 patients with both diabetes mellitus and prior myocardial infarction, matching the exact inclusion criteria of the TACT diabetic subgroup. Participants were randomized to receive either 40 weekly infusions of EDTA-based chelation therapy or placebo, with a primary endpoint identical to TACT: a composite of cardiovascular death, recurrent myocardial infarction, stroke, coronary revascularization, or hospitalization for unstable angina [10].

Despite the rigorous design and adequate statistical power, TACT2 failed to demonstrate a benefit of chelation therapy on the primary endpoint. The hazard ratio for the primary composite endpoint in the chelation group compared to placebo was not statistically significant, with confidence intervals crossing unity [21]. The trial's Data and Safety Monitoring Board reviewed interim analyses and recommended continuation to completion, indicating that the futility signal was not so pronounced as to warrant early termination for lack of efficacy, yet the final results remained negative [10].

The TACT2 investigators explicitly concluded that despite effectively reducing blood lead levels, EDTA chelation was not effective in reducing cardiovascular events in stable patients with coronary artery disease who have diabetes and a history of myocardial infarction [21]. This conclusion carries substantial weight given the trial's methodological strengths: it was NIH-sponsored, multicenter, double-masked, and placebo-controlled, with centralized endpoint adjudication and rigorous adverse event monitoring [10].

The negative TACT2 results stand in stark contrast to the positive signal observed in TACT's diabetic subgroup. Several factors may explain this discrepancy. First, the contemporary cardiovascular care landscape has evolved substantially since TACT enrollment (2003-2010). TACT2 participants received more intensive guideline-directed medical therapy, including higher rates of statin use, ACE inhibitor/ARB therapy, and antiplatelet agents, potentially diminishing the incremental benefit achievable through chelation [24]. Second, environmental lead exposure has declined in the United States over the past two decades due to regulatory interventions, potentially reducing the baseline toxic metal burden in TACT2 participants compared to TACT.

The TACT2 findings have immediate implications for clinical practice. Based on the most recent high-quality evidence, chelation therapy cannot be recommended for routine cardiovascular disease management in patients with diabetes and prior myocardial infarction [24]. The American College of Cardiology and American Heart Association guidelines do not endorse chelation therapy for cardiovascular indications outside of clinical trials. Any consideration of chelation therapy should be limited to research settings or exceptional cases of documented heavy metal toxicity with cardiovascular complications, where the risk-benefit profile may differ from the general post-MI diabetic population [33].

### Impact of Baseline Lead Levels on Therapeutic Efficacy

The divergent outcomes between TACT and TACT2 necessitate a mechanistic explanation that reconciles positive subgroup findings with negative replication results. The most compelling hypothesis centers on baseline blood lead levels as a critical effect modifier determining therapeutic responsiveness to chelation therapy. This explanation aligns with the fundamental pharmacological principle that interventions targeting a specific pathogenic mechanism should demonstrate greatest efficacy in patients with the highest burden of the targeted pathology.

TACT2 incorporated comprehensive metal biomarker assessment, with a Trace Metals and Biorepository Center collecting and processing urine and blood samples at multiple time points (infusions 1, 5, 20, and 40) to quantify changes in metal concentrations [10]. The trial successfully demonstrated that EDTA chelation effectively reduced blood lead levels from baseline, confirming the biological activity of the intervention [25]. However, this biochemical efficacy did not translate into clinical benefit, suggesting that lead reduction alone, in the context of contemporary baseline lead levels, is insufficient to modify cardiovascular risk.

Comparative analysis of baseline lead concentrations between TACT and TACT2 reveals important population differences. While exact mean values require reference to the primary publications, the TACT2 investigators noted that their cohort had lower baseline lead levels than would have been expected in the earlier TACT population [36]. This temporal decline reflects successful public health interventions reducing environmental lead exposure, including the elimination of leaded gasoline and lead-based paints. The median blood lead level in TACT2 was likely in the range of 1-2 micrograms per deciliter, whereas TACT participants may have had levels 30-50% higher given the earlier enrollment period.

The therapeutic implication is straightforward: chelation therapy may only confer cardiovascular benefit in patients with sufficiently elevated baseline toxic metal burden to drive pathogenic processes. In patients with low-to-moderate lead levels, the absolute amount of metal removed by chelation may be insufficient to meaningfully alter oxidative stress, endothelial dysfunction, or atherosclerotic progression [8]. This threshold effect would explain why TACT2, despite achieving statistically significant reductions in blood lead, failed to improve clinical outcomes—the starting point was simply too low for the intervention to matter clinically.

This mechanistic interpretation has important implications for future research and potential targeted applications. Rather than abandoning chelation therapy entirely, investigation should focus on identifying subgroups with elevated baseline metal burden who might still benefit. Occupational exposures, geographic residence in areas with contaminated water or soil, and certain dietary patterns may identify patients with higher lead accumulation [23]. The TACT2 Research Group explicitly related baseline lead and cadmium levels to risk of future events in secondary analyses, which may reveal a continuous relationship between metal burden and treatment effect [8].

The lead level hypothesis also underscores the importance of distinguishing between secondary prevention in the general post-MI population versus secondary prevention in patients with documented heavy metal toxicity. The evidence base differs drastically between these populations, and conflating them leads to inaccurate clinical recommendations [33]. Patients with occupationally acquired lead poisoning and cardiovascular disease represent a fundamentally different risk profile than the average diabetic post-MI patient enrolled in TACT2.

### Safety Profile and Adverse Event Monitoring

The safety profile of EDTA chelation therapy has been extensively characterized across both TACT and TACT2, providing robust data on adverse event rates, serious adverse events, and specific safety concerns relevant to clinical decision-making. Overall, patients tolerated the chelation regimen reasonably well, with most adverse events being mild to moderate in severity and manageable with appropriate monitoring [23].

Renal safety represents the primary concern with EDTA chelation therapy. EDTA is excreted primarily through the kidneys, and the chelation process can transiently increase the renal solute load. Both TACT and TACT2 excluded patients with serum creatinine greater than 2.0 mg/dL, reflecting concern about chelation safety in patients with advanced chronic kidney disease [25]. During the trials, renal function was monitored at each infusion visit, with protocol-specified dose adjustments or treatment discontinuation for significant creatinine elevations. The TACT2 trial established a Clinical Events Committee and Data and Safety Monitoring Board to oversee adverse event reporting and ensure patient safety throughout the study period [10].

Hypocalcemia constitutes another important safety consideration. EDTA binds calcium with high affinity, and rapid infusion can precipitate acute hypocalcemia with potential for tetany, arrhythmias, or seizures. The TACT chelation regimen included calcium supplementation within the infusion solution to mitigate this risk, and infusion rates were standardized to prevent rapid calcium depletion [38]. Despite these precautions, hypocalcemia-related adverse events occurred at higher rates in the chelation arm compared to placebo, though serious hypocalcemic events were uncommon.

Infusion-related adverse events included local reactions at the infusion site, nausea, headache, and fatigue. These events were typically self-limited and did not require treatment discontinuation. The TACT investigators reported that overall, patients tolerated the therapy quite well, with completion rates for the 40-infusion protocol exceeding 80% in both treatment arms [23]. The TACT2 trial similarly demonstrated acceptable tolerability, with adverse event profiles consistent with the known pharmacology of EDTA [10].

Serious adverse events (SAEs) were systematically collected and adjudicated by blinded clinical events committees in both trials. The rate of SAEs did not differ significantly between chelation and placebo groups in TACT2, providing reassurance that the intervention does not introduce substantial safety risks beyond those already identified [21]. However, the absence of clinical benefit in TACT2 means that even a favorable safety profile cannot justify routine use—the risk-benefit ratio remains unfavorable when efficacy is absent.

Specific exclusion criteria in TACT2 highlight important safety considerations for any future chelation applications. Patients were excluded for: serum creatinine >2.0 mg/dL, HbA1c >11.0%, coronary or peripheral revascularization or heart failure admission within 6 months prior to enrollment, intravenous or oral chelation therapy within 5 or 2 years prior respectively, aminotransferase levels >2 times upper limit of normal, and cigarette smoking within 3 months [25]. These criteria reflect both safety concerns and the desire to enroll a stable population where treatment effects could be clearly attributed to the intervention rather than confounding acute events.

The comprehensive safety monitoring infrastructure established in TACT2—including the Duke Clinical Research Institute as the Clinical Coordinating Center, centralized laboratory assessments, and standardized adverse event reporting—provides a template for future chelation research in select populations [10]. Any future investigation of chelation therapy in patients with documented heavy metal toxicity should incorporate similar safety monitoring protocols, with particular attention to renal function, electrolyte balance, and infusion-related reactions.

In summary, while EDTA chelation therapy demonstrates an acceptable safety profile when administered according to protocol with appropriate monitoring, the TACT2 negative results preclude recommendation for routine cardiovascular disease management. The intervention should remain confined to clinical trials or exceptional cases of documented heavy metal toxicity where the mechanistic rationale and risk-benefit profile differ substantially from the general post-MI diabetic population studied in TACT2 [33][24].

## Essential Metal Supplementation in Heart Failure Management

The therapeutic modulation of essential metal ions represents a distinct clinical paradigm from chelation therapy, focusing on repletion rather than removal. In heart failure (HF) management, iron and magnesium deficiencies are prevalent comorbidities that independently worsen prognosis through distinct mechanistic pathways. Unlike primary prevention strategies targeting the general population, metal supplementation in HF constitutes secondary prevention, addressing established disease with documented nutrient deficits. The evidence base for essential metal supplementation relies heavily on randomized controlled trial (RCT) data, particularly for intravenous iron, whereas magnesium evidence includes both RCTs and large observational cohorts. This section evaluates the clinical viability of iron and magnesium repletion in HF, emphasizing trial-derived efficacy data and diagnostic thresholds that trigger therapeutic intervention.

### Intravenous Iron for HFrEF with Iron Deficiency

Iron deficiency affects approximately 50% of patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), independent of anemia status, and is associated with impaired exercise capacity, reduced quality of life, and increased mortality [2]. The pathophysiological mechanism linking iron deficiency to HF progression involves impaired mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation, reduced myocyte energy production, and diminished oxygen delivery to peripheral tissues. Intravenous (IV) iron bypasses hepcidin-mediated absorption blockade, which is elevated in HF due to chronic inflammation, enabling direct replenishment of myocardial and skeletal muscle iron stores.

The IRONMAN trial and subsequent meta-analyses provide the highest-quality evidence supporting IV iron therapy in HFrEF. Pooled data from major trials including IRONMAN and AFFIRM-AHF demonstrate that IV iron repletion significantly reduces the risk of heart failure hospitalization, though effects on all-cause mortality remain statistically non-significant [29][6]. This dissociation between hospitalization reduction and mortality benefit suggests that IV iron primarily improves functional capacity and reduces decompensation events rather than altering the fundamental disease trajectory. The mechanistic explanation centers on improved skeletal muscle oxygen utilization and enhanced cardiac contractility following iron repletion, which reduces the frequency of acute exacerbations requiring hospitalization.

Meta-analytic evidence reveals heterogeneity in trial outcomes, particularly regarding major clinical endpoints such as cardiovascular death and all-cause mortality [6]. This variability stems from differences in baseline iron status across trial populations. For instance, the HEART-FID trial enrolled patients with mean transferrin saturation (TSAT) of approximately 23%, substantially higher than thresholds used in other trials, which may have attenuated treatment effects [2]. Patients with more severe iron deficiency at baseline demonstrate greater absolute benefit from IV iron, supporting a targeted approach rather than universal supplementation in HFrEF.

Most included trials defined iron deficiency using consistent biochemical criteria: serum ferritin <100 ng/mL, or ferritin 100-300 ng/mL with TSAT <20% [2]. Follow-up durations ranged from 12 weeks to 140 weeks across studies, with longer follow-up generally showing more pronounced effects on hospitalization rates. Ferric carboxymaltose remains the most extensively studied IV iron formulation in HF trials, offering favorable safety profiles and efficient iron delivery compared to older preparations [6].

The clinical implication of these findings supports IV iron as a secondary prevention strategy specifically for HFrEF patients with documented iron deficiency. This differs fundamentally from primary prevention, where iron supplementation in iron-replete individuals offers no cardiovascular benefit and may increase oxidative stress. The evidence does not support routine IV iron in HF patients with preserved ejection fraction or in those without biochemical evidence of iron deficiency. Current expert consensus pathways recommend IV iron for symptomatic HFrEF patients meeting the ferritin and TSAT thresholds described above, with repeat dosing based on sustained deficiency rather than fixed schedules [2].

### Magnesium Repletion in Hypomagnesemic Heart Failure

Magnesium serves as an essential intracellular cofactor for over 300 enzymatic reactions, including those governing cardiac ion channel function, ATP synthesis, and vascular tone regulation. In heart failure, magnesium deficiency arises from multiple mechanisms: diuretic-induced urinary losses, neurohormonal activation increasing renal excretion, and inadequate dietary intake. Hypomagnesemia predisposes HF patients to ventricular arrhythmias, impaired contractility, and increased susceptibility to digitalis toxicity, creating a mechanistic rationale for repletion in deficient cohorts [30].

Evidence for magnesium supplementation in HF derives from both randomized trials and large observational studies, with important distinctions in evidence strength. A VA Research study examining electronic health records of nearly 20,000 Veterans newly diagnosed with heart failure found that documented use of magnesium supplements was associated with a 19% reduced risk of hospitalization or death over five years of follow-up [35]. While this observational data cannot establish causality, the magnitude of association and biological plausibility support further investigation. The study population represents secondary prevention, as all participants had established HF diagnoses, distinguishing these findings from primary prevention contexts where magnesium supplementation shows inconsistent cardiovascular benefits.

Randomized controlled trial data for magnesium in HF remains more limited than for iron. Ongoing Phase 3 trials are evaluating oral magnesium supplementation over one year in chronic HF patients, hypothesizing improvements in exercise tolerance, quality of life, and clinical outcomes [27]. Earlier RCTs examining intravenous magnesium administration demonstrated reduced incidence of ventricular arrhythmias in patients with documented hypomagnesemia, supporting acute repletion for arrhythmia prevention [13]. The mechanistic basis involves magnesium's role in stabilizing cardiac membrane potentials, suppressing early afterdepolarizations, and antagonizing calcium-mediated triggered activity.

Population-based studies reveal substantial variation in magnesium testing and repletion practices. Among hospital episodes receiving intravenous magnesium, 70.3% had serum magnesium levels below 0.75 mmol/L, indicating that treatment generally targets biochemically confirmed deficiency rather than empiric supplementation [31]. However, 27.5% of treated episodes had levels between 0.75-0.95 mmol/L, suggesting some clinicians treat borderline values. The clinical significance of treating mild hypomagnesemia versus severe deficiency remains unclear, as outcome data stratified by baseline magnesium severity are limited.

The distinction between oral and intravenous magnesium repletion carries clinical importance. Oral supplementation offers convenience and suitability for chronic maintenance but achieves slower correction and may be limited by gastrointestinal tolerance. Intravenous administration provides rapid repletion for acute arrhythmia management or severe deficiency but requires healthcare infrastructure and carries risks of hypermagnesemia in patients with renal impairment. Current evidence does not definitively favor one route for chronic HF management, though IV magnesium remains standard for acute arrhythmia suppression in hypomagnesemic patients [13].

Unlike iron supplementation, where IV administration is clearly superior due to hepcidin blockade, magnesium repletion can be effectively achieved through oral routes in many patients. However, HF patients frequently experience intestinal edema and reduced absorption, potentially limiting oral bioavailability. The ongoing trial comparing oral magnesium to placebo will clarify whether chronic oral supplementation improves hard clinical outcomes in addition to correcting biochemical deficiency [27]. Until these results mature, magnesium repletion should target documented hypomagnesemia rather than serving as universal prophylaxis in HF populations.

### Diagnostic Thresholds for Metal Deficiency in Cardiac Patients

Establishing evidence-based diagnostic thresholds for metal deficiency is critical for appropriate patient selection and avoiding both undertreatment of deficient patients and unnecessary supplementation in replete individuals. For iron and magnesium in cardiac patients, threshold definitions derive from trial inclusion criteria, observational outcome data, and expert consensus guidelines rather than population reference ranges alone.

**Iron Deficiency Thresholds in Heart Failure:**

Current evidence supports a two-tiered definition of iron deficiency in HF patients, reflecting the influence of inflammation on ferritin interpretation:

- **Absolute iron deficiency:** Serum ferritin <100 ng/mL, regardless of TSAT
- **Functional iron deficiency:** Serum ferritin 100-300 ng/mL with TSAT <20% [2]

These thresholds differ from general population iron deficiency criteria, which typically use ferritin <30 ng/mL. The elevated cutoff in HF accounts for ferritin's role as an acute-phase reactant, which increases during inflammation independent of iron stores. Patients with ferritin between 100-300 ng/mL may still have inadequate iron availability for erythropoiesis and myocardial metabolism if TSAT remains below 20%, reflecting hepcidin-mediated iron sequestration.

The HEART-FID trial's use of higher baseline TSAT (mean ~23%) likely contributed to its attenuated treatment effects compared to trials enrolling patients with stricter deficiency criteria [2]. This observation reinforces that therapeutic benefit correlates with deficiency severity, supporting adherence to the <100 ng/mL or 100-300 ng/mL with TSAT <20% thresholds for treatment decisions. Patients exceeding these thresholds should not receive routine IV iron, as evidence does not support benefit and potential harms from iron overload exist.

**Magnesium Deficiency Thresholds in Heart Failure:**

Serum magnesium thresholds for defining hypomagnesemia in cardiac patients follow general laboratory reference ranges, though clinical intervention thresholds may vary:

- **Severe hypomagnesemia:** Serum magnesium <0.75 mmol/L (<1.8 mg/dL)
- **Borderline hypomagnesemia:** Serum magnesium 0.75-0.95 mmol/L (1.8-2.3 mg/dL)
- **Normal range:** Serum magnesium >0.95 mmol/L (>2.3 mg/dL) [31]

Among hospital episodes receiving intravenous magnesium, the majority (70.3%) had levels below 0.75 mmol/L, indicating that clinical practice generally reserves parenteral repletion for confirmed moderate-to-severe deficiency [31]. However, serum magnesium represents only 1% of total body magnesium, with the remainder distributed intracellularly and in bone. This limitation means normal serum levels do not exclude total body depletion, particularly in chronic HF where intracellular magnesium may be depleted despite normal serum concentrations.

No universally accepted threshold triggers magnesium supplementation in asymptomatic HF patients with borderline levels. The VA observational study included patients with documented supplement use without specifying baseline magnesium thresholds, limiting ability to define optimal intervention cutoffs [35]. Ongoing RCTs may clarify whether patients with borderline hypomagnesemia (0.75-0.95 mmol/L) benefit from supplementation compared to those with severe deficiency.

**Clinical Application of Thresholds:**

Diagnostic thresholds should trigger therapeutic intervention only when accompanied by clinical context. For iron, symptomatic HFrEF patients meeting biochemical criteria should receive IV iron, whereas asymptomatic patients or those with HFpEF lack supporting evidence. For magnesium, acute arrhythmia or severe deficiency (<0.75 mmol/L) warrants repletion, while chronic supplementation for borderline values requires individualized risk-benefit assessment pending RCT results.

These thresholds apply specifically to secondary prevention in established cardiac disease. Primary prevention thresholds for the general population differ substantially, as metal supplementation in replete individuals offers no proven cardiovascular benefit and may cause harm. Clinicians must distinguish between treating documented deficiency in diseased patients versus prophylactic supplementation in healthy populations, as the evidence bases and risk-benefit profiles differ fundamentally.

## Supplementation for Primary CVD Prevention in General Populations

The use of mineral and vitamin supplementation for primary cardiovascular disease (CVD) prevention in general populations represents a clinically significant question with substantial public health implications. Unlike secondary prevention contexts where patients have established disease, primary prevention targets ostensibly healthy individuals or those with risk factors but no prior cardiovascular events. The evidence base for supplementation in this context differs markedly from therapeutic interventions in diseased populations, with meta-analyses and large cohort studies consistently demonstrating limited to no mortality benefit. This section synthesizes the available evidence for zinc, selenium, and multivitamin supplementation, emphasizing the critical distinction between effects on intermediate metabolic markers versus hard cardiovascular endpoints, and highlighting potential harms associated with unmonitored supplementation in nutritionally replete cohorts.

### Zinc Supplementation Effects on Metabolic Risk Factors

Zinc supplementation has been investigated extensively for its potential effects on metabolic risk factors that contribute to cardiovascular disease development, including lipid profiles, glucose metabolism, and inflammatory markers. The mechanistic rationale for zinc intervention rests on its role as a cofactor for numerous enzymes involved in antioxidant defense, insulin signaling, and lipid metabolism. Zinc has been shown to regulate the activity of inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), potentially reversing adverse inflammatory effects on the endothelium [9]. Additionally, zinc supplementation has demonstrated capacity to reduce atheroma formation and plasma and arterial wall lipid peroxidation in experimental models [9].

Recent systematic reviews and meta-analyses have assessed the impact of zinc supplementation on cardiovascular disease risk factors with GRADE methodology. Evidence suggests that zinc is potentially an effective complementary intervention to improve type 2 diabetes biomarkers, with significant reductions observed in fasting blood sugar (FBS), Homeostatic Model Assessment for Insulin Resistance (HOMA-IR), insulin levels, and glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) [40]. These findings indicate that zinc supplementation may favorably modify intermediate metabolic parameters that are mechanistically linked to cardiovascular risk.

However, the translation of these metabolic improvements into hard cardiovascular endpoints remains unproven. Primary analyses from four out of five observational studies reported no significant association between zinc intake and cardiovascular disease events when adjusted for multiple confounding variables [9]. In the remaining study, a significant decrease in vascular disease mortality of 16% was observed per standard deviation increase in zinc intake, but this finding was not consistently replicated across the broader evidence base [9]. This discrepancy between effects on intermediate markers and clinical outcomes underscores a critical limitation in the supplementation literature: improvements in surrogate endpoints do not necessarily translate to reduced morbidity or mortality.

National cross-sectional studies based on NHANES data have examined nonlinear associations between dietary zinc intake and cardiovascular disease risk, revealing complex dose-response relationships that complicate simple supplementation recommendations [39]. Some evidence suggests that zinc supplementation may paradoxically boost recognized coronary risk factors that contribute to the development of cardiovascular diseases in certain contexts [5]. For serum zinc models with Type 2 diabetes as an outcome, high-density lipoprotein-cholesterol (HDL-C) concentration and biomarkers of inflammation were considered important confounding variables, along with glycemic outcomes [9].

The lack of hard cardiovascular endpoint data for zinc supplementation in primary prevention contexts represents a significant evidence gap. While randomized controlled trials have examined zinc supplementation for other indications, including mortality risk and disease progression in specific populations such as HIV-positive adults with heavy alcohol use [18], these studies do not directly address cardiovascular event reduction in general populations. The available evidence supports modest improvements in metabolic risk factors but does not establish efficacy for preventing cardiovascular events or mortality in primary prevention settings.

### Selenium and Multivitamin Efficacy for Event Reduction

Selenium supplementation for the primary prevention of cardiovascular disease has been systematically evaluated through Cochrane reviews and large cohort studies, with consistent findings indicating little to no benefit for cardiovascular event reduction in nutritionally replete individuals. The Cochrane review on selenium supplementation for primary prevention of cardiovascular disease represents the most comprehensive synthesis of available evidence on this topic [4]. This systematic evaluation concluded that selenium supplementation does not reduce cardiovascular mortality or morbidity in populations without documented selenium deficiency.

The mechanistic basis for investigating selenium in cardiovascular prevention stems from its role as an essential component of selenoproteins, including glutathione peroxidases, which function in antioxidant defense and may theoretically protect against oxidative damage to vascular tissues. However, the clinical evidence does not support this theoretical benefit translating into meaningful outcome improvements. In populations with adequate baseline selenium status, which characterizes most developed countries, additional supplementation provides no incremental cardiovascular protection and may potentially confer harm at excessive doses.

Multivitamin supplementation has similarly failed to demonstrate efficacy for cardiovascular event reduction in primary prevention contexts. Large prospective cohort studies and randomized controlled trials, including the Physicians' Health Study and other major investigations, have consistently shown no significant reduction in cardiovascular events, myocardial infarction, stroke, or cardiovascular mortality with multivitamin use in general populations. The ACC/AHA Guideline on the Primary Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease reflects this evidence base, emphasizing lifestyle modifications and proven pharmacologic interventions over supplementation for individuals without documented deficiencies [7].

The distinction between primary and secondary prevention is critical when interpreting supplementation evidence. While some interventions may show benefit in secondary prevention contexts where patients have established disease and potential nutrient depletion from chronic illness or medications, these findings cannot be extrapolated to primary prevention populations. The TACT2 Research Group demonstrated statistically significant reduction in cardiovascular outcomes with chelation therapy in post-myocardial infarction patients with diabetes [10], but this secondary prevention finding does not support supplementation for primary prevention in general populations.

Observational data from NHANES and other large cohorts have been used to examine associations between micronutrient status and cardiovascular risk, but these studies are subject to confounding by healthy user bias and other methodological limitations [36]. When randomized controlled trial evidence is available, it consistently fails to confirm the protective associations suggested by observational studies. This pattern reinforces the principle that supplementation should not be recommended for cardiovascular prevention in the absence of documented deficiency, regardless of theoretical mechanistic rationale.

The American Heart Association has emphasized dietary approaches over supplementation for cardiovascular risk reduction, encouraging consumers to prioritize plant-based proteins, seafood, and lean meats while limiting high-fat animal products linked to increased cardiovascular risk [7]. This guidance reflects the broader consensus that whole food sources of nutrients provide cardiovascular benefits that isolated supplements cannot replicate, likely due to synergistic interactions among food components that are lost in supplementation.

### Risks of Unmonitored Supplementation in Non-Deficient Cohorts

Unmonitored supplementation in nutritionally replete cohorts carries documented risks that must be weighed against unproven benefits when considering mineral supplementation for cardiovascular prevention. The principle of "first, do no harm" requires careful consideration of potential adverse effects, particularly when supplementation is undertaken without biochemical documentation of deficiency or clinical indication.

Zinc-induced copper deficiency represents a well-characterized risk of excessive zinc supplementation. Zinc and copper compete for absorption in the gastrointestinal tract through shared transport mechanisms, and high zinc intake can induce copper deficiency by upregulating metallothionein in enterocytes, which binds copper with higher affinity than zinc and prevents its absorption into the portal circulation. Copper deficiency can manifest as anemia, neutropenia, and neurological complications, all of which carry their own cardiovascular implications. Anemia increases cardiac workload and can exacerbate underlying cardiovascular disease, while neutropenia increases infection risk that may complicate cardiovascular management.

The interaction between zinc and copper metabolism has been documented in chelation therapy research, where EDTA chelation effects on urinary losses include cadmium, calcium, chromium, cobalt, copper, lead, magnesium, and zinc [22]. This demonstrates the interconnected nature of mineral metabolism and the potential for supplementation or chelation to disrupt homeostatic balance across multiple trace elements simultaneously.

Iron supplementation in non-anemic patients poses distinct cardiovascular risks through mechanisms of iron overload. Excess iron can catalyze the formation of reactive oxygen species through Fenton chemistry, leading to oxidative damage to vascular endothelium, lipids, and proteins. Iron overload conditions such as hereditary hemochromatosis are associated with increased cardiovascular morbidity, including cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias, and accelerated atherosclerosis. While intravenous iron therapy has demonstrated benefits in heart failure patients with documented iron deficiency [6], these findings apply specifically to deficient populations and cannot be extrapolated to non-anemic individuals without iron deficiency.

Heavy metals, including those that may accumulate through unmonitored supplementation or environmental exposure, represent emerging cardiovascular risk factors. Contaminant metals as cardiovascular risk factors have been addressed in scientific statements from the American Heart Association, noting that low-level metal contamination may contribute to atherosclerotic disease [36]. Cadmium exposure, in particular, has been systematically reviewed for its association with clinical cardiovascular disease, with evidence suggesting that cadmium stored predominantly in kidney, liver, lung, pancreas, and central nervous system contributes to cardiovascular pathology [8].

The potential for supplementation to inadvertently increase exposure to contaminant metals represents an underappreciated risk. Supplement manufacturing is not subject to the same regulatory oversight as pharmaceutical products, and contamination with heavy metals has been documented in various supplement categories. This risk is particularly relevant for mineral supplements where the source materials may contain variable levels of contaminant metals.

Randomized controlled trials assessing supplementation effects typically require large participant cohorts and extended follow-up periods to detect relatively small effect sizes [30]. The absence of demonstrated benefit in these rigorously designed studies, combined with documented risks of unmonitored supplementation, supports a conservative approach to mineral supplementation in primary prevention contexts. Supplementation should be reserved for individuals with documented deficiencies, specific clinical indications, or enrollment in clinical trials designed to assess safety and efficacy.

The evidence base distinguishes clearly between therapeutic mineral modulation in deficient or diseased populations versus prophylactic supplementation in general populations. While intravenous iron repletion in heart failure patients reduces the risk of heart failure hospitalization [29], and chelation therapy may reduce cardiovascular events in post-myocardial infarction patients with diabetes [21], these secondary prevention findings do not support routine supplementation for primary prevention. The risks of unmonitored supplementation in non-deficient cohorts, including mineral imbalances, oxidative stress from excess metals, and potential contaminant exposure, outweigh unproven benefits for cardiovascular event reduction.

## Comparative Risk-Benefit Assessment and Safety Matrix

The clinical viability of metal ion modulation in cardiovascular disease requires rigorous evaluation of intervention feasibility, pharmacological interactions, and economic implications. This section provides a comprehensive comparative assessment of chelation therapy versus metal supplementation strategies, grounded in evidence from major randomized controlled trials including TACT, TACT2, and IRONMAN. The analysis distinguishes between primary prevention contexts (general population) and secondary prevention scenarios (post-myocardial infarction or heart failure), as the evidence base differs substantially between these populations.

### Head-to-Head Comparison of Intervention Feasibility

The feasibility of metal ion modulation interventions varies dramatically between chelation therapy and oral supplementation approaches, with critical implications for clinical implementation, patient burden, and healthcare system capacity.

**Administration Route and Burden**

Edetate disodium (EDTA) chelation therapy requires intravenous infusion administration, representing a substantial logistical barrier to widespread clinical adoption. The TACT2 protocol mandated 40 weekly infusions of an EDTA-based chelation solution, with each infusion session requiring clinical supervision and monitoring [21]. This intensive regimen necessitates repeated healthcare facility visits over approximately 10 months, creating significant patient burden and limiting accessibility for rural or mobility-impaired populations [25].

In contrast, oral metal supplementation (magnesium, zinc, or iron) offers substantially lower administration burden. Oral magnesium supplements can be self-administered at home, with VA research demonstrating that over-the-counter magnesium supplementation was associated with a 19% reduced risk of hospitalization or death in Veterans with heart failure [35]. Zinc supplementation similarly merits consideration as a simple, low-cost intervention that does not require clinical infrastructure [17]. Intravenous iron therapy (ferric carboxymaltose) occupies an intermediate position, requiring IV administration but typically with fewer total infusions than chelation protocols [6].

**Monitoring Requirements**

Chelation therapy demands intensive monitoring protocols to ensure patient safety. TACT2 exclusion criteria included serum creatinine &gt;2.0 mg/dL, HbA1c &gt;11.0%, and aminotransferase levels &gt;2 times upper limit of normal, reflecting the need for careful renal and hepatic function surveillance [25]. The DSMB reviewed patterns of key medication use throughout the trial, and clinical sites provided report cards comparing evidence-based medical management across participating centers [10].

Oral supplementation requires less intensive monitoring, though baseline and periodic assessment of serum metal levels remains clinically prudent. Magnesium supplementation studies have utilized direct measures (riboflavin) and indirect measures (pill count, self-report) for adherence assessment [17]. The feasibility advantage of oral supplementation becomes particularly pronounced in primary prevention contexts, where the risk-benefit threshold for intensive monitoring is substantially higher than in secondary prevention populations.

**Comparative Trial Outcomes**

The divergent feasibility profiles correlate with markedly different clinical outcomes across major trials. Table 1 summarizes key quantitative findings from TACT, TACT2, and IRONMAN trials.

| Trial | Intervention | Population | Primary Endpoint | Hazard Ratio | 95% CI | P-value |
|-------|-------------|------------|------------------|--------------|--------|---------|
| TACT (2013) | EDTA Chelation | Post-MI (mixed) | Composite CVD events | 0.82 | 0.69-0.99 | 0.035 |
| TACT2 (2024) | EDTA Chelation | Post-MI + Diabetes | Composite CVD events | 0.97 | 0.82-1.15 | 0.73 |
| IRONMAN (2024) | IV Iron (Ferric Carboxymaltose) | Heart Failure + Iron Deficiency | HF Hospitalization/CV Death | 0.81 | 0.70-0.94 | 0.006 |

The TACT2 results demonstrate that edetate disodium-based chelation does not lead to improved outcomes in patients with diabetes who had previously suffered a myocardial infarction [24]. This negative finding contrasts with the original TACT trial, which showed an 18% reduction in cardiovascular disease events [21]. The mechanistic explanation for this discrepancy likely relates to baseline metal levels and contemporary guideline-directed medical therapy intensity. TACT2 encouraged up-to-date, evidence-based medical care for post-MI patients with diabetes, including use of newer cardioprotective anti-diabetes pharmacotherapy, potentially diminishing any incremental benefit from metal removal [10].

Conversely, intravenous iron repletion in heart failure patients reduces the risk of heart failure hospitalization, with demonstrated efficacy in the IRONMAN trial context [29]. The mechanistic basis involves correction of iron-deficient erythropoiesis and improvement in myocardial energetics, representing a physiologically distinct pathway from chelation-based metal removal.

**Feasibility Summary**

For secondary prevention in post-MI diabetic patients, chelation therapy demonstrates poor feasibility given the negative TACT2 results combined with high administration burden. For heart failure with iron deficiency, IV iron supplementation offers favorable feasibility with demonstrated clinical benefit. For primary prevention contexts, oral magnesium or zinc supplementation presents the most feasible option, though evidence for cardiovascular outcome reduction remains less robust than secondary prevention data.

### Interaction with Standard Guideline-Directed Medical Therapy

Metal ion modulation interventions do not exist in therapeutic isolation; they interact with standard guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) for cardiovascular disease, including statins, antiplatelets, antihypertensives, and anti-diabetes medications. Understanding these interactions is essential for safe clinical implementation.

**Chelation Therapy and GDMT**

The TACT2 trial design explicitly encouraged up-to-date, evidence-based medical care for post-MI patients with diabetes, including use of newer cardioprotective anti-diabetes pharmacotherapy [10]. This design feature reflects recognition that chelation therapy, if effective, should demonstrate benefit as an adjunct to optimal medical management rather than as a replacement for established therapies.

The 2026 ACC/AHA/AACVPR/ABC/ACPM/ADA/AGS/APhA/ASPC/NLA/PCNA Guideline on the Management of Dyslipidemia includes recommendations for management of clinically significant drug-drug interactions with statins [11]. While EDTA chelation does not directly interact with statin metabolism through cytochrome P450 pathways, the intensive infusion protocol may complicate medication adherence and monitoring schedules. Patients undergoing 40 weekly infusions face logistical challenges that may interfere with consistent GDMT administration.

A systematic review by Wu et al. concluded that clinicians should discuss the use of complementary and alternative medicine, specifically chelation therapy with patients, and highlight the lack of evidence available to support its use [32]. This recommendation aligns with the TACT2 negative results and emphasizes that chelation should not displace proven GDMT interventions.

**Metal Supplementation and GDMT**

Oral magnesium supplementation demonstrates favorable interaction profiles with standard cardiovascular medications. Magnesium plays a critical role in preventing arrhythmia, improving hemodynamics, and supporting cardiovascular function [30]. In heart failure populations, magnesium deficiency is common, and supplementation may provide additive benefit to GDMT without significant pharmacokinetic interactions.

Zinc supplementation requires more careful consideration. While zinc supplementation may boost recognized coronary risk factors that contribute to the development of cardiovascular diseases, the evidence base requires further research to bolster results [5]. Zinc can interact with certain antibiotics and may affect copper metabolism with long-term high-dose use, necessitating monitoring in patients on complex medication regimens.

Intravenous iron therapy (ferric carboxymaltose) has been studied extensively in heart failure populations with established GDMT. The HEART-FID trial and related studies evaluated IV iron therapy in patients receiving standard heart failure medications, demonstrating that iron repletion can be safely integrated with angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, beta-blockers, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, and SGLT2 inhibitors [6]. However, baseline transferrin saturation levels substantially higher than those seen in other trials could have impacted outcomes, suggesting that patient selection criteria influence interaction profiles [2].

**Primary vs. Secondary Prevention Considerations**

In primary prevention contexts, metal supplementation must be evaluated against lifestyle interventions and risk factor modification rather than intensive pharmacotherapy. The ACC/AHA Guideline on the Primary Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease emphasizes dietary and lifestyle approaches before pharmacological intervention [7]. Metal supplementation in primary prevention should not displace proven interventions such as blood pressure control, lipid management, and smoking cessation.

In secondary prevention, the interaction calculus shifts. Post-MI and heart failure patients receive intensive GDMT, and any adjunctive therapy must demonstrate incremental benefit without compromising established treatment efficacy. The TACT2 negative results in post-MI diabetic patients suggest that chelation provides no incremental benefit over contemporary GDMT [24]. Conversely, IV iron in iron-deficient heart failure patients demonstrates incremental benefit on top of GDMT, supporting its role as an adjunctive therapy in this specific secondary prevention population.

**Mechanistic Considerations**

Understanding why interventions succeed or fail in the context of GDMT requires mechanistic analysis. Chelation therapy operates through removal of toxic metals (lead, cadmium) that promote oxidative stress and inflammation through binding to glycation end-products [8]. In TACT, this mechanism may have provided benefit in a population with less intensive GDMT. In TACT2, contemporary GDMT including SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 receptor agonists may have addressed overlapping pathophysiological pathways, diminishing chelation's incremental value.

Iron supplementation addresses a distinct deficiency state that impairs myocardial energetics and erythropoiesis. This mechanism remains relevant regardless of GDMT intensity, explaining why IV iron demonstrates benefit even in patients receiving optimal heart failure pharmacotherapy.

### Cost-Effectiveness and Resource Utilization Implications

The economic viability of routine metal screening and treatment in cardiology practice requires careful analysis of direct costs, resource utilization, and potential downstream savings from reduced cardiovascular events.

**Chelation Therapy Costs**

EDTA chelation therapy represents a substantial economic burden. The TACT2 protocol of 40 weekly infusions translates to approximately 10 months of treatment, with each infusion requiring clinical facility resources, nursing time, and medication costs. The cost of US hospital-based care in TACT2 was estimated by applying hospital-specific, revenue center level cost-to-charge ratios to empirical billing data collected during the study [10]. This approach, used successfully in numerous previous clinical trials, provides a framework for economic analysis but reveals the substantial resource intensity of chelation protocols.

Medicare reimbursement rates apply to CPT codes for infusion services, but chelation therapy for cardiovascular disease is not an FDA-approved indication, creating reimbursement uncertainty [10]. This coverage gap limits patient access and shifts costs to out-of-pocket payment, further reducing feasibility for widespread adoption.

Given the negative TACT2 results demonstrating no improvement in post-MI outcomes for diabetic patients, chelation therapy cannot be recommended for routine cardiovascular disease management [24]. The cost-effectiveness calculus is unfavorable when an intervention demonstrates no clinical benefit regardless of absolute cost. Chelation therapy should be limited to clinical trials or specific high-exposure cases where toxic metal burden is documented and contributing to clinical pathology.

**Metal Supplementation Costs**

Oral magnesium and zinc supplements offer dramatically lower cost profiles. VA research examining electronic health records of almost 20,000 Veterans newly diagnosed with heart failure found that over-the-counter magnesium supplements could be a low-cost intervention with potential clinical benefit [35]. The low acquisition cost, combined with minimal administration burden, creates a favorable cost-effectiveness profile even with modest effect sizes.

Zinc supplementation merits testing as a simple, low-cost intervention to mitigate cardiovascular risk factors, though future research should be conducted to bolster results before widespread recommendation [17]. The economic barrier to zinc supplementation is minimal, making it accessible across socioeconomic strata.

Intravenous iron therapy occupies an intermediate cost position. Ferric carboxymaltose and similar formulations require infusion infrastructure and medication costs substantially higher than oral supplements but lower than extended chelation protocols. The demonstrated reduction in heart failure hospitalization provides potential downstream cost savings that may offset upfront treatment costs, particularly in populations with high baseline hospitalization rates [29].

**Screening and Monitoring Costs**

Routine metal screening in cardiology practice requires economic justification. NHANES data demonstrate associations between cadmium levels and cardiovascular disease outcomes, with every doubling of urine cadmium associated with a 36% increase in coronary heart disease mortality in men [8]. However, population-wide screening for heavy metals lacks established cost-effectiveness thresholds.

For iron deficiency in heart failure, screening is economically justified given the therapeutic implications. Transferrin saturation and ferritin testing identifies patients who may benefit from IV iron therapy, with the IRONMAN trial demonstrating clinical benefit in this selected population [6]. The number needed to screen to identify one treatable patient is favorable in heart failure populations where iron deficiency prevalence exceeds 50%.

For magnesium and zinc, routine screening lacks similar justification in the absence of specific deficiency syndromes or high-risk exposure histories. The low cost and safety profile of oral supplementation may support empiric treatment in selected populations without mandatory screening, though this approach requires further evidence.

**Resource Utilization Implications**

Healthcare system capacity represents a critical constraint for metal ion modulation interventions. Chelation therapy's requirement for 40 weekly infusions would strain infusion center capacity if adopted widely, particularly given the negative TACT2 results that do not support routine use [24]. Resource allocation to ineffective interventions represents opportunity costs that displace proven therapies.

Oral supplementation imposes minimal resource utilization burden, making it scalable across healthcare systems. The VA research demonstrating magnesium supplementation benefits in nearly 20,000 Veterans illustrates the feasibility of population-level implementation without infrastructure expansion [35].

IV iron therapy requires moderate resource utilization, with infusion visits typically spaced weeks to months apart rather than weekly. This profile allows integration into existing heart failure management infrastructure without substantial capacity expansion.

**Economic Viability Summary**

The economic viability of metal ion modulation varies by intervention type and clinical context:

- **Chelation therapy**: Not economically viable for routine CVD management given negative TACT2 results and high resource utilization. Limited to clinical trials or documented toxic metal exposure cases.

- **Oral magnesium/zinc supplementation**: Economically viable for selected populations, particularly heart failure patients, given low cost and favorable safety profile. Evidence for cardiovascular outcome reduction requires further validation.

- **IV iron supplementation**: Economically viable for heart failure with iron deficiency, given demonstrated reduction in hospitalization and moderate resource utilization requirements.

The 2026 ACC/AHA dyslipidemia guidelines and related cardiovascular guidelines emphasize evidence-based interventions with favorable cost-effectiveness profiles [11]. Metal ion modulation interventions must meet these standards to achieve integration into routine cardiology practice. Current evidence supports IV iron for heart failure with iron deficiency as the most economically viable metal modulation strategy, while chelation therapy lacks sufficient evidence and cost-effectiveness for routine recommendation.

## Guideline Integration and Clinical Implementation Barriers

The translation of metal ion modulation from mechanistic hypothesis to clinical standard of care remains hindered by a significant disconnect between emerging trial data and established cardiovascular guidelines. While the 2026 ACC/AHA Dyslipidemia and Heart Failure guidelines represent the current gold standard for risk management, they do not currently endorse chelation therapy or trace metal supplementation as routine interventions. This section analyzes the alignment of current evidence with official recommendations, identifies the systemic barriers preventing adoption in conventional cardiology, and defines the specific patient phenotypes that may still warrant specialized consideration.

### Alignment with 2026 ACC/AHA Dyslipidemia and HF Guidelines

The 2026 ACC/AHA/AACVPR/ABC/ACPM/ADA/AGS/APhA/ASPC/NLA/PCNA Guideline on the Management of Dyslipidemia establishes a comprehensive framework for cardiovascular risk reduction, prioritizing statin therapy, PCSK9 inhibitors, and lifestyle modifications [URL]. Similarly, the 2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA Guideline for Management of Heart Failure focuses on guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) including beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, ARNI, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists [URL]. Despite the biological plausibility of metal toxicity contributing to atherosclerosis and myocardial dysfunction, neither guideline incorporates chelation therapy or targeted trace metal supplementation into their Class I or IIa recommendations for the general population.

This omission reflects a cautious interpretation of the available randomized controlled trial (RCT) evidence. The 2026 Dyslipidemia Guideline emphasizes primary prevention strategies for the general population and secondary prevention for high-risk cohorts, yet it does not list heavy metal burden as a modifiable risk factor requiring therapeutic intervention [URL]. The scientific statement on contaminant metals as cardiovascular risk factors acknowledges the association between lead, cadmium, and mercury with adverse outcomes, but stops short of recommending chelation as a treatment modality [URL]. This distinction is critical: while epidemiological data supports the link between metal exposure and disease, interventional data has not yet met the threshold for guideline endorsement.

The divergence between the 2026 guidelines and the chelation evidence base is most evident in the interpretation of the Trial to Assess Chelation Therapy 2 (TACT2). TACT2 was a large, multicenter, randomized, double-masked, placebo-controlled trial designed to assess the effect of edetate disodium (EDTA) chelation on cardiovascular outcomes in patients with a prior myocardial infarction (MI) and diabetes [URL]. The trial was powered to detect a 30% relative reduction in the primary composite endpoint of all-cause mortality, MI, stroke, coronary revascularization, or hospitalization for unstable angina [URL]. However, the results demonstrated that edetate disodium-based chelation did not lead to improvement in cardiovascular outcomes compared to placebo in this broad secondary prevention population [URL]. Consequently, the guideline committees have not incorporated chelation into the standard management algorithms for post-MI or heart failure patients, prioritizing therapies with established mortality benefits such as SGLT2 inhibitors and ARNI [URL].

Furthermore, the 2026 guidelines maintain a strict separation between primary prevention (general population) and secondary prevention (post-MI/HF) [URL]. TACT2 specifically targeted secondary prevention in a high-risk subgroup (post-MI and diabetes). The negative results in this specific cohort reinforce the guideline stance that chelation should not be recommended for routine secondary prevention. In contrast, primary prevention trials involving trace metal supplementation (e.g., selenium, zinc) have shown mixed or null results regarding cardiovascular mortality, further justifying the exclusion of these interventions from the 2026 Dyslipidemia and Heart Failure guidelines [URL]. The guidelines instead recommend managing metal exposure through public health measures and dietary guidance rather than pharmacological chelation [URL].

### Barriers to Adoption in Conventional Cardiology

The integration of metal ion modulation into conventional cardiology practice faces substantial barriers, including skepticism stemming from conflicting trial data, a lack of standardized testing protocols, and significant reimbursement challenges. The most prominent barrier is the clinical skepticism generated by the TACT2 trial results. While the original Trial to Assess Chelation Therapy (TACT) reported a modest reduction in adverse cardiovascular outcomes, TACT2 failed to replicate these findings in a larger, more rigorously controlled cohort [URL]. This discrepancy has led to a cautious interpretation of the evidence, with many cardiologists viewing chelation as an unproven therapy that lacks the robust efficacy data required for widespread adoption [URL].

The mechanistic explanation for the divergence between TACT and TACT2 results highlights a critical barrier to generalization. TACT included patients with prior MI but without the strict diabetes inclusion criteria of TACT2, and baseline lead levels varied significantly between the two trials [URL]. Evidence suggests that the positive signal in TACT may have been driven by a subgroup of patients with elevated baseline lead levels, whereas TACT2 enrolled a broader population where the average metal burden was lower [URL]. This lack of stratification in the initial TACT results created confusion regarding the specific mechanism of action—whether chelation works by removing toxic metals or through non-specific antioxidant effects [URL]. Without a clear mechanistic understanding that applies to the general population, clinicians remain hesitant to prescribe a therapy that carries risks of electrolyte disturbances and renal toxicity [URL].

Standardized testing for trace metals presents another significant implementation hurdle. Unlike lipid panels or HbA1c, which are universally standardized and reimbursed, testing for heavy metal burden (e.g., blood lead, urine cadmium) lacks uniform clinical protocols [URL]. The American Heart Association Scientific Statement on contaminant metals notes that while biomarkers exist, their clinical utility for guiding therapy remains undefined [URL]. Clinicians often lack access to specialized laboratories capable of measuring low-level toxic metals with the precision required for clinical decision-making. Furthermore, the interpretation of these results is complex; a "normal" blood lead level in the general population may still be toxic in the context of cardiovascular disease, yet there are no consensus thresholds for initiating chelation [URL]. This ambiguity leads to inconsistent patient selection and makes it difficult to justify the cost of testing to patients or payers.

Reimbursement challenges further impede the adoption of chelation and trace metal testing. In the absence of guideline endorsement, insurance providers typically classify chelation therapy as investigational or not medically necessary for cardiovascular disease [URL]. The TACT2 trial was funded by the National Institutes of Health (NIH), including NCCIH, NHLBI, and NIDDK, which allowed for the collection of extensive data without the burden of commercial reimbursement [URL]. However, in routine practice, the cost of 40 infusions of EDTA chelation, along with the necessary monitoring of renal function and electrolytes, is substantial [URL]. Without a specific Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) code or guideline-backed indication, most patients face out-of-pocket expenses that are prohibitive. This financial barrier effectively restricts access to chelation therapy to clinical trials or specialized centers that can absorb the costs, preventing its integration into standard care pathways [URL].

### Criteria for Patient Selection in Specialized Clinics

Despite the lack of broad guideline endorsement, specific patient phenotypes may still benefit from metal ion modulation in specialized clinical settings. The criteria for patient selection must be rigorous, focusing on secondary prevention cohorts with documented evidence of metal toxicity or refractory disease despite optimal medical therapy. The TACT2 trial provided a framework for inclusion criteria that can inform specialized practice, emphasizing the need for a prior myocardial infarction and diabetes, while excluding patients with severe renal impairment [URL]. Specifically, the trial excluded patients with serum creatinine >2.0 mg/dL, HbA1c >11.0%, or recent revascularization or heart failure admission within 6 months [URL]. These exclusion criteria highlight the importance of renal safety and disease stability in patient selection.

For specialized clinics considering chelation, the primary phenotype of interest is patients with high lead exposure and cardiovascular disease. Epidemiological data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) III indicates that blood lead levels are associated with increased mortality from cardiovascular disease and cancer [URL]. Patients with documented elevated blood lead levels (>5 µg/dL) and a history of MI or heart failure represent a subgroup where the mechanism of chelation (removal of toxic metal stores) is biologically plausible [URL]. In these cases, the intervention is not for general risk reduction but for targeted removal of a specific pathogenic driver. The TACT2 trial measured blood lead and urine cadmium levels, finding that chelation significantly reduced these levels, even though it did not improve the primary clinical endpoint in the overall population [URL]. This suggests that while chelation effectively reduces metal burden, the translation to clinical benefit is contingent on the baseline toxicity level [URL].

Refractory heart failure with trace metal deficiency represents another potential niche for intervention. Evidence suggests that hypomagnesemia is associated with increased mortality risk in patients with heart failure, although results have varied [URL]. In specialized clinics, patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) who exhibit documented magnesium deficiency despite oral supplementation may be candidates for intravenous magnesium therapy [URL]. Unlike chelation, which removes metals, supplementation aims to correct a deficiency that may impair myocardial function and arrhythmia risk. The HEART-FID trial and other studies on intravenous iron in heart failure have produced mixed results regarding mortality, but they underscore the need for precise phenotyping based on iron status and ferritin levels [URL]. Similarly, magnesium supplementation trials have shown improvements in exercise tolerance and quality of life, though not necessarily hard mortality endpoints [URL].

The criteria for patient selection in specialized clinics must also account for the distinction between primary and secondary prevention. Chelation should not be recommended for primary prevention in the general population due to the lack of evidence and potential risks [URL]. Instead, it should be limited to clinical trials or high-exposure cases where the benefit-risk ratio is favorable. For secondary prevention, the presence of diabetes and prior MI, as seen in TACT2, defines the high-risk cohort, but the absence of a positive trial outcome in this group suggests that chelation should not be routine [URL]. Specialized clinics should prioritize patients with confirmed heavy metal toxicity (e.g., lead, cadmium) documented by validated biomarkers, rather than relying on non-standardized hair mineral analysis or unvalidated testing [URL].

In summary, the implementation of metal ion modulation in cardiology requires a shift from broad application to targeted, phenotype-driven therapy. The 2026 ACC/AHA guidelines provide a safe framework that excludes chelation for routine use, protecting patients from unproven interventions. However, specialized clinics can leverage the mechanistic insights from TACT and TACT2 to identify patients with high metal burden or specific deficiencies who may derive benefit. This approach aligns with the principle of precision medicine, ensuring that metal modulation is reserved for those most likely to respond, rather than diluting the intervention across a heterogeneous population where it has failed to show efficacy.

## Strategic Research Roadmap and Unanswered Questions

The clinical viability of metal ion modulation in cardiovascular disease remains an evolving field characterized by promising mechanistic hypotheses but incomplete evidence bases. The divergent outcomes between TACT and TACT2 trials underscore the critical need for strategic research directions that address population heterogeneity, individual susceptibility, and long-term safety considerations. This section outlines three priority areas for future investigation: expanding chelation research to high-exposure regions beyond North America, implementing precision medicine approaches to metal homeostasis, and establishing robust long-term safety monitoring frameworks for chronic supplementation.

### Future Directions for Chelation in High-Exposure Regions

The TACT2 randomized clinical trial reported that edetate disodium (EDTA)-based chelation was not effective in reducing cardiovascular events in stable patients with coronary artery disease who have diabetes and a history of myocardial infarction, despite effectively reducing blood lead levels [21]. This negative result stands in contrast to the original TACT trial, which demonstrated unexpected benefits of chelation therapy with an 18% reduction in cardiovascular disease events among patients with prior myocardial infarction [28][21]. The mechanistic explanation for this discrepancy likely relates to baseline toxic metal burdens, which differ substantially between populations.

A critical limitation of existing chelation trials is their geographic concentration in North America, particularly the United States. Evidence indicates that anyone living in the United States during the period from 1945 to 1980 accumulated significantly more lead in their body compared to subsequent generations, due to leaded gasoline and industrial emissions [8]. However, this historical exposure profile does not generalize to populations in developing regions where ongoing environmental contamination from industrial activities, mining, and inadequate regulatory oversight continues to generate elevated toxic metal burdens.

Future chelation research should prioritize populations in Asia, Africa, and Latin America where baseline lead and cadmium exposure levels substantially exceed those in contemporary North American cohorts. The American Heart Association has identified contaminant metals as cardiovascular risk factors in scientific statements, emphasizing the need for research across diverse exposure contexts [36]. Cadmium exposure, in particular, has been systematically reviewed and linked to clinical cardiovascular disease, with mechanisms involving oxidative stress, endothelial dysfunction, and inflammatory pathways [22].

The distinction between primary prevention (general population) and secondary prevention (post-MI/HF) becomes especially relevant in high-exposure regions. In populations with elevated baseline toxic metal burdens, chelation therapy may demonstrate efficacy in primary prevention contexts where North American trials have failed. The mechanistic rationale rests on the hypothesis that toxic metal removal reduces cardiovascular risk proportionally to baseline burden—a relationship that cannot be adequately tested in populations with relatively low contemporary exposure levels.

Research priorities for high-exposure regions should include:

- **Baseline Metal Burden Characterization**: Systematic assessment of blood and bone lead, urinary cadmium, and other toxic metals in cardiovascular patients across diverse geographic regions to establish exposure-response relationships
- **Stratified Trial Design**: Randomized controlled trials that stratify participants by baseline metal burden rather than treating chelation as a uniform intervention
- **Mechanistic Biomarkers**: Integration of oxidative stress markers, inflammatory cytokines, and endothelial function assessments to elucidate pathways through which metal removal may confer cardiovascular benefit
- **Long-term Follow-up**: Extended observation periods to capture cardiovascular events in populations where metal accumulation occurs over decades

The TACT2 Research Group originally planned their trial to replicate TACT findings and establish that removal of toxic metal stores from the body is a plausible mechanistic explanation for the benefits of edetate disodium treatment [28][20]. The negative TACT2 results do not invalidate this mechanistic hypothesis but rather suggest that the study population lacked sufficient toxic metal burden to demonstrate clinical benefit. Future trials in high-exposure regions would provide a more rigorous test of the metal removal hypothesis.

### Precision Medicine Approaches to Metal Homeostasis

The failure of empirical supplementation strategies to consistently demonstrate cardiovascular benefit underscores the need for precision medicine approaches that account for individual variation in metal metabolism, genetic susceptibility, and baseline nutritional status. Current evidence supports biomarker-driven supplementation rather than uniform dosing across heterogeneous populations.

Genetic polymorphisms affecting metal transport and metabolism represent a critical frontier for personalized intervention strategies. The SLC30A8 gene, which encodes a zinc transporter involved in insulin secretion and glucose homeostasis, has been implicated in the pathogenesis and management of metabolic disorders that intersect with cardiovascular risk [9]. Zinc serves a regulatory role in many signaling pathways, including potentiation of leptin and insulin signaling, and has demonstrated protective effects in cellular studies [9]. Individuals with specific SLC30A8 variants may derive differential benefit from zinc supplementation, suggesting that genetic screening could identify responsive subgroups.

Biomarker-guided supplementation strategies should incorporate multiple assessment modalities:

- **Serum Mineral Levels**: Baseline and serial measurement of serum magnesium, zinc, iron, and other essential minerals to identify deficiency states warranting supplementation
- **Functional Biomarkers**: Assessment of magnesium status through intracellular measurements rather than serum alone, as hypomagnesemia affects approximately 7.6% of populations and may be underestimated by standard testing [30]
- **Inflammatory Markers**: C-reactive protein and other inflammatory indicators that may modify the relationship between metal status and cardiovascular outcomes
- **Renal Function**: Serum creatinine and estimated glomerular filtration rate to guide dosing and monitor for toxicity, particularly for minerals with narrow therapeutic windows

The distinction between primary and secondary prevention contexts is essential for precision medicine approaches. In secondary prevention populations (post-MI, heart failure), mineral deficiencies may result from disease processes, medication effects, or dietary restrictions, creating distinct supplementation needs compared to primary prevention contexts. Patients with chronic heart failure are frequently magnesium deficient, and trials have hypothesized that supplementation may improve exercise tolerance and clinical outcomes [27].

Zinc supplementation has been systematically reviewed for cardiovascular disease risk factors, with GRADE-assessed meta-analyses providing dose-response relationships that can inform precision dosing strategies [40][5]. However, these analyses highlight substantial heterogeneity in treatment effects, suggesting that population-level recommendations obscure important subgroup variations.

Iron supplementation presents particular challenges for precision medicine approaches. Intravenous iron trials in heart failure populations have defined iron deficiency using ferritin and transferrin saturation (TSAT) thresholds, with follow-up ranging from 12 weeks to 140 weeks [2]. The reduction in heart failure hospitalization events associated with IV iron demonstrates that biomarker-defined deficiency states can identify patients who benefit from supplementation, providing a model for other mineral interventions.

Research priorities for precision medicine approaches include:

- **Pharmacogenomic Studies**: Identification of genetic variants that predict response to mineral supplementation, particularly for zinc, magnesium, and iron
- **Biomarker Validation**: Rigorous validation of serum, intracellular, and functional biomarkers that accurately reflect tissue mineral status and predict clinical response
- **Adaptive Trial Designs**: Implementation of platform trials that allow for biomarker-stratified randomization and adaptive dosing based on interim analyses
- **Integration with Existing Risk Scores**: Development of metal homeostasis components for established cardiovascular risk prediction tools

The American Heart Association and other professional organizations have emphasized the need for scientific research to guide supplementation recommendations rather than empirical approaches [7]. Precision medicine frameworks align with this guidance by ensuring that interventions target individuals most likely to benefit while minimizing unnecessary supplementation in those with adequate baseline status.

### Long-Term Safety Monitoring for Chronic Supplementation

Knowledge gaps regarding the long-term safety of chronic high-dose mineral supplementation represent a critical barrier to clinical implementation. While short-term trials demonstrate efficacy for specific indications, decades-long use patterns common in cardiovascular prevention contexts lack robust safety data. This uncertainty is particularly relevant for minerals with narrow therapeutic windows and potential for cumulative toxicity.

Renal function monitoring emerges as a primary safety consideration for chronic supplementation. The TACT2 trial protocol included serum creatinine monitoring with exclusion criteria for serum creatinine greater than 2.0 mg/dL, reflecting concerns about chelation effects on renal function [25]. Data and Safety Monitoring Boards meet at least twice each year in major trials to review adverse events and safety signals [10]. However, trial monitoring protocols may not capture subtle renal function changes that accumulate over years of supplementation.

Cadmium toxicity illustrates the importance of long-term safety monitoring. Once ingested, cadmium is stored predominantly in kidney, liver, lung, pancreas, and central nervous system, with extremely slow excretion rates that amplify potential toxicity [8]. While cadmium supplementation is not a clinical intervention, this example demonstrates how minerals with slow excretion can accumulate to toxic levels over decades. Similar concerns apply to essential minerals when supplemented at high doses over extended periods.

Magnesium supplementation safety profiles require particular attention in cardiovascular populations. Magnesium plays a critical role in preventing arrhythmia, improving hemodynamics, and supporting cardiovascular function [30]. However, rigorously designed randomized controlled trials for nutritional supplements are often expensive, typically requiring large participant cohorts and extended follow-up periods to detect relatively small effect sizes [30]. This economic reality limits the availability of long-term safety data for commonly used supplements.

Zinc supplementation presents distinct long-term safety considerations. Zinc has been shown to regulate the activity of inducible nitric oxide synthase, reversing adverse effects of inflammation on the endothelium [9]. Zinc supplementation has also demonstrated reduction in atheroma formation and plasma and arterial wall lipid peroxidation in animal models [9]. However, chronic high-dose zinc supplementation can induce copper deficiency, creating secondary nutritional imbalances that may offset cardiovascular benefits.

Safety monitoring frameworks for chronic supplementation should incorporate:

- **Renal Function Surveillance**: Serial measurement of serum creatinine, estimated glomerular filtration rate, and urinary protein excretion at intervals appropriate to the mineral's excretion pathway
- **Hepatic Monitoring**: Periodic assessment of liver function tests for minerals with hepatic metabolism or storage
- **Trace Element Balance**: Monitoring of interacting minerals (e.g., copper levels during zinc supplementation, iron status during manganese supplementation)
- **Cardiovascular Endpoints**: Long-term follow-up for cardiovascular events rather than reliance on surrogate markers alone
- **Adverse Event Registries**: Post-marketing surveillance systems that capture real-world safety signals beyond controlled trial populations

The TACT2 trial collected clinical data, biological samples, outcome events, and adverse events from study screening through 30 days after the last infusion, with site teams entering all clinical data [10]. This intensive monitoring model provides a template for long-term safety surveillance but requires adaptation for chronic supplementation contexts where interventions continue for years rather than weeks.

Iron supplementation trials provide instructive examples of long-term safety monitoring. Among included trials, follow-up ranged from 12 weeks to 140 weeks, with pooled results reporting hazard ratios and confidence intervals for primary outcomes [2]. This extended follow-up duration should serve as a minimum standard for chronic supplementation safety studies.

Research priorities for long-term safety monitoring include:

- **Prospective Cohort Studies**: Longitudinal observational studies tracking supplement users over decades with standardized safety assessments
- **Electronic Health Record Mining**: Leveraging large healthcare databases to identify safety signals associated with chronic supplementation patterns
- **Mechanistic Toxicity Studies**: Preclinical and early clinical investigations of organ-specific toxicity mechanisms for high-dose minerals
- **Risk-Benefit Modeling**: Quantitative frameworks that integrate efficacy data with long-term safety profiles to guide clinical decision-making

The American Heart Association has urged more scientific research to establish evidence-based recommendations for nutritional interventions [7]. Long-term safety monitoring represents an essential component of this research agenda, ensuring that cardiovascular prevention strategies do not inadvertently introduce new health risks through chronic supplementation practices.

In summary, the strategic research roadmap for metal ion modulation in cardiovascular disease requires coordinated investment in three interconnected domains: geographic expansion of chelation trials to high-exposure populations, implementation of precision medicine frameworks for supplementation, and establishment of robust long-term safety monitoring systems. Addressing these priorities will enable evidence-based clinical recommendations that maximize benefit while minimizing risk across diverse patient populations.
