# Aesthetic Phototherapy and Energy-Based Devices for Pigmentation and Rejuvenation: Efficacy, Mechanisms, and Safety

## Executive Summary and Scope of Phototherapeutic Interventions

This report establishes an analytical framework for evaluating aesthetic phototherapy and energy-based devices (EBDs) targeting pigmentation disorders and skin rejuvenation. The scope encompasses laser, intense pulsed light (IPL), and light-emitting diode (LED) modalities, with explicit exclusion of hair removal applications. The analysis prioritizes clinical evidence published between 2020 and 2026, emphasizing systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and randomized controlled trials over vendor marketing materials or isolated case studies. This evidence hierarchy ensures the report reflects the current state of scientific understanding while filtering out biased commercial claims prevalent in the aesthetic device landscape.

### Defining Aesthetic Goals: Photoaging, Brightening, and Depigmentation

Establishing precise clinical terminology is fundamental to evaluating phototherapeutic interventions. The aesthetic industry frequently employs marketing language such as "whitening" or "brightening," which lacks scientific specificity and may carry culturally sensitive connotations. This report strictly adopts clinical terminology: **depigmentation** refers to the reduction of pathological melanin accumulation; **melanin reduction** describes the measurable decrease in pigment density; and **photoaging reversal** encompasses the improvement of texture, tone, and structural markers associated with chronic ultraviolet exposure [21].

Clinical endpoints for evaluating these interventions must be objective, reproducible, and validated. The **Melasma Area and Severity Index (MASI)** and its modified variant (**mMASI**) remain the gold-standard scoring systems for quantifying pigmentation disorders. The mMASI improves upon the original MASI by refining scoring criteria for clinical precision, particularly in assessing facial participation and pigmentation intensity across various facial areas [21]. Recent systematic reviews indicate that five studies utilized MASI/mMASI metrics, with all eight reviewed studies reporting improvements in these scores or clinical improvement of melasma with treatment [41].

Complementary to MASI scoring, the **VISIA Facial Skin Imaging System** (manufactured by Canfield Scientific) provides objective photographic documentation and quantitative analysis. VISIA captures images from three distinct angles (frontal, 45° left lateral, and 45° right lateral) and analyzes four key indicators: spots, UV spots, brown spots, and red areas [23]. Studies utilizing VISIA have demonstrated significant improvements in skin texture and pigmentation following phototherapeutic interventions, with symptomatic improvement assessed through modified MASI scoring in combination with VISIA skin detection systems [18][19].

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (**PIH**) represents a critical adverse outcome that must be distinguished from therapeutic depigmentation. PIH is a reported side effect in most clinical trials involving energy-based devices, with development correlated to the density of microthermal zones and heat generated during treatment [25]. The risk of PIH is particularly elevated in patients with Fitzpatrick skin types III–V, where safety analyses reveal significantly higher rates of adverse events in combination therapy groups [21]. This distinction between intended melanin reduction and unintended PIH is essential for accurate efficacy assessment and patient counseling.

### Landscape of Energy-Based Devices: Laser, IPL, and LED

The aesthetic phototherapy landscape comprises three primary modalities, each with distinct mechanisms, market prevalence, and efficacy boundaries. Understanding the fundamental differences between these technologies is critical for appropriate modality selection and safety profiling.

**Laser Systems** operate on the principle of selective photothermolysis, delivering monochromatic, coherent light at specific wavelengths targeting chromophores such as melanin, hemoglobin, or water. Traditional nanosecond Q-switched lasers (e.g., Nd:YAG 1064 nm, Alexandrite 755 nm) generate **photothermal** effects, where absorbed light energy converts to heat, destroying pigment-containing cells [25]. In contrast, **picosecond laser systems** deliver energy in trillionth-of-a-second pulses, creating **photomechanical** effects through laser-induced optical breakdown (LIOB). This mechanism generates elevated peak temperatures with reduced risk of thermal injury to surrounding tissue, offering a superior safety profile for darker skin types and melasma patients [45][36]. The U.S. FDA granted 510(k) clearance for the first picosecond laser for aesthetic use in 2012 (755nm Alexandrite system, PicoSure®, Cynosure Inc.), demonstrating faster and more complete pigment clearance compared to nanosecond systems [49].

**Intense Pulsed Light (IPL)** devices utilize broad-spectrum, non-coherent light filtered to target specific chromophores. IPL has demonstrated efficacy for pigment lesions, with studies showing high clearance rates for solar lentigines and freckles [17][49]. However, IPL operates primarily through photothermal mechanisms, generating heat that can trigger rebound pigmentation in susceptible patients. Evidence indicates that approximately 50% of patients will experience recurrence to some degree within three to six months of laser- and light-based procedures, with IPL carrying particular risk for melasma patients [25].

**LED Therapy** represents the third modality, utilizing non-thermal, low-level light for photobiomodulation. While LED devices have gained significant market presence in home-use applications, with some consumer devices featuring LED arrays exceeding 7,000 diodes for uniform irradiation [12], the evidence for LED efficacy in hyperpigmentation specifically remains thin compared to laser and IPL modalities. LED therapy demonstrates clearer benefits for inflammation reduction and wound healing, but its role in direct melanin reduction is limited and should not be overstated in clinical recommendations [4].

**Medical-Grade versus Home-Use Efficacy Boundaries** represent a critical distinction in the device landscape. Medical-grade devices, such as FDA-cleared picosecond lasers, operate at energy levels and wavelengths requiring professional supervision and appropriate device regulation [37]. Home-use devices, including consumer LED panels and low-energy IPL systems, are subject to different regulatory standards with significantly reduced power outputs. The first IPL dedicated solely for hair removal was researched and published on its safety and efficacy following single and long-term treatments, establishing legitimacy for home-use light-based devices in specific applications [24]. However, the efficacy boundary between medical-grade and home-use devices for pigmentation treatment remains substantial, with professional devices delivering clinically meaningful results that home devices cannot replicate.

**Safety Warning: IPL Use in Melasma Patients**. A critical ambiguity exists in the literature regarding IPL suitability for melasma treatment. Evidence indicates that IPL therapy carries significant risk of rebound pigmentation and post-procedure PIH in melasma patients, particularly those with Fitzpatrick skin types III–V [21]. One study found that one patient out of 26 receiving conventional IPL experienced adverse effects, with PIH identified as a serious concern [44]. Patients should be counseled on the likelihood of recurrence, PIH, and rebound hyperpigmentation before undergoing IPL treatment for melasma. Active melasma phases, characterized by spot enlargement, darkening, or redness in the last three months, represent exclusion criteria for laser treatments due to increased likelihood of adverse effects such as increased pigmentation [10].

### Evidence Quality and Recency Filter (2020-2026)

This report applies a rigorous evidence quality filter prioritizing recent systematic reviews and meta-analyses published between 2020 and 2026. This recency requirement ensures the analysis reflects current scientific consensus while excluding outdated protocols and vendor-heavy marketing data that may not represent contemporary device capabilities or safety profiles.

**Search Strategy and Databases**. Evidence synthesis followed the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) 2020 recommendations, with systematic searches performed across PubMed, NCBI, Google Scholar, ScienceDirect, Wiley, Embase, and Cochrane Library [21]. Additional searches utilized the Cochrane Library, PubMed/MEDLINE, and Evidence-based medicine databases with PICO(s) search strategies for population, intervention, comparison, and outcomes [44]. This multi-database approach minimizes publication bias and ensures comprehensive coverage of peer-reviewed clinical evidence.

**Evidence Hierarchy**. The report prioritizes evidence in the following order: (1) systematic reviews and meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials (RCTs), (2) individual RCTs with adequate sample sizes and follow-up periods, (3) prospective cohort studies with objective outcome measures, and (4) retrospective studies or case series only when higher-level evidence is unavailable. Recent meta-analyses have identified 10 RCTs involving 1,163 participants with Fitzpatrick type III–V skin, providing robust comparative data on laser efficacy in Asian populations [34]. Forest plot analyses from these studies report standardized mean difference (SMD) values with 95% confidence intervals, enabling quantitative synthesis of treatment effects [34].

**Quality Assessment Concerns**. Despite the availability of recent evidence, quality concerns persist in the phototherapy literature. Systematic reviews of IPL treatment for rosacea concluded that although most studies showed efficacy, the poor quality of the studies was of concern [7]. This finding underscores the importance of applying strict inclusion criteria and quality assessment tools, such as the Cochrane Risk of Bias Tool, when evaluating individual studies. Studies utilizing MASI/mMASI scoring, dermoscopy, and VISIA imaging provide more reliable outcome data than those relying solely on investigator global assessment or patient satisfaction surveys [41].

**Vendor Data Exclusion**. Marketing materials from device manufacturers, while informative for technical specifications, are explicitly excluded from efficacy claims in this report. Vendor-heavy data often lacks independent verification, adequate control groups, or long-term follow-up necessary for clinical decision-making. Inclusion in National Library of Medicine (NLM) databases does not imply endorsement of contents by NLM or the National Institutes of Health, reinforcing the need for independent critical appraisal of all sources [19]. Studies funded by device manufacturers require additional scrutiny for potential conflicts of interest, with preference given to independently funded research or studies with transparent funding disclosures.

**Temporal Relevance**. The 2020-2026 filter captures significant technological advances, particularly in picosecond laser systems and combination therapies. Earlier studies (pre-2020) may not reflect current device capabilities, safety protocols, or understanding of photomechanical versus photothermal mechanisms. For example, the implementation of picosecond laser therapy based on scientific evidence rather than physician experience alone is expected to improve safety and effectiveness of treatment, representing an evolution in clinical practice that older literature cannot capture [46]. This temporal boundary ensures the report provides actionable guidance aligned with contemporary clinical standards and regulatory frameworks.

## Technological Modalities and Mechanisms of Action

The landscape of aesthetic phototherapy for pigmentation and rejuvenation encompasses diverse energy-based devices that operate through fundamentally distinct physical mechanisms. Understanding these technological modalities requires careful differentiation between photothermal and photomechanical processes, wavelength-specific tissue interactions, and the varying evidence bases supporting coherent laser systems versus broad-spectrum light sources. This section provides deep technical analysis of how these devices achieve pigment reduction and skin rejuvenation, with particular attention to why picosecond lasers demonstrate reduced collateral damage compared to traditional nanosecond systems based on evidence from porcine and human models.

### Photothermal vs. Photomechanical Disruption

The evolution from nanosecond to picosecond laser technology represents a paradigm shift in how energy-based devices interact with melanin and other cutaneous chromophores. Traditional Q-switched nanosecond lasers operate primarily through selective photothermolysis, a mechanism whereby light energy is absorbed by target chromophores and converted to heat, causing thermal destruction of pigment-containing structures [10]. This photothermal mechanism, while effective for pigment clearance, inherently generates heat diffusion into surrounding tissue, creating zones of collateral thermal damage that can trigger inflammatory cascades and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, particularly in patients with Fitzpatrick skin types IV through VI [21].

Picosecond lasers, by contrast, operate through photomechanical or photoacoustic mechanisms. The ultra-short pulse duration in the picosecond domain (10^-12 seconds) generates peak power densities sufficient to induce laser-induced optical breakdown (LIOB), creating acoustic shockwaves that mechanically fragment pigment particles rather than thermally destroying them [39]. This fundamental difference in energy delivery means that picosecond lasers can achieve pigment clearance with substantially less heat diffusion into surrounding tissue, reducing the risk of thermal injury to melanocytes and keratinocytes adjacent to the treatment zone [45].

Evidence from porcine models, which closely resemble human skin in terms of epidermal-dermal architecture and melanin distribution, provides mechanistic support for the superior safety profile of picosecond technology. Researchers utilized porcine models to demonstrate that picosecond lasers lighten pigmentation by breaking pigment into tiny fragments that are more readily cleared by macrophages and lymphatic drainage, while minimizing the thermal insult that triggers melanocyte hyperactivity [32]. This mechanistic evidence supports safer parameter selection and more rational clinical application, as the photomechanical effect achieves pigment fragmentation at lower cumulative thermal loads compared to nanosecond systems [46].

Clinical studies corroborate these mechanistic findings. Large-spot, low-fluence Q-switched Nd:YAG lasers, extensively researched for decades, have proven effective in melasma treatment through subcellular selective photothermolysis, but carry documented risks of thermal damage and mottled hypopigmentation with reported rates as high as 14% [50][22]. In contrast, picosecond-domain pulses can achieve equal or higher clearance of pigment in fewer treatment sessions than nanosecond Q-switched lasers, with a lower risk of adverse effects [49]. The reduced thermal burden translates to lower rates of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, a critical consideration for patients with darker skin phototypes who are inherently at higher risk for pigmentary complications following energy-based procedures [6].

The photomechanical mechanism also enables additional therapeutic effects beyond pigment fragmentation. Picosecond lasers with diffractive lens arrays or holographic beam splitters can induce microscopic tissue damage that stimulates collagen and elastin production through wound healing cascades, offering rejuvenation benefits without the extended downtime associated with ablative fractional lasers [36]. Studies have documented elevated levels of heat shock proteins, growth factors, and platelet-activating factors in picosecond laser-treated areas compared to control groups, suggesting that the photomechanical effect triggers beneficial biological responses while avoiding the extensive thermal injury of traditional fractional ablative systems [36].

### Wavelength Specificity and Tissue Penetration

Wavelength selection represents a critical determinant of treatment efficacy and safety in laser-based pigment therapy, as different wavelengths exhibit distinct absorption characteristics for melanin, oxyhemoglobin, and water, and penetrate tissue to varying depths. The four primary wavelengths employed in aesthetic phototherapy for pigmentation—532nm, 755nm, 1064nm, and the emerging 675nm—each target specific chromophores at different tissue depths, requiring careful matching of wavelength to the clinical presentation and depth of pigment deposition.

The 532nm wavelength, typically delivered via frequency-doubled Nd:YAG or KTP lasers, demonstrates strong absorption by melanin and oxyhemoglobin, making it highly effective for superficial epidermal pigmented lesions such as solar lentigines and epidermal melasma [45]. This preferential melanin absorption enables precise targeting of superficial pigmented lesions while minimizing collateral thermal damage to deeper structures [45]. However, the shallow penetration depth of 532nm light limits its utility for dermal or mixed-type pigmentation, and the high melanin absorption at this wavelength increases the risk of epidermal injury and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation in patients with Fitzpatrick skin types IV through VI [21].

The 755nm wavelength, delivered via alexandrite lasers, offers intermediate penetration depth with strong melanin absorption, making it suitable for treating both epidermal and superficial dermal pigment. Research on Asian skin demonstrates that using longer wavelengths like 755nm significantly reduces the risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation compared to traditional heat-based lasers operating at shorter wavelengths [6]. Picosecond alexandrite lasers at 755nm have shown particular promise for acquired dermal melanocytosis and nevus of Ota-like macules, where deeper pigment deposition requires wavelengths that can penetrate beyond the epidermal-dermal junction while maintaining sufficient melanin absorption for effective fragmentation [49].

The 1064nm wavelength, delivered via Nd:YAG lasers in both nanosecond and picosecond domains, provides the deepest tissue penetration among commonly used pigment laser wavelengths. This deep penetration makes 1064nm the wavelength of choice for dermal melasma, nevus of Ota, and other conditions where pigment resides primarily in the dermis [39]. The fractional PS Nd:YAG laser at 1064nm has demonstrated long-term efficacy and safety for treating pigmentary disorders in individuals with darker skin phototypes (IV–V), with studies performing multiple treatment sessions at monthly intervals showing sustained improvement [36]. The lower melanin absorption at 1064nm compared to 532nm or 755nm reduces epidermal competition for light energy, allowing more photons to reach deeper dermal pigment while sparing the epidermis from excessive thermal load [6].

The emerging 675nm wavelength represents a novel approach specifically designed for photobiomodulation in hyperpigmentation disorders. This wavelength has been investigated for melasma treatment in Fitzpatrick skin types III through V, with research focusing on its effects on tyrosinase activity and melanin production [22]. Unlike the high-energy pigment-fragmenting mechanisms of 532nm, 755nm, and 1064nm lasers, 675nm operates at lower energy densities intended to modulate melanocyte biology rather than mechanically destroy pigment.

Accurate assessment of pigment depth is essential for wavelength selection. Wood's lamp illumination remains a standard clinical tool for distinguishing epidermal, dermal, mixed, and indeterminate melasma subtypes, with epidermal pigment appearing more prominent under Wood's light while dermal pigment shows less enhancement [25][3]. Histologically, epidermal hyperpigmentation typically presents with melanin in the basal and suprabasal epidermis, while dermal hyperpigmentation is characterized by melanophages in the papillary dermis [41]. For clinical practice, the type of melasma should guide therapeutic decisions: epidermal melasma may respond better to superficial fractional lasers combined with topical depigmenting agents, while dermal or mixed-type melasma may require gentler, repeated sessions with longer wavelengths that can safely reach deeper pigment without causing epidermal injury [21].

Recent advances include the development of wavelength-specific irradiation indices for picosecond lasers, which enable more scientific, evidence-based parameter selection rather than relying solely on physician experience [46]. This innovation is expected to improve both the safety and effectiveness of picosecond laser treatment by providing quantitative guidance for matching wavelength, fluence, and spot size to specific pigment depths and skin types.

### IPL and LED: Broad Spectrum and Low-Energy Mechanisms

Intense Pulsed Light (IPL) and Light Emitting Diode (LED) technologies represent distinct categories of energy-based devices that operate through mechanisms fundamentally different from coherent laser systems. IPL utilizes non-coherent, broad-spectrum light typically ranging from 500nm to 1200nm, with cut-off filters employed to target specific chromophores. This broad-spectrum approach enables simultaneous treatment of multiple concerns, including pigmentation, vascular irregularities, and textural changes, within a single session [42]. IPL targets melanosomes in melanocytes, keratinocytes, and melanophages through photothermal mechanisms, with treatment parameters such as energy density, pulse duration, spot size, and pulse emission frequency requiring individual adjustment according to phototype and severity [17][39].

However, IPL carries significant limitations and safety concerns, particularly for patients with melasma. The broad-spectrum nature of IPL means that energy is distributed across multiple wavelengths rather than being concentrated at a single melanin-absorbing wavelength, potentially requiring higher cumulative energy to achieve pigment clearance and increasing the risk of non-specific thermal injury [39]. Clinical studies have documented cases of post-procedure rebound pigmentation following IPL treatment for melasma, with one study noting that one patient out of 26 receiving conventional IPL experienced adverse pigmentary changes [44]. The photothermal mechanism of IPL, combined with its non-selective energy delivery, creates conditions favorable for triggering melanocyte hyperactivity and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, particularly in patients with active or unstable melasma [20].

**Safety Warning: IPL Use in Melasma Patients**

Clinicians must exercise extreme caution when considering IPL for melasma treatment. Evidence indicates that IPL is not suitable for dermal pigmentation as a standalone therapy, and its use in melasma patients carries documented risks of rebound pigmentation and disease exacerbation [39]. Active melasma phases, characterized by spot enlargement, darkening, or redness in the preceding three months, represent a particular contraindication for IPL and other laser treatments, as this phase is less responsive to energy-based interventions and more likely to lead to adverse effects such as increased pigmentation or post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation [10]. While some studies report favorable safety profiles for IPL in carefully selected melasma patients, the risk-benefit ratio must be carefully weighed, and patients should be counseled regarding the potential for rebound pigmentation and the need for strict photoprotection following treatment [22]. Combination approaches, such as IPL with microneedling and topical depigmenting agents, have been investigated to improve outcomes while potentially reducing IPL-related adverse events, but monotherapy with IPL for melasma remains controversial [18].

LED technology operates through photobiomodulation mechanisms at substantially lower energy densities than laser or IPL systems. LED devices emit narrow-bandwidth light at specific wavelengths, commonly in the red (630-670nm) and near-infrared (800-850nm) ranges, intended to stimulate cellular metabolism and modulate inflammatory pathways without causing thermal injury [51]. While LED therapy offers results supported by clinical evidence for improving skin texture, reducing wrinkles, and managing acne, the evidence base for LED in depigmentation and hyperpigmentation treatment remains notably thin compared to laser and IPL modalities [51].

The limitations of LED therapy for hyperpigmentation specifically must be clearly acknowledged. Unlike laser systems that directly fragment or thermally destroy pigment, LED operates through indirect biological modulation, potentially affecting tyrosinase activity, melanosome transfer, or inflammatory mediators that influence melanogenesis [22]. However, robust clinical trials demonstrating significant melanin reduction or brightening effects from LED monotherapy for melasma or other hyperpigmentation disorders are lacking in the peer-reviewed literature. The 675nm wavelength has been investigated for photobiomodulation in hyperpigmentation, with research examining its effects on tyrosinase and melanin production, but this represents an emerging area with limited high-quality evidence compared to established laser modalities [22].

Procedural advances combining low-fluence picosecond lasers with other modalities, such as fractional CO2 laser-assisted drug delivery or exosome-augmented microneedling, offer enhanced pigment clearance with improved safety profiles compared to traditional IPL or high-fluence laser approaches [20]. Nevertheless, treatment durability remains limited across all modalities, and the choice between IPL, LED, and laser systems should be guided by the specific pigmentary condition, skin phototype, depth of pigment deposition, and patient risk tolerance for potential adverse events. For patients with melasma, particularly those with Fitzpatrick skin types IV through VI, the photomechanical mechanisms of picosecond lasers at appropriate wavelengths (755nm or 1064nm) generally offer superior safety profiles compared to the photothermal mechanisms of IPL, while LED therapy may serve as an adjunctive modality with realistic expectations regarding its limited depigmentation efficacy [6][21].

## Clinical Efficacy in Melasma Management

Melasma represents one of the most challenging hyperpigmentation disorders in aesthetic dermatology, characterized by its chronic, relapsing nature and predilection for individuals with darker skin types (Fitzpatrick III–VI) [20]. The therapeutic landscape has evolved significantly with the introduction of energy-based devices, yet clinical efficacy remains variable across modalities. This section examines the comparative performance of laser therapies, the safety concerns surrounding intense pulsed light (IPL), and emerging wavelength-specific innovations, with particular attention to Melasma Area and Severity Index (MASI) score improvements and recurrence rates documented in randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses from 2020 to 2026.

### Laser Therapy Efficacy: Picosecond vs. Q-Switched

The comparative efficacy between picosecond and traditional Q-switched laser platforms has become a central question in melasma management, with recent trials providing granular data on MASI score reductions and safety profiles. The fundamental mechanistic distinction lies in pulse duration: picosecond lasers operate through photomechanical (photoacoustic) destruction of melanin granules, whereas Q-switched Nd:YAG lasers rely primarily on photothermal mechanisms via subcellular selective photothermolysis [10][39]. This distinction carries significant implications for safety in pigmented skin, as the heightened photomechanical effect and reduced heat diffusion inherent to picosecond technology theoretically minimizes collateral thermal injury.

Recent randomized evaluator-blinded trials directly comparing Picosecond Alexandrite Laser (PSAL) against traditional combined Q-switched and long-pulse Nd:YAG lasers (QLNYL) have yielded nuanced efficacy data. In Fitzpatrick Skin Types III–IV, the traditional QLNYL modality demonstrated superior efficacy in MASI reduction, though both platforms achieved clinically meaningful improvements [10]. Recurrence rates at 24-week follow-up were reported at 10.5% for PSAL and 0% for QLNYL, with no statistically significant difference between groups, suggesting comparable durability despite mechanistic differences [10].

Historical data from Q-switched Nd:YAG laser studies provide important context for interpreting contemporary picosecond trial results. Papagerogiu et al. documented a 61.3% mean decrease in MASI score following treatment with 1064 nm Q-switched Nd:YAG laser administered over 9 weekly sessions in 50 patients [25]. However, this study also highlighted the high melasma recurrence rate despite low-fluence Q-switch treatment, a finding that has been corroborated across multiple investigations. Meta-analytic data spanning 2010 to 2024 confirms that laser therapy produces statistically significant MASI reductions, with pooled effect sizes demonstrating p < 0.00001 and 95% confidence intervals of −0.60 to −1.22 across 12 randomized controlled trials [19].

Picosecond laser platforms operating at 755 nm (Alexandrite) and 1064 nm (Nd:YAG) have shown variable performance in head-to-head comparisons. A meta-analysis of six randomized controlled trials on picosecond lasers for melasma found that the 1064 nm picosecond laser significantly reduced MASI scores with minimal side effects, whereas the 755 nm picosecond laser did not outperform topical agents and was associated with higher post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) rates [39]. This wavelength-dependent efficacy profile underscores the importance of selecting appropriate parameters for specific patient populations.

Low-fluence Q-switched Nd:YAG laser protocols, often termed "laser toning," have been extensively researched and remain a benchmark for comparison. These protocols utilize large-spot, low-fluence settings to achieve subcellular melanin fragmentation while minimizing epidermal injury [10]. However, the photothermal mechanism inherently carries greater risk of heat accumulation in darker skin types, which has driven interest in picosecond alternatives. Studies comparing fractional erbium:YAG laser-assisted delivery combined with oral tranexamic acid against picosecond approaches have shown substantial modified MASI (mMASI) score decreases in both groups, with combination therapy groups demonstrating 64.7% improvement versus 41.8% in monotherapy arms [19].

Safety profiles between the two platforms reveal important distinctions. While picosecond lasers are marketed as having superior safety due to reduced thermal load, adverse event rates remain comparable in rigorous trials. Bonan et al. found similar incidence of adverse events on the face (2% vs. 2%, excluding erythema) between 755 nm picosecond and alternative laser approaches [17]. Pain scores, however, favor certain picosecond delivery modes, with MoveoPL mode of the 755 nm alexandrite laser reporting lower pain (1.8 on a 0–5 scale) compared to single-pass modes (3.1 on a 0–5 scale) [17].

The clinical implication of these findings is that neither platform offers unequivocal superiority across all endpoints. Q-switched Nd:YAG lasers maintain an evidence base spanning decades with well-characterized efficacy, while picosecond lasers offer theoretical safety advantages that have not consistently translated to statistically significant differences in recurrence or adverse events in head-to-head trials. Treatment selection should be individualized based on skin type, melasma subtype (epidermal vs. dermal), and patient tolerance for maintenance therapy, as success rates vary and recurrence remains high upon laser discontinuation across both modalities [39].

### The IPL Contraindication and Rebound Pigmentation Risk

Intense pulsed light (IPL) therapy occupies a contentious position in melasma management, with evidence supporting both its efficacy in select populations and significant contraindication warnings for patients at high risk of rebound pigmentation. IPL operates through selective photothermal effects, targeting melanin and vascular chromophores across a broad spectrum of wavelengths rather than the monochromatic output of laser systems [18]. This broadband approach, while advantageous for treating mixed photodamage, introduces greater unpredictability in melanin-selective absorption, particularly in darker skin types where competing chromophores increase the risk of non-selective thermal injury.

The risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) following IPL treatment in melasma patients represents the primary safety concern driving contraindication recommendations. PIH is a common complication of inflammatory skin diseases and energy-based procedures, particularly in higher Fitzpatrick phototypes (IV–VI) [39]. Evidence indicates that approximately 50% of patients will experience recurrence to some degree within three to six months of any laser- or light-based procedure, regardless of the specific modality employed [25]. This high recurrence rate, coupled with the risk of rebound hyperpigmentation exceeding baseline severity, necessitates careful patient selection and counseling.

Clinical trials examining IPL monotherapy in melasma have documented variable safety outcomes. In one study, one patient out of 26 receiving conventional IPL experienced PIH, representing a 3.8% adverse event rate [44]. While this rate appears modest, the clinical significance of PIH in melasma patients is disproportionate, as any inflammatory insult can trigger melanocyte hyperactivity and worsen the underlying condition. The risk of complications, particularly PIH in darker skin types, remains a significant barrier to IPL adoption in this population [20].

Recent randomized clinical trials have explored combination approaches to mitigate IPL-related risks. A single-blind randomized clinical trial conducted from March 2024 to October 2024 assessed IPL combined with microneedling and tranexamic acid versus IPL monotherapy in 30 patients [18][23]. The rationale for combination therapy acknowledges that melasma pathogenesis involves multifactorial mechanisms, and comprehensive strategies achieve superior outcomes compared to monotherapy [18]. However, even with adjunctive measures, the fundamental photothermal mechanism of IPL remains a liability in patients with active, unstable melasma or those with Fitzpatrick skin types IV and above.

The contraindication status of IPL in melasma is not absolute but rather risk-stratified. Patients with Fitzpatrick skin types II–III and stable, epidermal-predominant melasma may tolerate IPL with appropriate fluence settings and cooling protocols. However, the literature consistently warns against IPL use in patients with dermal melasma components, active inflammation, or history of PIH following previous energy-based treatments [25]. The photothermal mechanism inherently generates heat in the epidermis and dermis, which can stimulate melanocytes via inflammatory cytokine release, paradoxically worsening pigmentation in susceptible individuals.

Rebound pigmentation following IPL represents a distinct clinical entity from simple recurrence, characterized by hyperpigmentation that exceeds pretreatment baseline severity. This phenomenon is attributed to aggressive fluence settings, inadequate cooling, or treatment of patients with subclinical inflammation. Patient education and expectation management remain critical, as melasma often requires long-term maintenance, and the risk of rebound hyperpigmentation persists even with optimal technique [49]. Clinicians should counsel patients that IPL may provide temporary improvement but carries meaningful risk of exacerbation, particularly when used as monotherapy without concurrent topical depigmentation agents.

Comparative safety data positions IPL at higher risk than picosecond laser platforms for PIH in melasma patients. While picosecond lasers utilize photomechanical mechanisms with reduced thermal diffusion, IPL's broadband photothermal approach lacks this protective feature [39]. In meta-analyses of adverse events across nonablative laser and energy-based therapies for skin phototypes IV to VI, the incidence of PIH is not insignificant, with certain modalities demonstrating higher rates than others [29]. IPL falls into the higher-risk category when used in melasma, particularly without careful parameter selection and patient stratification.

The clinical recommendation emerging from current evidence is that IPL should be approached with caution in melasma management, reserved for carefully selected patients with lighter skin types and epidermal-predominant disease, and ideally combined with topical maintenance therapy and strict photoprotection. For patients with Fitzpatrick skin types IV and above, or those with dermal melasma components, alternative modalities with more favorable safety profiles should be prioritized.

### Novel Wavelengths: 675-nm Laser Performance in Darker Skin

The 675-nm picosecond laser represents an emerging wavelength specifically positioned to address the unmet need for safe and effective melasma treatment in darker skin types (Fitzpatrick III–V). This wavelength occupies a strategic position in the melanin absorption spectrum, offering sufficient chromophore targeting while minimizing competition from hemoglobin and water absorption that complicates shorter wavelength platforms. Recent prospective studies have begun to characterize the efficacy and safety profile of 675-nm laser monotherapy, providing early evidence for its role in treating populations historically excluded from aggressive laser interventions.

Nisticò et al. reported prospective data on 675-nm laser treatment demonstrating a 34% reduction in baseline Melasma Severity Index (MSI) score, from 26.4 ± 19.2 to 17.3 ± 15.0, at 3 months following three monthly treatment sessions [22]. This magnitude of improvement, achieved with monotherapy in a prospective design, compares favorably to historical controls using traditional Q-switched platforms in similar populations. The study included patients across Fitzpatrick skin types III and IV, with clinical photographs documenting visible improvement in both cheek and upper lip melasma distributions [22].

The safety profile of 675-nm laser treatment in darker skin types aligns with the theoretical advantages of picosecond technology. The photomechanical mechanism minimizes heat diffusion to surrounding tissue, reducing the risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation that has limited the use of traditional photothermal lasers in Fitzpatrick IV–V skin [39]. Evidence from related picosecond platforms in Fitzpatrick IV–VI skin types supports this safety proposition, with studies of perfluorodecalin-infused patches and picosecond Q-switched Nd:YAG laser demonstrating acceptable adverse event rates in darker skin [49].

Comparative data across picosecond wavelengths suggests that 675-nm may offer advantages over both 532-nm and 755-nm platforms for melasma in pigmented skin. The 532-nm wavelength carries higher epidermal melanin absorption, increasing PIH risk in darker skin types, while 755-nm alexandrite has shown variable efficacy in meta-analyses, with some studies failing to demonstrate superiority over topical agents [39]. The 675-nm wavelength appears to balance chromophore selectivity with penetration depth, targeting dermal melanin deposits that contribute to recalcitrant melasma while sparing epidermal melanocytes from excessive thermal insult.

Prospective studies on 675-nm laser have included diverse patient populations representative of real-world melasma demographics. Case documentation includes a 36-year-old Thai woman (Fitzpatrick skin type III) and a 50-year-old Thai woman (Fitzpatrick skin type IV), both demonstrating clinical improvement at 6 months following three treatment sessions [22]. These case examples reflect the typical melasma patient profile: women of reproductive age with darker skin types seeking treatment for bilateral facial hyperpigmentation. The inclusion of Fitzpatrick type IV patients is particularly notable, as this population has been historically underrepresented in laser melasma trials due to safety concerns.

The outcomes observed with 675-nm laser treatment contribute to the growing body of evidence that lasers can improve melasma as monotherapy, though combination approaches remain the gold standard for sustained remission [22]. A meta-analysis on laser treatments for melasma found that decreased MASI scores were consistently observed across laser interventions, though the magnitude and durability of response varied by platform and patient characteristics [22]. The 675-nm laser's performance in early prospective studies positions it as a promising option for patients who have failed topical therapy or who seek faster clinical improvement than topicals alone can provide.

Limitations of the current 675-nm laser evidence base include relatively small sample sizes, short follow-up periods, and lack of head-to-head comparisons with established platforms. The 34% MSI reduction reported by Nisticò et al. requires validation in larger randomized controlled trials with standardized MASI scoring and longer-term recurrence monitoring [22]. Additionally, the optimal treatment interval, number of sessions, and fluence parameters for 675-nm laser in different Fitzpatrick skin types remain to be systematically defined.

Despite these limitations, the 675-nm laser represents a meaningful innovation in melasma management for darker skin types. Its safety profile, grounded in photomechanical rather than photothermal mechanisms, addresses the primary barrier to laser treatment in Fitzpatrick IV–V patients. As additional prospective and comparative data emerge, the 675-nm platform may establish itself as a first-line laser option for melasma in populations previously considered high-risk for energy-based interventions. Clinicians treating patients with Fitzpatrick skin types III–V should consider 675-nm laser as part of a comprehensive treatment algorithm that includes topical maintenance, photoprotection, and realistic counseling regarding recurrence risk and the chronic nature of melasma.

## Treatment of Solar Lentigines and Age Spots

Solar lentigines and age spots represent among the most common benign epidermal pigmented lesions encountered in aesthetic dermatology practice. These hyperpigmented macules result from chronic ultraviolet exposure and accumulate melanin within the epidermal basal layer and suprabasal epidermis. The treatment landscape has evolved significantly with the introduction of picosecond-domain laser technology, which operates through photomechanical rather than photothermal mechanisms. This section examines comparative efficacy data from split-face randomized controlled trials, safety profiles across different laser parameters, and emerging scientific indices for optimizing treatment parameters.

### Clearance Rates: Picosecond 532nm vs. Q-Switched 532nm

The comparative efficacy of picosecond 532nm versus Q-switched 532nm lasers for solar lentigines has been evaluated through multiple prospective randomized controlled trials, with split-face study designs providing the highest level of evidence for direct comparison. Solar lentigines demonstrate high clearance rates and patient satisfaction when treated with picosecond pulses, with evidence classified at level 2b [49]. However, the magnitude of advantage that picosecond technology holds over traditional nanosecond Q-switched lasers remains a subject of ongoing investigation in the clinical literature.

A pivotal split-face randomized controlled study directly compared 532nm Q-switched neodymium-doped (QSND) laser against 532nm picosecond (PS) laser treatment for solar lentigines, representing the first head-to-head comparison allowing clinical effects of both laser modalities to be accurately evaluated [26]. This prospective, randomized design eliminated inter-patient variability by treating contralateral facial sites with different laser technologies, enabling precise assessment of clearance rates and session requirements within the same individual.

Studies indicate that picosecond-domain pulses can achieve equal or higher clearance of pigmented lesions in fewer treatment sessions compared to nanosecond Q-switched lasers [49]. The photomechanical mechanism underlying picosecond laser action generates laser-induced optical breakdown (LIOB) at lower fluence thresholds, creating precise melanin fragmentation through acoustic shock waves rather than the photothermal heating characteristic of Q-switched nanosecond pulses. This fundamental mechanistic difference translates into clinical advantages in treatment efficiency.

Systematic reviews of 532/755nm laser therapy for facial pigmented lesions have synthesized data from randomized controlled trials retrieved from PubMed, Embase, and CINAHL databases, confirming that both picosecond and Q-switched platforms demonstrate efficacy for benign epidermal pigmentation [17]. However, some comparative data suggest that the advantage of picosecond over nanosecond Q-switched lasers in lentigines specifically is less clear than in other pigmentary conditions such as tattoos or dermal melanocytosis [49]. This nuance is important for clinical decision-making, as solar lentigines respond well to both modalities due to their superficial epidermal location and high melanin content.

Solar lentigines and age spots show rapid clearance due to their high melanin content and superficial epidermal location, making them particularly amenable to 532nm wavelength targeting [45]. The 532nm wavelength is strongly absorbed by melanin, enabling effective energy delivery to epidermal pigment with minimal penetration into deeper dermal structures. Both picosecond and Q-switched 532nm platforms leverage this wavelength-specific absorption, though the pulse duration difference fundamentally alters the tissue response.

Meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials evaluating laser therapies for pigmented lesions have calculated standardized mean differences with 95% confidence intervals to quantify treatment effects across studies [34]. These analyses encompass participants with Fitzpatrick skin types III-VI, providing evidence applicable to diverse patient populations. The inclusion of Asian populations in particular is critical, as pigmentary disorders present differently across skin phototypes and safety profiles vary accordingly.

The session count required for satisfactory clearance represents a key differentiator between technologies. While both modalities achieve meaningful pigment reduction, picosecond lasers may require fewer treatment sessions to reach equivalent clearance endpoints, though this advantage must be weighed against device availability and cost considerations. Some comparative studies suggest both technologies can achieve comparable final clearance rates when appropriate treatment parameters and session numbers are employed [49].

### Safety Profile in Epidermal Lesions

The safety profile of laser treatment for epidermal pigmented lesions, particularly regarding post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) and scarring, represents a critical consideration in modality selection, especially for patients with darker skin phototypes. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation is a common complication following laser treatment of pigmented lesions, occurring with particular frequency in higher Fitzpatrick phototypes [39]. PIH often consists of both epidermal and dermal components, with epidermal PIH typically presenting with melanin deposition in the basal and suprabasal epidermis [41].

The risk of complications, particularly post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation in darker skin types, remains a significant barrier to widespread adoption of certain laser technologies [20]. This concern is especially relevant for Fitzpatrick skin types IV-VI, where melanocyte reactivity is heightened and the threshold for triggering pigmentary complications is lower. Studies have shown that picosecond-domain pulses can achieve therapeutic endpoints with a lower risk of adverse effects compared to nanosecond Q-switched lasers [49]. This improved safety profile is attributed to the photomechanical mechanism of picosecond lasers, which generates less cumulative thermal diffusion into surrounding tissue.

Although the overall rate of adverse events in nonablative laser and energy-based therapies for skin phototypes IV to VI is low, the incidence of PIH is not insignificant [29]. Certain lasers, such as diode, Q-switched Nd:YAG, and erbium-doped lasers, tend to have higher rates of PIH compared to other modalities [29]. This differential risk profile informs treatment selection, particularly when addressing epidermal lesions in patients with constitutively darker skin or those prone to pigmentary disturbance.

The photomechanical versus photothermal distinction is critical for understanding safety differences. Picosecond lasers, approved by the FDA for skin applications in 2012, are characterized by extremely brief pulse durations and substantial peak power density, relying on photoacoustic destruction rather than photothermolysis [39]. The heightened photomechanical/photoacoustic effect and reduced heat diffusion inherent to picosecond technology contributes to a more favorable safety profile, particularly regarding PIH risk in epidermal lesions.

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation can result from excessive energy delivery to the skin, triggering melanocyte activation and subsequent melanin overproduction [43]. This mechanism underscores the importance of appropriate fluence selection and the value of technologies that achieve therapeutic endpoints at lower thermal loads. The reduced thermal diffusion of picosecond pulses minimizes collateral heating of surrounding keratinocytes and melanocytes, thereby reducing the inflammatory cascade that precipitates PIH.

In split-face trials comparing different laser modalities for pigmented lesions, adverse event profiles have been systematically documented. In one synthesis, data for erythema incidence were analyzed, with studies finding similar incidence of adverse events between different laser wavelengths when appropriately parameterized [17]. Mild erythema represents the most common immediate post-treatment finding and typically resolves within hours to days without intervention.

For epidermal lesions specifically, the superficial location of pigment allows effective treatment with 532nm wavelengths while minimizing risk to deeper structures. However, the same melanin absorption that enables efficacy also creates risk for epidermal injury if fluence thresholds are exceeded. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation has been reported with picosecond lasers, though at lower rates than traditional Q-switched platforms [22]. This residual risk necessitates careful patient selection, appropriate parameter selection, and consideration of pre- and post-treatment skincare regimens to mitigate pigmentary complications.

The safety profile in epidermal lesions must also consider scarring risk, though this complication is rare with non-ablative laser treatments when performed by experienced practitioners. The photomechanical mechanism of picosecond lasers, which creates precise intracellular melanin fragmentation without significant epidermal disruption, further reduces scarring risk compared to ablative or deeply penetrating thermal technologies.

### Wavelength-Specific Irradiation Indices

Recent advances in laser safety science have introduced wavelength-specific irradiation indices designed to optimize fluence thresholds and minimize complications in picosecond laser treatment of pigmented lesions. Scientists at Osaka Metropolitan University have developed the first wavelength-specific irradiation index for picosecond lasers used in clinical practice to treat pigmented lesions [46]. This research, led by specially appointed professor Toshiyuki Ozawa and colleagues, represents a significant advancement in evidence-based parameter selection for picosecond laser treatments [46].

The development of wavelength-specific irradiation indices addresses a critical gap in picosecond laser practice, where parameter selection has historically relied on empirical experience and vendor recommendations rather than standardized scientific metrics. To improve efficacy and reduce complications from picosecond laser treatment, this research group established quantitative thresholds that account for the unique interaction between specific wavelengths and pigmented tissue [46]. These indices provide clinicians with objective guidance for setting fluence levels that maximize therapeutic effect while minimizing adverse event risk.

The scientific basis for wavelength-specific indices recognizes that different wavelengths interact with melanin and surrounding tissue structures in distinct ways. The 532nm wavelength, strongly absorbed by melanin, requires different fluence thresholds than longer wavelengths such as 755nm or 1064nm. Understanding the distinct characteristics and applications of each wavelength enables informed treatment planning and optimal patient outcomes [45]. Wavelength-specific indices incorporate factors including penetration depth, melanin absorption coefficients, and tissue scattering properties to establish safe and effective treatment parameters.

Clinical studies have documented specific parameter ranges used in picosecond laser treatment of pigmented lesions. For example, picosecond 532nm laser treatment has been administered with wavelengths at 532nm, spot sizes ranging from 2.3-4.3mm, fluence between 0.3-0.4 J/cm², and frequencies of 2.0-5.0 Hz [40]. These parameters reflect the low-fluence approach increasingly favored for pigment treatment, which prioritizes safety and gradual improvement over aggressive single-session clearance.

The wavelength-specific irradiation index framework supports personalized laser therapy rooted in solid understanding of wavelength-tissue interactions [45]. As technology evolves, tools such as AI and imaging may enhance real-time decision-making during treatment, but the fundamentals of wavelength-specific targeting remain paramount for safe and effective outcomes. These indices provide a scientific foundation that transcends individual device platforms, offering universal principles applicable across different picosecond laser systems.

The development of these indices also addresses the challenge of treating diverse skin phototypes. Fitzpatrick skin types III-VI require modified parameter approaches due to increased melanin content and heightened PIH risk. Wavelength-specific thresholds enable clinicians to adjust fluence based on both the target wavelength and patient skin type, creating a more nuanced and safer treatment paradigm than one-size-fits-all parameter recommendations.

In the context of spot treatments for solar lentigines and age spots, wavelength-specific irradiation indices guide fluence selection to achieve effective melanin fragmentation while avoiding excessive thermal injury. The photomechanical mechanism of picosecond lasers operates optimally within specific fluence windows that vary by wavelength, and these indices help identify those windows precisely. This scientific approach to parameter selection represents an evolution from empirical trial-and-error toward evidence-based, reproducible treatment protocols that minimize complications while maintaining efficacy.

## Photoaging and Skin Brightening Protocols

Photoaging manifests through multiple cutaneous changes including erythema, telangiectasia, dyspigmentation, and textural degradation. Effective brightening protocols require distinguishing between vascular and pigmentary components, selecting appropriate energy-based devices, and understanding mechanism-specific safety profiles. This section evaluates the evidence for IPL and PDL in addressing vascular photoaging, critically examines LED therapy limitations for depigmentation, and analyzes collagen remodeling benefits from picosecond and fractional laser technologies.

### IPL and PDL for Vascular Components of Photoaging

Intense Pulsed Light (IPL) and Pulsed Dye Laser (PDL) represent the primary light-based modalities for targeting vascular components of photoaging, including erythema and telangiectasia. These vascular changes contribute significantly to overall skin brightness and rejuvenation outcomes, particularly in patients presenting with rosacea-associated photoaging or sun-induced vascular dilation.

**Efficacy in Erythema and Telangiectasia Reduction**

Multiple systematic reviews and randomized controlled trials demonstrate robust efficacy for both IPL and PDL in vascular lesion treatment. A comparative analysis found that both modalities show significant improvement in erythema and telangiectasia following treatment, with most studies reporting clearance after IPL application [7]. The mechanism involves selective photothermolysis of oxyhemoglobin, resulting in ablation of abnormally dilated vessels and reduction of extravascular leakage [7].

When directly compared, IPL demonstrates an advantage in achieving high clearance rates (>75%), while PDL shows superiority in reducing post-treatment pain [14]. This distinction is clinically relevant for patient counseling and protocol selection. Multiple treatment sessions (up to 15) at 1-to-3-week intervals may be necessary, with parameters requiring individual adjustment according to phototype, severity, and treatment response [7].

**Vascular Targeting in Pigmentary Disorders**

The vascular component of melasma has gained recognition as a therapeutic target. Passeron et al. suggested that targeting the vascular component with a copper bromide (CuBr) laser led to reduced MASI scores, supporting the concept that vascular proliferation contributes to melasma pathogenesis [25][3]. Other laser treatments including PDL (585 nm) and CuBr (511-578 nm) lasers work by targeting this vascular component [25].

However, this approach carries significant safety considerations. The combination therapy was beneficial in patients with Fitzpatrick skin types II and III, but half of the patients with darker skin developed post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) [25][3]. This finding underscores the critical importance of skin type assessment before vascular-targeted interventions.

**Safety Warning: IPL Use in Melasma Patients**

A dedicated safety warning is warranted for IPL use in melasma patients. Evidence indicates variable outcomes and significant risk of rebound pigmentation:

- **Rebound Pigmentation Risk**: Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation represents the most frequently reported adverse event, especially in patients with Fitzpatrick skin types III–VI [21]. Safety analysis revealed a significantly higher risk of adverse events in combination therapy groups, with pooled odds ratio of 8.96 (95% CI: 3.71–21.64) [21].

- **Contraindications**: IPL systems can effectively target pigment, but post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation is noted as resistant to treatment with IPL systems in certain contexts [39]. Melasma predominantly affects women with darker skin types (Fitzpatrick III–VI) and often leads to significant psychosocial distress due to its chronic, relapsing nature [20].

- **Parameter Individualization**: Protocol preferences must be individualized based on skin type [45]. Laser setting parameters such as energy density and pulse duration are fundamental, but should be expanded to include treatment spot size (affecting depth of light penetration) and pulse emission frequency (affecting temperature) [17].

- **Clinical Recommendation**: Given the PIH risk in darker skin types, IPL for melasma should be approached with caution, with thorough patient counseling about rebound pigmentation potential and consideration of alternative modalities for Fitzpatrick IV–VI patients.

### LED Therapy: Evidence Gaps in Depigmentation

Light Emitting Diode (LED) therapy has gained popularity in both clinical and home-use settings for various dermatological applications. However, significant evidence gaps exist regarding its efficacy for depigmentation compared to its established roles in inflammation modulation and wound healing.

**Established Roles: Inflammation and Wound Healing**

LED therapy demonstrates clear efficacy for specific indications. According to the American Academy of Dermatology, LED light therapy helps stimulate collagen production, minimize fine lines, and calm inflammation—making it one of the most effective anti-aging and skin-rejuvenation modalities for these purposes [51]. Different wavelengths target different concerns:

- **630nm–660nm (Deep Red)**: Best for skin texture, collagen, and wrinkles [52]
- **810nm–850nm (Near-Infrared)**: Best for deep muscle recovery, joints, and inflammation [52]

The mechanism involves photobiomodulation rather than selective photothermolysis, with effects mediated through mitochondrial cytochrome c oxidase activation and subsequent cellular signaling cascades.

**Evidence Gaps in Hyperpigmentation Treatment**

Despite marketing claims about skin brightening, the evidence for LED therapy in melanin reduction remains notably thin compared to laser and IPL modalities:

- **Lack of Direct Melanin Targeting**: Unlike laser and IPL systems that directly target melanin chromophores through selective photothermolysis, LED therapy lacks the spectral specificity and energy density required for effective melanin disruption [37]. Laser therapy offers a targeted method to reduce melanin and improve skin tone through medical device-based procedures requiring specialized equipment [37].

- **Comparative Efficacy**: When ranked in decreasing order of efficacy for pigmentary disorders, laser and light-based therapies (IPL, Q-switched lasers) demonstrate superior outcomes compared to LED modalities [22]. Q-switched Nd:YAG lasers specifically target melanin with established efficacy [21].

- **Clinical Evidence Limitations**: While LED therapy offers results supported by clinical evidence for texture, wrinkles, and acne, the evidence base for hyperpigmentation specifically remains limited [51]. Most peer-reviewed studies focus on inflammatory conditions (acne, rosacea) rather than pigmentary disorders.

- **Home Device Considerations**: The landscape of at-home red light therapy has evolved dramatically, with devices offering clinical wavelengths (660nm and 850nm combination) and FDA Class II clearance [53][52]. However, energy density and treatment protocols for home devices differ substantially from in-office laser systems, further limiting depigmentation efficacy.

**Clinical Positioning**

LED therapy should be positioned as an adjunctive modality for:
- Post-procedure healing and inflammation reduction
- Maintenance therapy following laser/IPL treatments
- Patients seeking mild anti-aging benefits without downtime

LED therapy should not be recommended as primary treatment for hyperpigmentation, melasma, or solar lentigines where laser and IPL demonstrate superior evidence-based outcomes.

### Collagen Remodeling and Texture Improvement

Picosecond and fractional laser technologies offer secondary benefits beyond pigment clearance, including collagen remodeling, texture improvement, and barrier function enhancement. Understanding the mechanistic differences between photothermal and photomechanical effects is critical for optimizing outcomes and safety.

**Photomechanical vs. Photothermal Mechanisms**

A fundamental distinction exists between traditional laser/IPL mechanisms and picosecond laser technology:

- **Photothermal (Traditional Laser/IPL)**: Relies on selective photothermolysis with thermal diffusion to surrounding tissue. This mechanism carries higher risk of thermal injury, particularly in darker skin types where epidermal melanin competes for light absorption [39].

- **Photomechanical (Picosecond)**: Approved by the FDA for skin applications in 2012, picosecond lasers are characterized by extremely brief pulse durations and substantial peak power density, relying on photoacoustic destruction rather than photothermolysis [39]. The heightened photomechanical/photoacoustic effect and reduced heat diffusion result in improved safety profiles [39].

This mechanistic difference explains why picosecond lasers demonstrate better safety profiles for darker skin types and melasma, with reduced risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation compared to nanosecond Q-switched systems [20].

**Collagen Remodeling Evidence**

Picosecond lasers induce collagen remodeling through laser-induced optical breakdown (LIOB) and subsequent wound healing responses:

- **Mechanistic Evidence**: Researchers used a porcine model closely resembling human skin to provide mechanistic evidence supporting safer parameter selection and clinical application of picosecond lasers [32]. These lasers lighten pigmentation by breaking pigment into tiny fragments while simultaneously stimulating dermal remodeling [32].

- **Growth Factor Elevation**: Studies of fractional PS Nd:YAG laser (1064 nm) found elevated levels of heat shock proteins, growth factors, and platelet-activating factors in laser-treated areas compared to control groups [36]. These findings suggest the mechanism of action is associated with neocollagenesis and dermal remodeling [36].

- **Histologic Visualization**: High-resolution OCT imaging of 1064-nm picosecond laser-treated areas describes immediate formation of vacuoles and gradual accumulation of cellular debris, confirming the photomechanical tissue effects [9].

**Texture and Pore Improvement**

Fractional picosecond lasers have gained popularity for skin rejuvenation and pore reduction:

- **Comparative Studies**: Randomized controlled trials compare fractional 1064nm picosecond laser with 1565nm nonablative fractional laser, demonstrating efficacy in texture improvement [49].

- **Clinical Outcomes**: Patient education and expectation management remain critical, as treatment often requires multiple sessions for optimal texture improvement [49].

- **Wavelength Selection**: The 1064 nm wavelength demonstrates excellent safety across all Fitzpatrick skin types, with dermal melanosis conditions including nevus of Ota and Hori's nevus responding favorably to 1064nm treatment protocols [45].

**Barrier Function Considerations**

Skin barrier integrity represents an important secondary outcome:

- **Microbiota Impact**: The skin microbiota is integral to preserving the integrity of the skin barrier function, and UV irradiation and photoaging disturb microbial composition [18]. Laser treatments that minimize epidermal disruption may better preserve barrier function.

- **Non-Ablative Advantage**: Fractional non-ablative approaches create microthermal zones while preserving surrounding tissue, supporting faster barrier recovery compared to fully ablative procedures [42].

**Safety in Darker Skin Types**

The photomechanical mechanism of picosecond lasers offers particular advantages for patients with darker skin:

- **Reduced PIH Risk**: Procedural advances including low-fluence picosecond lasers offer enhanced pigment clearance with improved safety profiles, though treatment durability remains limited by recurrence risk [20].

- **Wavelength Considerations**: The 1064 nm wavelength has the lowest absorption in surface melanin, meaning much lower risk of epidermal injury in darker skin types [6].

- **Clinical Evidence**: Studies in Fitzpatrick IV–V skin types demonstrate long-term efficacy and safety of fractional PS Nd:YAG laser (1064 nm) for various indications including striae treatment [36].

**Summary of Texture Improvement Modalities**

| Modality | Mechanism | Primary Texture Benefit | Safety in Darker Skin |
|----------|-----------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Picosecond Fractional | Photomechanical (LIOB) | Pore reduction, fine lines | Excellent (1064 nm) |
| Non-Ablative Fractional | Photothermal | Overall texture, scars | Good (with appropriate settings) |
| IPL | Photothermal | Mild texture, vascular | Moderate (PIH risk) |
| LED | Photobiomodulation | Minimal texture | Excellent |

The evidence supports picosecond fractional lasers as the preferred modality for patients seeking combined pigment clearance and texture improvement, particularly in darker skin types where photomechanical mechanisms offer superior safety profiles compared to traditional photothermal approaches.

## Safety Profiles and Contraindications by Skin Type

Safety represents the primary consideration when selecting energy-based devices for pigmentation and rejuvenation, particularly given the substantial variation in adverse event profiles across different skin phototypes. The Fitzpatrick skin phototype scale (I-VI) serves as the foundational framework for risk stratification, with types III-VI demonstrating markedly elevated susceptibility to pigmentary complications following laser and light-based interventions. This section provides comprehensive analysis of safety profiles, contraindications, and risk mitigation strategies organized by skin type classification, adverse event taxonomy, and patient tolerance metrics.

### Risk Stratification for Fitzpatrick Skin Types III-VI

Patients with Fitzpatrick skin types III-VI present distinct safety challenges that necessitate careful modality selection and parameter optimization. The fundamental concern stems from higher baseline epidermal melanin content, which competes with target chromophores for light absorption and increases the risk of non-specific thermal injury to surrounding tissue. Meta-analytic data confirms that adverse event rates are significantly elevated in this population, with post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) representing the most frequently reported complication [21].

**Wavelength Selection as Primary Risk Mitigation**

The 1064 nm Nd:YAG wavelength has emerged as the gold standard for treating darker skin phototypes (IV-VI) due to its favorable absorption characteristics. This wavelength demonstrates the lowest absorption coefficient in epidermal melanin compared to shorter wavelengths, allowing deeper penetration with minimal competition from surface pigment [6][36]. Clinical studies investigating fractional picosecond Nd:YAG laser (1064 nm) for striae treatment in individuals with Fitzpatrick skin types IV-V have confirmed acceptable safety profiles when appropriate parameters are employed [36].

The mechanistic advantage of 1064 nm extends beyond simple absorption coefficients. In darker Malaysian skin tones (typically Fitzpatrick IV-V), this wavelength leaves the epidermal surface largely untouched while targeting deeper pigment deposits, substantially reducing the risk of epidermal injury that precipitates PIH [6]. This contrasts sharply with 532 nm wavelengths, which demonstrate high melanin absorption and correspondingly elevated adverse event rates in pigmented skin.

**Photomechanical Versus Photothermal Mechanisms**

A critical distinction in safety profiling involves the underlying mechanism of action. Traditional Q-switched and long-pulsed lasers operate primarily through photothermal mechanisms, where light energy converts to heat, causing thermal destruction of target chromophores. This heat diffusion can extend beyond the intended target, particularly problematic in darker skin where epidermal melanin absorbs significant energy [39].

Picosecond lasers, approved by the FDA for skin applications in 2012, rely on photoacoustic or photomechanical destruction rather than photothermolysis. The extremely brief pulse durations (picoseconds versus nanoseconds) generate substantial peak power density with markedly reduced heat diffusion into surrounding tissue [39]. This mechanistic difference translates directly to improved safety profiles for Fitzpatrick III-VI patients, as the reduced thermal load minimizes collateral epidermal damage.

**Clinical Evidence for Risk Stratification**

The Passeron et al. study provides instructive evidence regarding risk stratification by skin type. In their investigation of combination therapy targeting the vascular component of melasma, patients with Fitzpatrick skin types II and III demonstrated beneficial outcomes, whereas approximately 50% of patients with darker skin types developed PIH [25][3]. This stark dichotomy underscores the necessity for conservative parameter selection and enhanced pre-treatment counseling in types IV-VI.

A systematic review and meta-analysis examining adverse events of nonablative lasers and energy-based therapies in subjects with Fitzpatrick skin phototypes IV to VI identified PIH as the predominant complication, with pooled analysis revealing significantly elevated risk in combination therapy groups [29][21]. The pooled odds ratio for adverse events in combination therapy reached 8.96 (95% CI: 3.71–21.64), with erythema and PIH representing the most frequently reported complications, particularly in Fitzpatrick skin types III-V [21].

**Parameter Optimization Guidelines**

For Fitzpatrick III-IV skin types, moderate fluence settings with appropriate cooling may be employed, though conservative approaches remain advisable. For types V-VI, the following parameters should be considered:

- **Wavelength**: Prioritize 1064 nm Nd:YAG over 532 nm or 755 nm
- **Pulse Duration**: Picosecond domains preferred over nanosecond
- **Fluence**: Start at lowest effective settings with gradual titration
- **Spot Size**: Larger spot sizes may reduce epidermal injury risk
- **Treatment Intervals**: Extended intervals (4-6 weeks minimum) between sessions
- **Pre-treatment**: Consider topical depigmenting agents for 4-6 weeks prior

Comparative efficacy and safety trials between picosecond alexandrite laser and traditional combined Q-switched and long-pulse Nd:YAG lasers in melasma treatment have demonstrated that traditional modalities show superior efficacy in Fitzpatrick skin types III-IV, though safety profiles must be carefully weighed against efficacy gains [10].

### Adverse Events: PIH, Hypopigmentation, and Scarring

Adverse events following energy-based device treatments for pigmentation and rejuvenation span a spectrum from transient erythema to permanent textural changes. Understanding the incidence, pathophysiology, and management of these complications is essential for informed consent and appropriate patient selection.

**Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation (PIH)**

PIH represents the most common adverse event following laser and light-based treatments in pigmented skin, with meta-analytic data indicating an overall rate of 8.1% across studied populations [29]. The pathophysiology involves inflammatory cascade activation following thermal or mechanical injury, stimulating melanocytes to increase melanin production and transfer to surrounding keratinocytes.

Risk factors for PIH include:

- **Skin Phototype**: Fitzpatrick types III-VI demonstrate substantially elevated risk [21]
- **Treatment Parameters**: Higher fluence and energy density correlate with increased PIH incidence [17]
- **Wavelength Selection**: Shorter wavelengths (532 nm) produce more adverse events than longer wavelengths (755 nm, 1064 nm) [17]
- **Treatment Density**: Laser density shows correlation with PIH development [29]
- **Pre-existing Conditions**: Active melasma phase characterized by spot enlargement, darkening, or redness increases adverse event likelihood [10]

Management of PIH typically involves topical agents including hydroquinone, tretinoin, corticosteroids, and emerging alternatives such as azelaic acid and tranexamic acid. The Kligman formula (4% hydroquinone, 0.1% dexamethasone, 3% vitamin C) remains a standard therapeutic approach, though long-term hydroquinone use requires monitoring for exogenous ochronosis [21]. Patients with Fitzpatrick skin types III to VI are most commonly affected, and for many, the remnant pigmentation represents a greater concern than the original condition [2].

**Hypopigmentation**

Mottled hypopigmentation represents a particularly challenging adverse event, as it may be permanent and difficult to treat. Low-fluence 1064-nm Q-switched Nd:YAG laser, despite its favorable safety profile for darker skin, carries a reported hypopigmentation rate as high as 14% [22]. Risk factors include:

- Short treatment intervals
- Excessive cumulative fluence
- Multiple treatment passes
- Individual susceptibility

The pathophysiology involves melanocyte destruction or functional suppression, with histological studies demonstrating reduced melanocyte density in affected areas. Unlike PIH, which typically resolves with time and topical therapy, hypopigmentation may persist indefinitely, making prevention through conservative parameter selection critical.

**Scarring**

Scarring represents the most severe adverse event, though incidence remains relatively low with modern devices when used appropriately. Risk factors include:

- Ablative rather than non-ablative modalities
- Excessive fluence or pulse stacking
- Inadequate cooling
- Pre-existing scarring tendencies
- Post-treatment infection or trauma

Meta-analytic data on nonablative lasers indicates that blistering rates remain low, with most skin-related adverse events characterized as mild and transient [16][29]. However, the consequences of scarring warrant extreme caution, particularly in patients with history of keloid formation or abnormal wound healing.

**The Rebound Phenomenon in Melasma**

A critical safety consideration specific to melasma treatment involves the rebound pigmentation phenomenon. Approximately 50% of patients experience recurrence to some degree within three to six months following laser- and light-based procedures, regardless of modality employed [25]. This recurrence risk necessitates comprehensive patient counseling regarding the chronic nature of melasma and the likelihood of requiring maintenance therapy.

Patient education and expectation management remain critical, as melasma often requires long-term maintenance, and the risk of rebound hyperpigmentation persists even with optimal treatment protocols [49]. Topical maintenance with anti-tyrosinase agents before and after treatment significantly improves durability of response [25].

**WARNING: IPL Use in Melasma Patients**

Intense Pulsed Light (IPL) requires specific safety warnings when considered for melasma treatment. IPL operates through selective photothermal effects, generating heat that can stimulate melanocytes and potentially exacerbate pigmentation [18]. While some studies report favorable safety profiles for IPL in melasma [22], the photothermal mechanism carries inherent risk of rebound pigmentation, particularly in:

- Active melasma phase (characterized by recent darkening or erythema)
- Fitzpatrick skin types IV-VI
- Patients with history of PIH
- Inadequately pre-treated skin

Side effects of IPL therapy include crusting lasting 1 to 2 weeks, and combination with aggressive topical maintenance treatment is recommended to minimize pathways for pigment recurrence [25]. Clinicians should exercise particular caution with IPL in melasma patients, considering alternative modalities with photomechanical rather than photothermal mechanisms when treating darker skin types or active disease.

**Comparative Safety: Picosecond Versus Traditional Lasers**

Picosecond-domain pulses demonstrate equal or higher clearance of pigment in fewer treatment sessions compared to nanosecond Q-switched lasers, with lower risk of adverse effects [49]. The reduced thermal load and enhanced pigment selectivity translate to improved safety profiles, particularly relevant for patients at elevated risk of PIH.

Studies comparing 532 nm and 755 nm lasers for facial pigmented lesions indicate that 755 nm produces fewer adverse events in vascular lesion treatment, though 532 nm may produce more painful experiences [17]. Histological findings indicate that 730 nm picosecond laser represents a superior pigment-selective modality with minimal disruption to the dermo-epidermal junction, suggesting reduced healing time and minimized adverse event risk [36].

### Pain Management and Patient Tolerance

Patient tolerance and pain perception significantly influence treatment adherence, satisfaction, and ultimately clinical outcomes. Understanding pain profiles across different modalities enables appropriate pre-treatment counseling and analgesic planning.

**Visual Analog Scale (VAS) Comparisons**

Pain assessment in laser dermatology typically employs the Visual Analog Scale (VAS), with scores ranging from 0 (no pain) to 10 (worst imaginable pain). Comparative data reveals meaningful differences between modalities:

**Picosecond Versus Traditional Q-Switched Lasers**

The 730 nm picosecond laser demonstrated lower pain scores compared to the 755 nm approach in comparative studies, though not all investigations included formal pain score assessment [17]. Bonan et al. (2021) reported that the MoveoPL mode of the 755 nm alexandrite laser produced pain scores of 1.8 on a 0-5 scale, compared to 3.1 on the same scale for single-pass mode, indicating that delivery method significantly influences pain perception independent of wavelength [17].

Meta-analysis conclusions indicate that both 532 nm and 755 nm lasers effectively treat facial pigmented and vascular lesions, with 755 nm showing slight advantage and 532 nm producing more painful experiences but fewer adverse events in vascular lesion cases [17]. This wavelength-dependent pain profile has practical implications for patient comfort and anesthesia requirements.

**Factors Influencing Pain Perception**

Multiple factors modulate pain perception during energy-based device treatments:

- **Wavelength**: Shorter wavelengths (532 nm) generally produce higher pain scores than longer wavelengths (755 nm, 1064 nm)
- **Pulse Duration**: Picosecond domains may reduce pain compared to nanosecond due to reduced thermal diffusion
- **Delivery Mode**: Multi-pass or scanning modes (e.g., MoveoPL) reduce pain compared to single-pass delivery
- **Treatment Area**: Facial skin demonstrates variable pain sensitivity by region
- **Individual Variation**: Pain tolerance varies substantially between patients

**Anesthesia and Comfort Measures**

Pain management strategies should be tailored to modality, treatment area, and individual patient factors:

- **Topical Anesthetics**: Lidocaine-based creams applied 30-60 minutes pre-treatment
- **Cooling**: Contact cooling, cryogen spray, or cold air devices reduce pain and protect epidermis
- **Oral Analgesics**: Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs for post-treatment discomfort
- **Distraction Techniques**: Music, conversation, or virtual reality for anxious patients

**Patient Tolerance and Treatment Completion**

Patient tolerance directly impacts treatment completion rates and clinical outcomes. Studies indicate that picosecond-domain pulses achieve pigment clearance in fewer treatment sessions than nanosecond Q-switched lasers [49], potentially improving overall treatment experience through reduced cumulative discomfort and fewer clinic visits.

However, the relationship between pain and adverse events requires careful consideration. While 532 nm wavelengths produce more painful experiences, they may demonstrate fewer adverse events in specific indications such as vascular lesions [17]. This paradox underscores that pain perception does not always correlate with tissue injury risk, and clinical decision-making should prioritize safety profiles over comfort metrics alone.

**Special Considerations for Darker Skin Types**

Patients with Fitzpatrick skin types III-VI may require modified pain management approaches due to:

- Increased anxiety regarding adverse events, particularly PIH
- Potential need for more conservative parameters requiring additional treatment sessions
- Higher likelihood of requiring pre- and post-treatment topical therapy

Comprehensive pre-treatment counseling addressing both pain expectations and adverse event risks improves patient satisfaction and treatment adherence. Patients should understand that conservative parameter selection, while potentially requiring more treatment sessions, significantly reduces complication risk in pigmented skin types.

## Combination Therapies and Adjunctive Agents

Combination therapy has emerged as the cornerstone of modern melasma and hyperpigmentation management, reflecting a paradigm shift from monotherapy approaches toward multi-mechanistic treatment strategies. Recent literature from 2020 to 2026 demonstrates that combining energy-based devices with pharmacological adjuncts yields superior efficacy compared to either modality alone, particularly for recalcitrant pigmentary disorders. This section examines three critical combination approaches: tranexamic acid integration with light therapy, microneedling-enhanced IPL protocols with postbiotic formulations, and hydroquinone alternatives for maintenance therapy. The evidence base prioritizes randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses, acknowledging that combination protocols require careful patient selection given the elevated risk of adverse events in darker skin types.

### Light Therapy Plus Tranexamic Acid

Tranexamic acid (TA) has established itself as the most extensively studied pharmacological adjunct to light-based therapies for melasma management. The mechanism of action involves inhibition of plasminogen activation in keratinocytes, which subsequently reduces prostaglandin and arachidonic acid production, ultimately suppressing melanocyte stimulation and melanogenesis. When combined with laser or intense pulsed light (IPL) therapy, TA addresses both the vascular and epidermal components of melasma pathogenesis, providing synergistic effects that monotherapy cannot achieve.

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses have documented the superior efficacy of topical tranexamic acid when used adjunctively with picosecond laser platforms. Papagerogiu et al. conducted a systematic review demonstrating that the adjunctive use of topical tranexamic acid with a 755nm picosecond alexandrite laser showed superior efficacy in reducing melasma severity and enhancing patient satisfaction compared to monotherapy or regimens involving hydroquinone alone [49]. This combination also demonstrated improved durability of results, addressing the high recurrence rate that plagues melasma treatment. The photomechanical mechanism of picosecond lasers, which generates laser-induced optical breakdown (LIOB) with minimal thermal damage, complements the anti-melanogenic effects of TA, creating a favorable safety profile particularly for Fitzpatrick skin types III–V.

Randomized controlled trials have further validated the IPL-tranexamic acid combination. Yan Zhao et al. published findings on improved clinical efficacy of melasma with combination therapy of intense pulsed light and tranexamic acid, utilizing VISIA skin imaging systems for objective assessment of pigment reduction [23]. The study evaluated IPL therapy as a standalone treatment versus IPL therapy combined with tranexamic acid microneedling for melasma management. Results indicated that the combination group achieved significantly greater reductions in modified Melasma Area and Severity Index (mMASI) scores compared to IPL monotherapy, with enhanced patient satisfaction scores and reduced recurrence rates at six-month follow-up.

Oral tranexamic acid has also been investigated in combination with laser therapy. Wang et al. conducted a split-face randomized controlled study where patients were divided into two groups receiving either oral TA and low-fluence Q-switched Nd:YAG laser treatment or low-fluence Q-switched Nd:YAG laser treatment alone [44]. While the laser-only group followed the same laser treatment schedule, the combination group received two laser sessions spaced 4 weeks apart alongside oral TA administration. The combination protocol demonstrated superior pigment clearance with acceptable safety profiles, though oral TA requires careful patient screening for thrombotic risk factors.

Microneedling-assisted tranexamic acid delivery has emerged as an innovative approach that enhances transdermal penetration while minimizing systemic exposure. In a randomized controlled study involving 45 patients with facial melasma, microneedling combined with topical TXA resulted in significantly greater reductions in mMASI scores compared to microneedling combined with metformin or modified protocols [37]. This approach leverages the collagen-induction therapy mechanism of microneedling to create microchannels that facilitate deeper penetration of tranexamic acid into the dermal-epidermal junction where melanocyte activity is heightened.

Local infiltration of tranexamic acid has also been evaluated. Tehranchinia Z conducted an evaluation of therapeutic efficacy and safety of tranexamic acid local infiltration in combination with topical 4% hydroquinone cream compared to topical 4% hydroquinone cream alone in patients with melasma [13]. The study concluded that the combination of 4% hydroquinone cream with tranexamic acid (whether oral or local infiltration) demonstrated superior efficacy compared to topical 20% azelaic acid after six months of therapy. However, the invasive nature of local infiltration limits its widespread adoption compared to topical or oral routes.

Safety considerations remain paramount when combining tranexamic acid with light therapy. A meta-analysis of combination therapies revealed a significantly higher risk of adverse events in the combination therapy group, with a pooled odds ratio of 8.96 (95% CI: 3.71–21.64) [21]. The most frequently reported adverse events were erythema and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH), especially in patients with Fitzpatrick skin types III–V. This finding underscores the importance of conservative energy settings, adequate cooling, and strict photoprotection protocols when implementing combination therapies.

### Microneedling and Postbiotic Formulations with IPL

The integration of microneedling with intense pulsed light therapy represents an emerging frontier in melasma management, particularly when combined with postbiotic formulations designed to support barrier repair and reduce inflammation. This tri-modal approach addresses multiple pathogenic pathways simultaneously: IPL targets existing pigment through selective photothermolysis, microneedling enhances transdermal delivery of therapeutic agents and stimulates collagen remodeling, and postbiotic formulations modulate the skin microbiome while supporting epidermal barrier function.

A clinical trial conducted at a Medical Cosmetic Clinic was designed to assess the efficacy and safety of the combination of IPL and microneedling with EVE-CHARM whitening lotion versus IPL monotherapy in melasma management [18]. From March 2024 to October 2024, a cohort of 30 participants was enrolled in this study, which was published in the Journal of Imaging. The study design incorporated standardized IPL protocols combined with mechanical stimulation through microneedling, followed by application of a postbiotic-containing formulation. All recruited subjects were aware of the study process and signed written consent forms, with consent for publication obtained for personal images used in the article [18].

The rationale for combining microneedling with IPL stems from complementary mechanisms of action. Microneedling, also known as collagen-induction therapy, has evolved into a versatile platform for enhancing transdermal delivery of depigmenting agents [20]. Recent studies demonstrate that microneedling combined with tranexamic acid, platelet-rich plasma (PRP), or topical cysteamine offers enhanced pigment clearance with improved safety profiles compared to either modality alone. When paired with IPL, microneedling creates microchannels that may facilitate deeper penetration of light energy while simultaneously delivering therapeutic agents to the basal layer where melanocytes reside.

Postbiotic formulations represent a novel adjunctive approach that addresses the inflammatory component of melasma pathogenesis. Postbiotics, which include metabolic byproducts of beneficial bacteria such as lactobacillus ferment lysates, have demonstrated anti-inflammatory and barrier-repair properties that may reduce the risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation following energy-based procedures. The combination of IPL with microneedling and postbiotic formulations aims to achieve pigment reduction while simultaneously strengthening the skin barrier, potentially reducing recurrence rates that characterize melasma treatment.

Procedural advances in this domain include low-fluence picosecond lasers, fractional CO2 laser–assisted drug delivery, and exosome-augmented microneedling, all of which offer enhanced pigment clearance with improved safety profiles [20]. However, treatment durability remains limited by the chronic and relapsing nature of melasma, necessitating long-term maintenance protocols. The VISIA skin imaging system has been utilized to objectively evaluate outcomes in studies combining IPL with tranexamic acid microneedling, providing quantitative assessment of pigment reduction and skin texture improvement [23].

Safety considerations for microneedling-IPL combinations mirror those of other combination therapies. The risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation remains elevated in patients with darker skin types, particularly when aggressive microneedling depths or high IPL fluences are employed. Procedural parameters must be carefully calibrated based on Fitzpatrick skin type, with conservative approaches recommended for Fitzpatrick IV–VI skin. Additionally, the timing of microneedling relative to IPL treatment requires optimization; some protocols recommend sequential treatment on the same day, while others advocate for staggered sessions to minimize cumulative irritation.

### Topical Depigmenting Agents: Hydroquinone Alternatives

While hydroquinone remains the gold standard for rapid depigmentation, its long-term use is limited by concerns regarding ochronosis, rebound hyperpigmentation, and potential carcinogenicity. Consequently, the integration of hydroquinone alternatives with light-based treatments has become essential for maintenance therapy and for patients requiring extended treatment durations. Thiamidol, niacinamide, azelaic acid, and other non-hydroquinone agents have emerged as viable alternatives that can be safely combined with laser and IPL therapies.

A comprehensive review by Suliman RS confirmed that hydroquinone remains the most effective rapid-response topical treatment for melasma, but its long-term use is limited by its adverse effect profile [8]. Crucially, thiamidol, tranexamic acid, and topical metformin have emerged as promising alternatives for patients requiring long-term therapy or maintenance regimens. These novel compounds complement or substitute hydroquinone in maintenance protocols following light-based treatments, incorporating antioxidants and non-hydroquinone brighteners that align with current trends toward safer, multi-mechanistic treatment of melasma [20].

Thiamidol, a potent tyrosinase inhibitor, has demonstrated efficacy comparable to hydroquinone in some studies while exhibiting a more favorable safety profile. When integrated with light-based treatments, thiamidol provides sustained melanogenesis inhibition during the post-treatment recovery period, reducing the risk of rebound pigmentation. Niacinamide functions through multiple mechanisms including inhibition of melanosome transfer from melanocytes to keratinocytes, anti-inflammatory effects, and barrier enhancement. Regarding antioxidants, the main agents include vitamin C (ascorbic acid) and its derivatives, vitamin E (α-tocopherol), and niacinamide, all of which can mitigate pigmentation by scavenging reactive oxygen species and modulating redox-sensitive steps of melanogenesis [37].

Azelaic acid, a dicarboxylic acid with tyrosinase-inhibiting and anti-inflammatory properties, has been extensively studied as a hydroquinone alternative. A randomized controlled trial compared the combination of 4% hydroquinone cream with oral tranexamic acid against topical 20% azelaic acid, concluding that the hydroquinone-TA combination demonstrated superior efficacy after six months of therapy [13]. However, azelaic acid remains a valuable option for maintenance therapy and for patients who cannot tolerate hydroquinone. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation can be reduced using topical azelaic acid, making it particularly suitable for post-procedural care following laser or IPL treatments [21].

Commercial formulations combining multiple non-hydroquinone agents have shown promise in clinical studies. Garnier Dark Spot Corrector contains combinations of active skin lightening ingredients including geraniol and other botanical extracts [27]. Neostrata Pigment Lightening Gel, which contains arbutin and other depigmenting agents, demonstrated significant hypopigmentation when compared to untreated controls in patients with Fitzpatrick IV/V skin types [27]. All formulated preparations showed significant reduction in pigmentation; however, only the arbutin (5%) formulation achieved statistical significance in certain outcome measures.

The Kligman formula, comprised of hydroquinone (4.0%), dexamethasone (0.1%), and vitamin C (3%), represents a traditional triple-combination approach that increases efficacy through synergistic mechanisms [21]. Topical therapy, such as a fixed-dose triple combination cream with 4% hydroquinone, can be administered daily or twice daily, with patient skin type and chemical tolerance guiding treatment duration. However, for long-term maintenance following light-based treatments, hydroquinone-free alternatives are increasingly preferred to minimize the risk of exogenous ochronosis and rebound hyperpigmentation.

Goldman et al. showed that IPL could be particularly helpful to treat moderate-to-severe melasma if combined with more aggressive topical maintenance treatment to minimize pathways for pigment recurrence [25]. This finding emphasizes the critical role of adjunctive topical therapy in sustaining the benefits of energy-based procedures. Side effects of IPL therapy included crusting that lasted 1 to 2 weeks, during which appropriate topical care is essential to support healing and prevent post-inflammatory complications.

**Safety Warning: IPL Use in Melasma Patients**

A dedicated safety warning is warranted for IPL use in melasma patients, particularly those with darker skin types. Evidence indicates that IPL carries a significant risk of rebound pigmentation and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation in melasma patients, especially when used without appropriate adjunctive therapy or in patients with Fitzpatrick skin types IV–VI. The combination therapy was beneficial in patients with Fitzpatrick skin types II and III, but half of the patients with darker skin developed PIH in certain studies [3]. 

Contraindications for IPL in melasma include active inflammation, recent sun exposure, pregnancy, and history of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. Patients must be counseled on the chronic and relapsing nature of melasma, with realistic expectations regarding the need for long-term maintenance therapy. Strict photoprotection with broad-spectrum sunscreen (SPF 50+) is mandatory before, during, and after IPL treatment. Conservative energy settings, adequate cooling, and test spots are essential precautions, particularly in patients with Fitzpatrick skin types III and above. The risk of adverse events is significantly elevated in combination therapy protocols, necessitating careful patient selection and experienced operator technique.

## Comparative Synthesis and Clinical Recommendations

This section synthesizes the comparative evidence across energy-based modalities for pigmentation and rejuvenation, providing actionable clinical guidance grounded in recent randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses. The analysis prioritizes mechanistic understanding, safety profiles across skin types, and long-term value considerations to support evidence-based treatment selection.

### Modality Selection Matrix by Condition

Selecting the appropriate energy-based modality requires matching device mechanism to diagnosis, skin phototype, and risk tolerance. The following matrix ranks Laser, IPL, and LED modalities by efficacy for specific conditions, incorporating safety considerations for Fitzpatrick skin types III–VI.

**Table 1: Modality Selection Matrix by Condition**

| Condition | First-Line Modality | Second-Line Modality | Third-Line/Adjunct | Key Safety Considerations |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| Melasma | Picosecond 1064 nm (low-fluence) | Q-Switched Nd:YAG 1064 nm | IPL (with caution) | High PIH risk in Fitzpatrick IV–VI; avoid active phase [10] |
| Solar Lentigines | Picosecond 532 nm | Q-Switched 532 nm | IPL 515–560 nm | Lower PIH risk; epidermal pigment responds well [26] |
| Rosacea (Erythema) | IPL 515–590 nm | Pulsed Dye Laser 585–595 nm | LED (adjunctive) | Targets oxyhaemoglobin; vascular component critical [7] |
| Photoaging (Texture) | Fractional Non-Ablative 1550 nm | IPL (broad spectrum) | LED Red/NIR | Multiple sessions required; downtime varies [25] |
| Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation | Picosecond 1064 nm (very low fluence) | Topical agents first | Avoid aggressive laser | High recurrence risk; treat underlying inflammation first [20] |

**Mechanistic Rationale for Modality Selection**

The distinction between photothermal and photomechanical mechanisms fundamentally informs safety and efficacy profiles. Traditional Q-switched and IPL devices operate primarily through photothermal mechanisms, where light energy converts to heat, causing selective photothermolysis of melanin-containing structures [45]. This thermal diffusion poses elevated risks for darker skin types, where epidermal melanin competes for energy absorption, increasing post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) incidence.

Picosecond lasers introduce a photomechanical (photoacoustic) mechanism, characterized by extremely brief pulse durations and substantial peak power density that rely on photoacoustic destruction rather than photo-thermolysis [39]. The heightened photomechanical effect and reduced heat diffusion improve safety profiles for Fitzpatrick IV–VI skin types, making picosecond platforms preferable for melasma and patients with darker phototypes [20].

**Fitzpatrick Skin Type Guidance**

Wavelength selection must account for melanin absorption spectra. The 1064 nm wavelength demonstrates the lowest absorption in surface melanin, making it the safest option for Fitzpatrick IV–VI skin types [6]. In contrast, 532 nm wavelengths carry higher epidermal absorption and should be used cautiously or avoided in darker skin types. Studies indicate that combination therapy was beneficial in patients with Fitzpatrick skin types II and III, but half of patients with darker skin developed PIH when inappropriate wavelengths or fluences were employed [25].

**Critical Safety Warning: IPL Use in Melasma**

IPL therapy for melasma requires explicit caution due to documented risks of rebound pigmentation and disease exacerbation. Evidence indicates that one patient out of 26 receiving conventional IPL experienced adverse effects including post-procedure rebound pigmentation [44]. The active melasma phase, characterized by spot enlargement, darkening, or redness in the preceding three months, is less responsive to laser treatments and more likely to lead to adverse effects such as increased pigmentation or post-inflammatory complications [10].

Goldman et al. demonstrated that IPL could be particularly helpful for moderate-to-severe melasma only if combined with aggressive topical maintenance treatment to minimize pathways for pigment recurrence [25]. Monotherapy with IPL in melasma patients, particularly those with Fitzpatrick IV–VI skin, carries unacceptable PIH risk and should be avoided without concurrent tranexamic acid or tyrosinase inhibitor protocols [23].

**LED Therapy Limitations for Hyperpigmentation**

While LED therapy offers clinically supported results for skin texture, wrinkle reduction, and acne improvement, evidence for hyperpigmentation specifically remains thin compared to Laser and IPL modalities [51]. LED should be positioned as adjunctive therapy for overall skin quality and barrier support rather than primary treatment for melanin reduction or depigmentation goals.

### Cost-Efficacy and Treatment Frequency Analysis

Treatment cost-efficacy extends beyond per-session pricing to encompass total sessions required, maintenance frequency, recurrence rates, and complication management costs. Picosecond and Q-switched platforms demonstrate distinct profiles in this regard.

**Session Requirements and Treatment Intervals**

Picosecond-domain pulses achieve equal or higher clearance of pigment in fewer treatment sessions than nanosecond Q-switched lasers, with a lower risk of adverse effects [49]. Clinical protocols typically involve:

- **Picosecond Lasers**: 3–5 sessions at 4–6 week intervals for solar lentigines; 6–10 sessions at 2–4 week intervals for melasma
- **Q-Switched Nd:YAG**: 5–8 sessions at 3–4 week intervals for melasma; 2–4 sessions for discrete lentigines
- **IPL**: 4–6 sessions at 3–4 week intervals for photoaging; variable for pigmentation with higher recurrence

A comparative study design assigned patients to five sessions of treatment spaced 3 weeks apart across different laser platforms, with assessments conducted at each interval and follow-ups at weeks 12 and 20 [19]. Non-ablative fractional laser (NAFL) treatment in general offers a more durable response compared with IPL and Q-switched laser treatments, especially when patients maintained treatment with topical anti-tyrosinase cream before and after treatment, though approximately four treatments are typically required [25].

**Long-Term Value Considerations**

The long-term value equation must account for recurrence rates and maintenance requirements. NAFL treatment demonstrates more durable responses compared to IPL and Q-switched laser treatments when combined with appropriate topical maintenance [25]. However, recurrence occurs with all energy-based treatments, necessitating ongoing maintenance protocols.

Younger patients show better responses to laser interventions, requiring fewer sessions and less fluence, which improves cost-efficacy profiles in this demographic [39]. This age-related response differential should inform treatment counseling and expectation setting.

**Comparative Platform Analysis: Picosecond vs. Q-Switched**

Recent randomized evaluator-blinded trials compare novel Picosecond Alexandrite Laser systems against traditional combined Q-switched and long-pulse Nd:YAG lasers in melasma treatment [10]. Key findings include:

- **Clearance Efficiency**: Picosecond platforms achieve target clearance in 20–30% fewer sessions
- **Safety Profile**: Lower PIH incidence with picosecond devices, particularly in Fitzpatrick IV–VI
- **Downtime**: Picosecond treatments typically involve 24–48 hours of erythema versus 3–7 days for some Q-switched protocols
- **Cost Per Session**: Picosecond platforms command 30–50% premium per session, but reduced session count may offset total cost

A split-faced comparison between 532 nm Q-switched Nd:YAG laser and 532 nm picosecond laser treatment for solar lentigines allowed clinical effects of both lasers to be more accurately evaluated, demonstrating the methodological rigor now applied to platform comparisons [26].

**Statistical Significance and Clinical Meaningfulness**

Meta-analyses of laser therapy for melasma treatment from 2010 to 2024 demonstrate significant efficacy with effect sizes reaching p < 0.00001 and 95% confidence intervals of −0.60 to −1.22 after merging data from 12 randomized controlled trials [19]. Patient satisfaction metrics utilizing 5-category scales show statistical significance at p < 0.05 thresholds when comparing pre-post treatment outcomes [18].

However, statistical significance does not always translate to clinically meaningful improvement. The Melasma Area and Severity Index (MASI) and modified MASI (mMASI) remain the standard outcome measures, with clinically meaningful improvement typically defined as ≥50% reduction from baseline [21].

**Total Cost of Ownership Framework**

Clinicians should counsel patients using a total cost of ownership framework that includes:

1. **Initial Treatment Series**: Session count × per-session cost
2. **Maintenance Therapy**: Quarterly or biannual touch-up sessions
3. **Topical Adjuncts**: Tranexamic acid, tyrosinase inhibitors, sunscreen
4. **Complication Management**: PIH treatment costs if adverse events occur
5. **Opportunity Cost**: Downtime and recovery period impact

Picosecond platforms, despite higher per-session costs, may demonstrate superior total cost-efficacy when complication rates and session counts are factored into long-term projections.

### Future Directions and Emerging Technologies

The field of aesthetic phototherapy continues to evolve with technological innovations addressing current evidence gaps and expanding personalized treatment capabilities. Several emerging directions warrant attention for clinical planning and research investment.

**Wavelength-Specific Irradiation Indices**

A significant innovation addresses the lack of standardized dosimetry in picosecond laser treatments. Scientists have developed the first wavelength-specific irradiation index for picosecond lasers, which are used in clinical practice to treat pigmented lesions [46]. This development, led by Osaka Metropolitan University researchers, aims to improve efficacy and reduce complications from laser treatment by providing quantitative guidance for energy delivery based on wavelength, skin type, and lesion characteristics.

The absence of standardized guidelines currently leads to variability in treatment outcomes, as patients may receive different recommendations depending on their healthcare provider's expertise and preferences [20]. Wavelength-specific indices represent a critical step toward evidence-based parameter selection rather than empiric adjustment.

**Personalized Phototherapy Through AI and Imaging**

As technology evolves, tools like artificial intelligence and advanced imaging may enhance real-time decision-making during treatment sessions [45]. VISIA skin imaging systems and similar platforms enable objective assessment of pigmentation distribution, vascular components, and treatment response over time [23].

Personalized laser therapy rooted in a solid understanding of both wavelengths and individual patient characteristics ensures safer, more precise, and more effective outcomes [45]. AI-driven analysis of treatment outcomes across large patient cohorts may identify optimal parameter combinations for specific phenotypes that would be difficult to discern through conventional clinical observation.

**Evidence Gaps Requiring Investigation**

Several critical evidence gaps limit current clinical guidance:

- **Long-Term Durability Data**: There is a need for more robust, long-term clinical studies to establish recurrence rates and optimal maintenance intervals beyond 12-month follow-up [20]
- **Darker Skin Type Protocols**: Evidence for Fitzpatrick V–VI skin types remains limited, with most studies concentrated on types III–IV
- **Combination Therapy Optimization**: While combination approaches (laser plus tranexamic acid, laser plus topical agents) show promise, optimal sequencing and timing require systematic investigation
- **Home Device Validation**: Consumer-grade LED and low-energy laser devices lack rigorous clinical validation compared to professional platforms

**Emerging Topical Adjuncts**

The review confirms that hydroquinone remains the most effective rapid-response topical treatment for melasma, but its long-term use is limited by its adverse effect profile [8]. Thiamidol, tranexamic acid, and topical metformin have emerged as promising alternatives with favorable safety profiles for maintenance therapy following energy-based interventions.

The adjunctive use of topical tranexamic acid with a 755 nm picosecond alexandrite laser has shown superior efficacy in reducing melasma severity and enhancing patient satisfaction compared to monotherapy or regimens involving hydroquinone [49]. This combination approach represents a paradigm shift toward multimodal management rather than device-only treatment.

**Technological Innovations on the Horizon**

Several technological developments show promise for improving treatment precision and safety:

- **Diffractive Lens Arrays**: Enable fractional photomechanical effects with picosecond platforms, creating laser-induced optical breakdown (LIOB) in the dermis while preserving epidermal integrity
- **Real-Time Cooling Systems**: Advanced contact cooling and cryogen spray technologies reduce epidermal injury risk during high-fluence treatments
- **Multi-Wavelength Platforms**: Devices combining 532 nm, 755 nm, and 1064 nm wavelengths in a single platform enable condition-specific wavelength selection without equipment changes
- **Reflectance Confocal Microscopy**: In vivo monitoring of melanolysis and pigment clearance enables treatment endpoint determination based on cellular-level changes rather than visual assessment alone [9]

**Regulatory and Standardization Developments**

The lack of standardized guidelines remains a barrier to consistent treatment outcomes across practices. Professional societies and regulatory bodies are working toward consensus statements on:

- Minimum training requirements for energy-based device operators
- Standardized outcome measures beyond MASI (including patient-reported outcomes and quality of life metrics)
- Adverse event reporting frameworks specific to pigmentary disorder treatments
- Contraindication criteria that account for hormonal status, medication use, and skin barrier function

**Research Priorities for 2025–2030**

Future research should prioritize:

1. **Head-to-Head Platform Comparisons**: Randomized trials directly comparing picosecond, Q-switched, and fractional platforms for identical indications
2. **Maintenance Protocol Optimization**: Defining evidence-based intervals and parameters for long-term maintenance therapy
3. **Biomarker Development**: Identifying molecular or imaging biomarkers that predict treatment response and recurrence risk
4. **Cost-Effectiveness Analyses**: Health economic studies incorporating total cost of ownership and quality-adjusted life year metrics
5. **Diverse Population Inclusion**: Ensuring clinical trials adequately represent Fitzpatrick V–VI skin types and underrepresented demographic groups

The trajectory of aesthetic phototherapy points toward increasingly personalized, mechanism-informed treatment selection with robust evidence supporting modality choice. Clinicians who integrate emerging technologies with established safety principles will be best positioned to deliver optimal outcomes while minimizing adverse events in diverse patient populations.
