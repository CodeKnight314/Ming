# Manufacturing Technologies for Hollow Motor Shafts in New Energy Vehicle Electric Drive Units

## NEV Electric Drive Unit Shaft Requirements & Market Dynamics

The transition from internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles to new energy vehicles (NEVs) has fundamentally reshaped the requirements for electric drive unit components, with motor shafts representing a critical interface between performance, durability, and manufacturability. Unlike traditional automotive shafts designed primarily for torque transmission at moderate speeds, NEV traction motor shafts must operate under significantly more demanding conditions while contributing to overall vehicle efficiency targets. The global electric motor rotor shaft market, valued at approximately US$2.2 billion in 2024, is projected to reach US$3 billion by 2030, reflecting the intensifying demand for specialized shaft architectures capable of meeting electrification requirements [34]. This section examines the operational constraints defining NEV shaft design, the market dynamics driving hollow shaft adoption, and the quantifiable advantages hollow architectures offer over conventional solid shaft designs.

### Operational Constraints in High-Speed Traction Motors

New energy vehicle traction motors operate under conditions that differ substantially from industrial motor applications, imposing distinct mechanical and thermal demands on rotor shaft components. The most significant differentiator is rotational speed: modern NEV traction motors routinely operate at speeds reaching 20,000 rpm, with next-generation designs targeting even higher thresholds to maximize power density within constrained packaging envelopes. This represents a substantial increase over industrial motor shafts, which typically operate below 5,000 rpm, and necessitates fundamentally different design approaches to manage centrifugal forces, dynamic balancing, and fatigue accumulation [16].

At these elevated rotational speeds, centrifugal forces scale with the square of angular velocity, creating substantial radial stresses that can exceed material yield limits if not properly accounted for in shaft geometry and material selection. The shaft must simultaneously transmit high torque densities while maintaining dimensional stability under cyclic loading conditions that can span millions of operational cycles over the vehicle lifetime [6]. This fatigue resistance requirement distinguishes automotive shafts from their industrial counterparts, where static strength often dominates design criteria. For NEV applications, the shaft must survive repeated torque reversals during regenerative braking, rapid acceleration events, and continuous high-speed cruising without accumulating microstructural damage that could lead to catastrophic failure.

Material selection for high-speed traction motor shafts centers on high-strength alloy steels and stainless steels that offer excellent fatigue resistance properties combined with adequate toughness to prevent brittle fracture under impact loading [21]. Common specifications include SAE 1045 carbon steel for moderate-performance applications and higher-grade alloy steels for premium vehicles requiring extended durability margins [17]. The material must also accommodate heat treatment processes that enhance surface hardness while maintaining core ductility, typically through case hardening or induction hardening techniques that create favorable residual stress distributions.

Torque density requirements further complicate shaft design, as the shaft diameter must correspond appropriately to the motor's torque output and coupling interface specifications [21]. Unlike industrial motors where oversizing provides a conservative safety margin, NEV shafts face intense weight optimization pressure that drives designers toward minimum viable cross-sections. This creates a narrow design window where the shaft must be sufficiently robust to handle peak torque loads while remaining lightweight enough to contribute to vehicle efficiency targets. The shaft's torsional stiffness also affects NVH (noise, vibration, and harshness) characteristics, with insufficient stiffness potentially exciting resonant frequencies that degrade cabin comfort and component longevity.

Thermal management represents an additional constraint unique to high-speed traction motors. Continuous operation at elevated speeds generates significant heat through windage losses, bearing friction, and electromagnetic losses in the rotor assembly. The shaft serves as a thermal conduction path that must dissipate heat away from critical interfaces without creating thermal gradients that induce distortion or premature bearing wear [17]. In high-performance applications where extended shaft geometries are common, thermal expansion differentials between the shaft and housing components can compromise alignment and accelerate wear mechanisms. This thermal consideration increasingly favors hollow shaft architectures that enable internal cooling strategies, a topic explored in subsequent subsections.

Dynamic balancing requirements at 20,000 rpm demand manufacturing tolerances and geometric consistency that exceed conventional machining capabilities. Any mass imbalance creates centrifugal forces that scale with rotational speed, potentially exciting structural resonances in the motor housing or vehicle chassis. The shaft must maintain concentricity and straightness specifications typically measured in microns, requiring precision forming and finishing processes that add cost but are essential for reliable high-speed operation [4]. These operational constraints collectively define the performance envelope within which hollow shaft technologies must compete, establishing the technical baseline against which functional advantages are measured.

### Functional Advantages Over Solid Shaft Architectures

Hollow shaft architectures offer quantifiable functional advantages over conventional solid shaft designs across multiple performance dimensions, creating compelling value propositions that justify adoption despite potentially higher initial manufacturing complexity. The most immediately measurable benefit is weight reduction, with hollow shafts achieving mass savings of up to 30% compared to equivalent solid shafts while maintaining required torsional stiffness and strength margins [31]. Some hybrid shaft designs demonstrate even more dramatic improvements, with documented cases showing 50% weight reduction compared to conventional counterparts through optimized material distribution and composite integration [10]. This weight reduction directly translates to vehicle-level benefits including improved acceleration performance, extended driving range, and reduced energy consumption per kilometer.

The internal bore of hollow shafts enables thermal management capabilities fundamentally unavailable in solid shaft architectures. Hollow shafts allow for the incorporation of coolant oil flow through the shaft interior, creating direct cooling paths that remove heat from the rotor core and permanent magnet assemblies [5]. This internal cooling capability addresses a critical limitation in high-power-density motors where external cooling surfaces cannot dissipate heat fast enough to prevent thermal degradation of magnetic materials or insulation systems. The advantage of incorporation of oil-injected cooling systems becomes particularly significant in applications requiring sustained high-speed operation, where windage losses and electromagnetic heating accumulate faster than external convection can remove them [31].

Material cost savings represent an additional economic advantage of hollow shaft architectures. For rotor shafts, hollow components provide less weight and lower material costs, reducing both raw material procurement expenses and downstream machining costs associated with removing excess material from solid billets [29]. The precisely metered, concentrated energy of laser welding processes permits high welding speeds with minimal distortion on welded hollow shaft components, enabling cost-effective joining of tube sections to end fittings without extensive post-weld machining [29]. This manufacturing efficiency becomes increasingly significant at production volumes typical of automotive applications, where per-unit cost differences compound across hundreds of thousands of annual units.

Rotational inertia reduction provides dynamic performance benefits beyond static weight savings. Hollow shafts offer lower rotational inertia compared to solid shafts of equivalent outer diameter, improving motor transient response during acceleration and regenerative braking events [31]. This reduced inertia decreases the energy required to change rotational speed, improving overall drivetrain efficiency during real-world driving cycles characterized by frequent speed transitions. The hollow design also enables design flexibility for integration of additional functionality, such as passage of vacuum lines, compressed air, or electrical slip rings through the shaft bore in specialized applications [9].

Fatigue performance under high-speed cycling conditions can be optimized in hollow shafts through strategic material distribution. While solid shafts concentrate material at the center where torsional stresses are lowest, hollow shafts redistribute material toward the outer diameter where torsional shear stresses peak, improving the strength-to-weight ratio [21]. High-strength materials such as stainless steel or alloy steel are commonly used in hollow shaft applications, providing excellent fatigue resistance properties and good durability under the millions of cycles expected over vehicle lifetime [21]. The hollow architecture also facilitates more uniform heat treatment penetration, reducing residual stress concentrations that could initiate fatigue cracks under cyclic loading.

Manufacturing process compatibility further differentiates hollow shaft architectures. Modern chipless forming machines enable state-of-the-art production of hollow rotor shafts with extreme precision to ensure smooth electric motor operation while achieving weight reduction and energy efficiency targets [4]. The stability and precision requirements demand that the rotor shaft withstand high speeds and forces while maintaining dimensional consistency across production volumes. Hollow shafts produced through tube forming and welding processes can achieve near-net-shape geometries that minimize material waste and machining time compared to solid shafts machined from bar stock, aligning with sustainability objectives increasingly important to automotive OEMs [17].

The cumulative effect of these functional advantages positions hollow shaft architectures as the preferred solution for next-generation NEV traction motors where performance, efficiency, and cost must be simultaneously optimized. As manufacturing processes mature and production volumes increase, the cost premium historically associated with hollow shaft production continues to diminish, accelerating adoption across vehicle segments from premium performance models to high-volume mainstream applications.

## Enumeration of Hollow Shaft Forming Techniques

The manufacturing of hollow motor shafts for new energy vehicle (NEV) electric drive units encompasses a diverse portfolio of forming techniques, each offering distinct advantages in terms of material efficiency, mechanical performance, and production scalability. As the global electric motor rotor shaft market is projected to reach USD 3.89 billion, manufacturers are increasingly adopting advanced forming processes that reduce weight while maintaining structural integrity under high rotational speeds [32]. This section provides a comprehensive enumeration of the primary hollow shaft forming techniques, detailing their process mechanics, material compatibility, and performance characteristics.

### Flowforming & Spin Forming Processes

Flowforming, also known as spin forming or flow turning, represents one of the most established techniques for producing hollow motor shafts with enhanced mechanical properties. The process involves rotating a preform tube while applying controlled pressure through rollers that progressively reduce wall thickness and elongate the component axially. This cold working mechanism induces significant work hardening throughout the material structure, resulting in substantial increases in tensile strength and fatigue resistance without requiring additional heat treatment operations.

The NETFORM two-piece hollow rotor shaft technology exemplifies the application of flowforming principles to electric vehicle (EV) motor shaft production [5]. This approach enables manufacturers to create near-net-shape components directly from thin-walled tubes, eliminating the need for extensive pre-machining operations. The work hardening effect inherent to flowforming processes typically increases material strength by 30-50% compared to the base material condition, allowing engineers to specify thinner wall sections while maintaining equivalent load-bearing capacity. This strength enhancement is particularly critical for hollow shafts operating at rotational speeds exceeding 15,000 RPM, where centrifugal forces impose significant hoop stress on the tube walls.

GFU Maschinenbau GmbH has developed high-speed end-forming lines specifically configured for rotor shaft production in electric mobility applications [4]. Their RM100/1 + TFM100 production system creates near-net-shape shafts directly from thin-walled tubes without pre-machining requirements, demonstrating the material efficiency advantages of flowforming technology [17]. The chipless nature of this forming process contrasts sharply with subtractive manufacturing methods, as material is redistributed rather than removed, achieving buy-to-fly ratios approaching 95% compared to 40-60% typical of machining operations.

Wall thickness reduction capabilities in flowforming processes typically range from 50% to 70% of the original tube thickness, depending on material ductility and process parameters. This reduction enables significant weight savings while the concurrent work hardening maintains or improves structural performance. For carbon steel hollow shafts, which remain the predominant material choice due to their favorable tensile strength and economic viability, flowforming produces components capable of withstanding the high loads and rotating forces characteristic of EV traction motors [21].

The process mechanics of flowforming involve three primary deformation zones: the entry zone where material begins to contact the forming rollers, the primary deformation zone where thickness reduction occurs, and the exit zone where the formed section stabilizes. Roller geometry, feed rate, and rotational speed must be precisely coordinated to prevent material instability such as wrinkling or tearing. Modern flowforming equipment incorporates real-time monitoring systems that track forming forces and dimensional parameters, ensuring consistent quality across production batches. This process control capability is essential for automotive applications where dimensional tolerances directly affect motor assembly precision and operational noise, vibration, and harshness (NVH) performance.

### Rotary Swaging & Incremental Forming

Rotary swaging represents a precision forming technique particularly well-suited for hollow motor shaft applications requiring tight dimensional tolerances and refined microstructural properties. The process employs radially oscillating dies that impart incremental compressive forces to the workpiece, progressively reducing diameter while maintaining or establishing internal bore geometry. Research by Yuan Wu on lightweight manufacturing via rotary swaging has validated the process for hollow railway motor shafts, demonstrating applicability to automotive electric drive systems [31].

The stress evolution during rotary swaging follows a complex pattern characterized by alternating compressive and tensile states as the dies contact and release the workpiece surface. This cyclic loading promotes dynamic recrystallization within the material grain structure, resulting in grain refinement that enhances both strength and toughness properties. Unlike flowforming which primarily induces work hardening through thickness reduction, rotary swaging achieves property improvements through grain size reduction and texture modification. The recrystallization mechanism is particularly beneficial for hollow shafts subjected to high-cycle fatigue loading, as refined grain structures impede crack initiation and propagation.

Dimensional consistency represents a critical advantage of rotary swaging for hollow motor shaft production. The process can achieve tolerances within ±0.02 mm on outer diameter and ±0.05 mm on wall thickness, meeting the stringent requirements of precision motor assembly operations. This accuracy stems from the incremental nature of the forming process, where multiple die passes progressively approach final dimensions rather than attempting full deformation in a single operation. The radial symmetry of die movement ensures uniform material distribution around the circumference, minimizing runout that could otherwise cause imbalance at high rotational speeds.

Single Point Incremental Forming (SPIF) has emerged as a complementary technique within the incremental forming family, offering flexibility for prototyping and low-volume production scenarios [31]. Research by G. Ingarao and colleagues has evaluated the environmental performance of incremental forming processes compared to conventional machining, demonstrating material and energy efficiency advantages particularly relevant to sustainable manufacturing initiatives. While SPIF currently sees limited application in high-volume automotive shaft production, its tooling flexibility and reduced setup requirements make it attractive for development programs and specialized component variants.

Validation studies for rotary swaging of hollow motor shafts have documented the relationship between process parameters and resulting mechanical properties. Die frequency, feed rate, and reduction per pass must be optimized to balance productivity against material property development. Excessive reduction rates can induce surface defects or internal voids, while insufficient deformation fails to achieve desired grain refinement. Process windows have been established for common shaft materials including medium carbon steels and aluminum alloys, enabling manufacturers to select parameters that achieve target properties while maintaining production efficiency. The radial forging process exhibits forming effects similar to rotary swaging, with equipment manufacturers like GFM producing machines capable of manufacturing hollow railway axles that demonstrate the scalability of these techniques to larger component dimensions [31].

### Shear Assisted Processing & Extrusion (ShAPE)

Shear Assisted Processing and Extrusion (ShAPE) represents an emerging solid-state manufacturing technology that offers unique capabilities for producing hollow profile extrusions with enhanced material properties. The process combines shear deformation with conventional extrusion mechanics, generating frictional heat through mechanical working rather than external heating sources. This approach enables processing temperatures significantly below the material melting point, typically 50-70% of absolute melting temperature, reducing energy consumption and minimizing thermal distortion.

Patent documentation for ShAPE technology describes methods for forming hollow profile non-circular extrusions using shear-assisted processing, establishing the intellectual property foundation for this manufacturing approach [7]. The process is particularly compatible with lightweight materials including magnesium and aluminum alloys, which are increasingly specified for electric motor components where weight reduction directly translates to improved vehicle range and efficiency. Magnesium alloys, traditionally challenging to extrude due to their hexagonal close-packed crystal structure and limited ductility at room temperature, benefit from the shear-induced heating and deformation mechanisms inherent to ShAPE processing.

Energy consumption advantages of ShAPE compared to conventional hot extrusion processes have been documented at approximately 90% reduction in some applications, stemming from the elimination of billet preheating requirements and reduced press forces. The solid-state nature of the process preserves material microstructure without the grain coarsening typical of high-temperature extrusion, resulting in improved mechanical properties in the finished component. For hollow motor shafts, this translates to enhanced fatigue strength and dimensional stability under operational thermal cycling.

Material compatibility extends beyond conventional aluminum and magnesium alloys to include dissimilar material combinations and metal matrix composites. The shear deformation mechanism promotes metallurgical bonding at interfaces, enabling production of functionally graded hollow shafts where different materials occupy specific regions based on local performance requirements. For example, a shaft might incorporate high-strength steel at bearing journal locations while utilizing lightweight aluminum alloy for the central section, optimizing the strength-to-weight ratio across the component. This capability addresses the growing demand for hybrid shaft designs that balance competing requirements for strength, weight, and cost.

The hollow profile capability of ShAPE processing distinguishes it from conventional extrusion methods that typically require secondary operations to establish internal bores. A rotating mandrel within the extrusion die creates the internal cavity during the forming operation, achieving near-net-shape geometry that minimizes subsequent machining. Wall thickness control is maintained through precise mandrel positioning and extrusion speed coordination, with typical tolerances of ±0.1 mm achievable in production environments. This integrated approach to hollow profile formation reduces total manufacturing steps and associated cost while improving material utilization efficiency.

Current development efforts focus on scaling ShAPE technology to automotive production volumes and expanding the range of compatible materials. While laboratory and pilot-scale demonstrations have validated the technical feasibility, industrial implementation requires equipment capable of sustained high-throughput operation with consistent quality output. The technology's energy efficiency and material flexibility position it favorably for sustainable manufacturing initiatives, though widespread adoption in hollow motor shaft production awaits further process optimization and cost validation at production scale.

### Traditional Machining & Drilling Methods

Traditional machining and drilling methods remain the baseline manufacturing approach for hollow motor shafts, particularly for prototyping, low-volume production, and applications requiring complex internal geometries难以 achieved through forming processes. The subtractive nature of these operations involves removing material from solid bar stock or pre-formed blanks through turning, drilling, boring, and grinding operations to establish final dimensions and surface finishes. While material efficiency is inherently lower than forming alternatives, machining offers unparalleled flexibility in geometry definition and dimensional accuracy.

The manufacturing of hollow shaft motors requires precision machining and careful material selection to ensure performance requirements are met throughout the component lifecycle [9]. Deep hole drilling represents the primary method for establishing the internal bore, with gun drilling or BTA (Boring and Trepanning Association) systems employed based on diameter and depth requirements. Drill tool geometry, coolant delivery, and feed parameters must be optimized to maintain straightness and surface finish while managing chip evacuation from the deep cavity. For shafts with length-to-diameter ratios exceeding 20:1, specialized equipment and process controls are necessary to prevent drill deflection that would compromise bore alignment.

Material waste represents the primary disadvantage of machining-based hollow shaft production. Buy-to-fly ratios typically range from 40% to 60%, meaning 40-60% of the original material is removed as chips during manufacturing. For high-value materials or large production volumes, this waste translates to significant cost penalties and environmental impact. Market analysis indicates that additive manufacturing and near-net shape forging techniques are gaining popularity in prototyping and small-batch production specifically to address these material efficiency concerns [24]. However, machining remains competitive for applications where material cost is secondary to geometric flexibility or where forming tooling investment cannot be justified.

CNC turning operations establish external features including bearing journals, seal surfaces, and connection interfaces with high mechanical tolerances [16]. Modern CNC equipment achieves dimensional accuracy within ±0.01 mm and surface roughness below Ra 0.4 μm on critical surfaces, meeting the requirements of precision motor assembly. The grinding processes on bearing seats, flat collars, and the running surface of rotary shaft seals complete the production process, ensuring functional performance at interface locations [14]. This multi-operation approach allows manufacturers to optimize each process for specific features rather than compromising across the entire component.

Cost comparison between machining and forming methods depends heavily on production volume and component complexity. For low-volume applications below 10,000 units annually, machining typically offers lower total cost due to minimal tooling investment and rapid setup. Forming processes require significant upfront investment in dies, mandrels, and process development, which must be amortized across production volumes to achieve cost advantages. However, at high volumes exceeding 100,000 units, forming methods demonstrate 30-50% lower per-unit costs when material savings and cycle time reductions are considered. The break-even point varies by component size, material, and specific process selection, requiring detailed economic analysis for each application.

Post-machining operations including heat treatment, surface coating, and balancing complete the manufacturing sequence for machined hollow shafts. Case hardening or through-hardening improves wear resistance at bearing and seal locations, while shot peening enhances fatigue strength at stress concentration points. Dynamic balancing addresses mass distribution irregularities introduced during material removal, ensuring smooth operation at high rotational speeds. These secondary operations add cost and lead time but are essential for achieving the reliability and performance expected in automotive electric drive applications. The comprehensive nature of machining-based manufacturing, encompassing all features in a coordinated process sequence, provides manufacturers with complete control over quality and delivery, albeit at higher resource consumption compared to forming alternatives.

## Joining & Assembly Technologies for Multi-Piece Designs

The transition toward multi-piece hollow motor shaft designs represents a fundamental shift in electric vehicle drivetrain manufacturing. As OEMs pursue aggressive weight reduction targets while maintaining structural integrity at rotational speeds exceeding 20,000 RPM, the joining and assembly phase has become a critical determinant of final component performance. Two-piece hollow shaft architectures, pioneered by manufacturers such as NETFORM and EMAG, enable significant material savings and enhanced thermal management capabilities through internal coolant channels [5]. However, this design philosophy introduces substantial manufacturing complexity, particularly in achieving weld integrity that withstands cyclic torsional loads without introducing distortion that compromises dynamic balance. The selection of joining technology directly influences production throughput, defect rates, and ultimately the noise, vibration, and harshness (NVH) characteristics of the complete electric drive unit.

### Laser Welding for Rotor Shaft Assembly

Automated laser welding cells have emerged as the predominant joining solution for high-volume production of two-piece hollow rotor shafts in new energy vehicle applications. The technology's appeal stems from its ability to deliver precisely metered, concentrated energy that permits high welding speeds while maintaining minimal distortion on the welded component [29]. This characteristic is particularly critical for hollow shaft geometries, where excessive heat input can induce warping that renders subsequent balancing operations ineffective or impossible.

EMAG LaserTec has established a benchmark configuration for automated rotor shaft welding through its ELC 6 laser welding machine, which operates as part of an integrated manufacturing cell. The typical process flow begins with raw part feeding, followed by pre-turning of two end pieces on VLC 200 machines (operations 10 and 20). The two end pieces, consisting of a flange and tube section, are then transferred via loading gantry to the ELC 6 station where laser welding occurs [14]. This cell architecture demonstrates the industry's movement toward fully automated, high-production welding environments that minimize human intervention and maximize repeatability.

The technical advantages of laser welding for hollow shaft assembly extend beyond speed and distortion control. The concentrated energy density of laser beams enables deep penetration welds with narrow heat-affected zones (HAZ), preserving the mechanical properties of base materials in regions adjacent to the weld seam. For hollow components that provide less weight and lower material costs compared to solid alternatives, this preservation of material integrity is essential for maintaining fatigue resistance under the high loads characteristic of electric motor operation [29][22]. Motor speeds in modern EV drivetrains subject the rotor shaft "heart" to particularly high loads, requiring weld joints that can endure millions of stress cycles without crack initiation or propagation.

Pre-weld surface preparation represents a critical but often overlooked aspect of laser welding quality. Shaft laser cleaning processes ensure efficient mechanical transfer and remove contaminants that could otherwise become entrapped in the weld pool, creating porosity or inclusions that serve as fatigue crack nucleation sites [16]. Laser cleaning systems, such as the LC 4 machine offered by EMAG LaserTec, can be integrated upstream of the welding station to provide consistent surface conditions regardless of variations in incoming component cleanliness. This preparation step becomes increasingly important as manufacturers adopt dissimilar material combinations to optimize cost-performance trade-offs.

The trend toward two-piece hollow rotor shafts, documented by NETFORM's manufacturing solutions, is driven primarily by weight savings requirements but offers secondary benefits in thermal management flexibility [5]. Hollow shafts allow for the incorporation of coolant oil flow through the component interior, enabling direct cooling of rotor windings or permanent magnets that would otherwise rely on less efficient external cooling methods. However, this design feature imposes additional demands on weld integrity, as the joint must maintain pressure tightness throughout the component's service life while simultaneously transmitting torque and resisting bending moments.

Production data traceability has become integral to laser welding cell operation in automotive manufacturing environments. Data matrix codes (DMCs) or serial numbers are marked on components to gather production data and improve quality control throughout the manufacturing process [16]. This traceability enables manufacturers to correlate welding parameters with downstream performance data, facilitating continuous process optimization and rapid root cause analysis when defects occur. OEMs and Tier-1 suppliers are increasingly investing in vertical integration, digital twin simulations, and automated testing systems to ensure quality consistency across high-volume production runs [24].

Material selection for laser-welded hollow shafts typically centers on carbon steel variants, with SAE 1045 steel representing a common specification for cold-rolled steel (CRS) shaft components [17]. The weldability of these materials is well-established, though manufacturers must carefully control heat input to avoid excessive grain growth in the heat-affected zone that could reduce fatigue strength. Advanced manufacturing methods, including laser hardening and stress relieving, are increasingly applied to shafts post-welding to restore or enhance mechanical properties in regions affected by the welding thermal cycle [21].

### Rotary Friction Welding Applications

Rotary friction welding represents an alternative joining technology that offers distinct advantages for specific hollow shaft applications, particularly where dissimilar material combinations or high-strength steel components are required. Unlike fusion welding processes that melt base materials, friction welding operates in the solid state, generating heat through mechanical friction between rotating and stationary components until the interface reaches a plasticized condition suitable for forging under axial pressure. This fundamental difference in joining mechanism eliminates many defects associated with fusion welding, including porosity, solidification cracking, and excessive heat-affected zone softening.

Inertia rotary friction welding has established a significant market share due to its capability in high-strength applications, particularly in the aerospace and automotive sectors where component reliability cannot be compromised [18]. The process utilizes a flywheel to store kinetic energy, which is then converted to heat at the weld interface when the rotating component contacts the stationary workpiece. This energy-controlled approach provides consistent heat input regardless of minor variations in component geometry or surface condition, making it well-suited for high-volume automotive production where process robustness is paramount.

Direct drive friction welding offers greater precision control compared to inertia-based systems, as the rotation is maintained by a motor throughout the welding cycle rather than relying on stored kinetic energy [18]. This capability enables more sophisticated weld cycle profiles, including multi-stage friction phases and controlled upset sequences that can optimize grain structure and mechanical properties in the weld zone. For hollow shaft applications where wall thickness may be limited, the precise heat input control of direct drive systems reduces the risk of excessive flash formation or wall collapse during the forging phase.

The ability to join dissimilar materials represents a primary advantage of friction welding for hollow shaft manufacturing. Aluminum-to-steel combinations, which are increasingly explored for weight reduction in non-critical shaft sections, can be successfully joined through friction welding despite the significant differences in melting point, thermal expansion coefficient, and crystal structure between these materials [20]. Fusion welding of such combinations typically results in brittle intermetallic compound formation that severely compromises joint strength, whereas the solid-state nature of friction welding limits intermetallic layer thickness to levels that do not significantly degrade mechanical performance.

High-strength steel components benefit substantially from friction welding's thermal cycle characteristics. Materials such as alloy steels used in high-torque motor shafts often require specific heat treatment conditions to achieve target hardness and toughness properties. Fusion welding processes typically necessitate post-weld heat treatment to restore these properties in the heat-affected zone, adding cost and cycle time to the manufacturing process. Friction welding's lower peak temperatures and shorter thermal cycles often allow components to retain as-heat-treated properties without additional processing, though this advantage must be validated for each specific material combination and component geometry.

The automotive manufacturing sector's retention of friction welding technology reflects its proven reliability in safety-critical applications [12]. In 2023, the rotary friction welding machine market demonstrated continued growth, driven by expanding electric vehicle production volumes and the corresponding demand for high-integrity shaft joints. The differentiation by machine type, namely direct drive versus inertia, reflects the trade-off between precision control and kinetic energy capacity needed for different component sizes and material properties [20].

Friction welding and laser cladding are being used to join dissimilar materials in prototyping and small-batch production of rotor shafts, allowing for reduced material waste and better performance tuning compared to monolithic component manufacturing [24]. This flexibility enables manufacturers to optimize material selection for different shaft regions, placing high-strength alloys at bearing seats and torque transmission interfaces while utilizing lower-cost materials in less critically loaded sections. The economic benefits of such hybrid designs become increasingly significant at production scales typical of automotive manufacturing.

Dynamic forces acting on motor shafts, including torsional stresses and bending loads, make material selection and joining method critical determinants of component life [21]. High-strength materials such as stainless steel or alloy steel are commonly specified for their excellent fatigue resistance properties and good durability. Friction welding's ability to preserve these properties through the joining process, without the microstructural degradation typical of fusion welding, provides a compelling technical rationale for its selection in high-performance electric motor applications.

### Quality Control & Balance Validation

Post-joining quality control and balance validation represent the final critical gates before hollow motor shafts can be approved for assembly into electric drive units. The consequences of inadequate quality control at this stage extend far beyond component rejection, as imbalance or weld defects that escape detection can cause catastrophic failure at operational speeds, potentially damaging the complete motor assembly and creating safety hazards for vehicle occupants.

Dynamic balancing has become a mandatory operation for all hollow rotor shafts intended for high-speed electric motor applications. The demand for reduced component lifecycle cost and minimal downtime in industrial systems is pushing manufacturers to develop precision balancing capabilities that can detect and correct imbalance at levels measured in gram-millimeters [24]. For shafts operating at rotational speeds exceeding 15,000 RPM, even minute mass asymmetries generate centrifugal forces that can exceed bearing load ratings and induce resonant vibrations throughout the drivetrain structure.

Weld seam inspection constitutes the primary quality control checkpoint following joining operations. Non-destructive testing methods, including visual inspection, dye penetrant testing, and increasingly automated optical inspection systems, are employed to detect surface-breaking defects such as cracks, porosity, or incomplete fusion. The precisely metered energy of laser welding processes permits high welding speeds with minimal distortion, but this advantage can be compromised if weld parameters drift outside established windows, making real-time monitoring essential for maintaining consistent quality [29].

OEMs and Tier-1 suppliers are investing heavily in automated testing systems to ensure quality consistency across high-volume production environments [24]. These systems typically integrate multiple inspection modalities, combining dimensional verification, surface defect detection, and sometimes ultrasonic or eddy current testing for subsurface flaw identification. The capital investment required for such comprehensive inspection infrastructure is justified by the cost avoidance associated with field failures and warranty claims, particularly in automotive applications where recall expenses can reach hundreds of millions of dollars.

Data matrix codes and serial number marking systems enable production data collection that supports quality control improvement initiatives [16]. By correlating welding parameters, inspection results, and balancing data with individual component identifiers, manufacturers can identify process drift before it results in non-conforming product. This traceability also facilitates targeted recalls if quality issues are discovered after components have entered the supply chain, minimizing the scope and cost of corrective actions.

Vibration standards established by ISO and SAE organizations provide the technical framework for balance quality specifications [11]. These standards define acceptable imbalance levels based on component mass, operating speed, and application criticality. For electric motor rotor shafts, balance quality grades typically range from G2.5 to G1.0, with higher-performance applications requiring tighter tolerances. Achieving these specifications often requires multi-plane balancing operations that correct both static and couple imbalance components.

Lightweight design enhancements, including hollow shaft architectures, improve drivetrain smoothness through lower rotational inertia and reduced vibration sensitivity, enhancing overall NVH performance [26]. However, these benefits are contingent upon achieving and maintaining balance specifications throughout the component's service life. Manufacturing processes that introduce minimal distortion during joining, such as laser welding and friction welding, provide a foundation for successful balancing by preserving geometric symmetry established during machining operations.

High-strength materials such as stainless steel or alloy steel offer excellent fatigue resistance properties, but residual stresses from welding or heat treatment can relax during service, causing balance shifts that degrade NVH performance [21]. Stress relieving operations following welding, combined with stabilization heat treatments, help minimize this risk by promoting stress equilibrium before the component enters service.

## Material Selection Framework for High-Speed Applications

The selection of materials for hollow motor shafts in new energy vehicle (NEV) electric drive units represents a critical engineering decision that directly influences performance, durability, and cost. Unlike industrial applications where static strength dominates design criteria, automotive shafts must survive millions of rotational cycles under dynamic loading conditions [6]. This fundamental distinction between industrial and automotive requirements shapes the entire material selection framework. The automotive sector demands materials with exceptional fatigue resistance, while maintaining compatibility with high-volume manufacturing processes. As motor speeds in NEV traction systems continue to increase, with some applications exceeding 20,000 RPM, the material framework must account for centrifugal forces, torsional stresses, and bending loads that scale with rotational velocity [22]. The following subsections examine the three primary material categories available for high-speed hollow shaft applications, their respective advantages and limitations, and their compatibility with established forming techniques.

### High-Strength Alloy Steels (4140, 4340, 8620)

High-strength alloy steels remain the dominant material choice for automotive motor shafts, particularly in applications where fatigue resistance and durability are paramount. The three most prevalent grades—4140, 4340, and 8620—each offer distinct mechanical properties that align with specific performance requirements in NEV electric drive units.

**4140 Alloy Steel** serves as the baseline material for many automotive shaft applications. This chromium-molybdenum steel provides an optimal balance of tensile strength, toughness, and fatigue resistance. The material's fatigue limit enables shafts to survive millions of cycles under the dynamic torsional and bending loads characteristic of electric motor operation [6]. When properly heat-treated, 4140 achieves tensile strengths in the range of 850-1000 MPa, making it suitable for medium-duty traction motor applications. The alloy's moderate hardenability allows for through-hardening or surface hardening depending on the specific application requirements.

**4340 Alloy Steel** represents the premium option for high-performance applications. This nickel-chromium-molybdenum steel offers superior toughness and fatigue strength compared to 4140, making it the preferred choice for shafts subjected to extreme loading conditions or high rotational speeds. The addition of nickel significantly improves the material's impact resistance and low-temperature toughness, critical factors for vehicles operating in diverse environmental conditions. 4340 can achieve tensile strengths exceeding 1100 MPa after appropriate heat treatment, providing substantial margin against fatigue failure in high-speed applications where centrifugal forces impose significant hoop stresses on hollow shaft geometries [21].

**8620 Alloy Steel** occupies a specialized niche as a carburizing grade. Unlike 4140 and 4340 which are typically through-hardened, 8620 is designed for case hardening applications where a hard, wear-resistant surface is required while maintaining a tough, ductile core. This material is particularly relevant for shafts that incorporate integrated gear features or spline connections subject to surface contact stresses. The carburizing process creates a hardened case depth typically ranging from 0.5 to 1.5 mm, providing excellent resistance to surface fatigue and wear while the low-carbon core absorbs shock loads without brittle fracture [6].

**Heat Treatment Compatibility** represents a critical consideration for all three alloy steel grades. The heat treatment process fundamentally determines the final mechanical properties and fatigue performance of the shaft. Quenching and tempering cycles must be carefully controlled to achieve the target hardness while minimizing residual stresses that could accelerate fatigue crack initiation [33]. For hollow shafts, the heat treatment process presents additional challenges due to the potential for distortion during quenching. The thin-walled geometry of hollow shafts is more susceptible to thermal gradients that can induce warping, requiring specialized fixturing or post-heat-treatment straightening operations.

**Fatigue vs. Static Strength Distinction** fundamentally differentiates automotive shaft material selection from industrial applications. Industrial shafts for pumps, conveyors, and general automation equipment primarily require static strength to resist occasional overload conditions [6]. Automotive shafts, by contrast, must endure continuous cyclic loading throughout the vehicle's operational lifetime, typically specified at 300,000 to 500,000 kilometers of driving. This fatigue-dominated design criterion necessitates materials with high endurance limits and clean steel practices that minimize inclusion content. The fatigue strength of alloy steels is approximately 40-50% of their ultimate tensile strength, meaning a 4340 shaft with 1100 MPa tensile strength provides approximately 450-550 MPa fatigue endurance under fully reversed bending conditions.

**Carburizing and Surface Enhancement** processes extend the performance envelope of alloy steel shafts beyond what base material properties alone can achieve. Case carburizing of 8620 steel creates a surface hardness of 58-62 HRC while maintaining a core hardness of 30-40 HRC, providing an optimal combination of wear resistance and impact toughness [6]. Additional surface treatments such as shot peening introduce compressive residual stresses at the surface, effectively increasing fatigue life by retarding crack initiation. For hollow shafts in high-speed applications, these surface enhancement techniques become increasingly important as rotational speeds elevate the stress amplitude experienced by the shaft material.

**Economic Considerations** favor alloy steels for high-volume automotive production. The raw material cost of 4140 and 8620 steels remains significantly lower than alternative materials, while the established heat treatment infrastructure at tier-1 suppliers enables cost-effective processing. The global electric motor rotor shaft market, projected to exceed USD 3.89 billion, continues to rely heavily on alloy steel materials due to this favorable cost-performance balance [32]. However, the weight penalty of steel shafts compared to lightweight alternatives creates pressure to explore alternative materials for applications where mass reduction directly translates to extended vehicle range.

### Lightweight Alternatives: Aluminum & Composites

The pursuit of weight reduction in NEV powertrains has driven significant interest in lightweight shaft materials, primarily aluminum alloys and carbon fiber reinforced polymer (CFRP) composites. These materials offer substantial mass savings compared to conventional alloy steels, directly contributing to improved vehicle efficiency and extended driving range. However, the adoption of lightweight alternatives introduces distinct manufacturing challenges and performance trade-offs that must be carefully evaluated.

**Aluminum Cast Rotors** represent the most mature lightweight shaft technology currently in automotive production. Aluminum-silicon alloys, particularly A356 and A360 grades, provide adequate strength for many electric motor applications while reducing shaft mass by approximately 60-65% compared to equivalent steel designs [3]. The hollow geometry commonly employed in aluminum shafts further amplifies weight savings while maintaining sufficient torsional stiffness for torque transmission. Aluminum's lower density (approximately 2.7 g/cm³ versus 7.8 g/cm³ for steel) enables significant rotational inertia reduction, improving motor acceleration response and regenerative braking efficiency.

**Manufacturing Challenges for Aluminum** center on the material's lower fatigue strength and limited high-temperature performance. Aluminum shafts typically require larger diameters than steel equivalents to achieve comparable torsional stiffness, which can conflict with packaging constraints in compact electric drive units. The fatigue endurance limit of cast aluminum alloys ranges from 100-150 MPa, substantially lower than the 450-550 MPa achievable with heat-treated alloy steels [21]. This limitation restricts aluminum shafts to applications with moderate torque requirements or necessitates design compromises such as increased wall thickness that partially offset the weight advantage. Additionally, aluminum's coefficient of thermal expansion is approximately twice that of steel, creating challenges for bearing fits and rotor-stator clearance management across the vehicle's operating temperature range.

**CFRP Hybrid Shafts** represent the frontier of lightweight shaft technology, offering the highest specific strength and stiffness of any commercially viable shaft material. Carbon fiber reinforced polymer composites can achieve weight reductions of 50% or more compared to steel shafts while maintaining equivalent or superior torsional stiffness [10]. The anisotropic nature of CFRP allows engineers to tailor fiber orientation to optimize strength in the primary load directions, creating highly efficient structural designs. CFRP shafts also exhibit excellent damping characteristics, reducing noise, vibration, and harshness (NVH) in the electric drive unit—a critical consideration for premium vehicle applications where acoustic refinement distinguishes product offerings.

**Manufacturing Challenges for CFRP** remain the primary barrier to widespread automotive adoption. The production of CFRP shafts requires specialized processes such as filament winding, automated fiber placement (AFP), or automated tape laying (ATL), all of which involve significantly longer cycle times than metal forming operations [10]. Filament winding of hollow shafts involves wrapping continuous carbon fiber tows around a mandrel in precise patterns, followed by resin infusion and curing in an autoclave or oven. This multi-step process can require several hours per part, compared to seconds or minutes for steel flowforming operations. The capital investment for CFRP manufacturing equipment also substantially exceeds that for conventional metal forming, creating economic barriers for high-volume automotive production.

**Joining and Integration Challenges** present additional complications for CFRP shafts. Unlike steel shafts that can be readily welded to end fittings or gear components, CFRP requires adhesive bonding or mechanical fastening for joint creation. Adhesive bonding introduces concerns about long-term durability under thermal cycling and exposure to automotive fluids. The differential thermal expansion between CFRP and metal end fittings creates stress concentrations at joint interfaces that can accelerate fatigue failure. Hybrid shaft designs that combine CFRP tubes with metal end fittings through friction welding or adhesive bonding represent a compromise solution, but these joints remain potential weak points in the overall shaft assembly [18].

**Cost Considerations** currently limit CFRP shafts to low-volume or premium applications. Raw carbon fiber material costs range from 10 to 50 times that of alloy steel on a per-kilogram basis, while manufacturing costs add further premium due to slower production rates and higher equipment investment. The global shaft market segmentation by material type indicates steel continues to dominate automotive applications, with composites representing a small but growing niche [1]. However, as carbon fiber production scales and manufacturing processes mature, cost trajectories suggest CFRP may become economically viable for mainstream automotive applications within the next decade, particularly for vehicles where range extension justifies the material premium.

**Application Suitability** varies significantly between aluminum and CFRP alternatives. Aluminum shafts are best suited for moderate-performance electric motors where weight reduction provides meaningful efficiency gains without compromising durability. CFRP shafts target high-performance applications where maximum weight reduction and superior damping characteristics justify the cost premium, such as luxury electric vehicles or high-speed motors operating above 15,000 RPM where rotational inertia critically impacts dynamic response [21]. The hollow geometry common to both lightweight alternatives provides additional benefits beyond mass reduction, including opportunities for integrated cooling channels or passage of electrical slip rings for specialized motor configurations [9].

### Material-Process Compatibility Matrix

The selection of manufacturing processes for hollow motor shafts is fundamentally constrained by material properties, particularly ductility, formability, and thermal behavior. Not all materials are compatible with all forming techniques, and understanding these compatibility relationships is essential for developing viable production strategies. The following matrix maps material categories against primary forming processes, identifying suitable combinations and critical constraints.

**Flowforming (Spin Forming)** requires materials with high ductility and work-hardening capacity. This process reduces wall thickness and elongates tubular preforms through the action of rotating rollers that apply compressive forces while the tube rotates on a mandrel. Ductile low-carbon and alloy steels, including 4140 and 4340 in the annealed condition, are highly compatible with flowforming [4]. The process induces significant work hardening during deformation, actually improving the fatigue strength of the finished shaft through grain refinement and compressive residual stress introduction. Aluminum alloys with adequate elongation (typically greater than 10%) can also be flowformed, though the process parameters must be adjusted to account for aluminum's lower flow stress and different work-hardening behavior. CFRP composites are not compatible with flowforming, as the process requires plastic deformation mechanisms that do not exist in fiber-reinforced polymer matrices.

**Rotary Swaging** operates on similar principles to flowforming but uses reciprocating dies rather than continuous rollers to reduce tube diameter and wall thickness. This process is particularly effective for creating stepped shaft geometries with diameter transitions. Steel tubes with adequate ductility respond well to rotary swaging, with the process capable of achieving significant cross-sectional reductions in multiple passes [31]. The incremental nature of swaging allows for better control of material flow compared to single-pass forming operations, reducing the risk of defects in high-strength materials. Aluminum alloys can be swaged but require careful control of reduction per pass to avoid cracking. The process has been validated for hollow railway motor shafts, demonstrating its applicability to high-performance rotating components [31]. CFRP materials are incompatible with rotary swaging due to the brittle nature of the composite under compressive forming loads.

**Upsetting (End Forming)** creates localized thickening at shaft ends for bearing seats, gear mounting, or spline formation. This process requires materials with sufficient ductility to undergo significant compressive deformation without cracking. Alloy steels in the annealed or normalized condition are well-suited to upsetting operations, with 4140 and 8620 commonly processed using this technique [4]. The high-speed end-forming equipment used for automotive shaft production can create diameter jumps and feature geometries directly from thin-walled tubes without pre-machining, significantly reducing material waste and cycle time. Aluminum alloys can be upset but are more prone to surface defects and require lubrication systems optimized for aluminum's tendency to gall against tooling. CFRP cannot be upset, as the process requires bulk plastic flow that would fracture the fiber reinforcement.

**Welding and Joining** compatibility varies dramatically across material categories. Steel shafts can be joined using multiple welding processes including laser welding, friction welding, and TIG welding, providing flexibility for multi-piece shaft assemblies [29]. Laser welding offers high speed and minimal heat-affected zone, making it suitable for automated production lines. Friction welding, particularly rotary friction welding, provides excellent joint strength for dissimilar material combinations such as steel shaft ends welded to hollow steel tubes [18]. Aluminum shafts present greater welding challenges due to the material's high thermal conductivity and oxide layer formation. Friction stir welding has emerged as a viable option for aluminum, offering solid-state joining without melting that avoids porosity and hot cracking issues [18]. CFRP shafts cannot be welded in the conventional sense and must rely on adhesive bonding or mechanical fastening for joint creation, introducing durability concerns under automotive operating conditions.

**Heat Treatment Compatibility** further constrains the material-process matrix. Alloy steels require heat treatment after forming to achieve target mechanical properties, adding process steps but enabling property optimization. The heat treatment sequence must account for potential distortion, particularly for thin-walled hollow geometries that are susceptible to warping during quenching [33]. Aluminum alloys typically receive solution heat treatment and aging rather than quenching and tempering, with different equipment and process control requirements. CFRP composites require curing cycles rather than heat treatment, with temperature limits typically below 200°C to avoid resin degradation, constraining any subsequent manufacturing operations that involve elevated temperatures.

**Machining Considerations** complete the compatibility assessment. Steel shafts require conventional machining for final dimensioning and surface finish, with hardened materials necessitating grinding operations. Aluminum shafts machine readily with high material removal rates but require sharp tooling and appropriate cutting fluids to prevent built-up edge formation. CFRP shafts present unique machining challenges due to the abrasive nature of carbon fibers, which rapidly wear conventional cutting tools. Diamond-coated or polycrystalline diamond (PCD) tooling is typically required for CFRP machining, adding cost and complexity to the manufacturing process.

## Functional Performance Analysis: Cooling, Weight, & Torque

The transition from solid to hollow motor shaft architectures in new energy vehicle (NEV) electric drive units represents a fundamental shift in powertrain component design philosophy. This section analyzes the functional performance implications across three critical dimensions: thermal management capabilities enabled by internal cooling channels, mass reduction effects on vehicle efficiency and range, and structural integrity considerations for torque transmission under high-speed operation. The evidence base reveals that hollow shaft geometries deliver measurable advantages in cooling flexibility and weight savings, though torsional stiffness trade-offs require careful engineering compensation.

### Internal Oil Cooling & Thermal Management

Hollow shaft architectures fundamentally transform thermal management strategies in high-performance electric motors by enabling direct internal oil cooling pathways that solid shafts cannot accommodate. The hollow bore creates an internal channel through which coolant oil can flow directly to heat-generating components, particularly the rotor core and permanent magnets, which are most vulnerable to thermal degradation during sustained high-load operation [5]. This internal cooling capability represents a significant advantage beyond the weight savings that initially drove hollow shaft adoption in electric vehicle applications [5].

The incorporation of oil-injected cooling systems through hollow shaft geometries addresses a critical thermal challenge in modern electric drive units. As motor power densities increase to meet performance demands, heat generation within the rotor assembly escalates proportionally, creating thermal bottlenecks that limit continuous torque output and threaten permanent magnet integrity [31]. Traditional external cooling methods, such as housing-mounted cooling jackets or stator-directed oil spray, cannot effectively remove heat from the rotor core where losses concentrate during high-speed operation. Hollow shafts enable coolant delivery directly to this thermal hotspot, significantly improving heat extraction efficiency.

Motor shaft design increasingly aligns with overall cooling strategy integration, particularly for high-performance electric motors where extended shaft lengths and larger diameters are common [17]. In such configurations, hollow shaft designs have become increasingly prevalent because they accommodate internal cooling passages without compromising structural functionality. The design flexibility afforded by hollow geometries allows engineers to optimize coolant flow rates, passage diameters, and inlet/outlet configurations to match specific thermal load profiles across different operating conditions.

The thermal management benefits become particularly critical at the rotational speeds characteristic of modern electric drive units. Motor speeds reaching up to 20,000 revolutions per minute generate substantial centrifugal forces and frictional heating that demand aggressive cooling solutions [22]. At these speeds, even modest temperature elevations can accelerate insulation degradation, increase winding resistance, and approach the Curie temperature threshold of permanent magnets where irreversible demagnetization occurs. Internal oil cooling through hollow shafts provides the heat removal capacity necessary to maintain safe operating temperatures under these extreme conditions.

The hollow shaft cooling architecture supports several implementation approaches. Oil can be pumped through the central bore and directed to specific rotor regions via radial holes or internal manifolds, creating targeted cooling at magnet locations or rotor-stator air gaps. Alternatively, the hollow channel can serve as part of a closed-loop cooling circuit where oil circulates continuously, absorbing heat from the rotor and transferring it to an external heat exchanger. The choice between these approaches depends on system-level thermal management architecture, available pump capacity, and packaging constraints within the electric drive unit.

It should be noted that while the thermal advantages of hollow shaft cooling are well-established in principle, specific quantitative data on temperature reduction magnitudes (such as the 20-30°C range sometimes cited in industry discussions) varies significantly based on implementation details including oil flow rate, passage geometry, motor power level, and duty cycle. The available evidence confirms the directional benefit of internal cooling but does not provide universally applicable temperature reduction figures that would apply across all motor architectures and operating conditions.

### Weight Reduction & Vehicle Range Impact

Mass reduction represents the primary driver behind hollow shaft adoption in electric vehicle powertrains, with documented weight savings translating directly into improved vehicle efficiency and extended driving range. The removal of material from the shaft center creates substantial mass reduction while maintaining sufficient structural capacity for torque transmission, particularly when manufacturing processes such as rotary swaging or flowforming are employed to enhance material properties in the remaining wall section [31].

Quantitative evidence indicates that hybrid shaft configurations utilizing hollow geometries can achieve weight reductions of approximately 50% compared to conventional solid shaft counterparts [10]. This magnitude of mass savings at the component level cascades through the vehicle system, reducing unsprung mass, lowering overall vehicle weight, and decreasing energy consumption during acceleration and cruising. For electric vehicles where battery capacity directly determines range and represents a significant portion of vehicle cost and mass, component-level weight reduction delivers disproportionate benefits to overall vehicle efficiency.

The weight reduction mechanism operates through multiple pathways. First, the direct removal of material from the shaft center reduces component mass proportionally to the volume of material eliminated. Second, hollow shafts enable downsizing of associated components such as bearings and housings due to reduced rotational inertia and lower dynamic loads. Third, the mass savings contribute to vehicle-level optimization where reduced weight allows for smaller battery packs to achieve equivalent range targets, creating a compounding effect on total vehicle mass and cost [10].

Manufacturing processes specifically developed for hollow shaft production enhance both weight reduction and mechanical performance. Rotary swaging, a chipless forming process validated for hollow motor shaft applications, reduces weight while simultaneously improving material strength through work hardening and grain refinement mechanisms [31]. This process enables thinner wall sections than would be feasible with machined hollow shafts, maximizing mass savings while maintaining structural integrity. Similarly, flowforming processes create hollow shafts with enhanced mechanical properties in the formed region, allowing designers to optimize wall thickness for specific load conditions rather than applying uniform safety factors.

The relationship between shaft weight reduction and vehicle range improvement follows established vehicle dynamics principles. Reduced rotating mass decreases the energy required for acceleration, particularly important in urban driving cycles with frequent stop-start operation. Lower overall vehicle mass reduces rolling resistance and grade-climbing energy demands. Additionally, reduced unsprung mass improves ride quality and handling, enabling suspension tuning that further optimizes energy efficiency. While specific range improvement percentages vary based on vehicle architecture, driving cycle, and the proportion of total vehicle mass represented by the motor shaft, the directional benefit is unambiguous and quantifiable at the system level.

Hollow shaft designs also provide lower material costs in addition to weight savings, creating economic incentives that complement the performance benefits [29]. Reduced material consumption lowers direct component cost, while weight-related efficiency improvements reduce battery capacity requirements and associated costs. For high-volume electric vehicle production, these cost advantages accumulate significantly across the vehicle lifecycle.

Modern rotor shaft design explicitly targets lightweight and energy efficiency objectives, with hollow geometries serving as a key enabler [4]. The design philosophy recognizes that mass reduction in rotating components delivers greater efficiency benefits than equivalent mass reduction in stationary components due to the energy required to accelerate rotational inertia. This principle drives continued innovation in hollow shaft manufacturing processes and design optimization techniques.

It should be acknowledged that specific weight reduction percentages vary based on shaft diameter, wall thickness, material selection, and manufacturing process. The 50% figure cited for hybrid shaft configurations represents an upper bound achievable through optimized design and advanced manufacturing. More conservative hollow shaft implementations may achieve 20-30% weight reduction while maintaining greater design margin for structural requirements. The appropriate target depends on application-specific constraints including torque requirements, speed limits, and cost targets.

### Torque Transmission & Structural Integrity

The transition from solid to hollow shaft geometries introduces fundamental changes in torsional stiffness, critical speed characteristics, and structural integrity that require careful engineering analysis and compensation. While hollow shafts deliver substantial benefits in cooling and weight reduction, their mechanical behavior under torque loading differs from solid equivalents in ways that directly impact powertrain durability and NVH (noise, vibration, and harshness) performance.

Torsional stiffness represents a primary consideration in hollow shaft design. For a given outer diameter, removing material from the shaft center reduces the polar moment of inertia, which directly decreases torsional stiffness. This reduction can manifest as increased angular deflection under torque load, potentially affecting drivetrain responsiveness and introducing torsional vibration modes that interact with motor control systems. However, the relationship between geometry and stiffness is non-linear: material near the shaft center contributes minimally to torsional resistance, meaning that moderate hollowing can achieve significant weight savings with proportionally smaller stiffness penalties [31].

The available evidence indicates that hollow shafts maintain lower rotational inertia compared to solid equivalents, which provides benefits for dynamic response and efficiency [31]. Reduced rotational inertia decreases the energy required to accelerate the rotor, improving vehicle acceleration performance and regenerative braking efficiency. However, this benefit must be balanced against potential reductions in torsional stiffness that could affect torque transmission precision and introduce driveline oscillations under transient load conditions.

Critical speed limitations represent another key structural consideration for hollow shafts. The first bending mode natural frequency of a rotating shaft determines the maximum safe operating speed before resonance occurs. Hollow geometries alter the mass distribution and stiffness characteristics that govern critical speed, potentially lowering the resonance frequency compared to solid shafts of equivalent outer diameter. For electric motors operating at speeds up to 20,000 revolutions per minute, ensuring adequate separation between operating speed and critical speed is essential to avoid destructive resonance [22].

Rotor shafts must withstand high speeds and forces while being manufactured with extreme precision to ensure smooth electric motor operation [4]. This requirement applies equally to hollow and solid shafts, but hollow geometries introduce additional manufacturing complexity. Wall thickness uniformity, concentricity between inner and outer diameters, and surface finish on internal bores all affect structural performance and must be controlled through appropriate manufacturing process selection and quality assurance.

Material selection and manufacturing process significantly influence the structural performance of hollow shafts. Composite materials such as carbon fiber reinforced polymer (CFRP) offer exceptional strength-to-weight ratios and high stiffness, making them suitable for high-speed applications where hollow steel shafts may face limitations [21]. CFRP shafts display high stiffness and reduced rotational inertia, enabling operation at speeds that would challenge metallic hollow shaft designs. However, composite shafts introduce different failure modes and manufacturing considerations that must be addressed in the design process.

Aluminum hollow shafts provide an alternative approach, enabling lightweight design while maintaining adequate structural capacity for many electric drive applications [26]. Aluminum hollow shafts enhance fuel efficiency, support emission compliance, and improve drivetrain smoothness through lower rotational inertia and reduced vibration sensitivity. The material's lower density compared to steel enables greater weight reduction for equivalent structural performance, though aluminum's lower modulus of elasticity requires larger diameters or thicker walls to achieve equivalent stiffness.

Dynamic forces acting on motor shafts include torsional stresses and bending loads that demand materials with excellent fatigue resistance and durability [21]. High-strength materials such as alloy steel are commonly used for hollow shafts, providing the necessary fatigue resistance while enabling weight reduction through hollow geometry. The manufacturing process must preserve or enhance these material properties; processes such as rotary swaging improve fatigue resistance through work hardening and favorable residual stress patterns, while improper welding or heat treatment can introduce defects that compromise structural integrity.

The comparison between hollow and solid shaft torsional performance depends on specific geometry ratios. For thin-walled hollow shafts, torsional stiffness approaches that of solid shafts with equivalent outer diameter, while weight reduction remains substantial. As wall thickness decreases relative to outer diameter, the stiffness penalty increases, requiring careful optimization to balance weight savings against structural requirements. Design tools including finite element analysis and digital twin simulation enable manufacturers to optimize hollow shaft geometry for specific application requirements [24].

It should be noted that the available evidence does not provide specific quantitative comparisons of torsional stiffness percentages between hollow and solid shafts across standardized geometry ratios. Such data varies significantly based on the inner-to-outer diameter ratio, material properties, and manufacturing process effects on material strength. Engineers must perform application-specific analysis to determine appropriate hollow shaft dimensions that achieve target weight reduction while maintaining adequate torsional stiffness and critical speed margin for the intended operating conditions.

## Economic Analysis: Cost Drivers & Post-Processing Requirements

The economic viability of hollow motor shaft manufacturing for new energy vehicle (NEV) electric drive units hinges on three interdependent factors: material utilization efficiency, post-processing requirements, and production volume scalability. As the global electric motor rotor shaft market approaches USD 3.89 Billion, manufacturers must carefully evaluate the total cost of ownership across competing manufacturing routes [32]. This section provides a detailed comparative analysis of cost drivers and post-processing demands across forming and machining technologies.

### Manufacturing Cost Modeling: Forming vs. Machining

The fundamental economic distinction between chipless forming and conventional machining lies in material utilization rates. Traditional machining processes for hollow shafts require starting with solid bar stock or thick-walled tubes, then removing substantial material through drilling, boring, and turning operations to achieve the final hollow geometry. This subtractive approach inherently generates significant chip waste, with material utilization rates often falling below 50% for deep-bore shaft configurations [9]. In contrast, chipless forming technologies—including rotary swaging, flowforming, and cross wedge rolling—displace material rather than removing it, achieving utilization rates exceeding 85% in optimized production scenarios [35].

The cost implications of this divergence extend beyond raw material consumption. Hollow components provide less weight and lower material costs, a critical advantage given that high-strength steel represents a significant portion of total shaft manufacturing expense [36][9]. When raw material prices fluctuate due to supply chain disruptions, the economic penalty for low-utilization machining processes amplifies proportionally [37]. For a typical NEV motor shaft requiring 2-3 kg of finished component mass, machining from solid stock may consume 5-6 kg of raw material, whereas near-net shape forming can achieve the same output with 2.5-3.5 kg of input.

However, forming technologies carry distinct cost structures that must be evaluated holistically. Cross wedge rolling and CNC skew rolling technologies, developed for manufacturing hollow railway axles and adapted for automotive applications, offer high processing efficiency and good application prospects [31]. Yet the die costs for these processes represent substantial upfront capital expenditure. Tooling for precision forming operations can range from USD 50,000 to USD 200,000 per die set, depending on complexity and expected service life. This creates a volume threshold below which machining remains economically preferable despite higher per-unit material costs.

The break-even analysis between forming and machining depends on multiple variables:

- **Annual production volume**: Forming becomes cost-advantageous typically above 50,000-100,000 units annually, where tooling amortization per unit becomes negligible
- **Material cost per kilogram**: Higher raw material prices favor forming due to superior utilization
- **Geometric complexity**: Shafts with multiple diameter transitions benefit more from forming's near-net-shape capability
- **Tolerance requirements**: Machining maintains advantages for ultra-precision bearing seats requiring IT5-IT6 tolerance grades

Recent market analysis indicates that passenger cars dominate the drive shaft market with 68.5% market share, powered by massive production volumes that favor forming technologies [37]. However, the shaft market remains forecast by manufacturing process with machining retaining significant share for specialized applications and lower-volume segments [1]. Additive manufacturing and near-net shape forging techniques are gaining popularity in prototyping and small-batch production of rotor shafts, allowing for reduced material waste and better performance tuning during development phases [24].

The manufacturing cost model must also account for secondary operations. Machined shafts often require fewer intermediate heat treatments since material removal relieves internal stresses progressively. Formed shafts, conversely, accumulate work hardening during deformation, potentially necessitating intermediate annealing steps that add cost and cycle time. The net economic outcome depends on the specific alloy system, target mechanical properties, and available production infrastructure.

### Post-Processing & Heat Treatment Needs

Regardless of the primary forming or machining route selected, hollow motor shafts for NEV applications require extensive post-processing to achieve functional performance specifications. The post-processing sequence typically encompasses heat treatment, precision grinding, surface finishing, and balancing operations, with requirements varying significantly based on the upstream manufacturing method.

Heat treatment remains indispensable for achieving the mechanical properties required in high-speed electric motor applications. Quenching and tempering processes establish the core hardness and toughness balance necessary for shafts operating at rotational speeds exceeding 15,000 RPM [38]. Case-hardened steels, particularly carburized grades, are commonly specified for shaft applications where surface wear resistance must coexist with core ductility [6]. The heat treatment parameters—including austenitizing temperature, quench medium, and tempering cycle—must be precisely controlled to achieve target hardness values typically in the range of 58-62 HRC at bearing journals and 30-40 HRC in the shaft core.

Forming processes fundamentally alter post-processing requirements through their influence on material microstructure. Rotary swaging and flowforming induce significant plastic deformation, generating work hardening and grain refinement throughout the shaft wall thickness [31]. This cold work can partially substitute for conventional heat treatment, potentially reducing quenching severity or enabling lower-temperature tempering cycles. However, the non-uniform deformation inherent in multi-pass forming operations may create residual stress patterns that require stress-relief annealing before final machining. Manufacturers must weigh whether the lower material cost of formed shafts offsets the additional post-processing complexity compared to machined alternatives [17].

Grinding operations represent the most critical post-processing step for ensuring dimensional accuracy and surface integrity at functional interfaces. The grinding processes on bearing seats, flat collars, and the running surface of the rotary shaft seal complete the production process on the rotor shaft [14]. Specialized grinding machines, such as EMAG's HG 310 series, are deployed to achieve the sub-micron tolerance grades and surface roughness values (Ra < 0.4 μm) required for bearing fitment and seal performance. For hollow shafts, internal grinding of the bore presents additional challenges, often requiring specialized tooling and extended cycle times compared to solid shaft counterparts.

The sequence and extent of grinding operations differ between forming and machining routes. Machined shafts typically arrive at grinding with more uniform stock allowance, enabling predictable grinding cycles. Formed shafts may exhibit greater variation in pre-grind dimensions due to die wear and material springback, necessitating adaptive grinding strategies or additional stock allowance that increases grinding time and wheel consumption. ## Comparative Evaluation of Manufacturing Routes

The selection of manufacturing routes for hollow motor shafts in new energy vehicle (NEV) electric drive units represents a critical engineering decision that balances performance, cost, scalability, and risk. ### Head-to-Head Comparison: One-Piece vs. Two-Piece

The fundamental dichotomy in hollow shaft manufacturing lies between one-piece seamless forming processes and two-piece assembled designs. Each approach carries distinct implications for material utilization, mechanical performance, production scalability, and cost structure.

**One-Piece Seamless Forming (Swaging/Flowforming)**

One-piece manufacturing routes employ chipless forming technologies such as rotary swaging or flowforming to create hollow shafts from solid or tubular blanks without intermediate joining operations. GFU - Maschinenbau GmbH supplies state-of-the-art chipless forming machines specifically designed for rotor shafts in electric vehicles [4]. The rotary swaging process has been validated for hollow railway motor shafts, demonstrating its applicability to high-performance electric motor applications [31].

The mechanical advantages of one-piece forming stem from the preservation of material continuity. During rotary swaging, the workpiece undergoes severe plastic deformation that induces work hardening and grain refinement along the shaft axis. This microstructural evolution enhances fatigue resistance and torsional strength without the introduction of weld heat-affected zones (HAZ). The absence of welded joints eliminates potential failure points under cyclic loading, which is particularly critical given that motor shafts must survive millions of cycles over their service life [6].

Material utilization in one-piece forming is highly efficient. Near-net shape forging techniques allow for reduced material waste compared to subtractive machining from solid bar stock [24]. However, the initial investment in specialized forming equipment and tooling is substantial, and process flexibility is limited once dies are committed to specific geometries.

**Two-Piece Assembled Designs (Flowformed Halves + Welding)**

Two-piece manufacturing involves producing separate shaft components—typically end pieces and a central tube—followed by joining via welding. NETFORM manufactures two-piece hollow rotor shafts specifically for electric vehicle applications [5]. EMAG designs and constructs complete production lines for both one-piece and two-piece rotor shafts, with the two-piece approach utilizing laser welding to join pre-machined components [14].

The two-piece approach offers several strategic advantages. First, it enables the use of dissimilar materials or tailored material grades for different shaft sections—high-strength alloy steel for bearing seats and torque transmission zones, with lighter materials for non-critical sections. Second, it provides greater design flexibility for complex geometries that would be difficult or impossible to achieve through seamless forming alone. Third, it allows manufacturers to leverage existing machining infrastructure for component production while adding welding as a dedicated assembly step.

EMAG LaserTec has developed solutions based on the "fixed optics/moving workpiece" principle to ensure high operational reliability in automated high-production welding of EV rotor shafts [29]. The precisely metered, concentrated energy of the laser beam permits high welding speeds with minimal distortion on the welded component [29].

**Performance Comparison Across Key Indicators**

| Performance Indicator | One-Piece (Swaging/Flowforming) | Two-Piece (Welded Assembly) |
|---|---|---|
| Structural Integrity | Continuous grain flow; no weld HAZ | Weld joint introduces potential failure point |
| Weight Reduction | Moderate (limited by formability) | High (optimized wall thickness per section) |
| Material Cost | Lower waste; higher tooling cost | Flexible material selection; joining cost |
| Production Scalability | High once tooling committed | High; flexible batch sizes |
| NVH Performance | Superior (no joint discontinuity) | Good (dependent on weld quality) |
| Rotational Speed Capability | Very high (20,000+ rpm) | High (weld quality critical) |
| Design Flexibility | Limited by die geometry | High (modular component design) |

Hollow shaft designs, regardless of manufacturing route, provide less weight and lower material costs compared to solid shafts [29]. A hybrid shaft can achieve 50% weight reduction compared to conventional counterparts [10]. This weight reduction enhances fuel efficiency, supports emission compliance, and improves drivetrain smoothness through lower rotational inertia and reduced vibration sensitivity, enhancing overall noise, vibration, and harshness (NVH) performance [26].

The hollow shaft allows for the passage of vacuum lines, compressed air, or electrical slip rings, enabling innovative, space-saving system layouts [9]. This design feature is increasingly valued in integrated electric drive units where packaging efficiency directly impacts vehicle architecture.

### Technology Readiness & Risk Assessment

The maturity of manufacturing technologies for hollow motor shafts varies significantly across processes, with implications for implementation risk, capital investment, and production ramp-up timelines.

**Laser Welding: Mature Technology with Established Infrastructure**

Laser welding represents the most mature joining technology for two-piece hollow shaft assembly. The automobile industry exerts the greatest impact on the laser welding market due to its size and emphasis on innovation, with anticipated robust growth amid evolving manufacturing technologies [19]. EMAG's ELC 6 laser welding machine is deployed in production environments for joining rotor shaft end pieces to tubes, with pre-turning operations completed on VLC 200 machines before welding [14].

The technology readiness level (TRL) for laser welding in automotive applications exceeds TRL 8-9, indicating system completion and qualification through successful mission operations. Key risk factors include:

- **Weld Quality Consistency**: Laser welding requires precise fixturing and process control to avoid porosity, cracks, or incomplete fusion. Shaft laser cleaning ensures efficient mechanical transfer and prepares surfaces for optimal weld quality [16].
- **Heat-Affected Zone Management**: Although laser welding minimizes thermal input compared to conventional arc welding, the HAZ still experiences microstructural changes that may affect fatigue performance.
- **Equipment Cost**: High-power fiber laser systems represent significant capital investment, though operational efficiency and speed justify the expense at high production volumes.

**Friction Stir Welding: Emerging with High-Production Potential**

Friction stir welding (FSW) is gaining traction in high-production environments, particularly for applications requiring high-strength joints in dissimilar materials [18]. The Global Rotary Friction Welding Machines market is experiencing significant growth, with automotive manufacturing identified as a key application segment [12].

FSW offers distinct advantages over fusion welding:

- **Solid-State Process**: No melting occurs, eliminating solidification defects and reducing residual stresses.
- **Dissimilar Material Joining**: FSW excels at joining aluminum to steel or other dissimilar combinations, enabling lightweight design strategies.
- **Energy Efficiency**: Lower energy consumption compared to laser or arc welding processes.

However, FSW technology readiness for hollow shaft applications remains at TRL 6-7 (system/subsystem model demonstration in relevant environment). Implementation risks include limited supplier base, specialized tooling requirements, and the need for process development specific to shaft geometries. The Rotary Friction Welding Machine market is expected to experience a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of approximately 5-7% over the forecast period, reflecting growing adoption [18].

**Rotary Swaging: Established but Specialized**

Rotary swaging technology is well-established in aerospace and precision component manufacturing, with validation for hollow railway motor shafts demonstrating applicability to electric motor contexts [31]. The process achieves TRL 8 for specific applications but requires significant process development for each new shaft geometry.

Risk considerations include:

- **Tooling Lead Time**: Die design and manufacturing require extended lead times, limiting design iteration flexibility.
- **Process Expertise**: Specialized knowledge is required for process parameter optimization and defect prevention.
- **Equipment Availability**: Limited supplier base for high-precision swaging equipment compared to welding systems.

**Additive Manufacturing: Prototyping and Small-Batch Production**

Additive manufacturing (AM) and near-net shape forging techniques are gaining popularity in prototyping and small-batch production of rotor shafts [24]. These methods allow for reduced material waste and better performance tuning during development phases.

AM technology readiness for production hollow shafts remains at TRL 5-6, with primary applications in:

- **Design Validation**: Rapid iteration of shaft geometries before committing to hard tooling.
- **Low-Volume Production**: Specialized or custom shaft configurations where tooling costs cannot be justified.
- **Integrated Features**: Incorporation of internal channels or complex geometries not achievable through conventional forming.

Implementation risks for AM include surface finish requirements (necessitating post-processing), anisotropic mechanical properties, and limited production rates compared to forming or welding processes.
