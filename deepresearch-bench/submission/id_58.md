# Horizontal Gene Transfer in Multicellular Eukaryotes: Mechanisms, Frequency, and Evolutionary Impact

## Reframing Eukaryotic HGT: From Anomaly to Evolutionary Force

The recognition of horizontal gene transfer (HGT) as a significant evolutionary force in multicellular eukaryotes represents one of the most substantial paradigm shifts in modern evolutionary biology. For decades, the prevailing view held that HGT was predominantly a prokaryotic phenomenon, with eukaryotic evolution proceeding primarily through vertical inheritance punctuated by rare, anomalous gene acquisitions. This section synthesizes the conflicting prevalence data that has emerged from genomic surveys, establishes the controversy surrounding eukaryotic HGT frequency as a central theme, and traces the conceptual evolution from endosymbiotic gene transfer to the recognition of direct horizontal acquisition as a legitimate evolutionary mechanism.

### Defining the Scope: Distinguishing Eukaryotic HGT from Prokaryotic Norms

The fundamental distinction between prokaryotic and eukaryotic horizontal gene transfer lies not merely in frequency but in the underlying biological architectures that govern genetic exchange. In prokaryotes, HGT operates as a routine, transformative mechanism that continuously reshapes genome content and drives adaptive evolution. Studies of bacterial and archaeal genomes reveal that less than 0.7 per cent of prokaryotic genes may be considered core genes, with the vast majority of genomic content subject to horizontal exchange [7]. This extraordinary fluidity reflects the operation of well-characterized molecular mechanisms—conjugation, transduction, and transformation—that facilitate DNA uptake and recombination across species boundaries [7]. The prokaryotic accessory genome, which can exceed 0.6 Mbp in some lineages, exists in dynamic equilibrium with a relatively small core genome of approximately 3.6 Mbp, enabling rapid adaptation to environmental challenges through gene acquisition [17].

By contrast, eukaryotic HGT has historically been considered rare, particularly in complex multicellular organisms. This perception stems from fundamental biological barriers that distinguish eukaryotic from prokaryotic life histories. The eukaryotic nuclear envelope creates a physical separation between cytoplasmic DNA and the genomic repository, requiring specialized transport mechanisms for nuclear entry. Germline sequestration in multicellular eukaryotes further restricts the heritability of horizontally acquired genes, as somatic integrations cannot be transmitted to offspring unless they occur in germ cells or their precursors [10]. These barriers, combined with the absence of prokaryotic-style conjugation and transformation machinery in most eukaryotic lineages, established the baseline assumption that HGT in eukaryotes would be negligible compared to vertical inheritance.

The methodological implications of this distinction are profound. Prokaryotic HGT detection benefits from the expectation of frequent exchange, with phylogenetic incongruence serving as a reliable indicator of horizontal acquisition. In eukaryotes, however, the rarity assumption created a higher burden of proof, requiring multiple lines of evidence to distinguish genuine HGT from alternative explanations such as incomplete lineage sorting, gene loss, or methodological artifacts [13]. This asymmetry in evidentiary standards contributed to the systematic underestimation of eukaryotic HGT frequency in early genomic studies, as researchers applied conservative filters that may have excluded genuine but unexpected transfer events.

Recent evidence suggests that the prokaryotic-eukaryotic dichotomy in HGT frequency may be more nuanced than initially assumed. While the absolute rate of horizontal acquisition remains lower in eukaryotes, the functional impact of individual transfer events may be disproportionately significant due to the larger genomic context and longer generation times characteristic of multicellular lineages [6]. Furthermore, certain eukaryotic groups—particularly those with intimate symbiotic associations or parasitic lifestyles—exhibit HGT frequencies that approach prokaryotic levels, challenging the notion of a universal eukaryotic barrier to horizontal exchange [5].

### The Frequency Debate: Quantifying "Rare" versus "Widespread" Claims

The tension between claims that HGT is negligible in multicellular organisms versus recent genomic surveys suggesting it is a dynamic, regular occurrence represents the central controversy in contemporary eukaryotic HGT research. This debate reflects both genuine biological variation and methodological challenges that complicate the quantification of transfer events across diverse eukaryotic lineages.

On one side of the debate, conservative estimates emphasize the substantial barriers to HGT in complex eukaryotes and the high rate of false positive detections in genomic analyses. High-profile erroneous reports of HGT in humans and other animals have likely had a chilling effect on the field, prompting researchers to apply increasingly stringent filters that may exclude genuine transfer events [8]. Contamination during genome sequencing remains a persistent concern, particularly for candidate genes located on short scaffolds or those with bacterial-like sequence characteristics [10]. The potential for multi-residence genes—sequences that genuinely exist in both prokaryotic and eukaryotic genomes due to ancient conservation rather than recent transfer—further complicates detection efforts [26].

On the other side, emerging genomic surveys reveal HGT frequencies that substantially exceed historical expectations. Analysis of 1,137 proteomes from the Pezizomycotina subphylum identified 15,242 HGT candidates, suggesting that horizontal acquisition may be far more common in fungal lineages than previously recognized [4]. The latest developments in next-generation sequencing technologies have opened new perspectives for HGT study, enabling the detection of transfer events that were invisible to earlier methodological approaches [13]. Comparative genomic approaches have accelerated discoveries, revealing numerous examples of HGT-mediated acquisition of new functions in higher eukaryotes that involve plants as donors or receptors [13].

The resolution of this debate requires careful consideration of detection methodology and the biological context of putative transfer events. Phylogeny-based HGT detection methods face several challenges in large-scale implementation, including the computational intensity of whole-genome analyses and the conceptual issue of false positive detection for housekeeping genes, where high sequence identity among distantly related taxa results from strong purifying selection through strict vertical inheritance rather than horizontal exchange [13]. Tools such as HGTector provide general approximations of detectable HGT candidates but may not be stringent enough to determine precise phylogenetic relationships between candidates and their homologs [4].

The frequency debate also reflects genuine biological variation across eukaryotic lineages. Parasitic species like Cuscuta, Rafflesia, and Striga exhibit particularly high HGT frequencies, where haustorial feeding linkages between parasites and their hosts facilitate close cellular interactions that enable gene transfer [6]. Multiple lines of evidence suggest that HGT spans across various domains of life, influencing ecosystems and the fitness of recipient organisms through transfers from bacteria to plants, plants to insects, fungi to plants, and numerous other directional combinations [6]. The increase in HGT probability with respect to abundance was higher in animal- and plant-associated microorganisms, suggesting that ecological proximity and interaction frequency modulate transfer rates [25].

Ultimately, the frequency debate cannot be resolved through a single universal estimate. Instead, HGT frequency in eukaryotes appears to be context-dependent, varying across lineages, ecological niches, and evolutionary timescales. The recognition of this variability represents a maturation of the field from simplistic rare-versus-widespread dichotomies toward a more nuanced understanding of the factors that modulate horizontal exchange in complex organisms.

### Historical Context: The Shift from Endosymbiosis to Direct Gene Acquisition

The conceptual evolution of eukaryotic HGT research reflects a fundamental shift from the recognition of endosymbiotic gene transfer to the acceptance of direct horizontal acquisition from unrelated species as a legitimate evolutionary mechanism. This transition required distinguishing between two fundamentally different processes that had been historically conflated: the massive gene flow from organelles to the nucleus following primary endosymbiosis, and the ongoing acquisition of genes from diverse donors throughout eukaryotic evolutionary history.

Endosymbiotic gene transfer (EGT) from mitochondria and plastids to the nuclear genome represents the most dramatic instance of horizontal exchange in eukaryotic evolution. The high propensity of mitochondrial genomes to engage in HGT relates to two peculiar biological features of plant mitochondria: they possess an active homologous recombination system, and they readily undergo organelle fusion, with many mitochondria being physically connected [9]. The outcome of horizontal transfer differs between the two DNA-containing organelles: whole-genome transfer occurs in chloroplasts, whereas recombined mosaic genomes characterize mitochondrial HGT [9]. This distinction has important implications for detection methodology, as mitochondrial HGT produces different phylogenetic signatures than plastid-derived transfers.

However, EGT represents only one component of eukaryotic horizontal acquisition. Direct horizontal acquisition from unrelated species—bacteria, fungi, viruses, or other eukaryotes—operates through distinct mechanisms and produces different evolutionary outcomes. Recent studies have demonstrated that HGT continues to shape plant genomes in modern evolutionary time frames, with bacterial gene acquisition by members of the Triticeae tribe providing evidence of ongoing transfer [5]. Fungi are also recognized as donors of foreign genes in plants, with HGT in microbial symbionts contributing to the evolution of mycorrhizal and rhizobial symbioses [5].

The distinction between EGT and direct HGT has important implications for understanding evolutionary mechanisms. EGT represents a one-time massive influx of genes following organelle establishment, with subsequent transfer rates declining as organelle genomes shrink and gene function is consolidated in the nucleus. Direct HGT, by contrast, represents an ongoing process that can introduce novel functions throughout evolutionary history. For instance, the transfer of a gene encoding a 448 amino acid protein between the parasitic plant Striga and Sorghum demonstrates that HGT can occur between distantly related plant species through mechanisms that remain incompletely understood [13].

The historical conflation of EGT and direct HGT contributed to skepticism about eukaryotic horizontal acquisition. Early reports of bacterial genes in eukaryotic genomes were often dismissed as EGT artifacts or contamination, reflecting the assumption that direct HGT was biologically implausible in complex organisms. This notion, combined with high-profile erroneous reports, created a conservative culture in which genuine HGT events were systematically underreported [8]. The recognition that HGT from bacteria has been a major force in plant evolution, providing novel genes that have shaped plant genomes and driven key adaptations over millions of years, represents a fundamental reframing of eukaryotic evolutionary history [6].

Recent progress in methodologies for omics and computational biological investigation has begun to open the black box of HGT mechanisms in eukaryotes. Although occurrences of HGT between plants and various prokaryotic and eukaryotic organisms are now well founded, mechanisms of this HGT remain vastly unknown [6]. The shift from endosymbiosis to direct gene acquisition as a research focus reflects the maturation of the field from documenting the existence of eukaryotic HGT toward understanding the molecular mechanisms, ecological contexts, and evolutionary consequences of horizontal exchange in multicellular organisms. This conceptual evolution continues to reshape our understanding of eukaryotic genome evolution, challenging the traditional view of vertical inheritance as the primary driver of genetic change in complex life.

## Molecular Mechanisms and Vectors of Transfer

Horizontal gene transfer (HGT) in multicellular eukaryotes operates through diverse molecular mechanisms that overcome the substantial barriers separating species genomes. Unlike prokaryotes, where conjugation, transformation, and transduction provide well-characterized routes for DNA exchange, eukaryotes require specialized vectors and physical pathways to facilitate genetic movement across reproductive boundaries. This section examines three primary mechanisms: biological vectors including viruses and endosymbionts, cargo-mobilizing transposable elements operating under the "Starship" hypothesis, and physical pathways unique to plants involving tissue fusion and grafting.

### Biological Vectors: Viruses, Parasites, and Endosymbionts

Biological vectors represent the most extensively documented conduits for horizontal DNA movement in eukaryotes, with viruses, parasitic organisms, and intimate endosymbiotic relationships serving as primary mechanisms for cross-species genetic exchange. The molecular architecture of these vectors enables them to bypass conventional reproductive barriers that typically isolate eukaryotic genomes.

Virus-mediated gene transfer occurs through a process termed endogenization, where viral DNA or reverse-transcribed RNA becomes incorporated into host germline genomes, forming endogenous viral elements (EVEs) [6]. This mechanism is particularly prevalent in plants, where widespread integration of EVEs into host genomes provides compelling evidence for virus-to-plant horizontal transfer [6]. Retroviruses uniquely possess the capacity to integrate their genetic material directly into host chromosomes through reverse transcription, creating permanent genomic modifications that can be vertically transmitted to offspring. Research on viral infection in sugar beet plants has demonstrated the generation of virus-host circular DNA, illustrating how viral vectors can capture and mobilize host genetic material [30].

Baculoviruses have been identified as significant vectors of DNA horizontal transfer between insects, with host DNA influx serving as a potent source of genetic diversity in viral populations [30]. This bidirectional exchange creates opportunities for genes to move between distantly related insect species through viral intermediaries, effectively circumventing species barriers that would otherwise prevent genetic exchange.

Endosymbiotic bacteria, particularly Wolbachia, represent another critical vector for horizontal gene transfer in arthropods and nematodes. Wolbachia-mediated transfer has been documented across numerous insect lineages, with the endosymbiont's intimate association with host germline cells providing direct access to heritable genetic material [6]. The mechanism involves bacterial DNA integration into host chromosomes, often facilitated by the close physical proximity between Wolbachia and host nuclei during oogenesis. This process has resulted in substantial portions of Wolbachia genomes being found integrated into insect host genomes, creating what researchers term "lateral gene transfer from endosymbiont to host."

Parasitic organisms establish specialized physical connections that enable direct genetic exchange with their hosts. In parasitic plants such as Cuscuta, Rafflesia, and Striga, haustorial feeding linkages facilitate close cellular interactions between parasite and host tissues [6]. The haustorium forms a physical bridge between the parasite and its host, acting as a transmission route that enables bidirectional movement of genetic material [5]. This specialized organ allows the parasitic plant to connect to the xylem and/or phloem of the host, enabling the regular transfer of water, nutrients, proteins, mRNAs, and pathogens alongside DNA [13].

The adaptive significance of vector-mediated HGT is exemplified by the BtPMaT1 gene transfer from plants to the whitefly Bemisia tabaci. This horizontally acquired gene enables the whitefly to detoxify plant phenolic glucosides, providing a clear selective advantage that has been maintained through natural selection [31]. This case demonstrates how vector-mediated transfer can result in functionally adaptive outcomes rather than neutral DNA integration, distinguishing consequential HGT events from genomic noise.

Parasitic plant-host interactions have produced documented cases of mitochondrial gene transfer, with the atp1 gene representing the first reported case of HGT between parasitic plants [13]. The vascular connections established through haustoria enable regular transfer of genetic material, creating opportunities for both nuclear and organellar genome exchange between species that would otherwise remain reproductively isolated.

### Transposable Elements as Cargo: The "Starship" Hypothesis

Transposable elements (TEs) have emerged as active agents in horizontal gene transfer, with recent evidence supporting the "Starship" hypothesis that positions certain TEs as cargo-mobilizing vectors capable of facilitating DNA movement between divergent species. This represents a paradigm shift from viewing TEs as purely selfish genetic elements to recognizing their role as facilitators of genomic innovation through horizontal transfer.

Starships constitute a newly discovered group of cargo-mobilizing transposable elements that function as active vectors of horizontally transferred DNA among eukaryotes [28]. Unlike conventional transposable elements that primarily move within genomes, Starships possess molecular machinery enabling them to package and transfer genetic cargo between species. This cargo-mobilizing capacity distinguishes them from standard TEs and positions them as key drivers of cross-species genetic exchange.

Over 3,000 cases of horizontal transposon transfer (HTT) have been reported across eukaryotic lineages, with TEs often associated with tissue damage, insect-mediated transfer, and root cell interactions [6]. These elements contribute to chromosomal rearrangements, genetic diversity, and novel gene functions in recipient genomes. The prevalence of HTT events suggests that transposable elements represent a significant fraction of horizontally acquired genetic material in eukaryotes, potentially exceeding the contribution of protein-coding genes in some lineages.

Mavericks and Penelope elements represent specific TE families with documented horizontal transfer histories. Wolbachia-mediated transfer has been associated with Mavericks, suggesting that endosymbiont-vector interactions can facilitate TE movement between host species [6]. Penelope retrotransposons have demonstrated remarkable cross-kingdom transfer capabilities, with evidence for an ancient horizontal transfer event approximately 340 million years ago from animals to plants [12]. This Penelope element is present in conifers but absent in other gymnosperm species, providing phylogenetic evidence for the horizontal nature of the transfer event.

The Tc1/Mariner superfamily represents the most common type among horizontally transferred TEs, with prevalence consistently documented across diverse eukaryotic lineages [23]. DNA transposons of this superfamily appear particularly adept at horizontal movement, potentially due to their molecular architecture that facilitates excision, packaging, and integration in novel genomic contexts. The widespread distribution of Tc1/Mariner elements across phylogenetically distant species provides strong evidence for repeated horizontal transfer events rather than vertical inheritance from common ancestors.

Horizontal transfer of transposable elements shapes eukaryote genomes through multiple mechanisms, including the introduction of novel regulatory sequences, creation of genomic rearrangements, and provision of raw material for gene evolution [27]. The contribution of TE superfamilies to horizontal transfer varies across lineages, with certain families showing particular propensity for cross-species movement. This variation reflects differences in TE molecular mechanisms, host defense systems, and ecological opportunities for transfer.

The Starship hypothesis extends beyond simple TE mobility to propose that certain elements actively recruit and transport additional genetic cargo during horizontal transfer events. This cargo may include protein-coding genes, regulatory sequences, or other TEs that become mobilized alongside the primary Starship element. The molecular mechanisms enabling this cargo recruitment remain under investigation, but evidence suggests that Starships possess specialized domains or associated proteins that facilitate the packaging and transfer of adjacent genomic sequences.

### Physical Pathways: Natural Grafting and Tissue Fusion in Plants

Plants possess unique physical pathways for horizontal gene transfer that are unavailable to animals, with natural grafting and tissue fusion representing mechanisms that enable whole genome or organelle transfer across species barriers. These physical pathways create direct cytoplasmic connections between distinct plant individuals, facilitating the movement of genetic material without requiring biological vectors.

Natural grafting, the process of tissue fusion between two distinct plant species, serves as a potential route for HGT in plants [1]. This phenomenon occurs when plant tissues from different species come into physical contact and establish vascular connections, enabling the exchange of cellular contents including organelles and nuclear DNA. The molecular mechanisms underlying natural grafting involve cell wall degradation, plasma membrane fusion, and establishment of plasmodesmatal connections that permit macromolecular traffic between grafted tissues.

Plastid capture represents a specific outcome of graft-mediated HGT, where transferred plastids replace those of the recipient species [13]. In this case, the transferred plastid genome becomes established in the recipient lineage, creating a discordance between nuclear and organellar phylogenies. Research has demonstrated that allopolyploid plants can be regenerated from calli derived from graft sites, providing experimental evidence for the potential of grafting to generate novel genetic combinations [13].

The high propensity of plant mitochondrial genomes to engage in HGT relates to two peculiar biological features: plant mitochondria possess an active homologous recombination system, and they readily undergo organelle fusion with many mitochondria being physically connected [9]. Whereas chloroplasts do not normally undergo recombination, plant mitochondria recombine very frequently, resulting in different outcomes for horizontal transfer between the two DNA-containing organelles. Whole-genome transfer characterizes chloroplast HGT, while recombined mosaic genomes result from mitochondrial HGT [9].

Parasitic plants establish vascular connections with host plants through haustoria that enable regular transfer of genetic material alongside nutrients and signaling molecules [13]. The haustorium-mediated transfer pathway has produced documented cases of HGT between parasitic plants and their hosts, with genes moving in both directions depending on the specific interaction. A well-documented example involves the transfer of a gene of unknown function from the parasitic plant Striga to its host sorghum, with the transferred gene being intronless in sorghum compared to its homolog in Striga, providing indirect evidence that the transfer occurred via mRNA rather than DNA [5].

Research has demonstrated that a gene encoding a 448 amino acid protein was transferred between Striga and Sorghum, with the hypothesis that the HGT mechanism involves transport through the haustorial connection [13]. This case illustrates how parasitic plant-host interactions create sustained opportunities for genetic exchange over evolutionary timescales, with the intimate physical connection enabling repeated transfer events that can result in stable genomic integration.

The outcome of horizontal transfer differs between chloroplasts and mitochondria due to their distinct recombination behaviors. Chloroplast capture often occurs in the absence of detectable nuclear introgression, suggesting that organellar genomes can move between species independently of nuclear gene flow [9]. This decoupling of organellar and nuclear inheritance creates complex phylogenetic patterns that require careful analysis to distinguish horizontal transfer from incomplete lineage sorting or other confounding factors.

Natural grafting between compatible plant species creates opportunities for bidirectional genetic exchange, with both nuclear and organellar genomes potentially moving between graft partners. The frequency of graft-mediated HGT in natural populations remains uncertain, but experimental evidence demonstrates the feasibility of this mechanism under controlled conditions. The evolutionary significance of graft-mediated transfer depends on the stability of transferred genetic material and its integration into germline cells that can transmit the acquired sequences to subsequent generations.

Plant-specific mechanisms for HGT reflect the sessile nature of plants and their reliance on physical connections rather than mobile vectors for genetic exchange. Unlike animals, which possess segregated germlines that protect heritable genetic material from somatic acquisition, plants maintain meristematic cells throughout their lifespan that can give rise to reproductive structures. This architectural difference means that somatic HGT events in plants have greater potential to enter the germline and become heritable, increasing the evolutionary impact of physical transfer pathways.

## Cellular and Germline Barriers to Integration

The rarity of horizontal gene transfer (HGT) in multicellular eukaryotes is not merely a consequence of infrequent contact between potential donor and recipient organisms. Rather, it reflects a multi-layered system of biological barriers that actively prevent foreign DNA from entering, integrating into, and being inherited through the germline. These barriers operate at distinct cellular and developmental stages, creating a series of filters that collectively explain why successful HGT events remain exceptional rather than routine. Understanding these barriers mechanistically is essential for explaining the differential rates of HGT observed across eukaryotic lineages and for distinguishing between neutral DNA integration and functionally adaptive transfer events.

### The Germline Segregation Bottleneck: Animals versus Plants

The most fundamental barrier to heritable HGT in multicellular eukaryotes is the timing of germline segregation during development. This developmental feature creates dramatically different opportunities for foreign DNA to become incorporated into the lineage that will be passed to offspring, and it represents the primary explanation for the observed disparity in HGT frequency between animal and plant kingdoms.

In animals, germline segregation occurs early in embryonic development. Primordial germ cells (PGCs), which serve as bipotential precursors of eggs and sperm, are distinguished from somatic cells during embryogenesis and are set aside as a protected lineage [3]. This early segregation creates a strict physical and developmental barrier: any foreign DNA that enters somatic cells during the organism's lifetime cannot be transmitted to the next generation because those cells are developmentally disconnected from the germline. For HGT to become heritable in animals, foreign genetic material must either directly target PGCs during their brief window of accessibility or integrate into the germline during the earliest stages of embryonic development before segregation occurs. This constraint severely limits the opportunities for successful transfer.

The protective isolation of animal germ cells is further reinforced by their physical sequestration. In many animal lineages, germ cells migrate to specialized gonadal tissues where they remain shielded from environmental exposure and potential vectors of foreign DNA. However, certain exceptions demonstrate that this barrier is not absolute. The close association of Wolbachia endosymbionts with germ cells of arthropods represents one mechanism by which this barrier can be circumvented, as the intimate physical proximity between the endosymbiont and host germ cells could promote stable HGT from these endosymbionts to their hosts [11]. Similarly, acquisition of bacterial endosymbionts during embryonic development could provide a window of opportunity for transfer before germline segregation is complete.

In stark contrast, plants exhibit late germline segregation, creating a fundamentally more permissive architecture for HGT. Unlike animals, the architecture of higher plants primarily arises from post-embryonic development, with germ cells differentiating from somatic meristematic tissues late in the life cycle [3]. This means that somatic cells in plants retain the potential to give rise to germline cells throughout the organism's lifetime. A foreign DNA integration event occurring in a somatic cell of a plant meristem could theoretically be incorporated into gametes if that cell lineage contributes to reproductive structures.

This permissive architecture is amplified by several plant-specific biological features. First, many plant species can reproduce asexually, allowing the generation of entire individuals from vegetative tissues. Vegetative reproduction enables clonal propagation enhanced by stress conditions through various organs, including roots, stems, and leaves, which could allow somatic HGT events to become fixed in a lineage without requiring sexual transmission [5]. Second, plant gametophyte development provides specific opportunities to uptake foreign DNA. In mosses, for example, researchers have hypothesized that at least two entry points exist for foreign genes to be acquired and integrated into the nucleus, with the stage of spore germination and early gametophyte development representing a particularly vulnerable window [10]. Moss gametophytes develop from haploid spores, and during this phase, cells may be more accessible to foreign genetic material.

Physical connections between plant tissues create additional routes for HGT that have no animal equivalent. Parasitic plants form vascular connections with host plants through specialized structures called haustoria, which enable regular transfer of water, nutrients, proteins, mRNAs, and pathogens between species [13]. This specialized organ allows the parasitic plant to connect to the xylem and/or phloem of the host, enabling the bidirectional movement of macromolecules including DNA. The mitochondrial atp1 gene transfer between parasitic plants and their hosts represents one documented case of HGT facilitated by this mechanism [13]. Natural grafting, the process of tissue fusion between two distinct plant species, represents another potential route for HGT in plants, creating direct cytoplasmic connections that could facilitate DNA exchange [1].

The differential timing of germline segregation thus establishes a foundational explanation for why plants exhibit higher documented rates of HGT compared to animals. The plant developmental program creates multiple windows of opportunity throughout the life cycle, while the animal program restricts heritable integration to a narrow embryonic window. This distinction does not mean that animal HGT is impossible, but rather that it requires exceptional circumstances—such as intimate endosymbiotic relationships or viral vectors capable of targeting germ cells directly—to overcome the germline segregation bottleneck.

### Physical Defenses: The Nuclear Envelope and Chromatin Structure

Once foreign DNA has entered a eukaryotic cell, it faces a second major barrier: the nuclear envelope. This structure represents a formidable physical obstacle that separates the cell's genetic material from the cytoplasm where most foreign DNA initially arrives. The nuclear envelope encloses genomic DNA and separates the nucleoplasm from the cytosol, forming a two-layered physical barrier comprised of the outer and inner nuclear membranes [14]. For horizontally transferred DNA to integrate into the host genome and become functionally expressed, it must traverse this barrier and access the chromatin within the nucleus.

The nuclear pore complex (NPC) regulates all molecular trafficking between the cytoplasm and nucleus, serving as the sole gateway for macromolecular transport [19]. The NPC does not permit passive diffusion of large molecules; instead, it requires specific transport receptors to mediate passage. Karyopherin β proteins serve as the main nuclear import receptors, recognizing cargo molecules bearing nuclear localization signals (NLS) and facilitating their transport through the pore [2]. Foreign DNA entering a cell typically lacks these specific signals, creating an immediate transport barrier.

The mechanism by which foreign DNA overcomes this barrier varies depending on the vector of transfer. In the case of Agrobacterium-mediated transfer to plants, the bacterium has evolved a sophisticated molecular machinery to actively transport DNA into the host nucleus. Agrobacterium transfers T-DNA to plant nuclear DNA through a type IV secretion system, with bacterial proteins VirD2 and VirE2 coating the DNA and providing the necessary signals for nuclear import [13]. This represents a highly specialized mechanism that has been naturally selected for efficient nuclear delivery. In natural HGT scenarios without such specialized vectors, foreign DNA must rely on alternative mechanisms, such as hitchhiking with host proteins during nuclear envelope breakdown during cell division, or exploiting damage to the nuclear envelope.

The nuclear lamina, a protein meshwork underlying the inner nuclear membrane, contributes structural support to the nucleus and plays additional roles in genome organization [14]. Nuclear envelope constituents engage in diverse cellular processes, especially in genome organization, by directly binding with chromatin and chromatin-associated protein complexes, organizing chromatin architecture at the nuclear periphery, and regulating nuclear accumulation of RNA and proteins [14]. This organization creates additional barriers for foreign DNA, as integration sites are not randomly distributed throughout the genome but are influenced by chromatin accessibility and nuclear positioning.

The randomness of integration sites in transgenic contexts illustrates the stochastic nature of successful integration. Even when foreign DNA successfully enters the nucleus, it must integrate into the host genome in a location that permits stable inheritance and expression. Integration into heterochromatic regions may result in silencing, while integration into essential genes may be deleterious and selected against. The nuclear basket of the NPC makes an intimate connection with both chromatin and the nucleoskeleton, assembled by eight flexible protein filaments that emanate from the NPC core and join in a distal ring [14]. This structure displays high flexibility and plasticity, which may influence which genomic regions are accessible for integration.

Importin β-binding domains mediate transport of specific cargoes through the nuclear pore, and such selective transport of molecules across the nuclear envelope plays an important role not only in basic cellular activity but also in cellular defense [19]. The specificity of this transport system means that foreign DNA lacking appropriate binding partners faces substantial barriers to nuclear entry. In plants, specific nucleoporins such as Nup160 and Nup96 form part of the evolutionarily conserved Nup107–Nup160 subcomplex, which is the largest subcomplex in the NPC [19]. Defects in these components can alter nuclear transport and potentially affect susceptibility to foreign DNA integration, though such defects typically carry fitness costs.

The physical barriers of the nuclear envelope and chromatin structure thus create a selective filter that permits only certain types of foreign DNA to successfully integrate. DNA delivered by specialized vectors with nuclear targeting mechanisms, DNA that enters during cell division when the nuclear envelope is temporarily disassembled, or DNA that can exploit host transport machinery have the highest probability of successful nuclear entry. This explains why many documented HGT events involve mobile genetic elements, viral vectors, or endosymbionts that have evolved mechanisms to overcome these physical barriers.

### Immune Surveillance: RNA Interference and Genome Defense Systems

Even when foreign DNA successfully traverses the nuclear envelope and integrates into the host genome, it faces a third layer of defense: the eukaryotic immune surveillance system. Eukaryotes have evolved sophisticated mechanisms to detect and suppress exogenous genetic elements, recognizing them as potential threats to genome integrity. These defense systems represent a final barrier that determines whether an HGT event results in stable inheritance or is eliminated through silencing or excision.

RNA interference (RNAi) serves as an evolutionarily conserved eukaryotic post-transcriptional gene expression regulation mechanism that plays a central role in genome defense [20]. This system uses small RNA molecules, including siRNAs and miRNAs, to recognize and silence foreign or aberrant RNA transcripts. When foreign DNA is integrated and transcribed, the resulting RNA can be recognized by the RNAi machinery as non-self, triggering degradation of the transcript and establishment of repressive chromatin marks at the integration site. This effectively silences the horizontally acquired gene, preventing its expression even if physical integration has occurred.

The RNAi system is particularly effective against transposable elements, which represent a major class of horizontally transferred genetic material. Horizontal transfers are frequently composed of transposable elements, and these mobile sequences are primary targets of RNAi-mediated silencing [13]. The host genome recognizes transposable element transcripts as threats and deploys small RNAs to suppress their activity. This creates a paradox for HGT: the same mechanisms that protect genome integrity from parasitic DNA also eliminate potentially beneficial horizontally acquired genes. Recent discoveries have identified Starships as a newly discovered group of cargo-mobilizing transposable elements that are active vectors of horizontally transferred DNA among eukaryotes [18]. These elements must overcome host RNAi defenses to successfully transfer and establish in new genomes.

Plant innate immunity systems deploy multiple defense strategies that extend beyond RNAi to include recognition of foreign nucleic acids and activation of broader immune responses [2]. The regulation of miRNA activity by importin proteins demonstrates the integration of nuclear transport and RNA silencing pathways. A forward genetic screen for miRNA activity led to the identification of mutants affecting miRNA loading into ARGONAUTE1, revealing that nucleoporins play specific regulatory roles in plant immune responses [19]. These results suggest that the functions of nucleoporins partially overlap with immune surveillance, but nucleoporins also play specific regulatory roles in plant immune responses.

Transmembrane nucleoporins can directly regulate immune responses. Constitutive Expresser of Pathogenesis Related Gene 5 (CPR5) is a plant-specific transmembrane nucleoporin that negatively regulates the immune response [14]. Upon effector triggered immunity signal transduction, CPR5 undergoes conformational switching, linking nuclear transport to immune activation. This integration means that attempts by foreign DNA to exploit nuclear transport machinery may trigger immune responses that eliminate the invading genetic material.

The effectiveness of these defense systems helps explain why most horizontally transferred DNA remains as non-functional genomic fossils rather than becoming adaptively significant. The fate of transferred sequences, whether they are at the origin of new functions or only remain in their recipient genome as fossils, has to be further investigated [13]. In one instance, however, two viral genes encoding specific functions were successfully integrated and maintained, suggesting that under certain circumstances, foreign genes can escape silencing and provide selective advantages.

The interplay between genome defense and HGT creates a selective filter that favors certain types of transfers. Genes that provide immediate selective advantages may be maintained despite silencing pressure if the benefit outweighs the cost of defense activation. The acquisition of bacterial genes by members of the Triticeae tribe demonstrates that HGT continues to shape plant genomes in modern evolutionary time frames, suggesting that some transfers successfully evade or overcome defense systems [5]. Fungi are also recognized as donors of foreign genes in plants, with multiple lines of evidence suggesting that HGT spans across various domains of life, influencing ecosystems and the fitness of recipient organisms [6].

The three-tiered barrier system—germline segregation, nuclear envelope, and immune surveillance—collectively explains the rarity of successful HGT in multicellular eukaryotes. Each barrier eliminates the majority of potential transfer events, with only exceptional circumstances allowing foreign DNA to pass through all three filters. This framework provides mechanistic explanations for observed patterns of HGT frequency across eukaryotic lineages and establishes the foundation for understanding which transfers become evolutionarily significant versus those that remain as genomic noise.

## Comparative Analysis: HGT Dynamics in Plants versus Animals

Horizontal gene transfer (HGT) in multicellular eukaryotes exhibits striking asymmetries between plants and animals, reflecting fundamental differences in cellular architecture, reproductive biology, and ecological interactions. While plants demonstrate remarkable permissiveness to foreign DNA acquisition, animals display a spectrum ranging from invertebrate flexibility to vertebrate rigidity. This section provides a comprehensive comparative analysis of HGT dynamics across these kingdoms, examining the mechanistic bases, frequency patterns, and evolutionary consequences of gene transfer events.

### Plant Permissiveness: Colonization of Land and Stress Adaptation

Plants have evolved as exceptionally receptive recipients of horizontally acquired genetic material, with HGT serving as a pivotal force in their evolutionary trajectory. The acquisition of bacterial genes has been a major driver in plant evolution, providing novel genetic functions that have shaped plant genomes and facilitated key adaptations over millions of years [6]. This permissiveness is particularly evident in the context of land colonization and stress adaptation, where horizontally acquired genes have conferred critical survival advantages.

The mechanistic basis for plant HGT permissiveness centers on the accessibility of plant cells to foreign DNA and the existence of multiple natural routes for genetic exchange. Intimate cell-to-cell contact appears to be a key condition for HGT in plants, with several well-documented pathways facilitating this process. Grafting represents one such mechanism, where tissue fusion between distinct plant species enables the exchange of genetic material across species boundaries [5]. Natural grafting, the process of tissue fusion between two distinct plant species, serves as another potential route for HGT in plant systems [1].

Parasitism constitutes a particularly effective vector for plant HGT. Parasitic plants form vascular connections with host plants through specialized structures called haustoria, which enable the regular transfer of water, nutrients, proteins, mRNAs, and pathogens between organisms [13]. This intimate physiological linkage creates opportunities for genetic material to cross species barriers. The first reported case of HGT involving the mitochondrial atp1 gene between parasitic plants demonstrated the evolutionary significance of these haustorial connections [13].

Plant cellular and organellar biology further enhances HGT susceptibility. Plant mitochondria possess two peculiar biological features that increase their propensity for horizontal transfer: they maintain an active homologous recombination system, and they readily undergo organelle fusion, with many mitochondria being physically connected within cells [9]. These characteristics contrast sharply with chloroplasts, which do not normally undergo recombination, resulting in different outcomes of horizontal transfer between the two DNA-containing organelles—whole-genome transfer in chloroplasts versus recombined mosaic genomes in mitochondria [9].

The adaptive significance of plant HGT extends beyond neutral DNA integration to functionally important acquisitions. Recent studies have demonstrated that HGT continues to shape plant genomes in modern evolutionary time frames, with bacterial gene acquisition by members of the Triticeae tribe representing a contemporary example [5]. Fungi are also recognized as donors of foreign genes in plants, expanding the pool of potential genetic contributors beyond bacterial sources [5].

Stress adaptation represents a key functional outcome of plant HGT. Horizontally acquired genes have been implicated in conferring resistance to environmental challenges, including cold stress, pathogen attack, and nutrient limitation. The HOS1 gene, which acts as an E3 ubiquitin ligase involved in degradation of transcription factors regulating plant cold stress, exemplifies how acquired genetic elements can integrate into existing regulatory networks [14]. Similarly, nucleoporin mutants such as nup160/sar1 exhibit sensitivity to chilling stress and defects in acquired freezing tolerance, highlighting the connection between nuclear transport components and stress response [19].

The evolutionary impact of HGT on plant adaptation to land colonization cannot be overstated. As plants transitioned from aquatic to terrestrial environments, they faced novel selective pressures requiring rapid acquisition of adaptive traits. HGT provided a mechanism for accessing pre-adapted genetic solutions from diverse donors, accelerating evolutionary innovation beyond the constraints of vertical inheritance alone. This process has been traced back to early land plant evolution, with bacterial gene acquisitions contributing to fundamental metabolic and regulatory capabilities [5].

### Animal Constraints: Invertebrate Flexibility versus Vertebrate Rigidity

In contrast to the relative permissiveness observed in plants, animals exhibit a pronounced dichotomy in HGT susceptibility, with invertebrates demonstrating considerable flexibility while vertebrates, particularly mammals, display extreme rigidity against foreign DNA integration. This divergence reflects fundamental differences in germline architecture, developmental biology, and the evolutionary pressures shaping animal genomes.

Invertebrate taxa have proven remarkably receptive to horizontal gene transfer, with some lineages showing substantial genomic contributions from foreign sources. Bdelloid rotifers represent an extreme example, with at least 8–9% of their gene content derived from HGT events [11]. Similarly, nematodes have acquired over 3% of their genome through horizontal transfer [11]. These percentages far exceed typical HGT rates observed in vertebrate lineages and approach levels more commonly associated with prokaryotic evolution.

A comprehensive analysis of 218 high-quality insect genomes revealed that insects have acquired 1,410 genes from 670 unique donors, including 25 plant species [6]. While lepidopterans demonstrate the highest rate of horizontally acquired genes, hemipteran insects also show substantial HGT activity [6]. This pattern suggests that ecological interactions, particularly those involving close physical contact with potential donor organisms, play a critical role in facilitating HGT in invertebrate systems.

Endosymbiotic bacteria represent a major source of horizontally acquired genes in invertebrates. Wolbachia transfers have been identified in the genomes of diverse invertebrate taxa, with the HGT to Drosophila ananassae from its Wolbachia endosymbiont representing the largest known transfer at approximately 1.4 Mbp [8]. The close association of Wolbachia with germ cells of arthropods promotes stable HGT from these endosymbionts to their hosts, as the bacteria's proximity to reproductive tissues increases the likelihood of germline integration [11].

Virus-mediated gene transfer provides another important mechanism for HGT in animals. Virus-mediated gene transfer occurs through endogenization, where viral DNA or reverse-transcribed RNA is incorporated into host germline genomes, forming endogenous viral elements (EVEs) [6]. Retroviruses uniquely possess the ability to integrate into host genomes as part of their replication cycle, creating permanent genetic records of past infections. This process has been documented across diverse invertebrate lineages, contributing to genomic innovation and adaptation.

The mechanistic basis for invertebrate HGT flexibility includes several permissive features. Many invertebrates lack the strict germline sequestration characteristic of vertebrates, allowing somatic cells to contribute to gamete formation in some lineages. This architectural difference reduces barriers to foreign DNA integration in reproductive tissues. Additionally, invertebrates often experience intimate ecological interactions with potential donor organisms, including parasitism, symbiosis, and predation, which increase opportunities for DNA exchange [12].

In stark contrast, vertebrates and particularly mammals exhibit extreme rarity of confirmed HGT events. This rigidity stems from multiple protective mechanisms that effectively shield vertebrate genomes from foreign DNA integration. The early sequestration of the germline during embryonic development creates a physical barrier that prevents somatic DNA acquisitions from being transmitted to offspring [11]. Unlike plants and many invertebrates, vertebrate germ cells are set aside early in development and remain physically isolated from somatic tissues throughout the organism's life.

High-profile erroneous reports of HGT in humans and other animals have likely had a chilling effect on the field, with many initially claimed vertebrate HGT events subsequently disproven upon rigorous re-analysis [8]. These false positives underscore the methodological challenges in distinguishing genuine HGT from contamination, assembly artifacts, and phylogenetic reconstruction errors. The stringent criteria required to confirm vertebrate HGT reflect both the biological rarity of such events and the scientific community's appropriate skepticism.

The barriers limiting bacteria-to-animal HGT in vertebrates extend beyond germline sequestration. Vertebrate genomes possess sophisticated DNA repair and immune surveillance mechanisms that actively degrade or silence foreign genetic material. The nuclear envelope and nuclear pore complex create additional physical barriers that restrict DNA access to the genome [14]. These protective systems, while essential for maintaining genomic integrity, simultaneously reduce the probability of successful HGT events.

Despite these barriers, some vertebrate HGT events have been confirmed, typically involving transposable elements or viral sequences rather than functional protein-coding genes. The rarity of adaptive vertebrate HGT contrasts sharply with the functional acquisitions documented in invertebrates and plants, suggesting that vertebrate evolution has relied more heavily on vertical inheritance and de novo mutation rather than horizontal acquisition for genomic innovation.

### Cross-Kingdom Events: The Whitefly-Plant Toxin Resistance Case

The transfer of the BtPMaT1 gene from plants to whiteflies (Bemisia tabaci) represents one of the most compelling and well-documented cases of functional cross-kingdom horizontal gene transfer between multicellular eukaryotes. This event exemplifies how HGT can drive adaptive evolution by enabling recipients to overcome host defense mechanisms, with profound implications for pest management and crop protection.

Bemisia tabaci acquired a plant-derived phenolic glucoside malonyltransferase gene through horizontal transfer [5]. This gene, designated BtPMaT1, encodes an enzyme that enables whiteflies to neutralize phenolic glucosides, which are defensive compounds synthesized by plants to deter herbivory [13]. The functional significance of this acquisition lies in its direct contribution to whitefly fitness: by detoxifying plant defense compounds, the horizontally acquired gene allows whiteflies to feed on host plants that would otherwise be toxic or nutritionally inadequate.

The mechanistic basis for this cross-kingdom transfer remains an area of active investigation, though several plausible routes have been proposed. Intimate cell-to-cell contact between whiteflies and plant tissues during feeding creates opportunities for DNA exchange, particularly given the prolonged physical interaction required for phloem sap ingestion. Virus-mediated transfer represents another potential mechanism, as viruses that infect both plants and insects could serve as vectors for genetic material exchange [30]. The discovery of this transfer has opened new avenues for understanding how plant-insect interactions can facilitate genetic exchange across kingdom boundaries.

The adaptive significance of BtPMaT1 acquisition extends beyond individual whitefly fitness to population-level evolutionary dynamics. Whiteflies possessing this gene can exploit a broader range of host plants, expanding their ecological niche and reducing competition with other herbivores. This expansion has agricultural implications, as Bemisia tabaci is a major pest of numerous crop species worldwide. The horizontally acquired detoxification capability contributes to the pest's success and complicates management strategies [31].

This case study distinguishes clearly between neutral DNA integration and functionally adaptive HGT events. Unlike many horizontally acquired sequences that persist as genomic fossils without phenotypic consequence, BtPMaT1 demonstrates clear functional activity and selective advantage. The gene is expressed in whitefly tissues, produces functional enzyme activity, and confers measurable resistance to plant defense compounds. This functional validation sets the whitefly-plant transfer apart from numerous candidate HGT events that lack evidence of biological activity.

The whitefly-plant HGT case also illuminates the broader pattern of plant-to-insect gene transfer. A recent comprehensive analysis has shown that many plant-feeding insects and mites possess genes resulting from HGT events, with 25 plant species identified as donors to insect genomes [6]. The discoveries of RIP-producing genes transfer from plants to whiteflies further demonstrate that the BtPMaT1 event is not isolated but part of a broader pattern of adaptive gene flow from plants to their herbivores [6].

The evolutionary implications of cross-kingdom HGT extend to co-evolutionary dynamics between plants and insects. As plants evolve novel defense compounds, insects may acquire counter-defense genes through HGT rather than waiting for de novo mutations. This process could accelerate the evolutionary arms race between plants and herbivores, with HGT serving as a mechanism for rapid adaptation. The BtPMaT1 case demonstrates that such transfers can occur on evolutionary timescales relevant to contemporary agriculture and pest management.

## Evolutionary Significance and Adaptive Outcomes

Horizontal gene transfer (HGT) in multicellular eukaryotes represents more than a curious genomic anomaly; it constitutes a fundamental evolutionary mechanism that has shaped the adaptive trajectories of diverse lineages. The integration of foreign genetic material into eukaryotic genomes challenges traditional views of inheritance and opens new avenues for understanding how organisms acquire novel functions. This section synthesizes current evolutionary theory regarding HGT in eukaryotes, examining the theoretical frameworks that explain how foreign genes enter multicellular genomes, the criteria for distinguishing functionally adaptive transfers from neutral DNA integration, and the profound implications for phylogenetic reconstruction and our understanding of the Tree of Life.

### The Weak-Link Model: Entry Points for Foreign Genes

The rarity of documented HGT events in multicellular eukaryotes compared to prokaryotes has long puzzled evolutionary biologists. Jinling Huang's Weak-Link Model provides a compelling theoretical framework that addresses this apparent scarcity by identifying specific developmental and life-cycle stages where foreign DNA integration becomes feasible [11]. The model posits that multicellular organisms possess vulnerable windows during which the barriers to foreign DNA uptake are temporarily relaxed, creating entry points for horizontally acquired genetic material.

The Weak-Link Model specifically emphasizes unicellular or early developmental stages as critical windows for HGT integration in multicellular lineages [29]. During these stages, cells exhibit increased permeability to exogenous DNA and reduced capacity for distinguishing self from non-self genetic material. This vulnerability is particularly pronounced in organisms with alternation of generations, where the haploid gametophyte stage represents a unicellular or simple multicellular phase that lacks the sophisticated defense mechanisms present in mature sporophytic tissues.

Empirical support for the Weak-Link Model comes from studies of bryophytes, where researchers have hypothesized that at least two entry points exist for foreign genes to be acquired and integrated into the moss nucleus [10]. The first entry point occurs during spore germination and early gametophyte development, when moss gametophytes develop from haploid spores that are particularly susceptible to DNA uptake. This developmental window provides a mechanistic explanation for how foreign genetic material can bypass the protective barriers that typically shield multicellular tissues from horizontal acquisition.

The model further suggests that early eukaryotes faced daunting challenges in surviving across various niches and diversifying into major lineages [11]. Given that point mutation, recombination, gene duplication, and genome rearrangement operate only on pre-existing genes, HGT offered a mechanism for introducing entirely novel genetic capabilities. The Weak-Link Model explains how these transfers could occur despite the multicellular organization that typically constrains genetic exchange to vertical inheritance pathways.

In some unicellular eukaryotes such as rumen ciliates and the red alga *Galdieria sulphuraria*, acquired genes account for 4–5% of the total genome, demonstrating that HGT can contribute substantially to genomic content when entry barriers are minimized [11]. Similarly, HGT has contributed to over 3% of the nematode genome and at least 8–9% of the gene content in bdelloid rotifers, indicating that the weak-link phenomenon operates across diverse eukaryotic lineages. These percentages suggest that what appears rare in well-studied model organisms may represent a more widespread phenomenon in lineages with life cycles that include extended unicellular phases or reduced multicellular complexity.

The Weak-Link Model also accommodates the role of endosymbiotic relationships in facilitating gene transfer. *Wolbachia* and other endosymbionts have been implicated in the acquisition and evolution of many traits through HGT mechanisms that exploit the intimate cellular associations between host and symbiont [8]. These relationships create sustained opportunities for DNA exchange that bypass the typical barriers to horizontal acquisition in multicellular contexts.

### Functional Validation: Distinguishing Adaptive Genes from Neutral DNA

Not all horizontally acquired genetic material confers a fitness advantage to the recipient organism. Distinguishing between adaptive HGT events and neutral DNA integration represents a critical challenge in evolutionary genomics, as the functional significance of transferred genes determines their evolutionary trajectory and long-term retention in recipient genomes. Multiple criteria have been established for determining whether an HGT event confers a fitness advantage versus representing neutral "junk" DNA that may eventually be lost through genetic drift.

One primary criterion involves assessing selection pressure through Ka/Ks ratio analysis, which compares the rate of non-synonymous to synonymous substitutions. Among transferred elements that diverged within genomes, Ka/Ks ratios significantly less than 1 indicate purifying selection, suggesting functional constraint and adaptive value [23]. This pattern distinguishes functional genes from neutral sequences, which would accumulate mutations at rates consistent with genetic drift rather than selective maintenance.

Functional validation through reverse genetics provides direct evidence for the adaptive role of horizontally acquired genes. However, due to the diversification of transferred genes following the original HGT event, validation of functional roles by reverse genetics presents significant challenges [5]. Despite these difficulties, recent links between specific horizontally acquired genes and resistance phenotypes have been drawn using forward genetics approaches, demonstrating that functional validation remains achievable through complementary methodologies.

A well-documented example of adaptive HGT involves the whitefly *Bemisia tabaci*, which acquired the *BtPMaT1* gene through horizontal transfer [31]. This gene enables the whitefly to detoxify phenolic glucosides produced by host plants, providing a clear fitness advantage in plant-insect interactions. The *BtPMaT1* case exemplifies how HGT can confer immediate ecological benefits by allowing recipients to overcome host defense mechanisms or exploit new resources.

Similarly, the extremophilic red alga *Galdieria sulphuraria* demonstrates adaptation facilitated by acquisition of genes from extremophile bacteria and archaea [15]. These horizontally acquired genes enable *Galdieria* to thrive in extreme environments characterized by high temperature, acidity, and heavy metal concentrations that would be lethal to most eukaryotes. This case illustrates how HGT can provide access to entirely new ecological niches through the acquisition of specialized metabolic capabilities.

Bayesian analyses of HGT dynamics have estimated that approximately 40% (with a confidence interval of 20–60%) of observed transfers are adaptive rather than neutral [17]. This substantial proportion suggests that while many HGT events may represent neutral DNA integration, a significant fraction confers measurable fitness advantages that drive their retention and propagation in recipient populations.

Phylogenetic criteria provide additional validation tools for distinguishing adaptive HGT from neutral integration. Gene-tree versus species-tree incongruence and patchy presence/absence patterns across taxa serve as indicators of horizontal acquisition [5]. When combined with evidence of intron conservation between donor and recipient species, these phylogenetic signatures provide strong indications of HGT occurrence and can help distinguish recent adaptive transfers from ancient neutral integrations.

Functional enrichment analysis using databases such as eggNOG enables researchers to identify functional categories that show significant enrichment or depletion in horizontally transferred genes [25]. This approach reveals which biological processes are most frequently acquired through HGT and helps identify transfers that likely confer adaptive advantages. Genes involved in metabolism, detoxification, and stress response consistently show enrichment among horizontally acquired sequences, supporting the hypothesis that HGT primarily serves adaptive functions rather than representing neutral genomic noise.

The validation of functional roles becomes increasingly challenging as transferred genes diversify following the original HGT event. However, the link between specific horizontally acquired genes and resistance phenotypes demonstrates that functional validation remains achievable through integrated genomic and phenotypic approaches [5]. This validation is essential for distinguishing truly adaptive HGT events from the background of neutral DNA integration that characterizes much of eukaryotic genomic evolution.

### Impact on Phylogeny: Challenging the Tree of Life

The widespread occurrence of horizontal gene transfer in eukaryotes fundamentally challenges traditional phylogenetic reconstruction methods and necessitates a reconceptualization of evolutionary relationships as network-based rather than strictly tree-based. HGT introduces phylogenetic incongruence that complicates the inference of species relationships and requires new analytical frameworks for understanding eukaryotic evolution.

Gene-tree versus species-tree incongruence represents a primary signature of HGT that disrupts phylogenetic reconstruction [5]. When horizontally acquired genes are included in phylogenetic analyses without appropriate filtering, they produce topologies that conflict with the species tree, creating apparent contradictions in evolutionary relationships. This incongruence has long been recognized as a problem in microbial phylogenetics, but its significance in eukaryotic evolution has only recently been fully appreciated.

The difficulty in differentiating between horizontal gene transfer and gene loss in phylogenetic trees represents a persistent analytical challenge [7]. Both processes can produce similar patterns of patchy gene distribution across taxa, making it difficult to determine whether absent homologs result from true absence in ancestral lineages or from secondary loss following horizontal acquisition. Careful comparison of multiple genes and the application of reconciliation methods help address this ambiguity, but uncertainty remains inherent in phylogenetic inference when HGT is prevalent.

Phylogeny-based approaches utilizing gene-species tree reconstruction and reconciliation have improved the detection of ancient HGT events across diverse eukaryotic lineages [26]. These methods overcome the limitation that statistically detectable sequence similarity fades over evolutionary time, enabling researchers to identify transfers that occurred deep in eukaryotic evolutionary history. The application of these approaches has revealed that HGT has played a more substantial role in eukaryotic evolution than previously assumed based on sequence similarity alone.

Reconciliation analyses using tools such as RANGER-DTL have identified millions of well-supported unique transfer events across thousands of species, demonstrating the scale of horizontal exchange in eukaryotic genomes [25]. These analyses reveal that host-associated specialist species are most likely to exchange genes with other host-associated specialists, whereas species found across different habitats show similar gene exchange rates irrespective of their preferred habitat. This pattern suggests that ecological proximity and interaction frequency drive HGT more strongly than habitat type alone.

The Tree of Life concept, traditionally represented as a bifurcating tree reflecting vertical descent, must be modified to accommodate the reticulate patterns introduced by horizontal gene transfer [10]. Network-based representations better capture the complexity of eukaryotic evolution, where genetic material flows not only from parent to offspring but also between contemporaneous lineages through horizontal exchange. This reconceptualization does not invalidate phylogenetic methods but requires their adaptation to account for non-tree-like evolutionary processes.

HGT rapidly alters genetic composition, enabling the evolution of new catabolic abilities and the spread of adaptive genes among populations [6]. This rapid alteration challenges phylogenetic methods that assume gradual divergence through vertical inheritance, as horizontally acquired genes can introduce substantial genetic change in single evolutionary events. The resulting phylogenetic patterns reflect both vertical descent and horizontal exchange, requiring analytical approaches that can disentangle these overlapping signals.

The impact of HGT on phylogeny extends beyond methodological challenges to fundamental questions about the nature of species and lineages in eukaryotic evolution. When significant portions of genomes derive from horizontal acquisition, the concept of a species genome as a coherent unit inherited through vertical descent becomes problematic. Instead, eukaryotic genomes emerge as mosaics assembled through both vertical inheritance and horizontal acquisition, with the relative contributions varying across lineages and evolutionary timescales.

Phylogenetic reconstruction in the presence of HGT requires careful consideration of which genes to include in analyses and how to weight conflicting signals. Universal single-copy marker genes provide a foundation for species tree reconstruction, but even these markers may be subject to horizontal transfer in some lineages [25]. The development of methods that can identify and exclude horizontally transferred genes from species tree inference, while simultaneously analyzing HGT patterns as evolutionary signals in their own right, represents an ongoing challenge in phylogenomic research.

The network-based view of eukaryotic evolution that emerges from acknowledging widespread HGT does not diminish the importance of vertical inheritance but rather contextualizes it within a broader framework of genetic exchange. Vertical transfer remains the dominant mode of inheritance for most genes in most lineages, but horizontal transfer introduces evolutionary novelty and adaptive potential that vertical processes alone cannot generate [12]. Understanding the interplay between these modes of inheritance is essential for reconstructing the evolutionary history of eukaryotic life and for predicting how lineages will respond to future environmental challenges through genetic innovation.

## Methodological Challenges and Detection Biases

The detection and quantification of horizontal gene transfer (HGT) in multicellular eukaryotes remain among the most contentious areas in evolutionary genomics. Unlike prokaryotes, where HGT detection pipelines are relatively standardized, eukaryotic HGT identification faces unique obstacles stemming from genome complexity, uneven taxonomic sampling, and methodological limitations. These challenges collectively contribute to the elusive consensus on HGT frequency across eukaryotic lineages. This section critically examines three interconnected sources of uncertainty: database biases that skew interdomain transfer detection, the tension between phylogenetic incongruence and sequence composition methods, and the hypothesis that current candidate sets represent only a fraction of actual historical transfer events.

### Database Bias in Interdomain Transfer Detection

The reliability of HGT detection is fundamentally constrained by the composition and coverage of reference databases used for comparative genomics. Uneven sequencing efforts across the tree of life create systematic biases that distort both the perceived frequency and directionality of interdomain gene transfers. When certain taxonomic groups are overrepresented in genomic databases while others remain undersampled, detection algorithms inevitably produce skewed results that reflect sampling artifacts rather than biological reality.

A prominent example of this bias emerges in fungal genomics, particularly within the Pezizomycotina subphylum. After conducting horizontal gene transfer searches over several years within Pezizomycotina, researchers identified a potential additional bias in detection approaches that may overlook HGT instances if potential homologs to candidate genes are excluded during clustering steps [4]. This Pezizomycotina bias illustrates how intensive sequencing of specific lineages can create false impressions of HGT hotspots, while simultaneously obscuring transfers in less-studied groups. The problem compounds when clustering algorithms remove sequences that appear as outliers, potentially eliminating genuine horizontally acquired genes that lack close relatives in the database.

Database imbalances also affect the perceived directionality of transfers. When bacterial genomes vastly outnumber eukaryotic genomes in reference databases, algorithms may preferentially identify bacterial-to-eukaryote transfers while missing eukaryote-to-bacteria or eukaryote-to-eukaryote events. This asymmetry is exacerbated by the fact that many detection pipelines rely on best-hit approaches that assume the most similar sequence represents the true donor. However, if the actual donor lineage is absent from the database, the algorithm may incorrectly assign a different taxon as the source, creating spurious transfer inferences.

The reliance on manual curation further restricts the scope of HGT studies and introduces additional bias. Limited understanding of HGT frequency is due in part from the reliance on manual curation of transposable elements and individual detection and validation of transfer events, which has restricted the scope of studies on horizontal transposable element transfer [23]. Manual curation, while valuable for reducing false positives, is inherently subjective and scales poorly with the exponential growth of genomic data. Different research groups applying different curation criteria to the same datasets often produce divergent HGT catalogs, undermining efforts to establish consensus estimates.

Contamination represents another critical source of database-related error. During genome sequencing and assembly, foreign DNA from associated microorganisms, parasites, or laboratory reagents can be inadvertently incorporated into host genome assemblies. Because of the potential contamination in the process of genome sequencing, any candidate of acquired genes located on a short scaffold was removed from further consideration in rigorous studies [10]. However, not all studies apply such stringent filters, and contamination-derived false positives continue to populate HGT databases. The problem is particularly acute for organisms with intimate microbial associations, such as insects harboring Wolbachia endosymbionts or plants colonized by endophytic fungi.

Sequencing technology itself introduces biases that affect HGT detection. The latest developments in sequencing technologies have opened new perspectives for HGT research, but different platforms produce different error profiles and assembly artifacts that can mimic or obscure transfer signals [13]. Short-read sequencing may fail to resolve repetitive regions where horizontally acquired genes often reside, while long-read technologies, though improving assembly continuity, introduce different systematic errors. The accuracy of detection pipelines may depend on sequencing depth and length, bioinformatic algorithms, tools, databases, and pipelines, creating additional layers of methodological variability [24].

### Phylogenetic Incongruence versus Sequence Composition

Two broad classes of methods dominate HGT detection in eukaryotes: phylogeny-based approaches that identify incongruence between gene trees and species trees, and composition-based approaches that detect anomalous sequence signatures. Each methodology carries distinct strengths and limitations, and the choice between them significantly influences detection outcomes. The tension between these approaches reflects a fundamental trade-off between sensitivity and specificity in HGT identification.

Phylogenetic incongruence remains the gold standard for HGT detection because it directly tests the evolutionary history of genes against the expected species phylogeny. Gene-tree versus species-tree incongruence and patchy presence/absence across taxa serve as primary phylogeny criteria for identifying candidate transfers [5]. However, reconstructing accurate phylogenetic relationships faces substantial challenges. In relation to phylogenetic incongruence, the problem is that it is difficult to differentiate between horizontal gene transfer and gene loss in a phylogenetic tree [7]. A gene that appears in distantly related taxa but is absent from intermediate lineages could represent either a horizontal transfer or multiple independent loss events, and distinguishing between these scenarios requires dense taxonomic sampling that is often unavailable.

The computational intensity of phylogeny-based methods further limits their application. Phylogenetic tree reconstruction and reconciliation across thousands of gene families demands substantial computational resources, making genome-wide surveys prohibitively expensive for many research groups. This, however, remains too computationally intensive and raises some conceptual issues for large-scale implementation [13]. As a result, many studies resort to composition-based shortcuts that sacrifice phylogenetic rigor for computational tractability.

Sequence composition methods detect HGT by identifying genes with atypical nucleotide or amino acid signatures relative to the host genome. Historically, sequence similarity and composition-based statistics such as GC content, oligonucleotide frequency and codon usage biases have been employed for HGT detection [26]. These methods operate on the assumption that horizontally acquired genes retain the compositional signature of their donor genome for some period after transfer, creating a detectable anomaly within the recipient genome. However, this assumption breaks down over evolutionary time as amelioration processes gradually adjust the composition of foreign genes to match the host genome.

Composition-based approaches suffer from high false positive rates, particularly for housekeeping genes under strong purifying selection. The false positive detection of house-keeping genes occurs when high sequence identity among distantly related taxa results from strong purifying selection through strict vertical inheritance rather than horizontal transfer [13]. Genes encoding essential cellular functions often exhibit unusual compositional patterns due to functional constraints rather than foreign origin, leading composition-based methods to incorrectly flag them as horizontally acquired. This problem is compounded by the fact that different genomic regions naturally exhibit compositional heterogeneity even without HGT, creating background noise that obscures genuine transfer signals.

Hybrid approaches that combine phylogenetic and compositional evidence offer improved accuracy but introduce their own complexities. Tools like HGTector attempt to balance these considerations, though this approach may not be stringent enough to determine the phylogenetic relationship between candidates and their homologs [4]. The integration of multiple lines of evidence requires careful weighting of different signal types, and there is no consensus on optimal integration strategies. Some pipelines prioritize phylogenetic incongruence and treat composition as supporting evidence, while others reverse this hierarchy, producing systematically different results from the same input data.

Intron conservation provides an additional layer of evidence that can strengthen HGT inferences when available. Intron similarity between the donor and recipient species provides a strong indication of HGT occurrence, particularly for eukaryote-to-eukaryote transfers where intron positions are often conserved [5]. However, this criterion applies only to a subset of transfers and requires high-quality gene annotations that are unavailable for many non-model organisms. The reliance on multiple independent criteria, while improving confidence in individual cases, further reduces the sensitivity of detection pipelines and contributes to underestimation of HGT frequency.

### The "Tip of the Iceberg" Hypothesis

The cumulative effect of methodological limitations and database biases has led to a growing consensus that current catalogs of horizontally acquired eukaryotic genes represent only a small fraction of actual historical transfer events. This "tip of the iceberg" hypothesis posits that the true contribution of HGT to eukaryotic evolution substantially exceeds current estimates, with most transfers remaining undetected due to methodological constraints rather than biological rarity.

The hypothesis gains support from case studies where improved detection methods or expanded taxonomic sampling reveal previously hidden transfers. Based on findings from extremophile organisms, researchers raise the hypothesis that horizontally acquired genes may have contributed more to adaptive evolution of eukaryotes than previously assumed, with current candidate sets of horizontally acquired eukaryotic genes potentially representing just the tip of an iceberg [15]. The red alga Galdieria sulphuraria, for instance, acquired numerous genes from extremophile bacteria and archaea that facilitated adaptation to harsh environments, demonstrating that massive HGT events can occur in eukaryotes when ecological conditions favor intimate cross-domain interactions.

Quantitative estimates from diverse eukaryotic lineages support the notion that HGT frequency has been systematically underestimated. In some unicellular eukaryotes such as rumen ciliates and red alga Galdieria sulphuraria, acquired genes account for 4–5% of the total genome; similarly, HGT has contributed to over 3% of the nematode genome and at least 8–9% of the gene content in bdelloid rotifers [11]. These percentages substantially exceed earlier estimates for multicellular eukaryotes, suggesting that detection biases rather than biological differences may explain the apparent disparity. As detection methods improve and taxonomic sampling expands, similar levels of HGT may be discovered in lineages currently thought to experience minimal horizontal transfer.

The lack of global quantification echoes limited knowledge about the full scope of horizontal transfer events. The lack of global quantification of horizontal transposable element transfer reflects the limited understanding due to reliance on manual curation and individual validation [23]. This limitation applies equally to protein-coding genes, where comprehensive surveys remain rare. Most published HGT catalogs derive from focused studies of specific taxa or gene families rather than systematic genome-wide analyses across the eukaryotic tree of life. The resulting patchwork of evidence makes it impossible to distinguish genuine biological variation in HGT frequency from methodological artifacts.

Ancient transfers pose particular challenges for detection and contribute significantly to the iceberg effect. Over evolutionary time, horizontally acquired genes undergo amelioration, losing the compositional signatures that initially marked them as foreign. Phylogenetic signals also degrade as sequences diverge, making it increasingly difficult to reconstruct donor lineages for ancient transfers. A candidate gene was determined to be horizontally acquired based on gene tree topology showing a green plant/donor clade with bootstrap support of over 80% from maximum likelihood or distance analyses, but such clear signals become rare for ancient events [10]. Many ancient transfers may be permanently invisible to current detection methods, existing only as unrecognizable contributions to eukaryotic genome content.

The hypothesis also accounts for transfers that occurred but failed to persist in modern genomes. Nature has lots of chances for making even the most improbable events happen, and many HGT events described may merely reflect random occurrences happening at low frequency in populations [8]. Most horizontally acquired genes are likely lost through drift or selection before they can be detected in modern genomes, creating a vast hidden population of transient transfers that never achieved fixation. The detectable HGT events represent only those rare cases where transferred genes provided sufficient selective advantage to persist over evolutionary timescales.

Recent comprehensive analyses continue to reveal previously unknown transfers, supporting the iceberg hypothesis. A recent comprehensive analysis of 218 high-quality insect genomes has shown that insects have acquired 1410 genes from 670 unique donors, including 25 plant species [6]. While lepidopterans demonstrate the highest rate of horizontally acquired genes, hemipteran insects such as whiteflies have also acquired functionally significant genes from plants. These findings emerged only after systematic screening of high-quality genomes, suggesting that similar surveys of other eukaryotic lineages would reveal comparable levels of previously undetected HGT.

The implications of the iceberg hypothesis extend beyond mere quantification to fundamental questions about eukaryotic evolution. If HGT has contributed substantially more genetic material to eukaryotes than currently recognized, then the boundary between vertical and horizontal inheritance becomes increasingly blurred. Multiple lines of evidence suggest that HGT spans across various domains of life, influencing ecosystems and the fitness of recipient organisms from bacteria to plants, plants to insects, fungi to plants, and beyond [6]. Accepting the iceberg hypothesis requires reconceptualizing eukaryotic genomes as dynamic mosaics shaped by continuous genetic exchange rather than closed systems evolving primarily through vertical descent.

The path forward requires coordinated improvements in detection methodology, database coverage, and validation standards. Accurate detection and classification of ancient and recent HGT events remains a major computational and conceptual challenge [26]. Until these challenges are addressed, consensus on HGT frequency will remain elusive, and the true extent of horizontal gene transfer's contribution to eukaryotic evolution will remain hidden beneath the visible tip of the iceberg.

## Conclusion and Future Research Directions

The accumulated evidence on horizontal gene transfer (HGT) in multicellular eukaryotes reveals a complex landscape where mechanistic uncertainty coexists with growing genomic documentation. This concluding section synthesizes the weight of evidence regarding HGT frequency, evaluates the translational potential of natural transfer mechanisms for biotechnology, and identifies the most critical gaps in understanding germline integration in mammals. The resolution of these questions will determine whether HGT represents a marginal evolutionary curiosity or a fundamental force shaping eukaryotic diversification.

### Summary of Evidence: Resolving the Frequency Paradox

The central paradox in eukaryotic HGT research concerns whether transfer events are genuinely rare biological occurrences or merely appear rare due to detection limitations and stringent verification criteria. The weight of current evidence supports a nuanced resolution: HGT is infrequent relative to prokaryotes but widespread enough to have shaped significant adaptive innovations across diverse eukaryotic lineages [15][8]. This conclusion rests on distinguishing between the raw frequency of DNA integration events and the subset of transfers that achieve fixation and functional significance.

Genomic surveys reveal substantial variation in HGT contribution across eukaryotic groups. In some unicellular eukaryotes such as rumen ciliates and the red alga *Galdieria sulphuraria*, acquired genes account for 4–5% of the total genome [11]. Similarly, HGT has contributed to over 3% of the nematode genome and at least 8–9% of the gene content in bdelloid rotifers [11]. These figures demonstrate that under certain ecological and physiological conditions, eukaryotic genomes can incorporate foreign DNA at rates approaching prokaryotic levels. However, such cases represent exceptions rather than the rule for multicellular eukaryotes, where germline sequestration and developmental complexity impose additional barriers to heritable transfer.

The Weak-Link Model provides a theoretical framework for understanding this variability [29][11]. This model posits that HGT frequency depends on the presence of ecological or physiological "weak links" that temporarily relax barriers between donor and recipient lineages. For early eukaryotes, surviving in various niches and diversifying into major lineages was probably daunting, and horizontally acquired genes may have contributed more to adaptive evolution than previously assumed [11]. Current candidate sets of horizontally acquired eukaryotic genes may represent only the tip of an iceberg, with many ancient transfers obscured by sequence divergence over evolutionary time [15].

Critically, the evidence distinguishes between neutral DNA integration and functionally adaptive HGT events. Many detected transfers represent genomic noise—DNA fragments that integrated without conferring selective advantage and were subsequently lost or degraded. In contrast, adaptive transfers like the *BtPMaT1* gene in whiteflies (*Bemisia tabaci*) demonstrate clear functional significance [31]. This plant-derived gene enables whiteflies to detoxify phenolic glucosides, providing a direct fitness advantage in plant-feeding contexts [13]. Such cases validate that when HGT occurs and persists, it can drive genuine evolutionary innovation rather than merely accumulating as neutral sequence baggage.

The frequency paradox ultimately resolves through recognizing that HGT in eukaryotes operates under different constraints than in prokaryotes. Physical proximity between donor and recipient remains a key condition, with most HGT mechanisms requiring intimate cell-to-cell contact [25][5]. Host-associated specialist species are most likely to exchange genes with other host-associated specialists, whereas species found across different habitats show similar gene exchange rates irrespective of their preferred habitat [25]. This ecological structuring explains why HGT appears patchy across the eukaryotic tree—lineages with sustained intimate associations (parasites, symbionts, grafting-compatible plants) experience elevated transfer rates, while isolated lineages show minimal evidence of foreign gene acquisition.

Detection methodology further complicates frequency estimates. Accurate detection and classification of ancient and recent HGT events remains a major computational and conceptual challenge [26]. Historically, sequence similarity and composition-based statistics such as GC content, oligonucleotide frequency, and codon usage biases served as primary detection tools, but these approaches may overlook HGT instances if potential homologs are excluded during clustering steps [4][26]. Phylogeny-based approaches can better detect ancient events, yet statistically detectable sequence similarity fades over evolutionary time [26]. The identification of 15,242 HGT candidates in Pezizomycotina fungi following rigorous filtering demonstrates both the potential scale of undetected transfers and the importance of methodological stringency [4].

The final judgment on the frequency paradox must therefore acknowledge both realities: HGT is rare enough that most eukaryotic lineages show minimal evidence of recent foreign gene acquisition, yet widespread enough that cumulative transfers across deep time have contributed meaningfully to eukaryotic genome evolution [32][8]. Many described HGT events likely reflect random occurrences at low population frequencies, but at other low frequencies, selective pressures can drive these transfers to fixation when they confer adaptive advantages [8]. The question is not whether HGT occurs in eukaryotes—it demonstrably does—but rather under what ecological, physiological, and molecular conditions transfer transitions from rare accident to evolutionary catalyst.

### Implications for Biotechnology and Synthetic Biology

Understanding natural HGT mechanisms offers substantial potential for advancing biotechnology and synthetic biology applications, particularly in crop engineering and gene therapy safety assessment. The mechanisms by which foreign DNA naturally crosses cellular and species barriers in eukaryotes provide blueprints for developing more efficient and targeted genetic modification strategies [6]. Recent progress in methodologies for omics and computational biological investigation is opening previously inaccessible mechanistic black boxes, illuminating pathways that biotechnology can exploit or must safeguard against [6].

Virus-mediated gene transfer represents one of the most promising natural mechanisms for biotechnological application. Horizontal gene transfer from viruses to plants occurs through endogenization, where viral DNA or reverse-transcribed RNA is incorporated into host germline genomes, forming endogenous viral elements (EVEs) [6]. This mechanism is remarkably prevalent, evidenced by the widespread integration of EVEs into plant genomes [6]. Deep sequencing of moth baculovirus populations revealed that 4.8% of viruses contained at least one host sequence, providing stronger evidence for the possible role of viruses as HGT vectors [13]. These findings support a role for baculoviruses as vectors of DNA horizontal transfer between insects and call for evaluation of possible gene or transposable element spread when using viral delivery systems in biotechnology [30].

For crop engineering, natural HGT pathways suggest novel strategies for introducing desirable traits. Grafting, consisting of joining tissues from different plant species, enables regular transfer of water, nutrients, proteins, mRNAs, and pathogens through vascular connections [13][1]. Experimental grafting between transplastomic *Nicotiana tabacum* and transgenic *N. glauca* or *N. benthamiana* plants demonstrated selection for intercellular gene transfer under kanamycin and spectinomycin resistance [9]. This natural mechanism could be harnessed for trait introgression without traditional transformation methods, potentially reducing regulatory barriers and public resistance associated with genetic modification.

Parasitic plant interactions offer another route for natural gene transfer with biotechnological implications. The transfer of a gene encoding a 448 amino acid protein from the parasitic plant *Striga* to sorghum demonstrates that intimate host-parasite relationships facilitate DNA movement [13]. In sorghum, the transferred gene is intronless, contrasting with its homolog in *Striga*, providing indirect evidence that the transfer occurred via processed mRNA or cDNA intermediates [5]. Understanding these mechanisms could inform strategies for engineering resistance genes that move between crops and wild relatives, or conversely, for preventing unwanted gene flow from genetically modified crops to weedy species.

The potential applications of HGT knowledge in plant biotechnology are pivotal for shaping the future landscape of bioengineering, including climate-smart crop development [6]. HGT-mediated acquisition of new functions in higher eukaryotes involving plants as donors or receptors has numerous documented examples [13]. For instance, genes involved in desiccation resistance in rotifers originated from plants through HGT, and phenolic glucoside synthesis genes transferred from plants to animals enable specialized feeding adaptations [13]. These natural precedents validate the feasibility of cross-kingdom trait transfer and suggest that engineered HGT could accelerate crop adaptation to climate stress.

Gene therapy safety considerations must account for natural HGT mechanisms to assess risks of unintended genetic consequences. The financial support from agricultural research institutions highlights growing recognition of HGT implications for biotechnology [6]. CRISPR/Cas9-based multiplex gene editing and tracking of gene editing outcomes must consider the possibility that edited sequences could transfer horizontally through natural vectors [20][31]. Viral vectors used in gene therapy could potentially mediate unintended HGT events, particularly given evidence that host DNA influx serves as a potent source of genetic diversity in viral populations [30].

However, significant knowledge gaps limit the translation of natural HGT mechanisms into biotechnological applications. Although occurrences of HGT between plants and various prokaryotic and eukaryotic organisms are now well founded, mechanisms of this HGT remain vastly unknown [6]. Recent progress in omics methodologies and computational biology seems to be opening these black boxes, but mechanistic understanding lags behind genomic documentation [6]. For biotechnology to safely and effectively harness HGT pathways, researchers must move beyond documenting that transfer occurred to elucidating how DNA crosses barriers at the molecular level.

The distinction between adaptive and neutral HGT events carries particular importance for biotechnology. A Bayesian model inferred that approximately 40% of observed transfers in experimental systems are adaptive, with repeatable fitness increases of about 5% signaling adaptive evolution by HGT [17][17]. This suggests that most spontaneous HGT events do not confer immediate fitness benefits, raising questions about the reliability of HGT-based engineering strategies. Biotechnological applications must therefore incorporate selection regimes that mimic natural adaptive pressures to ensure transferred genes provide functional advantages rather than accumulating as genomic burden.

### Unresolved Questions: Germline Integration in Mammals

The most significant gap in eukaryotic HGT research concerns the molecular mechanisms enabling germline integration in mammals and higher vertebrates. While genomic evidence increasingly documents foreign DNA in animal genomes, confirmed cases of functional germline HGT in mammals remain exceptionally rare, and the mechanistic pathways remain speculative [8]. This knowledge gap represents a critical barrier to understanding whether mammalian HGT represents genuine biological phenomenon or methodological artifact.

The fundamental challenge involves distinguishing between somatic and germline integration. With the enormous size of the human microbiome, there exists an equally enormous opportunity for HGT to somatic cells that could have profound effects in human health and disease [8]. However, somatic integration does not constitute heritable evolution. For HGT to impact mammalian evolution, foreign DNA must integrate into germline cells and transmit to offspring. The known and putative mechanisms of HGT summarized in recent reviews indicate that virus-mediated gene transfer occurs through endogenization, where viral DNA or reverse-transcribed RNA incorporates into host germline genomes [6]. Retroviruses uniquely possess the molecular machinery for this process, yet non-retroviral HGT in mammalian germlines lacks confirmed mechanistic explanations.

High-profile erroneous reports of HGT in humans and other animals have likely had a chilling effect on the field, creating skepticism that may obscure genuine but rare events [8]. Problems identifying bacteria-to-animal HGT in existing genome data include contamination, imbalanced databases, and methodological artifacts that generate false positives [4][8]. These technical challenges compound the biological barriers that limit HGT in mammals, including germline sequestration, immune surveillance, and epigenetic silencing of foreign DNA.

Several plausible hypotheses about ecological characteristics which allowed horizontal transfer to happen remain untested in mammalian systems. Overlapping habitat, sharing of parasites by the species involved, or overlapping habitats of ancestral lineages when ancient HGT events are detected represent recommended considerations for future research [12]. However, these ecological correlations do not explain the molecular mechanisms by which DNA crosses the multiple barriers protecting mammalian germlines. Phages, endosymbiotic bacteria, and a wide diversity of parasitic arthropods, mammals, and plant species could mediate horizontal transfer, but accumulated evidence showing these vectors in action remains limited [12].

The nuclear envelope and nucleocytoplasmic transport machinery represent potential barriers that require mechanistic investigation. Many nucleocytoplasmic transport components have been isolated, but further study is required for understanding the molecular mechanisms and physiological roles of nuclear transport systems [19]. In plants, nucleoporin mutants including *nup160*, *nup107*, *nua*, and *hos1/elys* exhibit alterations that could affect DNA import, suggesting parallel investigations in mammals could reveal vulnerability points for HGT [19]. The SUN-KASH domain proteins connecting the inner and outer nuclear membranes in animals represent conserved structures that could facilitate or block foreign DNA entry [2].

Mavericks, a class of transposable elements, gained a novel herpesvirus-like fusogen in nematodes, leading to widespread exchange of cargo genes between extremely divergent species, bypassing sexual and genetic barriers spanning hundreds of millions of years [30]. This demonstrates that mobile genetic elements can evolve mechanisms to overcome species barriers, raising the question of whether similar elements exist in mammals that could facilitate HGT. However, no equivalent mammalian system has been characterized, leaving this mechanism speculative for vertebrate germlines.

Future research must prioritize several specific directions to resolve these uncertainties. First, careful evolutionary genomic analyses will benefit understanding of the role of HGT in the innovation and evolution of eukaryotes [11]. Such work requires distinguishing genuine HGT from contamination through rigorous validation protocols, including PCR confirmation of integration sites, transcriptomic evidence of expression, and phylogenetic congruence across multiple genes [12]. Second, experimental systems that model mammalian germline exposure to foreign DNA under controlled conditions could test whether integration occurs and identify molecular requirements [8].

Third, improved computational pipelines for HGT detection must address current limitations. The RiboTaxa pipeline and similar tools show that accuracy depends on sequencing depth and length, bioinformatic algorithms, tools, databases, and pipelines [24]. Standardized pipelines like hgtseq are emerging, but field-wide adoption and benchmarking against known positive and negative controls remains incomplete [21]. Finally, several core and widespread genes least influenced by HGT could become useful markers for building robust phylogenies and addressing outstanding technical challenges to improve phylogeny-based genome-wide HGT detection methods for future applications [26].

The resolution of germline integration mechanisms in mammals will ultimately determine whether HGT represents a meaningful force in vertebrate evolution or remains confined to invertebrates, plants, and unicellular eukaryotes. Nature has lots of chances for making even the most improbable events happen, and many HGT events described may merely reflect random occurrence at low population frequency [8]. However, at other low frequencies, selective pressures can drive these transfers to fixation when they confer adaptive advantages. The task for future research is identifying the conditions under which improbable becomes inevitable, and distinguishing biological signal from methodological noise in the quest to understand horizontal gene transfer's true scope in multicellular eukaryotes.
