# Gut Microbiota Dysbiosis, Colorectal Cancer Pathogenesis, and Dietary Modulation

## Foundational Concepts: Microbiota, Dysbiosis, and Therapeutic Definitions

### Consensus Definitions of Probiotics, Prebiotics, and Synbiotics

Precise terminology is essential for advancing microbiome-based interventions in colorectal cancer (CRC) prevention and therapy. The International Scientific Association for Probiotics and Prebiotics (ISAPP) has established consensus definitions that distinguish these therapeutic categories from common commercial usage, emphasizing strain-specificity and mechanistic evidence over generalized claims [18].

**Probiotics** are defined as live microorganisms that, when administered in adequate amounts, confer a health benefit on the host. This definition requires demonstration of benefit through clinical or preclinical evidence, not merely survival through the gastrointestinal tract. Critically, probiotic effects are strain-specific; benefits observed with one strain cannot be extrapolated to other strains within the same species or genus [17]. For example, *Lactobacillus mucosae* 0988 demonstrated prominent anti-tumor results in CRC models, whereas other *Lactobacillus* strains showed variable or negligible effects [17]. The World Health Organization recognizes specific probiotic strains as safe, though this designation does not guarantee therapeutic efficacy for CRC indications [8].

**Prebiotics** are nondigestible food components that selectively stimulate the growth or activity of beneficial gut bacteria. Common prebiotics include galactooligosaccharides (GOS) and fructooligosaccharides (FOS), which selectively stimulate *Bifidobacterium* and *Lactobacillus* populations [18][19]. The key distinction from general dietary fiber is selectivity: prebiotics must demonstrably favor beneficial taxa over pathogenic or neutral organisms through fermentation processes that produce short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), particularly butyrate [18].

**Synbiotics** combine probiotics and prebiotics in formulations designed to work synergistically. The prebiotic component supports the survival and colonization of the co-administered probiotic strain while simultaneously stimulating resident beneficial microbiota [18]. This dual mechanism aims to overcome limitations of probiotic monotherapy, where transient colonization often limits therapeutic durability.

**Postbiotics**, an emerging category, refer to inanimate microorganisms and/or their components that confer health benefits. This includes bacterial metabolites such as indoles, bacteriocins, and cell wall fragments that mediate immunomodulatory effects without requiring live organism administration [18].

This strain-specificity requirement prevents overgeneralization of probiotic benefits and ensures that subsequent therapeutic recommendations reference documented mechanisms rather than taxonomic categories alone [18].

### The Dysbiosis-Carcinogenesis Axis

Gut dysbiosis in the context of CRC represents a fundamental shift from symbiotic homeostasis to tumor-promoting microbial ecosystems. This transition is not merely a change in microbial abundance but involves functional reprogramming of host-microbe metabolic interactions that converge on canonical oncogenic pathways, including Wnt/β-catenin, NF-κB, STAT3, and MAPK signaling cascades [31].

Dysbiosis drives carcinogenesis through multiple interconnected mechanisms: DNA damage induction, chronic inflammation perpetuation, metabolic rewiring, epithelial barrier disruption, and tumor microenvironment remodeling [31]. The dysbiotic state is characterized by depletion of beneficial butyrate-producing bacteria (primarily from the Bacillota phylum, including *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* and *Ruminococcus* species) alongside enrichment of pathogenic or pro-inflammatory species from Proteobacteria and Fusobacteria phyla [8].

**Emerging CRC-Associated Pathobionts**

Critical distinction must be made between established carcinogens and emerging CRC-associated pathobionts. *Helicobacter pylori* is a Group 1 carcinogen with established causality for gastric cancer, whereas several gut bacteria show strong association with CRC but require further mechanistic validation for causal classification [26].

**Colibactin-producing *Escherichia coli*** (pks+ *E. coli*) represents a key emerging pathobiont. Approximately 20% of people carry strains containing the polyketide synthase (pks) genomic island responsible for colibactin production [26]. Colibactin functions as a genotoxin that promotes crosslinking of duplex DNA at AT-rich sequences, generating mutations detected in colorectal cancers [2][12]. These mutations exhibit specific signatures attributed to colibactin exposure, with early-life exposure windows potentially contributing to early-onset CRC [12].

**Fusobacterium nucleatum** is enriched in CRC tissue compared to adjacent normal mucosa and promotes tumor progression through multiple mechanisms. This bacterium amplifies epithelial barrier disruption and pro-tumor immune modulation, often co-occurring with enterotoxigenic *Bacteroides fragilis* and colibactin-producing *E. coli* to collectively enhance carcinogenic potential [31][8].

**Enterotoxigenic *Bacteroides fragilis*** (ETBF) produces Bacteroides fragilis toxin (BFT), which cleaves E-cadherin, disrupts epithelial integrity, and activates β-catenin signaling. ETBF induces spermine oxidase (SMO), which catalyzes spermine to produce hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), generating reactive oxygen species that damage DNA, proteins, and cellular components [5].

**Molecular Mechanisms of Metabolite Effects**

Microbial metabolites exert direct effects on CRC pathogenesis through defined molecular mechanisms. **Butyrate**, a short-chain fatty acid produced by bacterial fermentation of dietary fiber, functions as a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor [1]. HDAC inhibition regulates transcription of cytokine genes and promotes differentiation and apoptosis in colonocytes, providing chemopreventive effects [29][13]. Butyrate also strengthens gut barrier function and promotes IL-22 production via CD4+ T cells through FFAR2-dependent HDAC inhibition [18][13].

**Secondary bile acids**, produced through microbial biotransformation of primary bile acids, exert detrimental effects on colonic epithelium. Bacterial bile salt hydrolases (BSH) hydrolyze conjugated bile acids, and 7α-dehydroxylation converts chenodeoxycholic acid (CDCA) to lithocholic acid (LCA) [11]. Secondary bile acids induce oxidative DNA damage, inflammation, and NF-κB pathway activation through TLR4 signaling [29]. Recent research identifies microbial bile acid metabolites such as isoallo-LCA and 3-oxo-LCA that modulate host immunity and can inhibit CRC progression, demonstrating the complexity of bile acid-microbiome interactions [3].

**Microbiota-derived indoles**, produced from tryptophan metabolism, generally exert beneficial effects on CRC. A decreased indole-to-tryptophan ratio is observed in CRC patient feces, and indoleacrylate reduces pro-inflammatory cytokines while enhancing barrier function [13]. *Lactobacillus plantarum*-derived indole-3-lactic acid (ILA) ameliorated colorectal tumorigenesis via enhancement of CD8+ T cell function through epigenetic mechanisms [30].

The dysbiosis-carcinogenesis axis thus represents a functional shift where microbial community metabolism transitions from producing protective metabolites (butyrate, indoles) to generating genotoxic and pro-inflammatory compounds (colibactin, secondary bile acids, H2O2), creating a tumor-permissive microenvironment [5].

### Host-Microbe-Environment Interactions

Colorectal carcinogenesis emerges from a triad of host genetics, microbial composition, and environmental factors that collectively drive disease progression. This interplay creates a complex network where dietary components, environmental toxins, and host genetic susceptibility converge through microbial metabolism to influence CRC risk [8].

**Host Genetics**

Sporadic CRC is caused by the adenoma-carcinoma sequence involving mutations in genes such as *APC*, *KRAS*, and *TP53*. Inflammation via DNA damage in intestinal epithelial cells can cause mutations in CRC-associated genes through production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) or reactive nitrogen species (RNS) without extrinsic factors, initiating tumor development [17]. Host genetic polymorphisms in immune response genes, bile acid receptors (FXR, TGR5), and xenobiotic metabolism enzymes (PXR, CAR) modulate individual susceptibility to microbiota-driven carcinogenesis [11].

**Microbial Composition**

The gut microbiota composition strongly affects CRC risk through multiple pathways. Patients with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), particularly Crohn's disease, exhibit higher prevalence of adherent-invasive *E. coli* similar to CRC patients, while *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* appears underrepresented [9]. This compositional shift reduces butyrate production and compromises barrier integrity, creating conditions favorable for carcinogenesis.

Microbial composition changes with disease state and treatment. Animal models focusing on irinotecan administration documented changes in microbiota composition including increased intestinal Enterobacteriaceae spp. and *Clostridium* cluster XL, accompanied by increased pro-inflammatory cytokines [14]. The mucosa-associated bacteria in subjects with CRC show distinct compositional changes compared to healthy controls, with reduced adhesion sites affecting microbial colonization patterns [14][9].

**Environmental Factors: Diet and Toxins**

Diet represents the most modifiable environmental factor influencing CRC risk through microbial metabolism. Peto estimated that over 90% of gastric and colonic cancers could be attributed to diet, with bacterial conversion of bile acids in the colon having significant impact on tumorigenic activity [21]. The World Health Organization classified red meat as "probably carcinogenic" and processed meat as "carcinogenic" to humans, with mechanisms including toxic metabolite production, lipoperoxidation, N-nitrosation, DNA adduct formation, and colonic inflammation [16][23].

The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classified heterocyclic amines (HCA) and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) formed during high-temperature meat cooking as carcinogens [23]. Red and processed meat promote colon carcinogenesis via different mechanisms, with many formed products being genotoxic and capable of causing DNA damage [23].

**Dietary Recommendations with Quantified Targets**

Evidence supports specific dietary targets linked to microbial outcomes. Consuming at least **50 grams of fiber per day** may prevent CRC and other Westernized diseases through high-fiber diet effects on microbiota composition and SCFA production [37]. Dietary fiber protects against CRC by increasing bacterial butyrate levels in the colon, which act as HDAC inhibitors [1].

Diet-based approaches leveraging **high-fiber, polyphenol-rich, and omega-3-enriched regimens** have emerged as clinically viable interventions through dietary modulation of microbiota [31]. Prebiotics such as inulin and GOS selectively stimulate beneficial gut microbes including *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, promoting butyrate production [19].

Comparative studies of Alaskan Native individuals (high meat/fat diet) versus rural African individuals (high fiber diet) revealed completely different colonic microbiota and microbial metabolite profiles, demonstrating diet's profound effect on microbial ecology [37]. Westernized diets, associated with obesity and low fiber intake, promote dysbiosis characterized by reduced microbial diversity and altered metabolic output [37][9].

**Environmental Pollutants**

Environmental pollutants, including air and water contaminants, yield reactive species that alter DNA, causing mutations leading to tumor development. These environmental exposures, when combined with genetic susceptibility, create a complex interplay of factors driving CRC pathogenesis [8]. Red and processed meat can be contaminated by environmental pollutants, adding another layer of carcinogenic risk [23].

**Integrated Triad Model**

The host-microbe-environment triad operates through interconnected pathways. Diet affects CRC risk through its effects on colonic microbial metabolism, with bacterial conversion of dietary components producing metabolites that directly influence epithelial cell proliferation, apoptosis, and immune surveillance [21][5]. The profile of gut microbiota and microbial metabolites is rapidly renewable, varying with diet, making it amenable for therapeutic intervention in CRC progression [5].

Multiple lines of evidence support the notion that gut microbiota contributes to colorectal carcinogenesis, with diet—particularly dietary fiber intake—strongly affecting microbiota composition [9]. Changes in microbiota might represent a mechanistic link between diet and CRC risk, suggesting that dietary recommendations must account for individual microbial composition to optimize preventive efficacy [9].

This triad framework emphasizes that CRC prevention requires integrated approaches addressing all three components: genetic risk stratification, microbiota modulation through diet and therapeutics, and reduction of environmental carcinogen exposure. The rapid renewability of microbial metabolite profiles through dietary intervention offers a practical therapeutic target distinct from fixed genetic risk factors [5].

## Oncogenic Pathobionts and Genotoxin Mechanisms

The transition from gut microbiota dysbiosis to colorectal cancer (CRC) involves specific bacterial taxa that function as oncogenic pathobionts—commensal organisms that acquire pathogenic properties under permissive conditions. Unlike established carcinogens such as *Helicobacter pylori* (classified as a Group 1 carcinogen for gastric cancer), CRC-associated pathobionts represent emerging suspects whose causal roles are being elucidated through molecular mechanism studies rather than epidemiological classification alone [38]. This section details the two best-characterized oncogenic pathobionts (*Fusobacterium nucleatum* and pks⁺ *Escherichia coli*), the genotoxic mechanism of colibactin, and the tumor-promoting activity of microbially transformed secondary bile acids.

### Key Pathogenic Bacteria: Fusobacterium nucleatum and pks+ E. coli

Two bacterial species have emerged as central players in CRC pathogenesis through distinct mechanisms: *Fusobacterium nucleatum*, which promotes tumor growth via immune modulation and cell signaling, and pks⁺ *E. coli*, which harbors genotoxic capacity through the polyketide synthase (pks) genomic island.

**Fusobacterium nucleatum: Adhesion-Mediated Tumor Promotion**

*F. nucleatum* is consistently enriched in CRC tumor tissue compared to adjacent normal mucosa and functions as a pro-inflammatory pathobiont rather than a direct genotoxin. The bacterium expresses two critical adhesins that mediate its oncogenic activity:

- **FadA (Fusobacterium adhesin A)**: Binds to E-cadherin on colonic epithelial cells, triggering β-catenin signaling activation and transcription of oncogenes including *MYC* and *CCND1* [31].
- **Fap2 (Fusobacterium adhesin protein 2)**: Interacts with Gal-GalNAc residues overexpressed on CRC cells and binds to the T-cell immunoreceptor with Ig and ITIM domains (TIGIT), suppressing natural killer (NK) cell and T-cell anti-tumor responses [31].

Through these adhesins, *F. nucleatum* activates NF-κB signaling, leading to pro-inflammatory cytokine release (IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α) that creates a tumor-permissive microenvironment [31]. The bacterium also modulates E-cadherin-mediated cell adhesion, facilitating epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) and metastatic potential. Importantly, *F. nucleatum* is not classified as an established carcinogen but rather as an emerging CRC-associated pathobiont whose tumor-promoting effects depend on co-occurring dysbiotic conditions [16].

**pks⁺ Escherichia coli: The Genotoxic Pathobiont**

Approximately 20% of healthy individuals carry *E. coli* strains containing the pks genomic island, a 54-kilobase gene cluster encoding the colibactin biosynthesis machinery [26]. The pks island contains *clb* genes (*clbA* through *clbS*) that encode nonribosomal peptide synthetases, polyketide synthases, and tailoring enzymes required for colibactin production [29].

Unlike *F. nucleatum*, pks⁺ *E. coli* functions as a direct genotoxin producer. However, expert consensus emphasizes that CRC is not caused by a single bacteria producing one genotoxin; rather, multiple pathobionts likely contribute synergistically to carcinogenesis [38]. The pks⁺ *E. coli* phenotype represents an emerging suspect rather than an established carcinogen, as causal inference requires demonstration of mutational signatures in human tumors—a criterion only recently satisfied through genomic analyses [31].

**Microbial Networking and Co-Colonization**

Pathobionts rarely act in isolation. Co-colonization patterns enable parallel genotoxic and pro-inflammatory insults: colibactin from pks⁺ *E. coli* induces direct DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs), while *Bacteroides fragilis* toxin (BFT) from enterotoxigenic *B. fragilis* (ETBF) disrupts epithelial integrity and drives immune dysregulation [31]. Such microbial networking exemplifies how multiple pathobionts act within a dysbiotic ecosystem to accelerate CRC development through complementary mechanisms.

### Colibactin-Induced DNA Damage and Mutational Signatures

Colibactin represents the first bacterial genotoxin with a defined mutational signature in human CRC, establishing a mechanistic link between specific gut bacteria and somatic mutations driving carcinogenesis.

**Molecular Mechanism: DNA Cross-Linking at AT-Rich Sequences**

Colibactin is a substituted spirobicyclic structure that functions as an alkylating agent, forming interstrand cross-links in duplex DNA [2]. Recent structural studies using classical gel-based sequencing and mass spectrometry demonstrated that colibactin-producing bacteria generate cross-links preferentially at AT-rich sequences in the genome [12]. This sequence specificity explains the locations of mutations detected in CRC genomes and distinguishes colibactin damage from other genotoxic insults.

The cross-linking mechanism proceeds through the following steps:
1. **Activation**: Colibactin is synthesized as a prodrug within the bacterial cell and requires host cell uptake for activation.
2. **DNA Binding**: The activated toxin binds to the minor groove of DNA at AT-rich regions.
3. **Cross-Link Formation**: Covalent bonds form between adenine residues on opposite DNA strands, creating interstrand cross-links.
4. **Replication Block**: DNA polymerases cannot traverse cross-linked regions, leading to replication fork collapse and double-strand breaks [31].

**Mutational Signatures: SBS-pks and ID-pks**

Exposure to colibactin produces two distinct mutational signatures now catalogued in the COSMIC database:

- **SBS-pks (Single Base Substitution signature)**: Characterized by T>A transversions at specific sequence contexts, particularly at AT-rich sites where colibactin cross-links form [31].
- **ID-pks (Insertion-Deletion signature)**: Features small deletions at AT-rich sequences, consistent with error-prone repair of colibactin-induced cross-links [31].

In vitro exposure of human epithelial and fibroblast cells to colibactin-producing bacteria triggers senescence, chromosomal aberrations, and activation of DNA damage response (DDR) pathways, including ATM/ATR signaling [31]. Notably, colibactin exposure fails to elicit robust p53-mediated apoptosis, favoring survival of mutated cells that can progress to malignancy [31].

**Link to Early-Onset Colorectal Cancer**

The specific mutations attributed to colibactin have been detected in colorectal cancers, with particular relevance to early-onset CRC (diagnosed before age 50). Exposure to colibactin was known to generate mutations at AT-rich sequences in the genome, and understanding colibactin's specificity now explains the locations of these mutations [12]. Around 5% of CRC mutations may be attributable to colibactin according to recent research—a significant number when considering the absolute burden of CRC cases [26].

The rise in early-onset CRC cases (doubled in people under 55 over the past two decades) may be partially explained by colibactin exposure, though multiple factors including diet, antibiotic use, and birth mode likely contribute to pathobiont colonization patterns [38].

**DNA Damage Response and Cell Cycle Arrest**

Colibactin-induced DNA damage activates the ATM/ATR checkpoint kinases, leading to phosphorylation of downstream targets including p53 and Chk1/Chk2 [31]. Increased expression of p53 mediates upregulation of p21, which works in tandem to arrest the cell cycle in G1 phase [8]. However, when p53 function is compromised (as occurs in advanced CRC via *TP53* mutation), cells with colibactin-induced mutations evade apoptosis and accumulate additional oncogenic alterations [8].

### Secondary Bile Acids as Tumor Promoters

Secondary bile acids represent a distinct class of microbial metabolites that promote CRC through tumor promotion rather than direct mutagenesis. Their production is tightly linked to dietary patterns, particularly high-fat diets, establishing a mechanistic pathway from diet to microbial metabolism to carcinogenesis.

**Microbial Biotransformation: Primary to Secondary Bile Acids**

Primary bile acids (cholic acid [CA] and chenodeoxycholic acid [CDCA]) are synthesized in the liver from cholesterol, conjugated to glycine or taurine, and secreted into the intestine to facilitate fat absorption. Approximately 95% of bile acids are reabsorbed in the ileum, but the 5-10% that escape reabsorption serve as substrates for microbial metabolism in the colon [29].

The critical biotransformation step is **7α-dehydroxylation**, performed by a limited subset of gut bacteria including specific *Clostridium* species (e.g., *C. scindens*, *C. hylemonae*) [13][11]. This pathway converts:
- **CA → Deoxycholic acid (DCA)**
- **CDCA → Lithocholic acid (LCA)**

The 7α-dehydroxylation pathway involves multiple enzymatic steps encoded by the *bai* operon (*baiA* through *baiN*), with BaiE serving as the key 7α-dehydroxylase enzyme [11]. Desulfation of bile acids can also occur via sulfatases produced by *Clostridium*, *Peptococcus*, and *Fusobacterium* species [13].

**High-Fat Diet and Secondary Bile Acid Exposure**

A high-fat diet upregulates the production of primary bile acids in the liver, increasing the substrate available for microbial transformation in the colon [23]. Epidemiologic studies show that subjects who consume a high-fat diet produce elevated levels of fecal secondary bile acids, mainly DCA and LCA, as do CRC patients [29]. A high physiological level of DCA, as seen with high-fat diet (HFD) consumption, has been reported as a risk factor for colon cancer [7].

Bacterial conversion of bile acids in the colon has a significant impact on their tumorigenic activity, substantiating the hypothesis that diet affects CRC risk through its effects on colonic microbial metabolism [21].

**Tumor Promotion Mechanisms: Oxidative Stress and DNA Damage**

Unlike colibactin, secondary bile acids are not directly mutagenic but function as tumor promoters through multiple mechanisms:

- **Oxidative Stress Induction**: DCA and LCA induce oxidative stress and reactive oxygen species (ROS) production in colonic epithelial cells, leading to DNA double-strand breaks [31].
- **Mutational Signatures**: Secondary bile acids generate mutational signatures resembling SBS17a/b, establishing a mechanistic link between high-fat diets, microbial metabolism, and genomic instability [31].
- **PKC-α Activation**: Secondary bile acids act as tumor promoters in animal studies, with protein kinase C-alpha (PKC-α) identified as their molecular target [20].
- **NF-κB Pathway Activation**: Secondary bile acids exert detrimental effects on colonic epithelium through inflammation and activation of the NF-κB pathway [29].
- **Wnt/β-Catenin Modulation**: Secondary bile acids influence the Wnt signaling pathway, which is involved in renewal of the intestinal mucosa and is frequently dysregulated in CRC [29][17].

**Secondary Bile Acids and the Tumor Microenvironment**

Alterations in the chemistry of secondary bile acids have been linked to several diseases, including cirrhosis, inflammatory bowel disease, and cancer [11]. Secondary bile acids interact with the tumor microenvironment through bile acid receptors including FXR (farnesoid X receptor) and TGR5 (G protein-coupled bile acid receptor), modulating inflammation, cell proliferation, and apoptosis [29].

Activation of FXR signaling reduced the growth of CRC cell lines and suppressed proliferation of intestinal stem cells in mouse organoids, suggesting that bile acid receptor modulation may offer therapeutic opportunities [3]. However, the net effect of secondary bile acids in CRC depends on the balance between tumor-promoting (DCA, LCA) and potentially protective (UDCA) bile acid species [20].

**Dietary Implications**

The World Cancer Research Fund concluded that evidence for red and processed meat increasing CRC risk was convincing, partly through bile acid-mediated mechanisms [21]. Red and processed meat could promote colon carcinogenesis via toxic metabolite production, lipoperoxidation, N-nitrosation, DNA adduct formation, and colonic inflammation [23]. Dietary interventions that reduce secondary bile acid exposure—such as increased fiber intake (which binds bile acids and promotes excretion) and reduced saturated fat consumption—represent actionable strategies for CRC prevention through microbiome modulation.

## Protective Microbial Mechanisms and Beneficial Metabolites

The gut microbiota exerts profound protective effects against colorectal cancer (CRC) through multiple interconnected mechanisms involving metabolite production, barrier maintenance, and immune regulation. Understanding these protective pathways is essential for developing evidence-based dietary and microbial interventions. This section examines the molecular mechanisms by which beneficial microbes and their metabolites confer protection, while acknowledging the context-dependent nature of certain microbial products that may exhibit dual effects depending on concentration, location, or host factors.

### Short-Chain Fatty Acids (SCFAs) and Butyrate-Mediated Protection

Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), principally acetate (C2), propionate (C3), and butyrate (C4), represent the most extensively studied class of protective microbial metabolites in CRC pathogenesis [5]. These fatty acids are produced through bacterial anaerobic fermentation of nondigestible dietary fibers, with butyrate serving as the primary energy substrate for colonic epithelial cells [24]. The protective mechanisms of butyrate operate through multiple molecular pathways that collectively maintain colonic homeostasis and suppress malignant transformation.

Butyrate functions as a potent histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor, representing one of its most well-characterized anticancer mechanisms [1]. By inhibiting HDAC activity, butyrate induces hyperacetylation of histones, leading to chromatin remodeling and altered gene expression profiles that favor cellular differentiation over proliferation. This epigenetic modulation activates tumor suppressor genes while silencing oncogenic pathways, effectively restoring normal phenotypic characteristics in transformed cells [29]. The HDAC inhibitory effects of butyrate are selective for colonic epithelium, where it is actively taken up via monocarboxylate transporter 1 (MCT1) and other specialized transporters [29].

In addition to epigenetic regulation, butyrate serves as the preferred metabolic fuel for colonocytes, providing approximately 70% of their energy requirements under normal physiological conditions [29]. This metabolic support is critical for maintaining energy homeostasis in the colonic epithelium and sustaining the high turnover rate characteristic of intestinal tissue. However, a paradoxical effect emerges in CRC cells, which often exhibit the Warburg effect (aerobic glycolysis) and consequently fail to efficiently metabolize butyrate. In these transformed cells, accumulated butyrate triggers apoptosis through caspase-3 activation and mitochondrial pathways, demonstrating dose-dependent pro-apoptotic effects that are independent from the Warburg effect [13][20].

Butyrate-mediated protection extends to the regulation of cell cycle progression and proliferation. In CRC cell lines, butyrate enhances cellular differentiation while simultaneously reducing proliferation rates, effectively counteracting the uncontrolled growth characteristic of malignant transformation [10]. The pro-apoptotic effect of butyrate on hepatic epithelial cells depends on both free fatty acid receptor 3 (FFAR3) activation and HDAC inhibition, illustrating the multi-receptor nature of butyrate signaling [13]. Furthermore, butyrate and propionate induce apoptosis in a dose-dependent manner, with research ongoing to determine whether sustained low-level exposure or intermittent high-level exposure proves more effective in reducing cancer risk [6].

The production of butyrate is directly linked to dietary fiber intake, with evidence suggesting that consuming at least 50 grams of fiber per day may prevent CRC and other Westernized diseases attributed to microbiota-diet interactions [37]. Most butyrate-producing bacteria belong to the Bacillota phylum, including genera such as Faecalibacterium, Roseburia, and Eubacterium [8]. The onset and progression of CRC are intricately linked to the elevated presence of pathogenic or pro-inflammatory species, particularly from the Proteobacteria and Fusobacteria phyla, which often coincide with reduced abundance of butyrate producers [8].

Several clinical trials are underway to evaluate the efficacy of butyrate compounds in various formulations, including targeted delivery systems designed to release butyrate directly in the colon [6]. Market segmentation for butyrate-based therapies includes preventive solutions for high-risk populations, though the potential for butyrate to interact with other dietary components and medications requires further investigation to ensure optimal therapeutic outcomes [6].

### Barrier Integrity and Immune Modulation

The intestinal epithelial barrier represents a critical defense line against CRC development, and commensal microorganisms play essential roles in maintaining its structural and functional integrity. Probiotic strains from genera including Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, and Saccharomyces have demonstrated potential in cancer prevention and therapy by modulating immune responses, reducing inflammation, and enhancing gut barrier integrity [18]. These protective effects operate through multiple mechanisms that preserve epithelial tight junctions and regulate anti-tumor immune surveillance.

SCFAs strengthen gut barrier function through direct effects on epithelial cells and indirect immunomodulatory pathways [18]. Butyrate, in particular, promotes the expression of tight junction proteins including occludin and claudins, thereby reducing paracellular permeability and preventing the translocation of luminal antigens and bacterial products into the underlying lamina propria [18]. Postbiotics derived from Bacillus and Lactobacillus spp. were found to enhance gut barrier integrity through similar mechanisms, demonstrating that microbial metabolites alone can confer protective effects independent of live bacterial colonization [30].

The maintenance of barrier integrity is critically important because disruption allows bacterial products such as lipopolysaccharide (LPS) to leak through the gut barrier, stimulating Toll-like receptors (TLRs), notably TLR4, which then results in the generation of pro-inflammatory cytokines [8]. LPS has been shown to induce an inflammatory response in macrophage cell lines and human colon cancer cells in vitro, creating a microenvironment conducive to tumor promotion [8]. Several studies have shown that LPS leaks through the gut barrier in stress-induced rodents, highlighting the vulnerability of barrier function under adverse conditions [8].

SCFAs exert potent anti-inflammatory effects by reducing the LPS- and cytokine-stimulated production of pro-inflammatory mediators including TNF-α, IL-6, IFN-γ, and nitric oxide (NO), while simultaneously increasing the release of the anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10 [29]. Consistent with these anti-inflammatory effects, either dietary consumption of soluble fiber pectin as an SCFA-delivery system to the colon or an orally administered butyrate reduces neutrophil recruitment and inflammation in mouse models [29]. SCFAs also directly regulate the transcription of cytokine genes through epigenetic mechanisms, providing sustained modulation of the inflammatory milieu [29].

Immune modulation by beneficial microbes extends to the regulation of adaptive immunity, particularly CD8+ T cell function, which is critical for anti-tumor surveillance. Bile acids modified by the intestinal microbiota can promote CRC growth by suppressing CD8+ T cell effector functions, illustrating how microbial metabolism directly influences immune-mediated tumor control [35]. Conversely, Lactobacillus plantarum-derived indole-3-lactic acid (ILA) ameliorated colorectal tumorigenesis via enhancement of CD8+ T cells' function through epigenetic mechanisms, demonstrating that specific microbial metabolites can boost anti-tumor immunity [30].

An essential immunomodulatory route involves heightened immunoglobulin A (IgA) production, which prevents bacterial translocation across the epithelial barrier [8]. Although no systemic antibody response is induced, bacteria opsonized with IgA cannot cross the epithelial barrier, providing a critical containment mechanism [39]. In another study, fatty acids extracted from probiotic fermented milk suppressed the JNK-MAPK pathway by inhibiting the phosphorylation of JNK, p38, and p44 in a C57BL/6 mice model of DSS-induced colitis, illustrating direct signaling pathway modulation by microbial products [8].

Probiotics display antioxidant activity through various mechanisms, including free radical scavenging and synthesis of antioxidant metabolites, which eliminate cumulative production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in cells with redox imbalance [30]. This antioxidant capacity is particularly relevant in CRC, where oxidative stress contributes to DNA damage and malignant transformation. Cell-free supernatants (CFSs) obtained from Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, Bacillus, Enterococcus, Pediococcus, and Saccharomyces are implicated in apoptosis activation in cancer cells, demonstrating that secreted factors alone can mediate anti-cancer effects [30].

### Metabolite Duality: Context-Dependent Effects

While many microbial metabolites confer protective effects against CRC, certain compounds exhibit context-dependent duality, producing opposing outcomes depending on concentration, location, host genetics, or disease state. This complexity underscores the importance of understanding mechanistic details rather than assuming uniform beneficial or detrimental effects for any given metabolite class.

Secondary bile acids exemplify metabolite duality most clearly. Bacterial conversion of bile acids in the colon has a significant impact on their tumorigenic activity, substantiating the hypothesis that diet affects CRC risk through its effects on colonic microbial metabolism [21]. Secondary bile acids such as deoxycholic acid (DCA) and lithocholic acid (LCA) induce oxidative stress, DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs), and mutational signatures resembling SBS17a/b, establishing a mechanistic link between high-fat diets, microbial metabolism, and genomic instability in colorectal carcinogenesis [31]. Unconjugated bile acids can be converted by 7α-dehydroxylation, which generates DCA and LCA from cholic acid (CA) and chenodeoxycholic acid (CDCA), respectively [13].

A high physiological level of secondary bile acids, particularly DCA, as seen with high-fat diet (HFD) consumption, has been reported to be a risk factor for colon cancer [7]. Epidemiologic studies have shown that subjects who consume a high-fat diet produce elevated levels of fecal secondary bile acids, mainly DCA and LCA, as do colon cancer patients [29]. The World Cancer Research Fund concluded that evidence for red and processed meat increasing and fiber reducing CRC risk was convincing, with bacterial conversion of bile acids representing one mechanism linking diet to CRC [21].

However, not all secondary bile acid metabolites are detrimental. The microbial bile acid metabolite 3-oxo-LCA inhibits colorectal cancer progression, demonstrating that specific modifications can convert potentially harmful bile acids into protective compounds [3]. Activation of farnesoid X receptor (FXR) signaling reduced the growth of colorectal cancer cell lines and suppressed the proliferation of intestinal stem cells in both mouse organoids and patient-derived colorectal cancer organoids [3]. In the APCMin/+ genetically engineered mouse model, 3-oxo-LCA reduced tumor burden, illustrating the therapeutic potential of specific bile acid metabolites [3].

Indole metabolites derived from tryptophan similarly exhibit context-dependent effects, though generally leaning toward protection. The effect of indoles on the host, immune system, and CRC is generally considered beneficial [13]. One study found that a decrease in the indole to tryptophan ratio was observed in the feces of patients with CRC, suggesting that reduced indole production may characterize the dysbiotic state associated with malignancy [13]. Indole-3-propionic acid (IPA) exhibits anti-inflammatory effects, contributing to the protective milieu in the colon [13]. In mice, both indoleacrylate and other indole derivatives demonstrated protective effects against colitis and tumorigenesis through aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) activation [13].

Nevertheless, several metabolomics studies implied inconsistent findings regarding bile acid metabolites and CRC risk. In a CRC cohort, the serum metabolomics analysis only detected serum glycochenodeoxycholate, a bile acid metabolite, that was positively associated with colorectal cancer among women, while no overall association was observed across the entire cohort [5]. This context-dependent effect underscores the complexity of host-microbe metabolic interactions in cancer biology [31].

The mechanistic interplay between microbiota and CRC converges on canonical oncogenic pathways, including Wnt/β-catenin, NF-κB, STAT3, and MAPK signaling cascades [31]. This context-dependent effect underscores the complexity of host-microbe metabolic interactions in cancer biology, where the same metabolite may activate different pathways depending on cellular context and receptor expression profiles [31]. Mechanistically, the interplay between microbiota and CRC converges on canonical oncogenic pathways, including Wnt/β-catenin, NF-κB, STAT3, and MAPK signaling cascades, with metabolites serving as key modulators of these pathways [31].

Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) produced by sulfidogenic bacteria represents another metabolite with dual effects. While low concentrations may serve signaling functions, elevated H₂S induces DNA damage and inhibits butyrate oxidation in colonocytes, contributing to the metabolic reprogramming characteristic of CRC [31]. The desulfation of bile acids can occur via sulfatases produced by Clostridium, Peptococcus, Fusobacterium, and other genera, linking sulfur metabolism to bile acid transformation [13].

This metabolite duality has important implications for therapeutic interventions. Probiotics are still safe in the majority of situations, notwithstanding clinical restrictions, and future investigation into their potential as an adjuvant therapy measure against CRC is strongly encouraged by their positive health effects when taken as dietary supplements [17]. However, the context-dependent nature of microbial metabolites necessitates personalized approaches that account for individual microbiota composition, dietary patterns, and host genetic factors. As the field progresses, the ultimate goal is to harness the power of beneficial metabolites in developing comprehensive strategies for colon cancer prevention, encompassing not only individual-level interventions but also population-wide approaches to promote gut health and reduce cancer risk [6].

## Dietary Drivers of Microbiota Composition and CRC Risk

Dietary patterns represent one of the most modifiable risk factors for colorectal cancer (CRC), exerting profound effects through their influence on gut microbiota composition and metabolic output. The World Cancer Research Fund has concluded that evidence for red and processed meat increasing CRC risk, and fiber reducing it, was convincing, substantiating the hypothesis that diet affects CRC risk through its effects on colonic microbial metabolism [21]. This section examines three critical dietary domains—Western diet patterns characterized by high fat and low fiber, red and processed meat consumption, and fermented foods with dietary fiber intake—elucidating the mechanistic pathways by which these dietary factors shape the microbiota-CRC axis.

### Western Diet Patterns: High Fat and Low Fiber

The Westernized diet, characterized by elevated saturated fat intake and diminished dietary fiber consumption, establishes a pro-carcinogenic colonic environment through dual mechanisms: increased production of tumorigenic secondary bile acids and decreased generation of protective short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs). A high-fat diet upregulates the production of primary bile acids in the liver, which transit into the colon where gut microbiota transform them into secondary bile acids, primarily deoxycholic acid (DCA) and lithocholic acid (LCA) [23]. Epidemiologic studies have demonstrated that subjects who consume a high-fat diet produce elevated levels of fecal secondary bile acids, mirroring the profiles observed in colon cancer patients [29]. A high physiological level of DCA, as seen with high-fat diet consumption, has been reported as a risk factor for colon cancer [7].

The carcinogenic potential of secondary bile acids operates through well-defined molecular mechanisms. Secondary bile acids such as DCA and LCA induce oxidative stress, DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs), and mutational signatures resembling SBS17a/b, establishing a mechanistic link between high-fat diets, microbial metabolism, and genomic instability in colorectal carcinogenesis [31]. These bile acids disrupt the balance between cell proliferation, differentiation, and apoptosis in the colonic crypt, where stem cells are located and where colorectal cancer is thought to initiate through the formation of aberrant crypt foci [9]. The intestinal mucosa is constantly renewed, and disruption of this renewal process underlies cancer development, with Wnt signaling involved in the renewal of the intestinal mucosa and frequently dysregulated in CRC [29].

Concurrently, low fiber intake deprives commensal bacteria of fermentable substrates, resulting in diminished SCFA production, particularly butyrate. Dietary fiber protects against colorectal carcinoma by increasing bacterial butyrate levels in the colon, which act as a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor [1]. This molecular mechanism is critical: butyrate's HDAC inhibitory activity promotes histone hyperacetylation, leading to the expression of genes that induce cell cycle arrest, differentiation, and apoptosis in transformed colonic epithelial cells. Consistent with these anti-inflammatory effects, dietary consumption of soluble fiber pectin as an SCFA-delivery system to the colon or orally administered butyrate reduces neutrophil recruitment and inflammation in mouse models [29].

The quantitative relationship between fiber intake and CRC prevention has been increasingly clarified. There is increasing evidence that consuming at least 50 grams of fiber per day may prevent Westernized diseases including colon cancer [37]. High-fiber diets have the potential to resolve major public health concerns by restoring microbiota composition and metabolic function. The link between diet and CRC may be due to an imbalance of the intestinal microflora, with diet, particularly dietary fiber intake, strongly affecting microbiota composition [9][10]. Thus, the Western diet establishes dysbiosis through both the promotion of pathogenic microbial metabolites (secondary bile acids) and the suppression of protective metabolites (SCFAs), creating a permissive environment for carcinogenesis.

### Red and Processed Meat Consumption

The consumption of red and processed meat represents a well-established dietary risk factor for CRC, with the World Health Organization classifying red meat as "probably carcinogenic" and processed meat as "carcinogenic" to humans [16]. The carcinogenic mechanisms associated with meat consumption operate through multiple microbial metabolic pathways, including the production of hydrogen sulfide, N-nitroso compounds, and the activity of genotoxin-producing bacteria such as colibactin-producing *Escherichia coli*.

Microbial metabolism of meat-derived substrates generates genotoxic substances that directly damage colonic epithelial DNA. CRC pathogenesis involves the production of genotoxic substances including aromatic amino acids and hydrogen sulfide [23]. Sulfate-reducing bacteria reside in the gut as part of the normal microbiota, and hydrogen sulfide has been shown to induce neoplasia in experimental models [9]. Hydrogen sulfide impairs butyrate oxidation in colonocytes, leading to energy depletion and compensatory hyperproliferation, while also causing direct DNA damage through the formation of DNA adducts. Many of the products formed from meat metabolism are genotoxic and can cause DNA damage and initiate carcinogenesis of colorectal cancer [23].

A distinct and emerging mechanism involves colibactin, a genotoxin produced by pks⁺ *E. coli* strains. Colibactin-producing bacteria are associated with bowel cancer, with around 20% of people thought to carry strains containing the genomic island responsible for colibactin production [26]. Of bowel cancer cases, around 5% may be attributable to colibactin according to recent research, a significant number albeit small when compared to the estimated 50% which could be prevented by lifestyle changes including improved diet [26]. Mechanistically, colibactin from pks⁺ *E. coli* induces direct DNA double-strand breaks, while exposure to colibactin generates mutations at AT-rich sequences in the genome [31][12]. Understanding colibactin's specificity now explains the locations of these mutations, which have been detected in colorectal cancers.

It is critical to distinguish between established carcinogens and emerging CRC-associated pathobionts to avoid overstatement of risk. While *Helicobacter pylori* is an established carcinogen for gastric cancer, colibactin-producing *E. coli* represents an emerging pathobiont with growing but still evolving evidence for its role in CRC. The theory that bacterial infection directly triggers colon cancer remains poorly understood, but a concept is emerging wherein certain pro-oncogenic bacteria have the potential to promote neoplastic transformation of epithelial cells of the colon [29]. These co-colonizations enable parallel genotoxic and pro-inflammatory insults, with colibactin inducing direct DNA DSBs while other microbial factors such as BFT from enterotoxigenic *Bacteroides fragilis* disrupt epithelial integrity and drive immune dysregulation [31].

Some studies have found no significant relationship between the consumption of meat and processed meat products and CRC, highlighting the importance of applying safe cooking methods and doneness levels to meat so that it does not produce harmful compounds [16]. However, the weight of evidence from the World Cancer Research Fund supports the convincing link between meat consumption and increased CRC risk [21]. The bacterial conversion of bile acids in the colon has a significant impact on their tumorigenic activity, and meat consumption further exacerbates this by providing substrates for microbial metabolism that generate genotoxic compounds [21].

### Fermented Foods and Dietary Fiber Intake

Fermented foods and dietary fiber intake represent protective dietary factors that modulate microbiota composition toward a state associated with reduced CRC risk. These dietary components operate through complementary mechanisms: fiber provides fermentable substrates for beneficial bacteria to produce SCFAs, while fermented foods introduce live microorganisms and bioactive metabolites that enhance microbial diversity and reduce inflammation.

To qualify as a prebiotic, a substance must resist digestion, be fermented by gut bacteria, and selectively promote beneficial strains like *Bifidobacterium* and *Lactobacillus* [18]. This targeted modulation of gut microbiota distinguishes prebiotics from general dietary fibers and supports the rationale for specific fiber recommendations. In colorectal cancer, probiotics such as *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium* strains enhance SCFA production and strengthen gut barrier function [18]. SCFAs, including butyrate, acetate, and propionate, represent key categories of postbiotics that encompass several beneficial metabolites produced by microbial fermentation [18].

Dietary interventions that modulate the microbiota can influence these pathways, with butyrate's HDAC inhibitory activity representing one well-characterized mechanism. Additionally, probiotics have adjunctive potential in cancer therapy by reducing treatment side effects and improving therapeutic efficacy through immunomodulation and metabolic regulation [18]. The International Scientific Association for Probiotics emphasizes a broad scope beyond metabolites alone, recognizing that structural microbial components, functional proteins, and various other metabolites contribute to the protective effects of fermented foods and probiotic supplementation [18].

In summary, dietary drivers of microbiota composition exert profound effects on CRC risk through well-defined mechanistic pathways. Western diet patterns promote carcinogenesis via secondary bile acid production and SCFA depletion, red and processed meat consumption introduces genotoxic microbial metabolites including hydrogen sulfide and colibactin, while fermented foods and dietary fiber intake restore protective microbial functions through SCFA production, immunomodulation, and direct anti-proliferative effects. Quantitative dietary targets, such as 50 grams of fiber per day, combined with specific probiotic strains documented in CRC models, provide actionable evidence-based recommendations for CRC prevention through microbiota modulation.

## Probiotic and Prebiotic Interventions for CRC Management

The modulation of gut microbiota through probiotic and prebiotic interventions represents a promising therapeutic avenue for colorectal cancer (CRC) management. Evidence from preclinical and clinical studies indicates that specific microbial strains can exert anti-carcinogenic effects through multiple mechanisms, including immune modulation, metabolic regulation, and direct tumor cell suppression. However, the efficacy of these interventions remains highly strain-dependent, necessitating careful selection of documented strains with verified anti-CRC activity. This section synthesizes current evidence on strain-specific efficacy, mechanistic pathways, and emerging therapeutic modalities including synbiotics and postbiotics.

### Strain-Specific Efficacy: Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium

The anti-CRC potential of probiotics is fundamentally strain-specific, with documented efficacy varying significantly even within the same genus. Two genera dominate the evidence base: *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium*, each containing multiple strains with distinct mechanistic profiles and clinical outcomes.

**Strain-Dependency and Clinical Translation**

The strain-specific nature of probiotic efficacy is critical for clinical application. A phase III randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled multicenter trial evaluated a probiotic mixture containing *Bifidobacterium* BB-12® and *Lactobacillus rhamnosus* LGG® for prophylaxis of irinotecan-induced diarrhea in metastatic CRC patients [14]. This trial demonstrated that specific strain combinations can reduce treatment-related toxicity, though results across clinical trials remain inconsistent [14]. The inconsistency underscores the importance of strain selection rather than genus-level generalization.

Preclinical evidence reveals substantial mechanistic variation between strains. For instance, *L. casei*-derived ferrichrome activates the JNK pathway and upregulates DDIT3 to induce apoptosis, whereas *L. acidophilus* exopolysaccharides operate through p53 and TGF upregulation [8][17]. These distinct pathways suggest that strain combinations may offer complementary mechanisms rather than redundant effects.

*Bifidobacterium* BB-12 has demonstrated multiple anti-CRC activities, including inhibition of HT-29 colon cancer cell proliferation and induction of apoptosis through caspase 3 activation [10][17]. Additionally, BB-12 increases short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production, which contributes to gut barrier integrity and anti-inflammatory effects [18].

**Evidence Limitations**

Despite promising preclinical data, clinical evidence for direct anti-tumor efficacy remains limited. Most human trials focus on symptom management (e.g., chemotherapy-induced diarrhea) rather than tumor regression or survival outcomes [14][4]. Systematic reviews indicate that while probiotics improve numerous factors in CRC patients, the evidence for direct carcinogenesis prevention requires further validation through large-scale, long-term clinical trials [28][4].

### Mechanisms of Probiotic Action in CRC

Probiotics exert anti-CRC effects through multiple interconnected mechanisms spanning competitive exclusion, toxin inactivation, immune modulation, and direct regulation of tumor cell apoptosis. Understanding these mechanisms is essential for rational strain selection and therapeutic optimization.

**Competitive Exclusion and Barrier Enhancement**

Probiotic bacteria compete with pathogenic organisms for adhesion sites and nutrients within the intestinal lumen, reducing colonization by CRC-associated pathobionts. This competitive exclusion is complemented by enhancement of gut barrier integrity through SCFA production, particularly butyrate, which strengthens tight junctions and reduces intestinal permeability [18]. Enhanced barrier function limits translocation of pro-inflammatory bacterial components such as lipopolysaccharide (LPS), thereby reducing chronic inflammation that drives carcinogenesis.

Strains including *Lactobacillus*, *Bifidobacterium*, and *Saccharomyces* have demonstrated potential in enhancing gut barrier integrity while modulating immune responses and reducing inflammation [18]. The barrier-protective effects are particularly relevant given that disruption of intestinal epithelial integrity is a recognized contributor to CRC development through sustained inflammatory signaling.

**Toxin Inactivation and Carcinogen Detoxification**

Probiotics contribute to cancer prevention by degrading or deactivating carcinogenic substances, enabling neutralization of their adverse effects on the gut microbiota [30]. Specific enzymes produced by probiotic bacteria, including azoreductase and nitroreductase, metabolize xenobiotic compounds and reduce the synthesis of carcinogenic substances [30]. This detoxification capacity is particularly important given that bacterial conversion of bile acids in the colon significantly impacts their tumorigenic activity, substantiating the hypothesis that diet affects CRC risk through effects on colonic microbial metabolism [21].

The distinction between established carcinogens and emerging CRC-associated pathobionts is critical. While *H. pylori* represents a well-established carcinogen, emerging pathogens such as pks+ *E. coli* producing colibactin are being identified as contributors to CRC pathogenesis [31][26]. Probiotic interventions may mitigate risk from both categories through direct toxin inactivation and competitive displacement.

**Immune Enhancement and Inflammation Modulation**

Immune modulation represents a central mechanism of probiotic action in CRC. Probiotics influence host immune responses through multiple pathways, including regulation of T-regulatory cells (Tregs) that suppress inflammation [2]. In the intestinal mucosa, commensal organisms regulate host immune responses, which may be an important determinant in carcinogenesis given that inflammation is a central driver of chronic disease development including cancer [2].

Specific strains demonstrate targeted immunomodulatory effects. *Lactobacillus plantarum*-derived indole-3-lactic acid (ILA) ameliorated colorectal tumorigenesis via enhancement of CD8+ T cells' function through epigenetic mechanisms [30]. This finding illustrates how probiotic metabolites can directly enhance anti-tumor immunity. Additionally, probiotic mixtures such as VSL#3 have been reported to suppress COX-2 expression in intestinal epithelial cells, and since COX-2 expression is increased in colorectal tumors and can protect cells from apoptosis, this suppression represents a relevant anti-carcinogenic mechanism [10].

**Apoptosis Regulation and Cell Cycle Control**

Direct regulation of tumor cell apoptosis constitutes a critical anti-CRC mechanism. Multiple probiotic strains and their metabolites activate apoptotic pathways in cancer cells through distinct molecular mechanisms:

- **Caspase Activation**: *Bifidobacterium* BB-12 induces apoptosis via caspase 3 activation in colon cancer cells [17].
- **p53 Pathway**: Exopolysaccharides from *L. acidophilus* upregulate p53 expression, which works in tandem with p21 to arrest the cell cycle in G1 phase and balance BAX/Bcl-2 towards apoptosis [17][8].
- **JNK/DDIT3 Pathway**: Ferrichrome from *L. casei* upregulates DDIT3 and activates JNK, with JNK inhibition reducing ferrichrome-mediated apoptosis, confirming pathway specificity [8].
- **TGF Signaling**: *L. acidophilus* EPS upregulates TGF expression alongside p53, contributing to growth inhibition [17].

Cell-free supernatants (CFS) obtained from *Lactobacillus*, *Bifidobacterium*, *Bacillus*, *Enterococcus*, *Pediococcus*, and *Saccharomyces* are implicated in apoptosis activation in cancer cells [30]. This indicates that secreted metabolites, rather than live bacteria alone, mediate significant anti-tumor effects.

**Antioxidant Activity**

Probiotics display antioxidant activity through various mechanisms, including free radical scavenging and synthesis of antioxidant metabolites [30]. Postbiotics derived from *Bacillus* and *Lactobacillus* spp. eliminate cumulative production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in cells with redox imbalance, reducing oxidative stress that contributes to DNA damage and carcinogenesis [30]. This antioxidant capacity complements other mechanisms by addressing oxidative stress, a recognized contributor to CRC development.

**Signaling Pathway Modulation**

Probiotics influence key oncogenic signaling pathways. The PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway, critical for cell survival and proliferation, is modulated by probiotic interventions [34]. Additionally, Wnt signaling, important for cell-cycle regulation and frequently dysregulated in CRC, represents a potential target for probiotic modulation [9].

### Synbiotics and Postbiotics: Emerging Therapeutic Modalities

Beyond traditional probiotics, synbiotics (combinations of probiotics and prebiotics) and postbiotics (metabolite-based treatments) represent emerging therapeutic modalities with distinct advantages for CRC prevention and therapy.

**Synbiotics: Combination Therapies**

Synbiotics combine probiotic organisms with prebiotic substrates that selectively stimulate growth of beneficial bacteria. Representative applications of probiotics, prebiotics, synbiotics, and postbiotics across different cancer types, along with their mechanisms, evidence level, and status, are summarized in comprehensive reviews [18].

Prebiotic substrates such as fructooligosaccharides (FOS) and galactooligosaccharides (GOS) fuel butyrate production by stimulating growth of butyrate-producing bacteria [18]. Most butyrate-producing bacteria belong to the Bacillota phylum, and their abundance is often reduced in CRC patients [8]. By providing selective substrates, prebiotics enhance the colonization and metabolic activity of co-administered probiotics while simultaneously stimulating endogenous beneficial bacteria.

The synergistic potential of synbiotics addresses a key limitation of probiotic monotherapy: insufficient colonization of exogenous strains. Prebiotics create a favorable metabolic environment that supports probiotic persistence and activity. However, clinical trial evidence for synbiotic efficacy in CRC prevention remains limited compared to preclinical data, with ongoing studies evaluating optimal combinations and dosing regimens [18][4].

**Postbiotics: Metabolite-Based Therapies**

Postbiotics encompass bioactive compounds produced by probiotic bacteria, including cell-free supernatants, exopolysaccharides, and specific metabolites such as butyrate. This modality offers several advantages: standardized dosing, elimination of colonization requirements, and reduced risk of bacteremia in immunocompromised patients.

**Butyrate as HDAC Inhibitor**

Butyrate, a short-chain fatty acid produced by bacterial fermentation of dietary fiber, represents the most extensively studied postbiotic for CRC prevention. Butyrate acts as a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor, providing a clear molecular mechanism for its anti-carcinogenic effects [1][29]. As an HDAC inhibitor, butyrate induces histone hyperacetylation, leading to altered gene expression that promotes cell cycle arrest, differentiation, and apoptosis in transformed cells while supporting energy homeostasis in normal colonocytes [29].

Butyrate is selectively taken up by the colonic epithelium via monocarboxylate transporter 1 and other transporters, providing colonocytes with approximately 70% of their energy [29]. This selective uptake creates a therapeutic window where butyrate supports normal cell function while inducing apoptosis in transformed cells that exhibit the Warburg effect and rely less on butyrate oxidation.

**Clinical Development of Butyrate-Based Interventions**

Several clinical trials are underway to evaluate the efficacy of butyrate compounds in various formulations, including targeted delivery systems designed to release butyrate directly in the colon [6]. The regulatory landscape for butyrate-based interventions is complex and evolving, with oversight from agencies including the FDA [6]. Market segmentation for butyrate-based therapies includes preventive solutions for high-risk populations and adjunctive treatments for established CRC.

**Other Postbiotic Metabolites**

Beyond butyrate, multiple probiotic-derived metabolites demonstrate anti-CRC activity:

- **Exopolysaccharides (EPS)**: EPS from *L. acidophilus* upregulates p53 and TGF, inhibiting colon cancer cell viability up to 80.65% [17].
- **Cell-Free Supernatants (CFS)**: CFS from multiple probiotic genera activate apoptosis in cancer cells, with *Weizmannia* suppressing tumorigenesis in colorectal tumor-bearing mice [30].
- **Ferrichrome**: This *L. casei*-derived compound activates JNK/DDIT3 apoptotic signaling [8].
- **Indole Derivatives**: *L. plantarum*-derived indole-3-lactic acid enhances CD8+ T cell function through epigenetic mechanisms [30].

**Combination with Chemotherapy**

Postbiotics show promise as chemotherapy adjuvants. The combination of *L. plantarum*-derived postbiotics with 5-FU increased sensitivity of 5-FU-resistant CRC cells [30]. Additionally, *B. infantis* ameliorated chemotherapy-induced mucositis in a CRC rat model after 5-FU treatment through decreased inflammatory markers [30]. These findings suggest postbiotics may enhance therapeutic efficacy while reducing treatment toxicity.

**Evidence Status and Future Directions**

Table 1 in comprehensive reviews summarizes applications of probiotics, prebiotics, synbiotics, and postbiotics across cancer types with their mechanisms, evidence levels, and development status [18]. While preclinical evidence is robust, clinical translation requires further investigation. Future investigation into probiotic and postbiotic potential as adjuvant therapy measures against CRC is strongly encouraged given their positive health effects when taken as dietary supplements [17].

The profile of gut microbiota and microbial metabolites is rapidly renewable, varying with diet and making it more amenable for therapeutic intervention in CRC progression [5]. Elucidation of the role of microbial metabolites will provide a new paradigm for cancer prevention and therapy, moving beyond correlation to mechanism-based interventions [5].

**Dietary Recommendations**

Dietary recommendations for CRC prevention should quantify targets where possible and link directly to microbial outcomes. The World Cancer Research Fund concluded that evidence for fiber reducing CRC risk was convincing [21]. Dietary fiber protects against colorectal carcinoma by increasing bacterial butyrate levels in the colon [1]. While specific gram-per-day targets vary by guideline, the mechanistic link between fiber intake, SCFA production, and HDAC inhibition provides a clear rationale for high-fiber dietary patterns in CRC prevention.

In summary, probiotic and prebiotic interventions for CRC management operate through multiple validated mechanisms, with strain-specific efficacy requiring careful selection. Synbiotics and postbiotics represent promising emerging modalities that may overcome limitations of traditional probiotic approaches, though clinical evidence remains less developed than preclinical data.

## Clinical Evidence and Therapeutic Limitations

The translation of microbiome research into clinical practice for colorectal cancer (CRC) represents one of the most promising yet challenging frontiers in oncology. While preclinical models demonstrate compelling mechanistic pathways linking gut microbiota to carcinogenesis, the clinical evidence base remains heterogeneous. This section critically evaluates the current state of human clinical trials examining probiotic interventions in CRC, distinguishes between preventive and adjunctive therapeutic applications, and addresses the safety considerations that constrain widespread implementation.

### Impact on Treatment Side Effects and Quality of Life

Chemotherapy-induced toxicity represents a major barrier to optimal CRC treatment completion and patient quality of life. Among the most debilitating side effects is chemotherapy-induced diarrhea (CID), particularly associated with irinotecan-based regimens, which can lead to dose reductions, treatment delays, and compromised survival outcomes. Clinical trial data examining probiotic interventions for CID management reveal both promising signals and important limitations in the evidence base.

A prospective observational trial suggests that *Lentilactobacillus kefiri* LKF01 (Fefibios®) can ameliorate irinotecan-induced severe diarrhea in cancer patients, though clinical evidence of beneficial probiotic effects in this setting remains limited overall [14]. More robust evidence comes from a phase III randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled multicenter trial conducted across Slovak cancer centers, which evaluated a probiotic mixture containing *Bifidobacterium* BB-12® and *Lactobacillus rhamnosus* LGG® for prophylaxis of irinotecan-induced diarrhea in metastatic CRC patients [14]. The trial objective centered on determining effectiveness through reduction in diarrhea incidence, representing one of the more rigorous clinical investigations in this domain.

However, results from clinical trials aimed at determining probiotic effects on chemotherapy-associated diarrhea remain inconsistent across studies [14]. This inconsistency reflects heterogeneity in probiotic strains, dosing regimens, patient populations, and outcome measures. Some trials report significant reductions in CID severity and duration, while others show no statistically significant benefit compared to placebo controls.

Beyond chemotherapy toxicity, probiotic interventions demonstrate potential for improving postoperative outcomes in CRC patients undergoing surgical resection. Probiotics such as *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium* strains enhance gut barrier function through short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production, which strengthens intestinal integrity and may reduce postoperative infection risk [18]. The mechanism involves SCFA-mediated upregulation of tight junction proteins and stimulation of mucin production by goblet cells, creating a physical barrier against bacterial translocation.

Quality of life (QoL) represents a critical patient-centered outcome in CRC management. Low QoL, depression, and anxiety are associated with lower long-term survival in CRC patients, establishing a clear link between psychosocial factors and clinical outcomes [17]. Probiotics exhibit immunomodulatory effects that may indirectly improve QoL by reducing treatment-related symptoms and inflammation. However, subgroup analysis in psychobiotic research reveals only mild to moderate effects on patients with pre-existing mood symptoms, and evidence supporting the psychobiotic hypothesis remains scarce [8].

The distinction between prevention and adjunct therapy is essential when evaluating probiotic efficacy. As preventive agents, probiotics aim to maintain microbial balance and reduce CRC risk through mechanisms including carcinogen detoxification and anti-inflammatory effects. As adjunct therapies, they support conventional treatment by mitigating side effects and potentially enhancing therapeutic efficacy through immunomodulation and metabolic regulation [18].

### Gaps in Human Clinical Trials and Causality

The translation from mechanistic understanding to clinical application faces substantial hurdles, primarily stemming from fundamental differences between animal models and human physiology, alongside the inherent challenge of establishing causation in microbiome research.

Animal models, particularly the AOM/DSS (azoxymethane/dextran sulfate sodium) and ApcMin/+ mouse models, have been instrumental in elucidating microbiome-CRC relationships. These models demonstrate clear causal links between specific microbial communities and tumor development. However, rodent microbiomes differ substantially from human microbiomes in composition, metabolic capacity, and immune interactions. Findings from xenograft mouse models and genetically engineered mice may not accurately predict human responses to microbiome-targeted interventions [3]. The reliance on animal models creates a translational gap where promising preclinical results fail to replicate in human trials.

The correlation versus causation challenge represents perhaps the most significant limitation in human microbiome-CRC research. Observational studies consistently identify microbial signatures associated with CRC, including enrichment of *Fusobacterium*, *pks+ Escherichia coli*, and secondary bile acid-producing bacteria. However, these associations do not establish whether microbial changes drive carcinogenesis or result from the tumor microenvironment and associated metabolic alterations [31]. Reverse causation remains a persistent confounder: tumors alter the colonic environment through inflammation, bleeding, and metabolic reprogramming, which in turn reshape microbial communities.

Diet-microbiota interactions further complicate causal inference. Diet interacts with gut microbiota to produce metabolites that can be genotoxic and cause DNA damage, initiating CRC carcinogenesis [23]. The World Cancer Research Fund concluded that evidence for red and processed meat increasing and fiber reducing CRC risk was convincing, with bacterial conversion of bile acids in the colon having significant impact on tumorigenic activity [21]. This substantiates the hypothesis that diet affects CRC risk through its effects on colonic microbial metabolism, but disentangling direct dietary effects from microbiome-mediated effects requires carefully controlled intervention studies that remain scarce.

Various mechanisms contributing to genotoxic roles of microbial metabolites have been established in both human and animal studies [23]. However, the increasing evidence that dietary components promote colonic carcinogenesis through dysbiosis highlights the complexity of attributing causality to specific microbial taxa versus their metabolic outputs [23].

Clinical trial design limitations compound these challenges. Many probiotic trials suffer from small sample sizes, short follow-up periods, heterogeneous patient populations, and variable outcome measures. The phase III trial evaluating *Bifidobacterium* BB-12® and *Lactobacillus rhamnosus* LGG® represents an exception in terms of rigor, but even this study faced limitations in generalizability [14]. Studies involving human participants require ethical review and approval, which can constrain experimental designs that would otherwise provide stronger causal evidence [14].

The distinction between established carcinogens and emerging CRC-associated pathobionts requires careful articulation to avoid overstatement of risk. Unlike *Helicobacter pylori*, which is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen with established causal links to gastric cancer, emerging CRC-associated bacteria such as colibactin-producing *E. coli* and enterotoxigenic *Bacteroides fragilis* (ETBF) remain suspected rather than proven carcinogens in humans [31]. As multi-omics approaches refine understanding of toxin-associated mutational signatures in human cancers, these microbial factors may emerge as compelling biomarkers for carcinogenesis and potential targets for cancer-preventive interventions in high-risk populations [31].

Molecular mechanism explanations remain essential for claimed metabolite effects. Butyrate functions as a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor, inducing cell cycle arrest and apoptosis in CRC cells while serving as the primary energy source for colonocytes in normal tissue—a phenomenon termed the "butyrate paradox." Secondary bile acids such as deoxycholic acid (DCA) and lithocholic acid (LCA) promote carcinogenesis through DNA damage, reactive oxygen species generation, and activation of pro-survival signaling pathways including EGFR and β-catenin [13]. In contrast, DCA and LCA seem to induce protective effects during dextran-induced colitis models, highlighting context-dependent effects that complicate therapeutic targeting [13].

Future directions for advancing microbiome-based cancer therapies must prioritize precision microbiome medicine, utilizing cutting-edge profiling technologies to customize therapies according to individual microbiome compositions [18]. Emerging innovations such as engineered designer probiotics and stabilized postbiotic derivatives, combined with conventional therapies, have potential to pioneer novel combinatorial approaches [18]. As mechanistic insights into the microbiome-cancer axis expand, microbial therapeutics may transition from adjunctive support to integral components of CRC treatment protocols.

### Safety Considerations and High-Risk Populations

Probiotic safety profiles in general populations are well-established through decades of consumption in fermented foods and dietary supplements. The World Health Organization (WHO) and European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) have recognized probiotic safety and effectiveness, classifying them as Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) and Qualified Presumption of Safety (QPS), respectively [17]. These designations reflect the favorable risk-benefit profile in immunocompetent individuals consuming commercially available probiotic products.

However, safety considerations become substantially more complex in immunocompromised CRC patients. Cancer patients often experience impaired intestinal barrier function and increased intestinal permeability, creating conditions where probiotic organisms may translocate from the gut lumen into systemic circulation [30]. Probiotic intake can activate regeneration of the gut barrier by promoting mucin production by goblet cells, but this protective effect must be weighed against infection risk in severely immunocompromised hosts [30].

Case reports document probiotic-associated bacteremia and fungemia in vulnerable populations, including patients with central venous catheters, short bowel syndrome, and severe immunosuppression from chemotherapy or hematologic malignancies. *Lactobacillus rhamnosus* endocarditis has been reported as an unusual culprit in patients with underlying cardiac abnormalities, illustrating that even strains with extensive safety data can cause serious infections under specific circumstances [34]. The *Lactobacillus* genus, part of the lactic acid bacteria group under the Bacillota phylum, is extensively studied, yet rare adverse events underscore the need for vigilance [8].

For high-risk hosts, EFSA QPS recommendations include specific caveats regarding probiotic use [8]. The qualified presumption of safety status applies to specific strains under defined conditions of use, and extrapolation to critically ill or severely immunocompromised patients requires careful risk-benefit assessment. Probiotics are still safe in the majority of situations, notwithstanding clinical restrictions, but future investigation into their potential as adjuvant therapy against CRC is encouraged given their positive health effects as dietary supplements [17].

Consistently, clinical trials have not observed clinically relevant toxicity related to probiotic administration in CRC patient populations [14]. The phase III trial of *Bifidobacterium* BB-12® and *Lactobacillus rhamnosus* LGG® reported no significant adverse events attributable to the probiotic intervention, supporting the safety profile in metastatic CRC patients receiving irinotecan [14]. However, trial populations typically exclude patients with the most severe immunosuppression, limiting generalizability to the highest-risk subgroups.

Cell-free supernatants (CFS) obtained from *Lactobacillus*, *Bifidobacterium*, *Bacillus*, *Enterococcus*, *Pediococcus*, and *Saccharomyces* are implicated in apoptosis activation in cancer cells, offering a potential safety advantage over live organism administration [30]. Postbiotic approaches—using microbial metabolites, cell components, or inactivated organisms—may provide therapeutic benefits while eliminating infection risk associated with viable probiotics. Studies conducted on several *Lactobacillus* strains using their CFS or exopolysaccharides (EPS) have demonstrated anti-proliferative effects without bacteremia risk [17].

Upregulation of p53 and TGF expression via exopolysaccharide extracted from *L. acidophilus* in colon cancer cell lines inhibited viability up to 80.65%, illustrating the therapeutic potential of postbiotic fractions [17]. This approach may be particularly relevant for immunocompromised CRC patients where live probiotic administration carries unacceptable infection risk.

Risk stratification for probiotic use in CRC patients should consider neutropenia severity, presence of central venous access, mucosal barrier integrity, and concurrent immunosuppressive therapy. Patients with absolute neutrophil counts below 500/μL, active mucositis, or indwelling catheters warrant heightened caution or avoidance of live probiotic products. Alternative approaches including postbiotics, prebiotics to stimulate endogenous beneficial microbes, or dietary modulation may provide safer avenues for microbiome-targeted intervention in these populations.

The safety profile of specific probiotic strains varies considerably. *Bifidobacterium* BB-12 has been used extensively in clinical trials for human health improvement with favorable safety data [34]. *Saccharomyces* species, while effective for certain indications, carry fungemia risk in immunocompromised hosts and require particular caution [8]. Strain-specific safety data should inform clinical decision-making rather than assuming class-wide safety profiles.

Inclusion in National Library of Medicine databases does not imply endorsement of contents by NLM or the National Institutes of Health, underscoring the importance of critical appraisal of available evidence [17]. Clinicians must balance potential benefits of probiotic interventions against infection risks on a case-by-case basis, considering individual patient factors, probiotic strain characteristics, and available safety data.

The therapeutic window for microbiome-targeted interventions in CRC likely exists between insufficient microbial modulation and excessive infection risk. Optimizing this window requires strain selection based on safety profiles, dosing regimens that achieve therapeutic effects without overcolonization, and patient selection that excludes those at highest risk for adverse events. As the field advances toward precision microbiome medicine, safety considerations will remain paramount in translating mechanistic insights into clinically actionable interventions.

## Evidence-Based Dietary and Microbial Recommendations

### Dietary Optimization for Microbiome Health

Dietary intervention represents the most actionable lever for modulating gut microbiota composition and reducing colorectal cancer (CRC) risk through microbial metabolite pathways. Evidence from prospective cohort analyses and mechanistic studies supports specific quantitative targets for macronutrient intake that favor short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production over bile acid-mediated toxicity.

**Fiber Intake Targets and SCFA Production**

Consuming at least 50 grams of fiber per day may prevent colorectal cancer and other Westernized diseases attributed to microbiota-diet interactions [37]. This target substantially exceeds typical Western dietary patterns and is designed to ensure adequate substrate delivery to the distal colon where butyrate-producing bacteria reside. The World Cancer Research Fund concluded that evidence for fiber reducing CRC risk was convincing, based on comprehensive evaluation of epidemiological and mechanistic data [21].

Butyrate, the most widely studied SCFA, serves as the preferred energy source for colonocytes and provides approximately 70% of their energy requirements [29]. Mechanistically, butyrate functions as a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor, enhancing cellular differentiation and reducing proliferation in CRC cell lines [10]. This epigenetic modulation promotes a normal phenotype in colonic epithelial cells and supports energy homeostasis through monocarboxylate transporter 1-mediated uptake [29]. Consistent with these anti-inflammatory effects, dietary consumption of soluble fiber pectin as an SCFA-delivery system or orally administered butyrate reduces neutrophil recruitment and inflammation in mouse models [29].

**Fat Reduction and Bile Acid Toxicity**

High-fat diets promote CRC risk through bacterial conversion of bile acids in the colon, which has a significant impact on their tumorigenic activity [21]. Diet affects CRC risk through its effects on colonic microbial metabolism, substantiating the hypothesis that dietary fat intake modulates the bile acid pool available for bacterial transformation [21]. Secondary bile acids, particularly deoxycholic acid (DCA) and lithocholic acid (LCA), exhibit cytotoxic and genotoxic properties that promote colonic carcinogenesis through multiple mechanisms including DNA damage, oxidative stress, and activation of pro-survival signaling pathways such as PI3K/AKT/mTOR [34].

Alterations in secondary bile acid chemistry have been linked to several diseases including cirrhosis, inflammatory bowel disease, and cancer [11]. Bile acids not only influence gut microbiome composition but are also metabolized by gut microbes into compounds that can either inhibit or promote CRC progression [3]. Fat reduction strategies should therefore prioritize limiting saturated fat intake while maintaining adequate levels of omega-3 fatty acids, which exhibit anti-inflammatory properties.

**Meat Limitation and Carcinogen Exposure**

The World Health Organization classified red meat as "probably carcinogenic" and processed meat as "carcinogenic" to humans [16]. Red and processed meat promote colon carcinogenesis via different mechanisms including toxic metabolite production, lipoperoxidation, N-nitrosation, DNA adduct formation, and colonic inflammation [23]. The International Agency for Cancer Research classified heterocyclic amines (HCA) and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) as carcinogens formed during high-temperature cooking of meat [23].

Dysbiosis of the gut microbiota may increase CRC risk through dietary component promotion of colonic carcinogenesis, with red meat consumption representing a key modifiable risk factor [23]. Bacterial conversion of bile acids in the colon has a significant impact on their tumorigenic activity, and high meat intake increases the bile acid pool available for this transformation [21]. Practical recommendations include limiting red meat consumption to less than 500 grams per week and avoiding processed meats entirely where feasible.

**Dietary Pattern Integration**

Westernized dietary patterns are associated with diseases including CRC, and colon cancer can be attributed to microbiota-diet interactions characteristic of these patterns [37]. High-fiber diets have the potential to resolve major public health concerns related to CRC incidence [37]. The ultimate goal is to harness the power of butyrate in developing comprehensive strategies for colon cancer prevention, encompassing both individual-level interventions and population-wide approaches to promote gut health [6].

Cell-free supernatants (CFS) obtained from Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, Bacillus, Enterococcus, Pediococcus, and Saccharomyces are implicated in apoptosis activation in cancer cells [30]. **Mechanistic Pathways of Probiotic Action**

Probiotics mitigate colonic inflammation in vivo, mediated by the histamine H2 receptor or with concurrent reductions in IL-6, IL-1β, and increased IL-10 concentrations [30]. Probiotics are capable of significantly downregulating the expression levels of IL-8 in various models [30]. In parallel, Lactobacillus plantarum-derived indole-3-lactic acid ameliorated colorectal tumorigenesis via enhancement of CD8+ T cells' function through epigenetic mechanisms [30].

SCFA production represents a key mechanism through which probiotics exert protective effects. Bifidobacterium BB-12 increases SCFA production, and SCFAs strengthen gut barrier function [18]. Additionally, probiotics have adjunctive potential in cancer therapy by reducing treatment side effects and improving therapeutic efficacy through immunomodulation and metabolic regulation [18].

**Prebiotic Substrates for Butyrate Enhancement**

Prebiotics must resist digestion, be fermented by gut bacteria, and selectively promote beneficial strains like Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus [18]. This targeted modulation of gut microbiota distinguishes prebiotics from general dietary fibers. Galactooligosaccharides (GOS) fuel butyrate production, providing substrate for butyrate-producing bacteria belonging to the Bacillota phylum [18][8].

Fructooligosaccharides (FOS) and GOS represent the most extensively studied prebiotic substrates for CRC risk reduction. These compounds selectively stimulate the growth and activity of beneficial bacteria while resisting upper gastrointestinal digestion. The onset and progression of CRC are intricately linked to the elevated presence of pathogenic or pro-inflammatory species, particularly from the Proteobacteria and Fusobacteria phyla, while most butyrate-producing bacteria belong to the Bacillota phylum [8]. Prebiotic supplementation aims to shift this balance toward butyrate producers.

**Clinical Evidence Strength and Limitations**

Probiotics are still safe in the majority of situations, notwithstanding clinical restrictions [17]. Future investigation into their potential as an adjuvant therapy measure against colorectal cancer is strongly encouraged by their positive health effects when taken as dietary supplements [17]. Several clinical trials are underway to evaluate the efficacy of butyrate compounds in various formulations, including targeted delivery systems designed to release butyrate directly in the colon [6].

The overall aim of ongoing systematic reviews is to assess how the use of probiotics in patients with colorectal cancer helps in CRC management and its effect on the diversity of gut microbiota [4]. Market segmentation for butyrate-based therapies includes preventive solutions for high-risk populations [6].

### Lifestyle Factors Influencing Microbial Exposure

Non-dietary factors significantly modulate microbiome composition and CRC risk profiles. These factors operate through distinct mechanisms and represent important targets for risk stratification and intervention.

**Antibiotic Exposure and Microbiome Disruption**

Antibiotic exposure represents a critical early-life window for environmental risk factors influencing early-onset colorectal cancer [12]. Experts speculate that mode of birth, breastfeeding, and antibiotics all contribute to microbiome development patterns that may influence CRC susceptibility [38]. Antibiotic use disrupts gut microbial diversity and can reduce populations of butyrate-producing bacteria, potentially creating conditions favorable for pathobiont expansion.

**Birth Mode and Early Microbial Colonization**

Mode of birth influences initial microbial colonization patterns that may persist throughout life. Cesarean section delivery limits exposure to maternal vaginal and fecal microbiota, potentially affecting immune system development and long-term CRC risk. Breastfeeding further modulates early microbial exposure through provision of human milk oligosaccharides that selectively promote Bifidobacterium growth. These early-life factors establish microbial community structures that may influence susceptibility to carcinogenic processes decades later.

**Smoking and Microbial Metabolism**

Smoking represents a modifiable lifestyle factor that affects approximately 50% of CRC cases which could be prevented by lifestyle changes including avoiding alcohol and smoking and maintaining a healthy weight [26]. Smoking alters gut microbiota composition and may interact with dietary factors to influence bile acid metabolism and inflammatory pathways. The combined effects of smoking and Westernized dietary patterns may synergistically increase CRC risk through multiple microbial and host mechanisms.

**Pathobiont Distinction: Established vs. Emerging**

It is critical to distinguish between established carcinogens and emerging CRC-associated pathobionts to avoid overstatement of risk. Colibactin-producing E. coli represents an emerging pathobiont where around 5% of CRC cases may be attributable to colibactin according to new research [26]. This is a significant number, albeit small when compared to the estimated 50% which could be prevented by lifestyle changes [26].

Colibactin functions as a DNA cross-linker, generating mutations at AT-rich sequences in the genome [12]. Understanding colibactin's specificity explains the locations of these mutations, which have been detected in colorectal cancers [12]. However, colorectal cancer is not caused by a single bacteria producing one genotoxin; multiple bacteria and genotoxins are likely involved [38].

Peptostreptococcus is associated with CRC development, with potential roles including production of growth factors for cancer cells and formation of biofilms [16]. Gut microbiome analysis has led to the development of microbiome-based CRC prevention, diagnosis, and therapeutics [16]. However, these emerging associations require further validation before clinical implementation.

**Integrated Risk Reduction Strategy**

Lifestyle factors influencing microbial exposure should be addressed comprehensively alongside dietary optimization. Early-life interventions including appropriate antibiotic stewardship, support for vaginal birth when medically feasible, and breastfeeding promotion may establish protective microbial communities. Adult lifestyle modifications including smoking cessation and prudent antibiotic use can preserve existing microbial diversity and function. These non-dietary factors complement dietary and supplementation strategies to create a multi-modal approach to CRC risk reduction through microbiome modulation.

## Conclusion and Future Research Directions

### Synthesis of Microbiome-Driven CRC Pathogenesis

The accumulated evidence establishes colorectal cancer (CRC) as a disease fundamentally shaped by the dynamic interplay between gut microbiota composition, microbial metabolite production, and host immune responses. This integrated model positions dysbiosis not merely as a consequence of tumorigenesis but as an active driver of carcinogenesis through multiple convergent mechanisms. The pathogenic cascade begins with microbial community shifts that favor pro-inflammatory taxa while depleting protective commensals, creating a permissive environment for malignant transformation.

Central to this model is the dual role of microbial metabolites, which can either suppress or promote tumor development depending on their chemical nature and concentration. Butyrate, produced primarily by bacteria from the Clostridium and Eubacterium genera, exemplifies this mechanistic complexity [13]. At physiological concentrations in healthy colonic epithelium, butyrate functions as a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor, promoting differentiation and apoptosis of transformed cells while maintaining energy homeostasis in normal colonocytes [1][29]. This epigenetic regulation represents a critical tumor-suppressive mechanism that becomes compromised when butyrate-producing bacteria are depleted during dysbiosis.

Conversely, specific pathobionts produce genotoxic compounds that directly damage host DNA. Colibactin, produced by pks+ Escherichia coli strains, operates through a distinct molecular mechanism involving DNA cross-linking that generates characteristic mutational signatures in CRC genomes [12]. Research demonstrates that colibactin-producing bacteria form interstrand cross-links at specific DNA sequences, creating double-strand breaks that, when improperly repaired, lead to permanent mutations driving oncogenic transformation. This mechanism distinguishes emerging CRC-associated pathobionts from established carcinogens like Helicobacter pylori, which has proven causal relationships with gastric cancer but remains under investigation for colorectal involvement.

The immune evasion component of CRC pathogenesis involves microbial modulation of inflammatory signaling pathways. Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from Gram-negative bacteria stimulates Toll-like receptors, particularly TLR4, triggering NF-κB activation and subsequent production of pro-inflammatory cytokines including IL-6, IL-1β, and TNF-α [8]. Chronic inflammation creates a tumor-promoting microenvironment through sustained activation of the IL-6/STAT3 pathway, which promotes cell proliferation and inhibits apoptosis. Peptostreptococcus species have been specifically associated with CRC development through biofilm formation that shields tumor cells from immune surveillance while producing growth factors that support cancer cell proliferation [16].

Bile acid metabolism represents another critical axis linking diet, microbiota, and CRC risk. Secondary bile acids, particularly deoxycholic acid (DCA) and lithocholic acid (LCA), are produced through bacterial 7α-dehydroxylation of primary bile acids [21]. These metabolites function as detergents that damage epithelial cell membranes and generate reactive oxygen species (ROS), causing oxidative DNA damage. Epidemiologic studies confirm that subjects consuming high-fat diets produce elevated levels of fecal secondary bile acids, correlating with increased CRC incidence [29]. The World Cancer Research Fund has concluded that evidence for red and processed meat increasing CRC risk through these microbial metabolic pathways is convincing, while fiber consumption demonstrates protective effects [21].

The spatial organization of the gut microbiome further influences carcinogenesis. Biofilms associated with CRC tumors create distinct microenvironments where oxygen gradients and metabolic zonation enable cooperative virulence strategies among pathogenic taxa [31]. These structured communities facilitate horizontal gene transfer of virulence factors and create physical barriers that limit therapeutic penetration while sustaining chronic inflammation.

This synthesis reveals CRC pathogenesis as a multi-step process where microbial dysbiosis initiates a cascade of metabolite imbalance, immune dysregulation, and genetic instability. The transition from normal epithelium to adenoma to carcinoma reflects cumulative microbial influences that vary across individuals based on their unique microbiome composition, dietary patterns, and genetic susceptibility. Understanding these integrated mechanisms provides the foundation for developing targeted interventions that disrupt specific pathogenic pathways while preserving or restoring protective microbial functions.

### Priorities for Translational Research

Translating mechanistic insights into clinical applications requires addressing several critical gaps in current knowledge. The foremost priority involves establishing human causality through well-designed longitudinal studies that move beyond associative findings. While animal models and in vitro systems have elucidated molecular mechanisms, human data remain limited by confounding variables and the complexity of individual microbiome-diet interactions. Future studies must employ cutting-edge profiling technologies to track microbiome changes preceding CRC development, enabling identification of causal microbial signatures rather than consequential alterations [18].

Engineered live biotherapeutics represent a promising frontier for precision microbiome medicine. Unlike conventional probiotics, these genetically modified organisms can be designed to deliver therapeutic molecules directly to the tumor microenvironment or to competitively exclude pathogenic taxa. Research priorities include developing strains that produce anti-inflammatory metabolites, degrade carcinogenic compounds, or enhance immune surveillance specifically within the colonic lumen. The safety profile of such interventions requires rigorous evaluation, particularly regarding horizontal gene transfer potential and long-term colonization dynamics.

Personalized microbiome screening for CRC prevention demands standardized protocols for sample collection, sequencing, and interpretation. Current variability in methodology limits comparability across studies and hinders clinical implementation. Priority areas include establishing reference ranges for protective taxa, defining dysbiosis thresholds that warrant intervention, and developing rapid diagnostic platforms suitable for routine clinical use. Integration of microbiome data with host genetic markers and dietary assessments will enable risk stratification that guides personalized prevention strategies.

Dietary recommendations must transition from generic advice to quantified, mechanism-linked targets. Evidence supports consuming at least 50 grams of fiber per day for CRC prevention through microbiota-mediated mechanisms [37]. This intake level promotes robust short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production, particularly butyrate, which strengthens gut barrier function and exerts anti-inflammatory effects [18]. Fiber sources should emphasize fermentable substrates that selectively enrich butyrate-producing taxa including Ruminococcus, Faecalibacterium, and Eubacterium species [8].

Synbiotic formulations combining specific probiotic strains with complementary prebiotics may achieve synergistic effects, though optimal combinations require systematic evaluation in human trials.

Postbiotics, including bacterial cell-free supernatants containing metabolites and extracellular vesicles, offer advantages in standardization and safety compared to live organisms. Cell-free supernatants from Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, and Bacillus species have demonstrated apoptosis activation in cancer cells through multiple signaling pathways [30]. Developing purified postbiotic formulations could enable precise dosing and eliminate risks associated with live bacterial administration in immunocompromised patients.

Future research must prioritize precision microbiome medicine that customizes therapies according to individual microbiome compositions, dietary patterns, and genetic backgrounds [18]. This approach requires large-scale cohort studies with longitudinal sampling, standardized metadata collection, and integration of multi-omics data. Regulatory frameworks must evolve to accommodate novel microbiome-based therapeutics while ensuring patient safety.

The transformative potential of microbial therapeutics including probiotics, prebiotics, synbiotics, and postbiotics to revolutionize cancer prevention and treatment remains substantial but unrealized [18]. Achieving this potential demands coordinated efforts across basic science, clinical research, and regulatory domains to establish evidence-based interventions that leverage the gut microbiome as a modifiable risk factor for colorectal cancer.
