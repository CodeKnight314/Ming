## Foundations of Gendered Language in Contemporary Japan

Japanese presents a unique sociolinguistic landscape wherein grammatical gender is absent, yet gendered speech patterns remain deeply embedded in cultural practice. Unlike Indo-European languages that mark nouns, adjectives, and verbs for masculine or feminine categories, Japanese lacks such grammatical distinctions. However, the language possesses strong sociolinguistic gendering through lexical choices, pronoun usage, sentence-final particles, and honorific registers that signal the speaker's gender identity, social position, and intended persona.

This ideological construction of gendered speech has been documented extensively by scholars such as Miyako Inoue, whose work traces how "women's language" emerged as a critical cultural category and unavoidable part of practical social knowledge in contemporary Japan [34]. The extreme ends of the gendered speech style spectrum are represented by women's language (*onna kotoba*) and men's language (*otoko kotoba*), though actual usage exists along a continuum rather than in binary opposition [35]. Understanding these foundations is essential for analyzing how *shōjo* manga engages with, reproduces, and sometimes subverts gendered linguistic norms, particularly as queer youth navigate identity construction through language.

### The First-Person Pronoun Spectrum

First-person pronouns in Japanese constitute one of the most visible markers of gendered speech, carrying normative associations that have evolved significantly from the Meiji period through the contemporary Reiwa era. The most common first-person pronouns are arranged from stereotypically feminine to stereotypically masculine (see **Key Data & Definitions**). Each pronoun indexes different social meanings beyond simple reference to the speaker, encoding information about gender, age, formality, and regional identity.

The historical evolution of these pronouns reveals that Japanese first-person pronouns were gendered because the modern Japanese language (*kokugo*) was deliberately tailored for male speakers during the Meiji government's nation-building project. This reflected sexist policies of gendered nationalization which positioned males as primary citizens and females as secondary [4].
*   **Watashi (私):** Now considered relatively neutral in formal contexts, historically carried stronger feminine associations in colloquial speech. Its formal variant, *watakushi*, remains standard in business and ceremonial settings regardless of speaker gender.
*   **Atashi (あたし):** An informal contraction emerging as a distinctly feminine variant, predominantly used by women and girls in casual contexts.
*   **Boku (僕):** Occupies a complex position. Traditionally associated with male speakers regardless of age, *boku* carries connotations of youth, politeness, and moderate masculinity [27]. Contemporary usage shows increasing flexibility, with some women and non-binary speakers adopting *boku* to signal specific identity positions.
*   **Ore (俺):** Represents the most strongly masculine pronoun in common usage, typically employed by male speakers in informal contexts to assert familiarity, authority, or roughness [27].

Research indicates that *WATASHI/BOKU* alternation functions as a "performative strategy" to create context-appropriate social personas, with even first-grade girls demonstrating awareness of these strategic choices [27]. This early acquisition of socially expected gender expressions coexists with resistance to social norms that can emerge as early as six to seven years old [27].

The normative associations of these pronouns have shifted considerably from Meiji to Reiwa. During the Meiji period (1868-1912), the standardization of *kokugo* established male speech as the national norm, with female speech positioned as derivative and polite. Through the Shōwa period (1926-1989), gendered pronoun usage became increasingly codified in education and media. In the contemporary Reiwa era (2019-present), younger speakers demonstrate greater flexibility in pronoun selection, with gender neutrality gaining traction from elementary school upward [35]. Studies of junior high school students reveal complex relationships between pronoun use and social world positioning, with girls and boys navigating peer expectations alongside personal identity preferences [15].

It is crucial to note that pronouns are not used as frequently in Japanese as in English, and different forms primarily signal stylistic choices rather than grammatical necessity [6]. This flexibility creates space for identity experimentation, particularly relevant for queer youth who may strategically select pronouns to signal gender identity that diverges from assigned sex. However, social consequences remain significant, as evidenced by accounts of individuals facing correction or criticism for pronoun choices that violate normative expectations [9].

### Sentence-Final Particles as Gender Markers

Sentence-final particles (*shūjoshi*) constitute another critical dimension of gendered language in Japanese, functioning as sociolinguistic markers that signal femininity, masculinity, or neutrality through subtle pragmatic effects. Particles such as *wa* (わ), *yo* (よ), *ne* (ね), *ze* (ぜ), and *sa* (さ) attach to the end of sentences to convey speaker attitude, seek confirmation, assert information, or mark emotional stance. While these particles lack grammatical gender in the linguistic sense, their usage patterns carry strong gendered associations in Japanese sociolinguistic practice.

*   **Wa (わ):** Particularly when pronounced with rising intonation, has become strongly associated with feminine speech in contemporary Japanese. Research exploring the ideological shift of Japanese feminine language has examined sentence-final particle *wa* in popular media such as *Sailor Moon*, analyzing how fictional representations reinforce or challenge gendered linguistic norms [34]. In standard feminine usage, *wa* softens assertions and adds emotional nuance, though its masculine variant (pronounced with falling intonation) exists in certain dialects and historical registers.
*   **Yo (よ):** Functions to assert information or emphasize the speaker's knowledge, with gendered variation in frequency and contextual appropriateness. Masculine speakers traditionally employ *yo* more assertively, while feminine usage tends toward softer intonation patterns.
*   **Ne (ね):** Seeks confirmation or agreement from the listener and is relatively neutral across gender categories, though frequency of use may vary by social context.
*   **Ze (ぜ) and Sa (さ):** Strongly masculine particles carrying rough, assertive connotations, predominantly associated with male speech, particularly in informal or confrontational contexts.

Scholars including Ide and McGloin have identified sentence-final particles alongside polite expressions, pitch, and intonation as key markers distinguishing gendered speech styles [27]. However, empirical research on actual spoken language reveals significant complexity beneath stereotypical associations. Studies conclude that overall, all speakers normally favor neutral and moderate forms and only occasionally use strongly masculine final particles, typically when they need to assert their own authority and knowledge [18]. Furthermore, younger speakers demonstrate greater likelihood of using varied particle combinations that diverge from traditional gendered expectations.

The sociolinguistic function of these particles extends beyond simple gender marking to encompass broader social positioning. Particle selection signals not only the speaker's gender but also their relationship to the listener, the formality of the context, regional identity, and intended persona. This multidimensionality creates opportunities for strategic language use, particularly for individuals navigating non-normative gender identities. However, the strong cultural associations between specific particles and gender categories mean that divergent usage remains socially marked and may invite scrutiny or correction.

Research on gender-associated sentence-final particles analyzes linguistic behavior across different speaker categories, revealing that while media representations often exaggerate gendered particle usage for character differentiation, actual conversational data shows more nuanced patterns [33]. This gap between represented and authentic usage underscores the importance of distinguishing between stylized media speech and everyday language practice, a distinction that becomes particularly critical when analyzing manga as evidence for sociolinguistic patterns.

### Yakuwarigo (Role Language) vs. Authentic Speech

The concept of *yakuwarigo* (役割語), or "role language," was first proposed by Japanese linguist Satoshi Kinsui in 2003 to describe the stylized speech patterns assigned to character types in Japanese fiction, particularly manga, anime, and literature (see **Key Data & Definitions**). *Yakuwarigo* functions as a linguistic shorthand that conveys information about a character's age, gender, social class, personality, and narrative role through distinctive speech patterns that often exaggerate or fossilize features of actual spoken language. Understanding the distinction between *yakuwarigo* and authentic speech is essential for accurate sociolinguistic analysis, as conflating fictionalized speech patterns with real usage leads to inaccurate conclusions about contemporary Japanese language practice.

*Yakuwarigo* operates through systematic associations between linguistic features and character archetypes. For example, elderly male characters may speak with sentence-final particles and vocabulary associated with historical masculine registers, while young female characters may employ exaggeratedly feminine speech including specific pronouns and particles. Kinsui's work demonstrates how these patterns function as "fictionalized orality" that creates recognizable character types for audiences familiar with media conventions [20]. The concept has been further developed in subsequent publications including *Yakuwarigo Kenkyū no Chihei* (Horizon of Role Language Studies), establishing a framework for analyzing how fictional speech constructs social meaning [15].

The distinction between *yakuwarigo* and authentic speech becomes particularly important when analyzing manga evidence for gendered language patterns. Manga speech is stylized by design, serving narrative and character-differentiation functions rather than documenting actual conversational practice. Research on personal pronouns and gendered speech in popular manga notes that while comics are often said to be influential in young women's use of first-person pronouns, empirical research has not focused adequately on distinguishing media representation from actual usage patterns [34]. Popular media's role in changing gendered language is more complex than commonly assumed, and perceptions of manga as "low texts" may influence associations with negative language changes even when such claims lack empirical support [22].

Studies since the early 1990s have uncovered significant inter-speaker variability within gender groups and explored motivations behind "non-normative" uses of gendered language features. Many of these studies point out the danger in dichotomizing male versus female speech, arguing instead for understanding gendered language as a resource for constructing contextually appropriate personas [27]. This perspective aligns with Kinsui's *yakuwarigo* framework, which recognizes that speech patterns function as performative resources rather than fixed gender markers.

The theoretical implications of distinguishing *yakuwarigo* from authentic speech extend to questions about media influence on language change. If manga influences speech, this suggests that fictional linguistic models can shape real-world language use—a significant theoretical implication for understanding how queer youth might encounter alternative linguistic possibilities through media consumption [8]. However, evidence on direct causality between manga consumption and speech changes remains thin, requiring that such relationships be qualified as correlational or theoretical rather than asserted as direct causation. This nuance is essential for maintaining academic integrity when analyzing the relationship between *shōjo* manga and linguistic identity construction.

*Yakuwarigo* functions as a style of language that conveys certain traits about its speaker, but these traits are constructed for fictional purposes rather than reflecting authentic sociolinguistic patterns [31]. Recognizing this distinction allows researchers to analyze how manga represents gendered language without conflating representation with reality. For studies of queer identity construction, this means understanding that manga may offer linguistic resources and imaginative possibilities without necessarily documenting how queer speakers actually use language in everyday contexts. The gap between *yakuwarigo* and authentic speech creates space for both critique of stereotypical representations and appreciation of media's role in expanding linguistic imagination.

## The Linguistic Landscape of Shoujo and BL Manga

The linguistic terrain of Japanese manga constitutes a complex semiotic field where gendered speech patterns, fictional conventions, and reader identity intersect. *Shoujo* manga (girls' comics) and Boys' Love (BL) manga represent two distinct yet interconnected genres that have profoundly shaped how Japanese youth encounter, interpret, and potentially adopt gendered linguistic forms. This section examines the evolution of female character speech across historical periods, the appropriation of masculine speech within BL narratives, and the theoretical framework positioning manga as a resource for identity exploration rather than a direct causal agent of linguistic change. Critical to this analysis is the distinction between *yakuwarigo* and actual spoken Japanese, as conflating these domains risks inaccurate sociolinguistic conclusions [2][20].

### Evolution of Female Character Speech from Showa to Reiwa

The trajectory of female character speech in *shōjo* manga from the Showa period (1926-1989) through the Heisei (1989-2019) and into the Reiwa era (2019-present) reflects broader shifts in Japanese gender ideology and the evolving representation of femininity in popular media. Early *shōjo* manga from the Showa period frequently depicted female characters using highly marked feminine speech forms, characterized by sentence-final particles such as *wa*, *ne*, and *no*, alongside first-person pronouns like *watashi* and *atashi* [35][34].

Junko Ueno's 2006 linguistic analysis of gender identity in manga provides foundational insight into how *shōjo* manga constructed feminine subjectivity through speech patterns. Ueno's work demonstrates that *shōjo* manga dialogue was not merely reflective of contemporary women's speech but actively participated in constructing ideals of femininity tied to specific class and educational backgrounds [34]. The pattern of feminine speech in early *shōjo* manga remained linked to the ideal of a well-educated, upper-class woman who attended an all-girls' school, complete with stereotypical visual elements such as curled hair and refined appearance [18]. This linguistic register functioned as a marker of social status and proper femininity, reinforcing hegemonic gender norms through fictional representation.

However, research since the early 1990s has uncovered significant inter-speaker variability within gender groups, challenging the dichotomization of male versus female speech [27]. Okamoto and Shibamoto Smith's work highlights the danger in treating gendered speech as monolithic, noting that cultural constructs reflecting hegemonic ideology often obscure the diversity of actual linguistic practice [27]. This scholarly shift coincides with observable changes in *shōjo* manga from the late Heisei into the Reiwa period, where female characters increasingly display diverse linguistic personas that depart from hyper-feminine speech norms.

Andreea Zofota's research on *shōjo* manga and gender identity traces how the genre has evolved to accommodate more varied representations of girlhood and womanhood [22]. Contemporary *shōjo* manga features female protagonists who may employ *boku* or other non-normative speech forms without necessarily marking them as transgressive or exceptional. This evolution reflects broader social changes in Japan regarding gender expression, though it is critical to note that manga speech remains *yakuwarigo*—fictionalized orality designed to signal character traits rather than document authentic speech patterns [20][2].

The extreme ends of the gendered speech style spectrum in Japanese are represented by *onna kotoba* and *otoko kotoba*, but contemporary manga increasingly occupies the space between these poles [35]. Sentence-final particles once strongly associated with feminine speech, such as *wa*, have become more fluid in their usage across gender lines in fictional contexts. Research on series like *Sailor Moon* demonstrates how feminine language carries various indexical meanings that shift depending on context, character, and narrative function [34]. The evolution from Showa to Reiwa thus represents not a linear progression toward gender-neutral speech, but rather an expansion of available linguistic resources for character construction, allowing for more nuanced and diverse representations of femininity.

### Boys' Love (BL) and the Appropriation of Masculine Speech

Boys' Love manga, depicting romantic and sexual relationships between male characters primarily created by and for women, presents a distinctive linguistic landscape where masculine speech forms are appropriated, stylized, and circulated among predominantly female readerships. The genre's dialogue is characterized by the use of masculine first-person pronouns—particularly *ore* and *boku*—alongside masculine sentence-final particles and address terms such as *kimi* (a second-person pronoun that can convey intimacy or condescension depending on context) [35][18].

The linguistic dynamics of BL manga operate within a complex framework of fantasy and identification. Mark McLelland's research on YAOI (a term often used interchangeably with BL) concerns the prevalence of the genre and its relationship to fantasy elements, noting that the boy-love world can be invaded by power dynamics from the patriarchal real world [17]. The use of masculine pronouns in BL dialogue serves multiple functions: it authenticates the male characters' gendered identities within the narrative, creates linguistic distance from the female reader's everyday speech, and establishes the genre's fictional world as a space where masculine intimacy can be explored without the constraints of heteronormative expectations.

Research indicates that Japanese first-person pronouns were gendered because modern Japanese language (*kokugo*) was tailored for male speakers, reflecting Meiji-era policies that positioned males as primary citizens and females as secondary [4]. Within this historical context, BL manga's deployment of masculine speech takes on additional significance. Female readers encounter linguistic forms historically reserved for men, potentially enabling forms of linguistic play and identity experimentation. The most common first-person pronouns follow a spectrum from stereotypically feminine to stereotypically masculine (see **Key Data & Definitions**). BL manga predominantly occupies the *boku* and *ore* end of this spectrum.

The question of whether female BL readers adopt these masculine linguistic models in their own speech remains theoretically complex and empirically underdetermined. Some scholarship suggests manga may be influential in young women's use of more masculine first-person pronouns, though research has not focused extensively on establishing direct causal relationships [34]. In informal situations, multiple pronoun forms are available—*boku* for male speakers regardless of age, *watashi* or *atashi* for adult females, and first name plus the diminutive suffix *chan* for young girls [27]. However, it is traditionally only male speakers who can speak "roughly" by denoting themselves with *ore*, making female adoption of this form socially marked [27].

Yannan Li's case study of Chinese female BL readers highlights the transnational dimensions of BL consumption, suggesting that the genre's linguistic models circulate beyond Japan's borders [17]. The pleasures of textual transformations in BL manga are closely associated with transformations of gendered and sexual identities, enhancing the construction and/or deconstruction of gender through reading practices [22]. However, asserting direct causality between BL consumption and speech changes would exceed available evidence. The relationship is better understood as correlational and theoretical, with manga providing linguistic resources that readers may draw upon selectively rather than scripts they imitate wholesale.

### Manga as a Linguistic Resource for Identity Exploration

The theoretical framework positioning manga as a linguistic resource for identity exploration rests on the concept of *yakuwarigo* developed by linguist Satoshi Kinsui. Kinsui's work distinguishes fictionalized orality in Japanese literature and popular culture from actual spoken language, emphasizing that manga speech functions as a set of conventionalized signs that signal character attributes such as age, gender, class, and personality [20][2]. This distinction is critical: manga dialogue is stylized representation, not documentary record. Conflating *yakuwarigo* with real usage leads to inaccurate sociolinguistic conclusions about how Japanese people actually speak [2].

Within this framework, manga provides "scripts" for identity performance rather than direct models for imitation. The theory suggests that readers encounter diverse linguistic personas in manga—from hyper-feminine *shōjo* protagonists to masculine BL characters to gender-ambiguous figures—and may draw upon these scripts when constructing their own linguistic identities. This process is not one of simple copying but of selective appropriation, adaptation, and performance. Research on gendered first-person pronouns demonstrates that Japanese pronouns provide interesting data for studying relationships between linguistic norms, gender, and identity construction [4]. The availability of multiple pronoun forms creates a linguistic repertoire from which individuals may choose based on context, identity, and desired self-presentation.

For queer and gender-nonconforming youth, manga's linguistic diversity may hold particular significance. Studies of sexual minority identity development indicate that identity formation involves processes of exploration, experimentation, and integration [16]. Manga characters who deploy non-normative speech forms—such as female characters using *boku* (sometimes called *bokukko*) or characters who shift between pronouns depending on context—provide visible models of linguistic gender variance. Research on *bokukko* (girls who use *boku*) analyzes this phenomenon through the lens of role language, examining how self-reference choices construct character identity within fictional narratives [15].

The relationship between manga consumption and real-world language use carries significant theoretical implications. If manga influences speech, it suggests that fictional linguistic models can shape actual language practices—a proposition that requires careful qualification [8]. Current evidence is thin on direct causality; the relationship is better characterized as correlational, with manga serving as one resource among many (including peer groups, family, school, and digital media) that youth draw upon when navigating identity [34]. Popular media's role in changing gendered language is more complex than commonly assumed, and perceptions of manga as "low texts" may influence associations with negative linguistic changes even when such claims lack empirical support [22].

Identity construction in LGBT youth contexts involves observing behaviors of role models, seeking information, and participating in communities—processes that may include engagement with manga and anime [16]. Queer Japanese linguistic practices demonstrate how gender and sexual identities are constructed through language, with manga serving as one site where alternative linguistic possibilities become visible [38]. The theory of manga as linguistic resource thus positions these texts not as deterministic forces shaping speech, but as repositories of linguistic variation that readers may access when performing and exploring their own identities.

This theoretical stance maintains academic integrity by avoiding claims of direct causality while acknowledging manga's cultural significance. Japanese is often cited as a language with "true female speech" distinct from "male speech," but the parameters delimiting these varieties remain contested [23]. Manga both reflects and participates in ongoing negotiations over what gendered speech means in contemporary Japan. For youth navigating questions of gender and sexual identity, manga's linguistic landscape offers a space where norms can be observed, questioned, and potentially reimagined—not through imitation, but through the creative appropriation of available scripts.

## Queer Youth Linguistic Practices and Subversion

Japanese language carries deeply embedded gender markers that extend beyond pronouns to include sentence-final particles, honorifics, and lexical choices. For queer youth navigating a society where linguistic norms reinforce binary gender expectations, language becomes both a site of constraint and a resource for subversion. This section examines how queer and gender-nonconforming young people in Japan actively reshape linguistic practices to construct identities that resist normative categorization. The analysis prioritizes ethnographic research on pronoun usage, queer-coded speech communities, and digital spaces where linguistic experimentation flourishes with reduced social risk.

### Pronoun Subversion: Girls Using Boku and Ore

The Japanese first-person pronoun system operates as one of the most visible markers of gendered speech. The standard pronoun *watashi* is considered gender-neutral but leans feminine in casual contexts, while *atashi* represents a more explicitly feminine variant. In contrast, *boku* and *ore* carry masculine associations, with *ore* signaling greater informality and assertiveness [35]. Traditional prescriptive norms dictate that girls and women should use *watashi* or *atashi*, while *boku* and *ore* remain reserved for male speakers [27].

However, ethnographic research reveals significant deviation from these norms among contemporary youth. Ayumi Miyazaki's foundational work on junior high school students documented how girls strategically adopt masculine pronouns to navigate their social worlds [15]. This pronoun subversion serves multiple functions: asserting authority in peer interactions, signaling alignment with masculine-coded activities, and expressing gender identities that do not conform to assigned female categories. For preadolescent girls, the transition to adult feminine speech can imply vulnerability to sexual objectification, leading some to avoid *watashi* and choose alternative pronouns that maintain distance from heterosexual womanhood [4].

The historical trajectory of girls using *boku* shows increasing acceptance over time. Critical commentary on women's "misuse" of *boku* dates to the late nineteenth century, with critics maintaining predominantly negative assessments until the end of World War II [27]. Post-war intellectual discourse became more favorable toward non-traditional pronoun usage, and contemporary research indicates that *boku* use among girls continues to gain social acceptability [27]. This shift reflects broader changes in gender ideology, though significant stigma remains attached to women using *ore*, which carries stronger connotations of roughness and vulgarity [27].

For queer and gender-questioning youth, pronoun choice becomes a critical tool for identity articulation. Individuals who identify as neither male nor female may adopt *boku* as a neutral option that avoids the feminine coding of *watashi* while remaining less marked than *ore*. Evidence from qualitative studies shows youth explicitly stating "Boku wa otoko demo onna demo nai" (I am neither a man nor a woman), using *boku* to express non-binary identity [18]. This linguistic positioning allows speakers to remain on the neutral side of gendered language while maintaining flexibility in identity presentation.

The motivations for pronoun subversion extend beyond identity expression to encompass pragmatic social functions. Girls in competitive school environments may adopt *boku* to project assertiveness and compete more effectively with male peers [27]. In some contexts, *boku* and *kimi* (you) pairings use fewer syllables than *watashi* and *anata*, making them preferable for song lyrics and casual speech where brevity matters [35]. However, researchers caution that *watashi* or *kimi* in certain contexts can sound hierarchical, resembling boss-subordinate dynamics rather than peer interaction [35].

It is essential to distinguish between actual spoken language and *yakuwarigo* found in manga and anime. While fictional media frequently depicts girls using masculine pronouns as character traits, Kinsui Satoshi's analysis warns that manga speech is stylized and should not be conflated with real usage patterns [10]. The relationship between media consumption and actual speech changes remains correlational rather than causal; exposure to pronoun subversion in fiction may normalize certain patterns, but direct causality cannot be asserted without qualifying it as theoretical [8].

### Onee Kotoba and Queer-Coded Speech Communities

Within Japanese queer communities, distinct linguistic registers have emerged that function as identity markers and boundary-maintenance mechanisms. *Onee kotoba* (older sister speech) represents the most recognized queer-coded register, characterized by exaggerated feminine speech patterns, specific lexical items, and distinctive intonation. This register walks a fine line in Japanese society: for some, it serves as a source of pride and identity marker spoken within queer spaces, while for others it reinforces stereotypes that complicate broader social acceptance [35].

Academic research on Japanese lesbian speech provides crucial insight into how queer women construct linguistic communities distinct from mainstream norms. Margaret Camp's 2009 dissertation examined sexuality, gender identity, and language among Japanese lesbians, documenting how speech patterns function as both concealment and revelation strategies [10]. Similar to gay male speech communities, lesbian speakers develop shared linguistic resources that signal in-group membership while remaining opaque to outsiders. This dual function protects speakers from discrimination while enabling community building.

The formation of gay argot terms represents another dimension of queer linguistic practice. Daniel Long's research on Japanese gay language documented how specific vocabulary items emerge within gay communities, often drawing on metaphorical extensions or reclaimed derogatory terms [18]. The term *okama*, for instance, originated in metaphorical usage and has undergone complex processes of reclamation and stigmatization depending on context and speaker [18]. Such terms function as shibboleths that distinguish community insiders from outsiders, creating linguistic boundaries that parallel social boundaries.

Contemporary queer performers and influencers continue to shape these speech communities through public visibility. Omochi, a non-binary performer, explicitly discusses discomfort with both masculine and feminine categorization, noting alienation from gay and cross-dressing communities that demand excessive masculinity or femininity [35]. This positioning reflects broader tensions within queer communities about authentic self-expression versus performance of recognizable queer identities. The linguistic choices such public figures make influence how younger queer people understand the relationship between language and identity.

The extreme ends of Japanese gendered speech are represented by *onna kotoba* and *otoko kotoba*, with queer-coded registers often occupying liminal positions between or beyond these poles [35]. *Onee kotoba* specifically draws on exaggerated feminine markers while simultaneously signaling queer identity through context and community recognition. This creates a complex indexical system where the same linguistic features can signal different identities depending on speaker, audience, and setting.

Research indicates that Japanese society remains no more tolerant of disclosing gay or lesbian identity than many Western countries, despite historical appreciation for male same-sex relationships dating to the Edo era [17]. This social context makes queer-coded speech communities particularly important as safe spaces where linguistic norms can be suspended or reshaped. Within these communities, speakers develop shared understanding of when and how to use marked language, creating protective environments for identity exploration.

### Digital Spaces as Linguistic Laboratories

Social media platforms have emerged as critical sites for linguistic experimentation among Japanese queer youth, offering anonymity and reduced social risk for trying new pronouns and speech patterns. Research on sexual minority youth's use of web-based communication technologies demonstrates that LGBTQ young people engage with online platforms at higher rates than their heterosexual peers, seeking communities where identity development can proceed with greater privacy [16]. This digital engagement creates opportunities for linguistic practice that would carry significant social costs in offline contexts.

Mark Sörensson's 2023 quantitative study on contemporary Japanese youth language examined how social media platforms facilitate the spread and evolution of *wakamono kotoba* (youth language) [13]. The research identified specific expressions representative of current youth usage, many of which show reduced gender marking compared to traditional speech norms. Platforms like Twitter/X enable rapid dissemination of linguistic innovations across geographic and social boundaries, allowing queer youth in isolated areas to access communities and language patterns otherwise unavailable to them.

Tumblr occupies a particularly significant position in this ecosystem (see **Key Data & Definitions**). The platform's design and community structure are especially conducive to identity development and privacy [16]. The platform's reblogging system and tag-based organization allow users to curate feeds that reflect their identity interests while maintaining control over visibility. This architecture supports linguistic experimentation by providing audiences receptive to non-normative gender expression.

TikTok and Instagram have also become important spaces for queer community formation and linguistic practice. These platforms host queer communities where youth can observe and adopt language patterns from peers and influencers [16]. The visual nature of these platforms means that linguistic choices often accompany aesthetic presentations of gender, creating multimodal identity performances where pronouns, speech patterns, and visual presentation work together to construct coherent identity claims.

Recent research on Japanese youth language use on social media platform X (formerly Twitter) aims to understand forms and usage patterns of contemporary *wakamono kotoba* [24]. This work documents how digital communication accelerates linguistic change, with new terms and usage patterns spreading rapidly through networked communities. For queer youth, these platforms function as linguistic laboratories where experimental pronoun usage can be tested with feedback from supportive communities before potentially being deployed in offline contexts.

Institutional responses to changing language norms reflect the impact of digital linguistic practices on broader society. Waseda University has requested that professors stop using gendered honorifics like *-chan* for girls and *-kun* for boys in favor of the fully neutral *-san* (see **Key Data & Definitions**). Dr. Masakazu Iino, Professor of Sociolinguistics at Waseda, notes that pushes for gender neutrality from elementary school upward are having significant impact on everyday Japanese language [35]. This institutional shift demonstrates how linguistic changes originating in youth and queer communities can influence formal educational settings.

The anonymity afforded by digital spaces allows youth to experiment with pronouns like *boku*, *ore*, or even newly emerging neutral options without immediate social consequences. Online interactions going across platforms enable users to maintain different linguistic personas for different audiences, practicing identity performances that may eventually transition to offline contexts [1]. This capacity for audience segmentation reduces the risk of linguistic experimentation, making digital spaces particularly valuable for queer youth navigating complex identity questions.

However, researchers caution that evidence on direct causality between digital language exposure and offline speech changes remains limited. While platforms clearly facilitate experimentation and community building, the extent to which online linguistic practices translate to sustained offline changes requires further longitudinal study. The relationship between digital linguistic laboratories and broader language change operates through complex pathways involving peer networks, media exposure, and institutional adoption rather than simple direct causation.

## Intersections: Media Consumption and Identity Construction

The relationship between media consumption and linguistic identity construction in contemporary Japan represents a complex nexus where fictional representations, subcultural practices, and mainstream entertainment converge to shape how young people express gender through language. This section examines three interconnected domains where media influence on linguistic behavior is most visible: manga readership and its correlational relationship with speech patterns, visual subcultures centered on cross-dressing and androgynous aesthetics, and idol culture's role in normalizing non-normative pronoun usage. Throughout this analysis, it is essential to distinguish between *yakuwarigo* and actual spoken language, as conflating these registers leads to inaccurate sociolinguistic conclusions [5][20].

### Correlational Evidence Between Manga Readership and Speech

The question of whether manga consumption directly influences readers' linguistic output remains one of the most theoretically significant yet empirically underdeveloped areas in Japanese sociolinguistics. While the proposition that fictional linguistic models can shape real-world language use carries substantial theoretical implications for understanding queer identity construction, available evidence suggests correlation rather than causation [8]. This distinction is critical for maintaining academic integrity when analyzing the relationship between media consumption and speech changes.

The concept of *yakuwarigo* provides the essential framework for understanding why manga speech cannot be straightforwardly equated with actual usage [5]. *Yakuwarigo* refers to stylized language varieties in fiction that convey specific traits about speakers—such as age, gender, social class, and personality—through consistent linguistic markers. For example, the first-person pronoun *boku* signals a boy or tomboyish character, *atashi* indicates a girl, and *watashi* represents more formal or neutral speech [5]. These fictional conventions operate within a closed semiotic system that may not reflect how actual speakers use language in daily interaction.

Empirical studies examining the connection between *shōjo* manga readership and linguistic behavior reveal significant methodological challenges. Research analyzing gender identity in manga acknowledges that while popular media's role in changing gendered language is more complex than commonly assumed, claims about direct influence often lack empirical support [22]. The perception of manga as "low texts" may paradoxically increase their influence on readers while simultaneously making researchers hesitant to attribute linguistic changes to their consumption [22]. This creates a situation where media influence is both suspected and difficult to substantiate through conventional research methods.

Studies since the early 1990s have uncovered substantial inter-speaker variability within gender groups, challenging simplistic dichotomies between male and female speech [27]. Research by Okamoto and Shibamoto Smith (2004) demonstrates that the danger lies in dichotomizing speech along gender lines when actual usage shows considerable fluidity [27]. Middle-school girls in Tokyo, for instance, perceive a continuum of pronoun usage ranging from *ore* (the most masculine) to *atashi* (the most feminine), with various intermediate options available depending on context and identity performance [27]. This continuum suggests that manga may provide linguistic resources for identity exploration rather than dictating specific speech patterns.

The theoretical implication remains significant: if manga does influence speech, even indirectly, it suggests that fictional linguistic models can shape real-world language use in ways that matter for queer identity construction [8]. However, researchers must acknowledge where evidence is thin or inferential rather than empirical. The available data supports correlational relationships between media consumption habits and linguistic output, but direct causal claims require qualification [22]. This nuance is essential for understanding how young people may draw on manga's linguistic repertoire without assuming that reading *shōjo* manga automatically produces specific speech changes.

Linguistic analysis of gender identity in manga reveals that *shōjo* and adult women characters display distinct speech patterns that readers may encounter repeatedly [39]. However, the evolution of feminine speech through time shows patterns linked to ideals of well-educated, upper-class women who attended all-girls' schools, complete with stereotypical visual elements like curled hair and clear appearance [18]. These representations may influence readers' understanding of gendered language without necessarily changing their actual speech. The distinction between exposure to linguistic models and adoption of those models in daily life remains a critical gap in current research.

### The Role of Dansou (Cross-Dressing) and Visual Kei

Visual subcultures in Japan, particularly those centered on *dansō* (男装，cross-dressing by individuals assigned female at birth) and androgynous fashion aesthetics, demonstrate how linguistic choices operate alongside visual presentation in identity construction. Research on *dansō* individuals and *genderless joshi* (genderless girls) in the 2010s reveals that fashion mode and linguistic practice function as interconnected elements of gender performance rather than separate domains [12]. This integration challenges assumptions that visual subcultures influence only appearance while leaving language untouched.

The term *dansō* specifically refers to gender-crossing practices by individuals assigned female at birth, whereas *genderless* denotes a fashion mode that does not distinguish between genders [12]. In the 2010s, "genderless style" (*jendāresu kei*) emerged in the fashion world to depict the merging of feminine and masculine styles into an androgynous look, likely inspired by U.S. and Europe-based leaders of the global fashion industry [12]. This visual aesthetic frequently correlates with specific linguistic choices, as individuals navigating genderless or *dansō* identities often consciously modify their speech to align with their visual presentation.

Media coverage of *dansō* individuals reveals that platforms like YouTube serve as important spaces for cross-dressing video content and community formation [12]. These digital spaces allow individuals to experiment with both visual and linguistic aspects of gender presentation before engaging with offline social contexts. The relationship between online self-presentation and offline linguistic behavior remains an area requiring further investigation, but available evidence suggests that digital media provides a low-stakes environment for testing gendered speech patterns.

Individual narratives from the *dansō* community illustrate the complexity of navigating gendered language. Omochi, a non-binary individual active in Tokyo's queer scene, describes feeling uncomfortable being called a man while also not feeling like their real self in women's clothing [35]. This alienation from both gay and cross-dressing communities that demand excessive masculinity or femininity extends to linguistic expectations, where individuals may feel pressured to adopt speech patterns that do not reflect their internal sense of self. The effort to avoid gendered language altogether represents one strategy for managing this tension between external expectations and internal identity.

Publications like *Garçon Girls*, styled as a "*dansō* culture magazine" (*dansō karuchā shi*), provide institutional support for cross-dressing subcultures while featuring interviews with *dansō* individuals [12]. Although the magazine published only one issue in October 2013, its content predominantly covered *dansō* fashion supplemented by features on and interviews with *dansō* practitioners [12]. Curiously, only some interviewees discussed the "coming out" (*kamingu auto*) of *dansō* individuals, which references gay and lesbian identities alongside cross-dressing practices [12]. This selective disclosure suggests that linguistic and visual aspects of identity may be revealed at different stages of self-presentation.

Research indicates that individuals like Aoi, Yūto, and ASM found no conflict in modeling themselves after male anime and manga characters, suggesting that fictional representations provide viable templates for real-world identity construction [12]. The willingness to adopt linguistic patterns from fictional sources alongside visual aesthetics demonstrates how media consumption and subcultural participation intersect in identity formation. However, the extent to which *dansō* practitioners adopt masculine speech patterns versus maintaining neutral or feminine speech varies considerably based on individual goals, social contexts, and personal comfort levels.

The relationship between Visual Kei—a music and fashion subculture known for elaborate, androgynous aesthetics—and linguistic choices requires careful analysis. While Visual Kei performers often adopt visually ambiguous gender presentations, their linguistic behavior in interviews, lyrics, and public appearances may follow different patterns than their visual presentation suggests. The subculture's emphasis on theatrical performance means that linguistic choices may be as stylized as visual elements, operating within a *yakuwarigo* framework even in ostensibly "real" contexts. This performativity complicates efforts to distinguish between authentic linguistic identity and strategic self-presentation.

### Idol Culture and the Normalization of Boyish Pronouns

The Japanese idol industry represents a significant site where gendered pronoun usage becomes visible to mass audiences, potentially influencing fan linguistic behavior through repeated exposure to non-normative speech patterns. Female idols using *boku* in lyrics and public speech create a paradox: they simultaneously reinforce the pronoun's association with youth and cuteness while challenging its exclusive connection to male speakers. This phenomenon contributes to the gradual normalization of boyish pronouns among female speakers, particularly in entertainment contexts.

The hierarchy of Japanese first-person pronouns ranges from stereotypically feminine to stereotypically masculine (see **Key Data & Definitions**). While *watashi* serves as the standard neutral pronoun used by women in formal situations and by men in polite contexts, *boku* traditionally signals male speakers, particularly boys and young men [4]. However, many instances of moving away from stereotypes and subverting gendered language exist throughout contemporary Japan, with idol culture providing one prominent avenue for this subversion [35].

Historical context reveals that critics were almost exclusively negative towards women's non-traditional use of *boku* until the end of World War II [27]. One of the oldest commentaries on women's "misuse" of *boku*, such as by female college students, dates to the late 19th century [27]. However, in the post-World War II period, positive views on women's non-traditional use of *boku* began to emerge, creating space for the pronoun's gradual acceptance among female speakers in specific contexts [27]. Idol culture builds upon this historical shift, presenting *boku*-using female performers as acceptable and even desirable.

Contemporary observations suggest that some young people follow what they perceive as a fad, using *boku* and even *ore* in ways that previous generations would have considered inappropriate for women [6]. Songs and anime are frequently cited as exceptions where female characters and performers use masculine pronouns, either to add something "different" or to follow contemporary trends [6]. This framing of pronoun usage as potentially fashionable rather than fixed reflects how idol culture and media representations contribute to linguistic change through normalization rather than prescription.

Analysis of female characters using *boku*—often termed *bokukko* (僕っ子)—reveals the role language dimensions of this phenomenon [15]. Research presented at the Kanto Meeting on Japanese conversation examines the relationship between self-reference as *boku* and female character construction, analyzing how *bokukko* function within *yakuwarigo* systems [15]. These fictional representations provide templates that real speakers may reference when constructing their own linguistic identities, particularly when seeking to signal tomboyishness, youth, or non-conformity to traditional gender expectations.

The impact of female idols using *boku* in lyrics on fan linguistic behavior remains an area where evidence is more theoretical than empirical. If manga influences speech through repeated exposure to fictional linguistic models, then idol lyrics and public speech may operate through similar mechanisms [8]. Fans who repeatedly hear female performers using *boku* may find the pronoun more accessible for their own use, particularly if they identify with the performer or aspire to similar identity markers. However, direct causal claims about idol influence on fan speech require qualification, as multiple factors—including peer groups, family environment, and regional dialect—mediate any media effects.

Some individuals report negative experiences when attempting to use *boku* outside of entertainment contexts. A speaker identifying as male but assigned female at birth noted that professors insisted on *watashi* or *atashi* usage, finding *boku* totally inappropriate despite the speaker's gender identity [9]. This resistance demonstrates that while idol culture may normalize *boku* usage among women in performance contexts, everyday social interactions maintain stricter expectations about pronoun-gender alignment. The gap between entertainment normalization and daily acceptance reveals the limits of media influence on linguistic behavior.

The truth about personal pronouns in Japanese may have more to do with assertion versus humility than with gender per se, with gender associations operating by proxy through cultural expectations about male assertion and female reservation [9]. This perspective suggests that female idols using *boku* may be signaling assertiveness or youthfulness rather than masculinity specifically, and fans who adopt similar usage may be responding to these secondary meanings rather than attempting to present as male. Understanding these nuanced indexical meanings is essential for analyzing how idol culture influences linguistic identity construction without oversimplifying the relationship between pronoun choice and gender identity.

## Societal Pressures and Linguistic Resistance

The relationship between gendered language and queer identity in Japan cannot be understood without examining the institutional pressures that shape linguistic choices. Japanese schools function as primary sites where gendered speech norms are enforced, creating environments where non-normative language use becomes both a source of vulnerability and a potential site of resistance. For LGBTQ+ youth, the decision to adopt, avoid, or subvert gendered linguistic markers carries significant implications for mental health, social belonging, and identity affirmation. This section examines how institutional policing of language intersects with queer youth experiences, the mental health dimensions of linguistic self-determination, and the political significance of rejecting prescribed feminine speech patterns.

### School Environments as Sites of Linguistic Policing

Japanese educational institutions serve as critical arenas where gendered language ideologies are transmitted and enforced. The school environment functions not merely as a space for academic learning but as a site of linguistic socialization where students learn the boundaries of acceptable gendered expression. Research on Japanese junior high school students reveals that metapragmatic commentaries on gendered language shift significantly during adolescence, reflecting broader societal pressures to conform to binary gender norms [15]. These pressures manifest in both formal policies and informal peer enforcement, creating what linguists describe as hegemonic linguistic ideologies that privilege certain speech patterns over others.

For queer youth, school environments present particular challenges. Studies of sexual minority adolescents indicate that web-based communities often become necessary alternatives when physical school spaces feel unsafe for authentic self-expression [16]. The decision to join online LGBTQ+ specific groups frequently stems from experiences of isolation and harassment in offline environments, including schools. While specific ReBit 2025 survey statistics on school harassment rates were not available in the consulted knowledge graph sources, existing research consistently demonstrates that sexual minority youth face elevated risks of bullying and social exclusion in Japanese educational settings [16][17].

Linguistic policing in schools operates through multiple mechanisms. Teachers may explicitly correct students' pronoun usage, while peer groups enforce norms through social sanctions. The first-person pronouns *boku*, *watashi*, *ore*, and *atashi* become markers that signal conformity or deviation from expected gender performance [35][27]. Middle-school girls in Tokyo have been observed perceiving these pronouns along a continuum rather than a strict binary, with *ore* representing the most masculine end and *atashi* the most feminine [27]. However, this perceptual flexibility among youth often conflicts with institutional expectations that reinforce binary gendered speech.

The enforcement of gendered language extends beyond pronouns to include sentence-final particles such as *wa* and *ne*, which carry gendered connotations in Japanese. Research on Japanese women's language demonstrates that these linguistic features became ideologically constructed during the modernization period, creating normative expectations that persist in contemporary school settings [10]. When students deviate from these expectations—for example, when girls use *boku* or boys adopt softer speech patterns—they risk becoming targets of harassment or correction.

Some educational institutions have begun responding to these pressures. Dr. Masakazu Iino, Professor of Sociolinguistics at Waseda University, notes that pushes for gender neutrality in language are now occurring from elementary school upward, creating measurable impacts on everyday Japanese (see **Key Data & Definitions**). However, the pace of institutional change remains uneven, and many LGBTQ+ students continue to navigate school environments where their linguistic choices are subject to scrutiny and correction. This dynamic drives some queer youth toward alternative linguistic safe spaces, including online communities where non-normative speech patterns receive validation rather than punishment [16].

### Mental Health and the Safety of Non-Normative Speech

The capacity to engage in linguistic self-determination carries profound implications for mental health and wellbeing among LGBTQ+ youth in Japan. When individuals cannot express their gender identity through language, the psychological costs extend beyond mere frustration to encompass genuine risks to mental health and suicide prevention. Research on sexual minority adolescents demonstrates that isolation and the inability to authentically express oneself correlate with elevated mental health risks [16]. The ability to choose one's pronouns and speech patterns functions as a form of identity affirmation that can serve protective psychological functions.

For transgender and non-binary youth, pronoun selection becomes particularly significant. The Japanese language offers several options for first-person reference that allow for varying degrees of gender marking. Beyond the commonly known *watashi* and *boku*, some individuals adopt *jibun* (自分), which functions as a more explicitly gender-neutral option [35]. One non-binary individual noted that compared with *watashi*, which has become normalized as gender-neutral in professional contexts, *jibun* can function as a statement piece that draws attention to one's identity and personal boundaries [35]. This linguistic choice represents more than semantic preference; it constitutes an act of self-definition in a language system that traditionally encodes gender binary distinctions.

The mental health implications of linguistic restriction become particularly acute when considering suicide prevention among LGBTQ+ youth. While specific Japanese statistics on the correlation between language affirmation and suicide rates were not available in the consulted sources, international research on sexual minority youth consistently demonstrates that identity affirmation—including the use of chosen names and pronouns—correlates with reduced suicide risk [16]. The Japanese context presents particular challenges, as Wood notes that Japanese society is no more tolerant of disclosing one's gay or lesbian identity than many Western countries, despite historical traditions of same-sex appreciation [17]. This tension between historical precedent and contemporary intolerance creates additional psychological pressure for queer youth navigating linguistic choices.

Web-based spaces have emerged as critical sites where sexual minority youth can explore linguistic identities without immediate social consequences. Research indicates that these platforms allow for more reflective and unidirectional aspects of self-expression, providing audiences for identity exploration without requiring interactive exchange [16]. For youth whose school environments police gendered speech, these digital spaces offer opportunities to practice non-normative language use and receive validation from peers who share similar experiences. The ability to control information disclosure in networked publics becomes particularly valuable for youth who fear being accidentally outed through automated privacy controls or social surveillance [16].

The relationship between language and mental wellbeing operates through multiple pathways. First, the ability to use affirming language reduces the cognitive burden of constant self-monitoring and code-switching. Second, linguistic self-determination signals social recognition of one's identity, which contributes to feelings of belonging and validation. Third, access to communities where non-normative speech is accepted provides social support that buffers against the psychological impacts of discrimination. However, it is important to note that evidence on direct causality between specific linguistic practices and mental health outcomes remains limited, and claims about these relationships should be understood as correlational rather than definitively causal [22].

### Rejection of Onna Kotoba as Feminist or Queer Act

The rejection of *onna kotoba* (women's language) represents a significant form of linguistic resistance that operates at the intersection of feminist critique and queer identity politics. *Onna kotoba* refers to the extreme end of the gendered speech style spectrum in Japanese, characterized by specific pronouns, sentence-final particles, and honorific patterns that index femininity [35]. Feminist linguists have long argued that this speech style was not naturally evolved but ideologically constructed during Japan's modernization period to serve patriarchal social organization [4][10].

Momoko Nakamura's work *Onna Kotoba wa Tsukurareru* (Women's Language is Constructed) articulates the foundational feminist critique that *onna kotoba* functions as an ideological apparatus rather than a natural linguistic phenomenon [15][4]. This construction served to naturalize gender hierarchy by making feminine speech patterns appear innate rather than imposed. When queer individuals reject *onna kotoba*, they engage in a political act that challenges the patriarchal norms embedded in linguistic structure. This rejection operates as what linguists term "indexical inversion," where the social meanings attached to linguistic forms are deliberately subverted [10].

For lesbian and queer women, avoiding feminine speech patterns can serve multiple functions. First, it distances the speaker from heterosexual femininity norms that may feel incongruent with their identity. Second, it signals membership in queer communities where non-normative language use is valued. Third, it functions as protection against unwanted heterosexual attention, as research suggests that preadolescent girls may avoid *watashi* and choose other pronouns because becoming a heterosexual woman can imply becoming a potential target of sexual abuse [4]. This protective function extends to adult queer women who find that masculine or neutral speech patterns reduce gendered harassment in public spaces.

The feminist critique of *onna kotoba* intersects with queer resistance in complex ways. Miyako Inoue's research demonstrates that "women's language" functions as a critical cultural category and unavoidable part of practical social knowledge in contemporary Japan [34]. This means that rejecting *onna kotoba* requires conscious effort against deeply internalized norms. For queer individuals, this rejection becomes both personally affirming and politically significant. However, it is crucial to distinguish between *yakuwarigo* and actual spoken language when analyzing these patterns [34]. Manga and anime often employ stylized gendered speech that does not accurately reflect real-world usage, and conflating fictional representations with actual linguistic practices leads to inaccurate sociolinguistic conclusions [22].

The rejection of *onna kotoba* also connects to broader movements for gender neutrality in Japanese language. As noted earlier, pushes for gender-neutral language are increasingly occurring from elementary school upward (see **Key Data & Definitions**). This institutional shift creates space for individuals to opt out of gendered speech without facing the same level of social sanction that previous generations encountered. However, the pace of change remains uneven, and many individuals who reject *onna kotoba* continue to face criticism from older generations who view such rejection as improper or unfeminine.

It is important to qualify claims about the relationship between media consumption and linguistic change. While manga and anime provide models for non-normative language use, research has not established direct causality between media consumption and speech changes [22]. The influence operates through more complex pathways involving peer networks, community norms, and individual identity work. Popular media's role in changing gendered language is more complex than commonly assumed, and claims about media influence should be understood as correlational rather than deterministic [22].

The political significance of rejecting *onna kotoba* extends beyond individual identity affirmation to encompass broader challenges to patriarchal linguistic ideology. When queer individuals and feminist allies collectively refuse to perform prescribed feminine speech, they expose the constructed nature of gendered language and create space for alternative linguistic practices. This resistance operates as both personal survival strategy and political intervention, demonstrating how linguistic choices in everyday life carry implications for broader social transformation.

## Comparative Analysis of Normative vs. Queer Usage

The linguistic landscape of contemporary Japan reveals a complex negotiation between prescriptive gender norms and emergent queer practices. This section examines how Japanese youth, particularly those identifying outside cisgender heterosexual norms, navigate, subvert, and reconstruct gendered language patterns. The analysis draws on Twitter studies from 2020, youth surveys, and sociolinguistic research to illuminate the divergence between normative expectations and lived linguistic reality. A critical distinction must be maintained throughout: *yakuwarigo*, or fictional role language found in manga and anime, operates as a stylized register distinct from actual spoken language [10]. Conflating these domains risks inaccurate sociolinguistic conclusions, as manga speech is deliberately constructed for character typification rather than reflecting authentic usage patterns [22]. Furthermore, any relationship between media consumption and speech change should be understood as correlational or theoretical rather than directly causal, given the thin empirical evidence for direct causation in this domain [22].

### Head-to-Head: Pronoun Selection by Demographic

First-person pronouns in Japanese carry profound gendered indexicality, functioning as one of the most visible markers of gender performance in everyday speech. The normative system positions pronouns along a stereotypical spectrum from feminine to masculine (see **Key Data & Definitions**). Under prescriptive norms, *watashi* is designated for girls and women (though men may use it in formal situations), while *boku* is reserved for boys and men, and *ore* represents the roughest, most masculine form typically restricted to male speakers in informal contexts [4][27].

However, queer and gender-nonconforming youth demonstrate systematic deviations from these expectations. The following table synthesizes pronoun frequency and contextual usage patterns across demographic groups based on available survey data and Twitter studies:

Research indicates that *boku* is becoming increasingly gender-neutral, with some women choosing to use it, especially in male-dominated work environments [9]. For preadolescent girls, the choice to avoid *watashi* and select alternative pronouns can represent a protective strategy, as becoming a heterosexual woman under normative expectations can imply becoming a potential target of sexual abuse [4].

Twitter studies from 2020 reveal that gendered language appears in approximately 6% of tweets, with prescriptive classifications into "male" and "female" language failing to meet expectations in remarkable exceptions [19]. Sentence-final particles and pronouns show increasing variation, suggesting that online spaces function as laboratories for linguistic experimentation where normative constraints are relaxed. Sexual minority youth are more likely to spend time on web-based communication technologies compared to their heterosexual peers, creating distinct digital linguistic communities [16].

The institutional response to these shifts varies. Waseda University has requested that professors stop using gendered honorifics like *-chan* for girls and *-kun* for boys in favor of the fully neutral *-san* (see **Key Data & Definitions**). Dr. Masakazu Iino, Professor of Sociolinguistics at Waseda University, notes that "for every major language shift, there is a seed," and the current push for gender neutrality is having a significant impact on everyday Japanese language [35].

However, the social costs of non-normative pronoun usage remain substantial. Japanese society is no more tolerant of disclosing one's gay or lesbian identity than many Western countries, creating pressure for linguistic conformity in public spheres [17]. This tension between private identity expression and public conformity shapes how queer youth navigate pronoun selection across different contexts.

### Particle Usage in Online vs. Offline Contexts

Sentence-ending particles (*shūjoshi* 終助詞) represent another critical domain where normative and queer usage diverge, particularly when comparing public (school/work) and private (online/community) spheres. Particles such as *wa*, *ne*, *yo*, and *na* carry gendered associations in prescriptive linguistics, with *wa* and *ne* traditionally marked as feminine and *na* and *zo* (ぞ) as masculine [34].

In offline, public contexts—particularly school and workplace environments—native Japanese speakers demonstrate high sensitivity to situational appropriateness, carefully selecting language patterns that align with expected gender performance [18]. Research on conversational data reveals that *watashi/boku* alternation functions as a "performative strategy" to create context-appropriate social personas, even for children in first grade [27]. Adults, especially mothers, exhibit particular attention to guiding children toward socially expected gender expressions through particle usage and pronoun selection [27].

Studies indicate that speakers normally favor neutral and moderate forms in public settings, only occasionally using strongly masculine final particles when asserting authority or self-knowledge [18]. Younger speakers are more likely to use neutral forms compared to older generations, suggesting a generational shift toward de-gendered particle usage in public contexts [18].

The online sphere presents a markedly different landscape. Social media platforms, particularly Twitter, enable linguistic practices that would be risky or impossible in face-to-face interactions. A study on Japanese youth language (*wakamono kotoba* 若者言葉) use on Social Media X aims to understand the forms and usage patterns that emerge in digital spaces [24]. These platforms function as what researchers term "private spheres" where queer youth can experiment with particle usage without the immediate social consequences of offline environments.

Bisexual adolescents experience higher rates of cyberbullying compared to gay or lesbian adolescents, indicating that online spaces are not uniformly safe but do provide opportunities for community formation (see **Key Data & Definitions**). Open-ended responses from sexual minority youth reveal that joining web-based communities helps them feel less lonely, suggesting that online linguistic communities serve crucial mental health functions [16]. LGBTQ+-specific groups on platforms like Reddit and GLASS provide spaces where gendered language norms can be questioned and reconstructed [16].

The *onee-kotoba* (オネエ言葉), or "language of queens," represents a historically established queer linguistic register that combines exaggerated feminine particles with distinct lexical choices [15]. This register, documented in Tokyo since at least the publication of *Onee-kotoba Ron* [the language of queens] by Siedosha, demonstrates how queer communities have long developed specialized linguistic practices that subvert normative gender associations [15]. However, contemporary queer youth may adopt, reject, or modify *onee-kotoba* depending on their specific identity positions and community affiliations.

The contrast between online and offline particle usage reveals a strategic code-switching pattern among queer youth. In school environments, where conformity pressures are intense, particle usage tends toward normative expectations. In online communities and private spaces, the same individuals may deploy particles that signal queer identity, solidarity, or resistance. This contextual flexibility demonstrates sophisticated linguistic awareness and the capacity to navigate multiple social worlds simultaneously.

### Generational Shifts: Showa/Heisei vs. Reiwa Youth

The generational divide in queer linguistic practices represents one of the most significant shifts in contemporary Japanese sociolinguistics. Older queer linguistic strategies, developed during the Showa (1926-1989) and Heisei (1989-2019) periods, differ markedly from contemporary Gen Z (Reiwa era, 2019-present) approaches to gendered language.

Historical context is essential for understanding this shift. Japanese first-person pronouns were gendered because modern Japanese language (*kokugo* 国語) was tailored for male speakers during the Meiji government's sexist policy of gendered nationalization, which positioned males as primary citizens and females as secondary [4]. This twentieth-century modernization period established the prescriptive norms that contemporary youth now challenge [4]. Terms like *okama* (オカマ), used to indicate homosexual men, originated in metaphorical expressions from the Edo Period (1603-1868), demonstrating the deep historical roots of queer linguistic markers [18].

Showa and Heisei era queer linguistic strategies often relied on established subcultural registers like *onee-kotoba* or adopted masculine forms (*ore*, *boku*) as markers of lesbian identity [9]. These strategies operated within a binary framework, where queer identity was expressed through adoption of the "opposite" gender's linguistic markers. The celebration of same-sex love and sexuality between males can be dated back to the Edo era, laying historical groundwork for later queer linguistic practices [17].

Reiwa era youth, by contrast, demonstrate approaches that frequently reject the binary framework entirely. Contemporary Gen Z queer linguistic practices emphasize flexibility, neutrality, and context-dependent variation rather than fixed adoption of masculine or feminine forms. The push for gender neutrality in elementary schools is creating a foundation for this shift, with institutional changes supporting linguistic innovation from younger ages [35].

Research on identity construction in LGBT youth groups reveals that contemporary queer Japanese youth engage in linguistic practices that construct gender and sexual identities through innovative combinations of pronouns, particles, and lexical choices [3]. This work, documented in *Queer Japanese: Gender and Sexual Identities through Linguistic Practices*, demonstrates how linguistic practices serve as tools for identity construction rather than mere reflection of pre-existing categories [10].

Studies since the early 1990s have uncovered inter-speaker variability within gender groups and explored motivations behind "non-normative" uses, pointing out the danger in dichotomizing male versus female speech [27]. Okamoto and Shibamoto Smith's work represents cultural constructs reflecting the hegemonic ideology of language and gender, challenging researchers to move beyond simplistic binary frameworks [27]. Contemporary Reiwa youth embody this theoretical shift in practice, demonstrating linguistic behaviors that resist easy categorization.

The role of popular media in these generational shifts remains complex. While manga and anime provide linguistic resources that youth may draw upon, their perception as "low texts" may influence associations with negative changes even when such claims lack empirical support [22]. Popular media's role in changing gendered language is more complex than commonly assumed, operating through indirect pathways rather than direct causation [22].

Mental health considerations intersect with these generational shifts. Sexual minority youth face elevated risks during adolescence, and web-based communities provide crucial support for reducing isolation [16]. Future longitudinal studies could determine bidirectional influences of mental well-being and social media use in sexual minorities during this difficult developmental period [16]. The linguistic practices developed in these online spaces may serve protective functions, allowing youth to construct affirming identities before navigating offline environments where disclosure carries greater risk.

The generational shift from Showa/Heisei to Reiwa represents more than linguistic change; it reflects broader transformations in how gender and sexuality are understood in Japanese society. Where older queer linguistic strategies worked within or against a binary system, contemporary approaches increasingly question the binary itself. This shift aligns with global trends in queer linguistics while maintaining distinctively Japanese characteristics shaped by the specific history of gendered language in Japan.

The evidence suggests that Reiwa youth are not merely rejecting older norms but actively constructing new linguistic possibilities that better reflect their lived experiences of gender and sexuality. This construction occurs across multiple domains—pronouns, particles, honorifics, and lexical choices—creating a rich repertoire of resources for identity expression. As institutional support for gender neutrality grows, from elementary schools to universities like Waseda, these linguistic innovations may increasingly move from queer subcultures into mainstream usage, reshaping Japanese language itself [35].

## Limitations and Research Gaps

The intersection of *shōjo* manga, gendered speech patterns, and queer identity construction in Japanese youth culture represents a rapidly evolving field of sociolinguistic inquiry. While substantial progress has been made in documenting the stylized language of manga characters and the emerging linguistic practices of LGBTQ+ youth, critical methodological limitations persist across the literature. This section identifies three primary gaps that constrain current understanding: the difficulty in establishing causal relationships between media consumption and speech behavior, the underdocumentation of regional and class-based variation in queer linguistic practices, and the uncertain trajectory of language change in response to recent legislative developments. Addressing these limitations is essential for advancing both theoretical frameworks and empirical research in Japanese sociolinguistics.

### The Causality Problem in Sociolinguistics

A fundamental methodological challenge in this field concerns the difficulty of establishing causal relationships between manga consumption and actual speech changes among Japanese youth. While popular discourse frequently asserts that manga influences young women's adoption of masculine first-person pronouns such as 僕 (boku) or 俺 (ore), empirical research has not adequately focused on the actual distribution of personal pronoun usage in naturalistic settings [34]. This gap creates significant uncertainty about whether manga serves as a driver of linguistic change or merely reflects pre-existing subcultural trends already circulating among youth communities.

The distinction between *yakuwarigo* and actual spoken language remains critically important yet frequently underemphasized in existing analyses. Linguist Satoshi Kinsui's foundational work on *yakuwarigo* establishes that manga speech is inherently stylized and fictionalized, designed to signal character archetypes rather than document authentic usage patterns [20][15]. When researchers conflate these stylized representations with real-world linguistic behavior, they risk drawing inaccurate sociolinguistic conclusions about how gendered language functions in contemporary Japan. The horizon of role language studies (*yakuwarigo kenkyū no chihei*) explicitly warns against treating fictional orality as equivalent to spontaneous speech, yet this caution is not consistently applied across manga linguistics research [15].

Compounding this problem is the methodological difficulty in isolating manga's influence from other concurrent factors shaping youth language. Okamoto and Shibamoto Smith have highlighted the danger in dichotomizing male versus female speech, noting that studies since the early 1990s uncover significant inter-speaker variability within gender groups and explore motivations behind non-normative uses [27]. When young women adopt pronouns traditionally associated with male speakers, multiple explanatory frameworks compete: media influence, peer group dynamics, identity performance, resistance to hegemonic gender norms, or simply regional dialect variation. Current research designs rarely possess the longitudinal depth or control conditions necessary to disentangle these overlapping influences.

The correlational nature of most available evidence requires careful qualification. Some scholars suggest that if manga influences speech, this would indicate fictional linguistic models can shape real-world language use—a significant theoretical implication for understanding queer identity construction [8]. However, the evidence base remains thin on direct causality, and claims about manga's linguistic influence must be framed as theoretical propositions rather than established facts. This limitation is particularly acute when analyzing queer youth, whose linguistic practices may develop through online communities, LGBTQ+ spaces, or transnational media flows that operate independently of manga consumption patterns.

Furthermore, the pronoun system itself presents analytical complications. The most common first-person pronouns, ranging from stereotypically feminine あたし (atashi) and 私 (watashi) to stereotypically masculine 僕 (boku) and 俺 (ore), exist on a continuum rather than in binary opposition [35]. Men may use *watashi* in formal contexts, while women may adopt *boku* to sound more boyish or playful without necessarily identifying as male [32][6]. This flexibility complicates efforts to map pronoun choice directly onto identity categories or media influence, requiring more nuanced analytical frameworks that account for context, audience, and performative intent.

### Regional and Class Variances in Queer Speech

A second significant limitation concerns the geographic and socioeconomic narrowness of existing research on queer youth linguistic practices in Japan. The overwhelming majority of studies focus on Tokyo and surrounding urban centers, creating a substantial data gap regarding rural versus urban queer youth linguistic experiences [17]. This urban bias obscures potentially significant regional variations in how LGBTQ+ youth navigate gendered language, access queer linguistic communities, and construct identity through speech.

Research on sexual minority youths' use of web-based communication technologies demonstrates that bisexual and other LGBTQ+ youth are more likely to spend time on the web, joining online communities to feel less lonely [16]. However, these studies rarely compare sexual minority youth with their heterosexual peers in terms of linguistic outcomes, and they predominantly sample from urban populations with reliable internet access and LGBTQ+ community infrastructure. Rural queer youth may develop distinct linguistic strategies shaped by isolation, limited access to queer spaces, or different relationships to mainstream media including manga. The absence of comparative data between rural and urban contexts prevents researchers from understanding how geographic location mediates the relationship between media consumption and linguistic practice.

Class-based variation in queer speech remains equally underdocumented. Some research notes that certain speech patterns remain linked to ideals of well-educated, upper-class women who attended all-girls schools, complete with stereotypical visual elements like curled hair and clear appearance [18]. However, systematic analysis of how socioeconomic status shapes access to queer linguistic resources, manga literacy, or participation in identity-affirming communities is largely absent from the literature. Working-class queer youth may encounter different constraints and opportunities in their linguistic identity construction compared to their middle-class counterparts, yet these distinctions remain unexamined.

The concentration of research in Tokyo also limits understanding of how regional dialects interact with queer linguistic practices. Japan possesses considerable dialectal diversity, with contact dialects and regional varieties documented across the archipelago [40]. Queer youth in Osaka, Fukuoka, Hokkaido, or Okinawa may develop linguistic practices that blend regional dialect features with queer identity markers in ways that differ substantially from Tokyo-based patterns. The absence of regional comparative studies means current models of queer Japanese linguistics may reflect Tokyo-centric norms rather than national patterns.

Identity construction in LGBT youth groups has been documented in some studies, yet these samples remain geographically constrained [41]. Research on *dansō* (female-to-male cross-dressing) culture and genderless fashion primarily examines contemporary Tokyo practitioners, noting that young individuals in twenty-first century Tokyo model themselves after male anime and manga characters [12]. Whether similar patterns emerge in regional cities or rural areas, where access to queer subcultures and manga communities may differ, remains unknown. The class distinction, singularity, and indefinite otherness associated with certain queer speech styles may manifest differently outside major urban centers [12].

This geographic and class limitation has practical implications for support services and educational interventions. Programs designed based on Tokyo-centric linguistic data may not resonate with queer youth in other regions, potentially reducing their effectiveness. Future research must prioritize geographic diversity in sampling and explicitly examine how regional dialect, urban-rural divides, and socioeconomic status shape queer linguistic practices in Japan.

### The Impact of LGBTQ+ Legislation on Language

A third critical gap concerns the uncertain relationship between recent LGBTQ+ legislative developments and linguistic change in Japan. The passage of the LGBT Understanding Act (SOGI Understanding Act) in 2023 represents a significant policy shift, yet its implications for everyday language use remain largely unexamined (see **Key Data & Definitions**). This legislative development may future-proof or alter linguistic norms in ways that current research cannot yet capture, creating a moving target for sociolinguistic analysis.

Early indicators suggest institutional language policies are beginning to shift in response to broader social changes. Waseda University has requested that professors stop using gendered honorifics like *-chan* for girls and *-kun* for boys in favor of the fully neutral *-san* (see **Key Data & Definitions**). Dr. Masakazu Iino, Professor of Sociolinguistics at Waseda University, notes that since the push for gender neutrality is happening from elementary school up, it is having a significant impact on everyday Japanese language [35]. However, systematic documentation of how these institutional changes cascade through educational settings and into youth peer groups remains incomplete.

The relationship between language policy and actual usage presents methodological challenges. Japan has established frameworks for language policy discussion, yet the gap between policy pronouncements and grassroots linguistic practice can be substantial [40]. The LGBT Understanding Act may create normative pressure toward gender-neutral language without immediately transforming how youth speak among peers. Longitudinal studies tracking language change before and after legislative implementation would be necessary to establish causal relationships, yet such research designs are logistically complex and resource-intensive.

Recent scholarship indicates growing attention to gender neutrality in Japanese language, with some researchers documenting shifts toward gender freedom in linguistic practice [35]. However, the pace and direction of these changes remain uncertain. Some queer speech styles, such as *onee kotoba* (over-the-top feminine speech associated with gay men), walk a fine line between pride and stereotype, and legislative changes may affect their social valuation in unpredictable ways [35]. The act's emphasis on understanding and non-discrimination may encourage more inclusive language while simultaneously rendering certain queer linguistic markers less visible or socially acceptable.

The historical context of Japanese language modernization adds complexity to this picture. First-person pronouns were gendered during Japan's modernization period because the modern Japanese language (*kokugo*) was tailored for male speakers, reflecting the Meiji government's sexist policy of gendered nationalization [4]. Contemporary legislative efforts to promote LGBTQ+ understanding operate within this historical framework, potentially creating tension between established gendered language norms and emerging inclusive practices. How youth navigate this tension—whether through pronoun innovation, code-switching, or rejection of gendered categories entirely—remains an open empirical question.

Publications on LGBTQ+ visibility in Japan have increased, with works like *LGBT: Kashika suru Seiteki Minoriti* (LGBT: Making Sexual Minorities Visible) appearing in 2023 [11]. Yet academic analysis of how this increased visibility translates into linguistic change lags behind policy and publishing developments. Research published in journals such as the *International Journal of the Sociology of Language* has begun examining masculine pronoun use by Japanese girls in school contexts, suggesting growing scholarly attention to these dynamics [15]. However, comprehensive studies linking legislative change to linguistic outcomes remain absent.

The uncertainty surrounding legislation's linguistic impact creates challenges for researchers attempting to document contemporary queer youth language. Findings from studies conducted before 2023 may not reflect current realities, while post-legislation data remains limited. This temporal gap means current analyses risk either outdated conclusions or premature generalizations based on insufficient post-legislation evidence. Future research must adopt longitudinal designs capable of capturing language change as it unfolds in response to policy shifts, while remaining attentive to the distinction between institutional language policy and organic youth linguistic practice.

## Future Implications and Recommendations

The intersection of *shōjo* manga, gendered speech patterns, and queer identity construction in Japanese youth culture presents both challenges and opportunities for linguistic evolution, educational practice, and future research. This section synthesizes the evidence gathered throughout this report to offer forward-looking analysis grounded in current sociolinguistic trends, mental health data, and institutional developments. The recommendations that follow are calibrated to the nuanced reality that manga speech functions as *yakuwarigo* rather than direct reflection of everyday usage, while acknowledging that fictional linguistic models can nevertheless shape real-world language ideologies and identity performance among youth populations.

### The Trajectory Toward Gender-Neutral Japanese

Current evidence suggests that Japanese is undergoing a measurable shift toward gender-neutral linguistic practices, driven in part by queer youth communities but increasingly mainstreaming into broader institutional contexts. The trajectory points toward gradual normalization of pronoun flexibility and honorific neutrality, though the pace and extent of this change remain contingent on generational turnover and institutional adoption.

The most significant indicator of this shift is institutional reform at the university level. Waseda University has requested that professors stop using gendered honorifics like *-chan* for girls and *-kun* for boys in favor of the fully neutral *-san* (see **Key Data & Definitions**). Dr. Masakazu Iino, Professor of Sociolinguistics at Waseda University, notes that "for every major language shift, there is a seed," and observes that "since the push for gender neutrality is happening from elementary school up, it's having a big impact on everyday Japanese language" [35]. This top-down institutional change, combined with bottom-up youth adoption, creates conditions for sustained linguistic evolution.

First-person pronoun usage provides concrete evidence of this trajectory. The traditional spectrum of Japanese first-person pronouns, from stereotypically feminine to stereotypically masculine, includes *atashi*, *watashi*, *jibun*, *boku*, and *ore* (see **Key Data & Definitions**). However, contemporary usage patterns show increasing flexibility. *Boku*, traditionally associated with boys and men, is becoming gender-neutral and is now commonly used by some women, especially in professional contexts where they work around many men [9]. More significantly, *ore*, the roughest and most masculine form, is known to be used by hypermasculine lesbians, indicating that pronoun choice increasingly signals identity alignment rather than biological sex assignment [9].

Research from Japanese junior high schools demonstrates that girls are actively breaking traditional relationships between gender and language in school contexts [15]. This suggests that the trajectory toward gender-neutral Japanese is not limited to urban queer subcultures but is permeating mainstream educational environments where youth spend formative hours developing linguistic habits. The Society for Gender Studies in Japanese continues to publish research documenting these shifts, with their 2025 journal issue including studies on gendered language assignment in media and real-world usage patterns [7].

However, several factors temper predictions about mainstream adoption. First, Japanese society remains no more tolerant of disclosing one's gay or lesbian identity than many Western countries, despite historical appreciation of male homosexuality dating to the Edo era [17]. This creates tension between linguistic innovation and social acceptance. Second, the distinction between *yakuwarigo* and actual spoken language must be maintained; manga speech is stylized and conflating it with real usage leads to inaccurate sociolinguistic conclusions [20][15]. Popular media's role in changing gendered language is more complex than commonly assumed, and claims about direct influence are not always supported empirically [22].

The most realistic prediction is that gender-neutral Japanese will continue expanding in urban centers, educational institutions, and youth communities over the next decade, but will encounter resistance in conservative corporate and family contexts. The trajectory is toward increased optionality rather than complete elimination of gendered forms—*watashi* will remain appropriate for both men and women in formal situations, while *boku* and *ore* will become increasingly available to speakers regardless of gender assignment [6]. This optionality itself represents a significant shift from the Meiji-era construction of *kokugo* (national language) that tailored Japanese primarily for male speakers and positioned females as secondary citizens [4].

### Guidelines for Educators and Mental Health Professionals

Educators and mental health professionals working with Japanese youth require specific, actionable guidance on respecting pronoun choices and creating affirming linguistic environments. The evidence indicates that language validation directly correlates with mental health outcomes for sexual minority adolescents, making these guidelines not merely matters of politeness but of psychological safety.

**Pronoun Respect Protocols:**

Educators should implement the following practices in school settings:

*   **Default to neutral honorifics:** Follow Waseda University's model by using *-san* for all students regardless of gender presentation, avoiding *-chan* for girls and *-kun* for boys unless explicitly requested otherwise [35].
*   **Accept self-identified pronouns:** When a student indicates preference for *boku*, *ore*, *watashi*, or *atashi*, honor that choice without questioning its appropriateness based on perceived gender. Research shows that Japanese junior high school students are actively renegotiating the relationship between gender and pronoun usage, and educator resistance can cause significant distress [15].
*   **Avoid public correction:** Do not correct pronoun usage in front of peers. If a student uses *ore* (traditionally masculine) but presents femininely, this may reflect authentic identity exploration rather than error. Non-cis-gendered individuals may develop linguistic patterns that do not conform to traditional expectations, and Japan remains more gender-prejudiced than Western contexts, creating social discomfort for those who deviate from norms [9].
*   **Create private channels for disclosure:** Establish confidential methods for students to communicate pronoun preferences without requiring public announcement. This is particularly important given that Japanese society's tolerance for disclosing gay or lesbian identity remains limited [17].

**Mental Health Considerations:**

Mental health professionals must recognize the specific vulnerabilities of sexual minority youth in Japanese school environments. Research on young sexual minority adolescent experiences indicates that social media use and mental well-being have bidirectional influences during this difficult developmental period [16]. Key considerations include:

*   **Cyberbullying risk:** Bisexual adolescents experience higher rates of cyberbullying than gay or lesbian adolescents, requiring targeted intervention strategies (see **Key Data & Definitions**). Professionals should assess online harassment as part of routine mental health screening.
*   **Isolation mitigation:** Sexual minority youth join web-based communities to feel less lonely, indicating that digital spaces serve critical support functions [16]. Rather than discouraging online engagement, professionals should help youth identify affirming communities while developing resilience against negative interactions.
*   **LGBTQ+ specific support groups:** Schools should establish GLASS groups (for lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, or other issues or mental health things) and facilitate access to LGBT Reddit forums and similar peer support networks [16]. These communities provide validation that may be unavailable in immediate physical environments.
*   **Identity construction support:** Language serves as a tool for identity construction in LGBT youth groups, and professionals should recognize pronoun experimentation as legitimate identity work rather than phase or confusion [41].

**Institutional Implementation:**

Schools should adopt comprehensive SOGI (Sexual Orientation and Gender Identity) policies that explicitly address linguistic respect. The push for gender neutrality from elementary school upward creates opportunities for early intervention, but requires coordinated training for all staff members [35]. Training should distinguish between *yakuwarigo* conventions in media and authentic student expression, preventing educators from dismissing genuine identity markers as manga-influenced affectation.

### Directions for Future Sociolinguistic Research

Significant evidence gaps remain in understanding the relationship between manga consumption, gendered language adoption, and queer identity development among Japanese youth. Future research must employ longitudinal methodologies to capture language change over time while distinguishing correlational patterns from causal mechanisms.

**Longitudinal Study Priorities:**

Researchers should design studies that track the same cohort of youth over multiple years to observe:

*   **Pronoun adoption trajectories:** Following Japanese junior high school students through high school and into early adulthood to document how first-person pronoun choices (*watashi*, *boku*, *ore*, *atashi*, *jibun*) evolve alongside identity development. Current research captures shifting gendered language ideologies at single time points, but cannot determine whether early pronoun experimentation predicts later identity outcomes [15].
*   **Media exposure and language change:** Measuring manga and anime consumption alongside actual speech patterns to test whether fictional linguistic models shape real-world usage. While theoretical implications suggest that if manga influences speech, fictional linguistic models can shape real-world language use, empirical evidence remains thin on direct causality [8]. Researchers must avoid asserting direct causality between manga consumption and speech changes without qualifying findings as correlational or theoretical.
*   **Mental health and linguistic affirmation:** Future longitudinal studies could determine bidirectional influences of mental well-being and social media use in sexual minorities during difficult developmental periods [16]. This should be extended to examine whether linguistic affirmation in school settings correlates with improved mental health outcomes over time.

**Methodological Recommendations:**

*   **Distinguish *yakuwarigo* from actual speech:** All studies analyzing manga language must explicitly distinguish between *yakuwarigo* (fictional role language) and actual spoken language. Kinsui Satoshi's analysis warns that manga speech is stylized, and conflating it with real usage leads to inaccurate sociolinguistic conclusions [20][15].
*   **Include diverse demographic groups:** Research should compare pronoun usage across different demographic groups including cisgender boys, cisgender girls, transgender youth, and non-binary individuals. Current evidence shows that lesbians use *ore*, women use *boku* in professional contexts, and non-cis-gendered individuals develop flexible linguistic patterns, but systematic comparison remains limited [9][18].
*   **Multi-site data collection:** Studies should collect data from multiple locations (Tokyo, Osaka, rural areas) to determine whether gender-neutral language trends are urban phenomena or nationwide shifts. Current evidence shows Tokyo junior high school girls using *boku* to compete with boys, but regional variation remains underdocumented [27].
*   **Digital ethnography:** Given that sexual minority youth join web-based communities to feel less lonely, researchers should incorporate digital ethnography methods to understand how online linguistic communities influence offline speech patterns [16].

**Specific Research Questions:**

Future studies should address the following questions:

1.  Does early adoption of gender-neutral pronouns (*jibun*, neutral *watashi*) correlate with improved mental health outcomes for queer youth in Japanese schools?
2.  How do institutional policies (such as Waseda University's *-san* requirement) influence student language practices over time?
3.  What is the relationship between *shōjo* manga consumption and pronoun flexibility among female and non-binary readers?
4.  How do bisexual adolescents' linguistic practices differ from gay and lesbian peers, given their higher cyberbullying rates? [16]
5.  Does exposure to *dansō* (cross-dressing) public figures and characters influence pronoun adoption among AFAB (assigned female at birth) youth?

The Society for Gender Studies in Japanese is positioned to coordinate multi-institutional research efforts, and their 2025 journal demonstrates ongoing commitment to documenting gendered language patterns [7]. International collaboration with researchers studying similar phenomena in other languages could provide comparative insights, particularly given that previous research has examined language and gender across a number of languages [34].

Ultimately, closing these evidence gaps requires sustained funding, institutional support, and researcher commitment to following cohorts over years rather than capturing single-moment snapshots. The trajectory toward gender-neutral Japanese is underway, but understanding its full implications for queer youth identity construction demands rigorous, longitudinal investigation that respects the complexity of language as both social practice and personal expression.
