## Conceptualizing Need for Cognitive Closure in Information Processing

### Defining the Construct: Urgency, Permanence, and Ambiguity Intolerance

The Need for Cognitive Closure (NfCC) represents a fundamental dimension of individual differences in epistemic motivation. It describes the desire for firm, definite answers to questions as a means of reducing the psychological tension that arises from cognitive uncertainty [20]. This construct captures a motivated tendency to seek clarity and certainty rather than a cognitive deficit, positioning closure-seeking as a proclivity that varies systematically across individuals and situations [10]. At its theoretical core, NfCC reflects an aversion to ambiguity and confusion, driving individuals toward cognitive structures that provide clear, unambiguous interpretations of events and information [20].

The construct is characterized by two interrelated tendencies that operate in sequence: **urgency** and **permanence**.
*   **Urgency:** Reflects the desire to attain closure quickly, prompting individuals to seize upon available information and reach conclusions rapidly.
*   **Permanence:** Reflects the desire to maintain closure once achieved, leading individuals to freeze on their judgments and resist subsequent information that might challenge their established cognitive structures [8].

These dual processes—**seizing and freezing**—form the behavioral manifestation of high NfCC. They create a pattern of rapid judgment formation followed by rigid maintenance of those judgments, even in the face of contradictory evidence [28].

Ambiguity intolerance constitutes a central component of the NfCC construct. Research demonstrates a remarkably reliable relationship between high need for closure and discomfort with uncertain or complex situations [25]. Individuals scoring high on NfCC measures exhibit low cognitive complexity and close-mindedness, preferring straightforward explanations over nuanced interpretations that acknowledge multiple possibilities [25]. This intolerance for ambiguity functions as a motivational force that shapes how individuals process information, evaluate evidence, and form beliefs about the world around them [16].

The theoretical foundation of NfCC draws from earlier work on dogmatism and authoritarian personality, sharing conceptual overlap with intolerance of ambiguity and uncertainty avoidance [25]. Research indicates that authoritarianism is, in part, explained by the need for cognitive closure, suggesting that NfCC provides a motivational underpinning for rigid ideological commitments and resistance to perspective change [16]. The construct encompasses dimensions such as close-mindedness and discomfort with ambiguity, positioning NfCC as a broader epistemic motivation that can manifest in various domains including political attitudes, social judgments, and belief formation [16].

Historically, the development of NfCC as a distinct construct emerged from recognition that existing measures of dogmatism and rigidity did not fully capture the motivational aspects of closure-seeking behavior [18]. While concepts like need for structure and tolerance for ambiguity share similar predictions in psychological research, NfCC distinguishes itself through its explicit focus on the motivated tendency to achieve and maintain cognitive certainty [18]. This theoretical refinement allowed researchers to differentiate epistemic motivation from related personality constructs, establishing NfCC as a unique predictor of information processing patterns and belief adoption [10].

### Operationalization and Measurement: The Need for Closure Scale (NFCS)

The Need for Closure Scale (NFCS) was developed by Arie Kruglanski, Donna Webster, and Adena Klem in 1993 to operationalize the NfCC construct [6][14]. The scale has undergone multiple revisions and validations across cultural contexts, with researchers establishing its psychometric properties through extensive empirical testing [30]. The original NFCS was treated as a unidimensional instrument possessing strong discriminant and predictive validity, though subsequent research has examined its dimensional structure in greater detail [6].

The NFCS comprises five distinct subscales that capture different facets of the closure-seeking tendency (see **Data Sources & Definitions**). Psychometric analyses have demonstrated acceptable internal consistency for these subscales, with Cronbach's alpha values ranging from 0.78 to 0.88 depending on the specific revision and sample characteristics [20][11].

The scale has been translated into multiple languages and validated across diverse cultural contexts including Croatia, Italy, the United States, and the Netherlands, supporting its cross-cultural applicability [30]. Short versions of the questionnaire have been developed by researchers at institutions including Cardiff University, allowing for more efficient assessment while maintaining psychometric integrity [14]. Roets and Van Hiel (2007) produced a revision of the original Webster and Kruglanski (1994) scale, incorporating reverse-coded items to improve measurement quality and reduce response bias [11].

A critical distinction in NfCC measurement concerns whether the construct operates as a stable personality trait or a situational state. Evidence suggests that NfCC functions as both: individuals possess relatively stable dispositional levels of closure need, but situational factors such as time pressure, cognitive load, or environmental uncertainty can temporarily elevate closure motivation [8]. Experimental manipulations have successfully lowered need for cognitive closure in research conditions, demonstrating the construct's sensitivity to contextual influences [8]. This dual nature—trait and state—has important implications for understanding how NfCC influences information processing across different circumstances.

Beyond the NFCS, researchers have developed complementary measures to capture related epistemic constructs. The Efficacy at Fulfilling Need for Closure (EFNC) Scale, developed by Bar-Tal and colleagues, measures an ability-like variable independent of epistemic motivation [3][26]. The EFNC Scale achieves good psychometric properties and correlates with constructs representing the ability to use a preferred epistemic process, distinguishing it from measures of closure motivation itself [3]. This distinction proves crucial for understanding when high NfCC translates into actual closure-seeking behavior, as individuals must possess both the motivation and the perceived efficacy to achieve closure [26].

Psychometric debates have surrounded the NFCS, with some researchers questioning its dimensionality and discriminant validity [26]. Neuberg, Judice, and West (1997) published critiques examining what the Need for Closure Scale measures and what it does not measure, arguing for differentiation among related epistemic motives [26]. These methodological discussions have refined understanding of the scale's capabilities and limitations, contributing to more precise operationalization of closure-related constructs in subsequent research [26].

### The Epistemic Process: From Knowledge Acquisition to Judgment Freezing

The Lay Epistemic Theory framework, developed by Kruglanski and colleagues, provides the theoretical architecture for understanding how NfCC regulates the transition from open information scanning to rigid judgment maintenance [18][30]. This theory posits a formal analogy between scientific and lay knowledge formation, suggesting that everyday individuals engage in epistemic processes similar to those employed by scientists, albeit with varying degrees of rigor and motivation [26]. Within this framework, NfCC functions as a motivational regulator that determines when individuals transition from the hypothesis generation phase to the hypothesis testing and validation phase [18].

The epistemic process unfolds in distinct stages governed by closure motivation:
*   **Knowledge Acquisition:** Individuals with low NfCC engage in extensive information scanning, considering multiple hypotheses and evaluating evidence comprehensively before reaching conclusions [18]. In contrast, individuals with high NfCC accelerate through this phase, seizing upon early-available information that provides clear, unambiguous answers to reduce uncertainty quickly [8]. This seizing process reflects the urgency dimension of NfCC, where the desire for rapid closure overrides the motivation for thorough evidence evaluation [28].
*   **Judgment Freezing:** Once a judgment is formed, the freezing process takes effect, particularly for individuals high in NfCC. During this phase, information arriving at later stages that suggests alternative possibilities is ignored because of its incongruency with the established cognitive structure [18]. The freezing mechanism serves to protect the achieved closure from disruption, creating resistance to belief revision even when confronted with contradictory evidence [18]. This process explains why high NfCC individuals demonstrate persistent attitude maintenance and reduced susceptibility to persuasion attempts that challenge their existing beliefs [30].

The mechanism of susceptibility to misinformation operates through these seizing and freezing processes rather than through simple correlation. High NfCC determines the method of processing information, creating a cognitive style that prioritizes speed and certainty over accuracy and comprehensiveness [20]. When misinformation provides clear, definitive explanations for complex events, individuals high in NfCC are more likely to seize upon these explanations during the initial knowledge acquisition phase [20]. Subsequently, the freezing process protects these beliefs from correction, as contradictory information is dismissed as inconsistent with the established cognitive structure [18].

Research demonstrates that the EFNC Scale moderates the effect of NFC on epistemic freezing, indicating that motivation alone does not determine closure outcomes [3]. Individuals must possess both the desire for closure and the perceived efficacy to achieve it through their preferred epistemic process [3]. This interaction suggests that interventions targeting misinformation acceptance should consider not only closure motivation but also individuals' confidence in their ability to process information effectively [3].

The epistemic process framework also illuminates how NfCC interacts with other psychological variables to shape information processing. Trust characteristics may underlie differences in misinformation vulnerability, with individuals high in NfCC showing reduced alignment with expert assessments when those assessments introduce uncertainty or complexity [1]. Political conservatism and NfCC jointly predict reduced alignment with expert information, suggesting that epistemic motivation operates within broader ideological frameworks [1]. Understanding these interactions requires examining how the seizing and freezing processes unfold within specific informational and social contexts, rather than treating NfCC as an isolated predictor [1].

Social groups function as important providers of epistemic certainty for individuals high in NfCC, motivating protection of in-group beliefs and norms [25]. This group-level dynamic extends the epistemic process beyond individual cognition, positioning closure-seeking as a socially embedded phenomenon where shared beliefs provide collective certainty [25]. The preference for aggressive actions in response to in-group threat emerges only when ideological cues align with closure motivation, demonstrating how epistemic processes interact with social and political contexts to shape behavioral outcomes [25].

## Mechanistic Pathways: How NfCC Drives Misinformation Acceptance

The Need for Cognitive Closure (NfCC) operates through distinct psychological mechanisms that systematically increase vulnerability to misinformation. Rather than representing a simple correlation between personality trait and belief adoption, NfCC drives misinformation acceptance through identifiable cognitive processes that affect how information is initially processed, how memories are subsequently retrieved, and how corrected beliefs persist over time. Understanding these mechanistic pathways is essential for distinguishing between memory distortion in eyewitness contexts and belief formation in political or conspiracy domains, as the underlying cognitive architecture differs meaningfully between these applications [17][8].

### Heuristic Processing and the Reduction of Cognitive Load

The primary mechanism through which NfCC increases misinformation susceptibility is through accelerated heuristic processing and the systematic reduction of cognitive load during information evaluation. Individuals with high NfCC exhibit a motivated tendency to reach definitive conclusions rapidly, which fundamentally alters their epistemic strategy from systematic verification to reliance on superficial cues and readily accessible information [18][20].

This process operates through two distinct phases identified in lay epistemology theory: **seizing** and **freezing**. The seizing phase describes the urgent search for any information that can provide immediate cognitive closure, while the freezing phase represents the subsequent protection of that achieved closure against contradictory evidence [30][26]. High-NfCC individuals demonstrate more extensive and faster use of epistemic freezing, meaning they lock onto initial information and resist updating their cognitive structures even when presented with disconfirming evidence [18].

The cognitive mechanism underlying this susceptibility involves the prioritization of accessibility over accuracy. When confronted with information, high-NfCC individuals seize on easily accessible cues rather than engaging in effortful verification processes. This manifests as heightened susceptibility to the primacy effect in impression formation, where early-presented information disproportionately shapes final judgments [8]. The information arriving at later stages suggesting alternative interpretations is ignored because of its incongruency with the cognitive structure already established during the seizing phase [18].

This heuristic processing strategy serves a specific psychological function: the reduction of tension resulting from cognitive uncertainty. The need for closure is fundamentally related to the search for and possession of clear, certain, and definitive knowledge [20]. When misinformation provides simple and structured answers for difficult questions, it becomes particularly attractive to high-NfCC individuals because it satisfies this epistemic need more efficiently than nuanced, uncertain explanations [8].

The reduction of cognitive load through heuristic processing has measurable consequences for information assessment accuracy. Research by Ksiazkiewiz et al. found that respondents with high Need for Cognitive Closure were less aligned with expert assessments at information evaluation tasks, while those with high Cognitive Reflection Test scores and trust in medical scientists showed greater alignment with expert judgments [1]. This suggests that NfCC actively undermines the cognitive processes necessary for accurate information evaluation, independent of educational background or general cognitive ability.

Importantly, the relationship between NfCC and heuristic processing is moderated by several critical variables. Political trust emerges as a significant factor, with research finding strong effects for political trust in explaining conspiracy beliefs alongside NfCC effects [4][29]. The Efficacy at Fulfilling Need for Closure (EFNC) construct further moderates these effects, measuring an individual's perceived ability to use their preferred epistemic process effectively [3]. The EFNC Scale demonstrates predictive validity for moderating the effect of NFC on epistemic freezing, suggesting that individuals who feel capable of achieving closure through their preferred methods may exhibit different misinformation susceptibility patterns than those who feel epistemically frustrated [3].

The dimensional structure of NfCC reveals additional nuance in how heuristic processing operates. Research distinguishes between different facets of the need for closure, with some dimensions reflecting freezing tendencies and others reflecting seizing tendencies [16][26]. This differentiation matters because individuals may exhibit high seizing without corresponding high freezing, or vice versa, creating distinct vulnerability profiles for different types of misinformation exposure.

### Retrieval-Induced Forgetting and Memory Distortion Mechanisms

Beyond initial information processing, NfCC operates through memory mechanisms that systematically distort recall and amplify misinformation effects over time. The critical distinction here involves **retrieval-induced forgetting**, a cognitive phenomenon where the act of retrieving certain information actively suppresses the accessibility of related but non-retrieved information [32].

Research by Pica, Pierro, Bélanger, and Kruglanski (2014) demonstrated in three experiments that NfCC augments retrieval-induced forgetting, which in turn magnifies misinformation effects in eyewitness situations [32][30]. This finding establishes a direct mechanistic link between the need for closure and memory distortion, explaining why high-NfCC individuals show greater susceptibility to post-event misinformation even when original information was accurately encoded.

The mechanism operates through the freezing component of NfCC. Once information has been seized upon and frozen into a cognitive structure, competing or contradictory memories become increasingly difficult to retrieve. This is not merely a failure of memory storage but an active suppression process driven by the epistemic need to maintain closure. When high-NfCC individuals retrieve information consistent with their established cognitive structure, they experience enhanced accessibility for that information while simultaneously experiencing reduced accessibility for information that would threaten closure [18].

This retrieval-induced forgetting has specific implications for distinguishing between memory distortion in eyewitness contexts and belief formation in political domains:
*   **Eyewitness Situations:** The mechanism primarily affects episodic memory for specific details, where post-event misinformation fills in details that were never encoded or suppresses original memory traces [17]. The NfCC amplification of this effect means that high-closure individuals show greater memory distortion even when controlling for general cognitive ability and encoding quality.
*   **Cognitive Architecture:** The effect involves source monitoring failures. High-NfCC individuals demonstrate reduced capacity for discrepancy detection when faced with post-event misinformation, partly because the freezing process reduces the cognitive resources available for evaluating information sources [27]. Future research directions suggest investigating how cognitive abilities protect against misinformation distortions by contributing to more detailed encoding of memory and enhancing discrepancy detection in the face of post-event misinformation [27].

Individuals who possess cognitive and perceptual abilities leading to more detailed recollections would theoretically be less susceptible to misinformation overall, but NfCC can override these protective factors through the motivated freezing process [17]. This explains why education and cognitive ability alone do not fully protect against misinformation susceptibility when NfCC levels are high—the motivational drive for closure can supersede capacity-based protections.

The retrieval-induced forgetting mechanism also interacts with the temporal dynamics of memory. Information arriving at later stages becomes increasingly difficult to integrate as the freezing process consolidates the initial cognitive structure [18]. This creates a time-dependent vulnerability where early-exposed misinformation becomes progressively more resistant to correction as retrieval-induced forgetting suppresses alternative memory traces.

Research on cognitive ability and misinformation susceptibility indicates that significant associations between intelligence and higher-order cognition may reflect the role of general cognitive ability in reducing the likelihood of episodic memory distortions [17]. However, NfCC operates as a motivational factor that can override these cognitive protections, creating a distinct pathway to misinformation acceptance that is not fully addressed by cognitive ability interventions alone.

### Belief Regression and the Persistence of Corrected Misinformation

The third mechanistic pathway through which NfCC drives misinformation acceptance involves **belief regression**—the phenomenon where corrected beliefs revert to pre-correction levels over time. This mechanism explains why misinformation corrections often show only transient effects, with beliefs returning to misinformation-consistent positions despite successful initial correction [7].

Memory failure serves as a primary predictor of belief regression after misinformation correction. Research from Harvard Medical School's Translational Research Center identifies that failures to retrieve corrective information or failures to integrate corrective information into one's mental model drive the regression effect [7][15]. For high-NfCC individuals, this memory failure is not accidental but systematically produced by the freezing mechanism that protects established cognitive structures from updating.

The persistence of corrected misinformation involves two distinct memory sins: **transience** and **misattribution**. Swire-Thompson et al. (2022) found evidence for both occurring within the context of belief regression, where individuals experience failures to retrieve corrective information (transience) or commit the sin of misattribution by being unsure whether information was presented as true or false [15]. High-NfCC individuals are particularly vulnerable to both sins because the freezing process reduces the accessibility of corrective information while maintaining high accessibility for the original misinformation that provided initial closure.

This mechanism links directly to the NfCC drive for stable, unchanging conclusions. Once a belief has been established and frozen, the epistemic system actively resists updating because updating would reintroduce the uncertainty that closure was designed to eliminate [20]. Corrections that successfully change beliefs in the short term often fail to produce lasting change because the motivational architecture driving NfCC continues to favor the original, closure-providing belief over the corrected but uncertainty-introducing alternative.

Research on correction effectiveness shows that people by-and-large adjust their beliefs at least somewhat when confronted with counterevidence, but the durability of these adjustments varies substantially [15]. For high-NfCC individuals, the adjustment is often superficial and temporary because the underlying motivational drive for closure remains unchanged. The correction may be acknowledged cognitively but not integrated into the frozen cognitive structure that governs long-term belief maintenance.

The interaction between NfCC and political trust further complicates belief regression patterns. While some research finds only small effects for individual differences in NfCC with strong effects for political trust in explaining conspiracy beliefs, other studies identify significant moderating relationships [4][1]. This suggests that belief regression may be more pronounced in contexts where political trust is low, as the combination of low trust and high NfCC creates particularly strong resistance to corrective information from distrusted sources.

A critical finding challenges standard mitigation assumptions: education benefits may disappear at high levels of NfCC and narcissism [35]. This interaction effect indicates that traditional interventions relying on education and information provision may fail for individuals with specific trait combinations, requiring alternative approaches that address the motivational architecture underlying NfCC rather than simply providing more information.

Misinformation tags and correction labels show modest positive effects on reducing belief in misinformation, but initial reports of backfire or boomerang effects warrant careful consideration [1]. For high-NfCC individuals, correction attempts similar to misinformation tag labels may be less effective because the freezing mechanism protects the original belief structure from integration of corrective markers. The correction is processed but not incorporated into the frozen cognitive schema that governs belief expression.

The distinction between memory distortion and belief formation becomes particularly relevant in understanding belief regression. In legal memory contexts, regression primarily involves episodic memory failures for specific details. In political belief contexts, regression involves the reactivation of worldview-congruent misinformation that provides epistemic closure [15]. High-NfCC individuals show greater regression in both contexts, but the political belief context shows stronger effects because the closure-providing function of conspiracy beliefs and simplified political narratives directly serves the NfCC motivational architecture.

Research on conspiracy theory adoption demonstrates that NfCC is positively related to endorsement of politically salient views, with the relationship strengthening when competitive schemas are temporarily salient [8]. This suggests that belief regression is not merely a memory failure but an active re-selection of closure-providing beliefs when corrective information creates epistemic discomfort. The regression represents a motivated return to beliefs that satisfy the need for closure rather than a passive forgetting of corrections.

The persistence of corrected misinformation through belief regression has significant implications for intervention design. Corrections that do not address the underlying NfCC motivation—either by providing alternative closure-satisfying narratives or by reducing the epistemic threat of uncertainty—will show limited durability regardless of their factual accuracy or presentation quality. Understanding this mechanism is essential for developing interventions that produce lasting belief change rather than transient correction effects.

## Divergent Manifestations: Eyewitness Memory vs. Political Belief Formation

The Need for Cognitive Closure (NfCC) operates as a fundamental epistemic motive that shapes how individuals process, retain, and act upon information. However, its manifestations diverge substantially across contexts. In legal and forensic settings, NfCC influences compliance with misleading post-event information, directly impacting memory accuracy and eyewitness reliability. In political and conspiracy contexts, the same psychological drive fuels endorsement of false narratives that offer simple explanations for complex societal problems. This section examines these divergent pathways, clarifying the mechanisms through which NfCC produces distinct cognitive outcomes and identifying critical moderators that alter its effects.

### Eyewitness Context: Susceptibility to Post-Event Misinformation

In legal and forensic contexts, the misinformation effect represents one of the most documented cognitive vulnerabilities: following exposure to post-event misinformation, incorrect details are remembered instead of the details from the original event [17]. This phenomenon carries profound implications for judicial proceedings, where eyewitness testimony often determines case outcomes. Research demonstrates that individuals with high Need for Cognitive Closure exhibit heightened susceptibility to these memory distortions through specific epistemic mechanisms.

The core mechanism linking NfCC to eyewitness vulnerability involves retrieval-induced forgetting. In three experiments, Pica, Pierro, Bélanger, and Kruglanski (2014) demonstrated that NfCC augments retrieval-induced forgetting, which in turn magnifies misinformation effects in eyewitness situations [30][32]. This occurs through the "seizing and freezing" processes central to lay epistemology theory. High-NfCC individuals rapidly seize upon initially available information and freeze their cognitive structures, making them less likely to revise memories when confronted with contradictory post-event information [18].

The epistemic freezing mechanism operates as follows: in the absence of contradicting information at the initial encoding stage, a participant classifies an event in a specific manner. Information arriving at a later stage that suggests alternative interpretations is ignored because of its incongruency with the established cognitive structure [18]. For high-NfCC individuals, this freezing process is more extensive and faster, creating rigid memory representations that resist correction. This preference manifests in more reliance on heuristic processing rather than systematic, piecemeal evaluation of new information [18].

Individual differences in cognitive abilities may offer some protection against these distortions. Future research indicates that cognitive abilities can protect against misinformation by contributing to more detailed encoding of memory, enhancing discrepancy detection in the face of post-event misinformation, or improving source monitoring [27]. However, when NfCC is elevated, these protective mechanisms are compromised. The urgency to achieve closure overrides the motivation to engage in effortful discrepancy detection, leading individuals to accept post-event suggestions without critical evaluation.

The legal implications are significant. Eyewitness memory is not a passive recording but an active reconstruction process vulnerable to epistemic motives. High-NfCC witnesses may confidently report details that were suggested after the event rather than actually witnessed, creating a dangerous combination of high confidence and low accuracy. This underscores the importance of understanding NfCC as a risk factor in forensic contexts, where the stakes of memory distortion extend beyond individual cognition to societal justice outcomes.

### Political and Conspiracy Contexts: Endorsement of False Narratives

In political and conspiracy contexts, NfCC operates through a different pathway: rather than distorting memory of specific events, it drives the motivated adoption of belief systems that provide definitive answers to complex, uncertain societal questions. The need for closure is fundamentally related to the search for and possession of clear, certain, and definitive knowledge to reduce the tension resulting from cognitive uncertainty [20]. During periods of societal crisis, such as the COVID-19 pandemic, this motive becomes particularly salient.

Research examining NfCC and belief in COVID-19 conspiracy theories reveals a nuanced relationship. While NfCC creates epistemic conditions favorable to conspiracy endorsement, its direct effects are often smaller than contextual factors like political trust. One study found only small effects for individual differences in NfCC but strong effects for political trust in explaining conspiracy beliefs [4]. This suggests that NfCC operates as a predisposing factor that interacts with trust in institutions to determine misinformation vulnerability.

The mechanism in political contexts differs from eyewitness settings. Rather than unintentional memory distortion, political belief formation involves motivated cognition where individuals actively seek narratives that satisfy their epistemic needs. Kossowska and Van Hiel (2003) demonstrated that NfCC was positively related to the endorsement of political views that are chronically salient [8]. When competitive schemas are temporarily salient, this relationship becomes even stronger; when cooperative schemas are salient, it weakens significantly [8]. This contextual sensitivity indicates that NfCC does not deterministically produce conspiracy beliefs but amplifies whatever explanatory frameworks are most accessible in the individual's environment.

Critical moderators substantially alter the NfCC-misinformation relationship in political contexts. Three factors warrant particular attention:
*   **Political Trust:** Trust in informational sources—including news media, elected officials, and medical scientists—underlies political differences in misinformation vulnerability [1]. Respondents with high trust in medical scientists were more aligned with experts at information assessment tasks, while those with high NfCC and conservative political leanings were less aligned [1]. Political trust can either buffer or amplify NfCC effects depending on whether trusted sources provide accurate or misleading information.
*   **Narcissism:** Recent evidence reveals that education's protective benefits against conspiracy beliefs may disappear at high levels of NfCC and narcissism [35]. This finding challenges standard mitigation assumptions that education universally reduces misinformation susceptibility. When narcissistic needs for uniqueness combine with high NfCC, individuals may reject expert consensus not due to lack of knowledge but due to motivated resistance to externally imposed cognitive closure.
*   **Efficacy at Fulfilling Need for Closure (EFNC):** The EFNC construct measures an individual's perceived ability to use their preferred epistemic process to achieve closure [3]. The EFNC Scale achieves good psychometric properties and correlates with constructs representing ability to use preferred epistemic processes [3]. High EFNC may paradoxically increase misinformation susceptibility by giving individuals confidence in their closure-achieving strategies, even when those strategies rely on heuristic rather than systematic processing.

The appeal of conspiracy theories for high-NfCC individuals lies in their provision of simple, definitive explanations for complex problems. Unlike scientific explanations that acknowledge uncertainty and complexity, conspiracy narratives offer clear causal agents and intentional designs. This epistemic simplicity satisfies the closure motive while simultaneously providing a sense of special knowledge that may appeal to narcissistic tendencies.

### Comparative Analysis: Memory Accuracy vs. Belief Endorsement

The divergence between eyewitness and political contexts reveals fundamental differences in how NfCC produces misinformation acceptance. Understanding these distinctions is critical for developing targeted interventions and avoiding the conflation of legal memory errors with political belief adoption.

*   **Mechanism Distinction:** In eyewitness contexts, NfCC produces unintentional memory distortion through retrieval-induced forgetting and epistemic freezing [30][32]. The individual does not deliberately adopt false information; rather, their cognitive system fails to adequately discriminate between original and post-event details due to premature closure. In political contexts, NfCC drives motivated belief adoption where individuals actively endorse narratives that provide epistemic certainty [20]. The process is less about memory failure and more about selective acceptance of belief systems that satisfy psychological needs.
*   **Outcome Characteristics:** Eyewitness misinformation results in specific factual errors about discrete events (e.g., the color of a vehicle, the presence of a weapon). These errors are typically unconscious and the individual remains confident in their accuracy [17]. Political conspiracy beliefs involve broader explanatory frameworks about societal causation (e.g., pandemic origins, election integrity). These beliefs are often consciously held and may be defended against contradictory evidence through motivated reasoning.
*   **Moderator Sensitivity:** The two contexts show different sensitivity to moderating variables. In eyewitness settings, cognitive abilities such as perceptual acuity and working memory capacity may offer some protection by enabling more detailed encoding and better discrepancy detection [27][17]. In political contexts, psychosocial factors like political trust, narcissism, and EFNC play larger roles [1][35][3]. This suggests that interventions effective in one domain may not transfer to the other.
*   **Correction Dynamics:** Memory plays a fundamental role in belief regression after correction. Research indicates that belief regression was more pronounced for misinformation than facts, but this was greatly attenuated once pre-test belief was equated [7]. In eyewitness contexts, repeated corrections can be effective if they address the memory trace directly. In political contexts, corrections face the additional challenge of motivated resistance, where high-NfCC individuals may reject corrections that threaten their established cognitive structures.
*   **Intervention Implications:** The mechanistic differences suggest distinct intervention approaches. For eyewitness vulnerability, procedures that delay closure (e.g., encouraging witnesses to consider alternative interpretations before finalizing statements) may reduce retrieval-induced forgetting. For political conspiracy beliefs, interventions must address both epistemic motives and the psychosocial factors that make certain narratives appealing. The finding that education benefits disappear at high NfCC and narcissism levels [35] indicates that information-based interventions alone are insufficient for certain subpopulations.

| Dimension | Eyewitness Context | Political/Conspiracy Context |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Outcome** | Memory distortion of specific events | Endorsement of explanatory narratives |
| **Mechanism** | Retrieval-induced forgetting, epistemic freezing | Motivated belief adoption, heuristic processing |
| **Consciousness** | Typically unconscious | Often consciously held and defended |
| **Key Moderators** | Cognitive abilities, perceptual acuity | Political trust, narcissism, EFNC |
| **Correction Efficacy** | Moderate (memory-based corrections) | Limited (motivated resistance) |
| **Intervention Focus** | Delay closure, enhance discrepancy detection | Address epistemic motives, build institutional trust |

This comparative analysis clarifies that NfCC is not a unitary risk factor but a psychological motive that manifests differently depending on contextual demands. Recognizing these divergent pathways is essential for developing evidence-based responses to misinformation that account for the specific cognitive and motivational processes at play in each domain.

## Moderating Factors: Trust, Narcissism, and Cognitive Efficacy

The relationship between Need for Cognitive Closure (NfCC) and misinformation acceptance is not uniform across all individuals or contexts. Rather, this relationship is moderated by several psychological and social factors that can either amplify or attenuate the effects of closure motivation on belief formation. Understanding these moderating factors is critical for developing targeted interventions, as they reveal when and for whom NfCC poses the greatest risk. This section examines four key moderators: political and institutional trust, narcissism and the need for uniqueness, efficacy at fulfilling the need for closure (EFNC), and education and cognitive ability as conditional protections. Each factor interacts with NfCC in distinct ways, shaping whether high closure motivation leads to rigid heuristic processing or can be channeled toward more adaptive epistemic outcomes.

### The Role of Political and Institutional Trust

Trust in political and medical institutions serves as a critical boundary condition for the relationship between NfCC and conspiracy belief susceptibility. When individuals possess high NfCC, they experience heightened discomfort with ambiguity and uncertainty, creating a motivational drive to seize upon readily available explanations that provide cognitive structure [20]. However, whether this drive leads toward evidence-based conclusions or conspiracy theories depends substantially on the level of trust individuals place in authoritative information sources.

Empirical evidence demonstrates that trust characteristics may underlie political differences in misinformation vulnerability, sharing behavior, and attitudes [1]. Research by Ksiazkiewiz et al. found that respondents with high Trust in Medical Scientists were more aligned with experts at information assessment tasks, while respondents with high Need for Cognitive Closure and those who lean politically conservative were less aligned with experts [1]. This pattern suggests that institutional trust can function as a protective factor, potentially offsetting the tendency for high-NfCC individuals to gravitate toward simplistic or conspiratorial explanations.

The mechanism underlying this interaction operates through the epistemic authority individuals assign to different information sources. When trust in government and medical institutions is high, individuals with elevated NfCC may satisfy their closure needs by accepting official narratives, as these sources provide the clear, definitive knowledge they seek. Conversely, when institutional trust is low, the same closure motivation drives individuals toward alternative explanatory frameworks, including conspiracy theories, which offer equally definitive but factually unsupported accounts of events [8].

Notably, some research findings indicate complexity in this moderating relationship. One study concluded that it finds only small effects for individual differences in the need for cognitive closure but strong effects for political trust in explaining conspiracy beliefs, while finding no support for a moderating effect of political trust [4]. This suggests that political trust may operate as an independent predictor rather than a pure moderator in certain contexts, though the correlation between politics, trust, and conspiracy beliefs remains well-established [29].

Cross-national comparisons reveal additional nuance in how trust and NfCC interact. Research comparing the United States and China on need for cognitive closure and trust towards government found differential patterns in predicting pandemic behavior and mental health outcomes [9]. These findings indicate that the moderating role of institutional trust may vary across cultural and political contexts, with the protective effects of trust potentially stronger in societies where institutions maintain higher baseline credibility.

The practical implication is that low trust exacerbates conspiracy belief susceptibility among high-NfCC individuals through a dual mechanism: it removes the protective epistemic authority of official sources while simultaneously increasing the attractiveness of alternative narratives that promise certainty. Interventions aimed at reducing misinformation acceptance among high-NfCC populations must therefore address trust deficits alongside closure motivation, as restoring institutional credibility may be as important as addressing cognitive processing tendencies.

### Narcissism and the Need for Uniqueness

Narcissistic traits interact with Need for Cognitive Closure in ways that can negate the protective effects of cognitive ability against misinformation sharing. This interaction represents a particularly concerning moderator combination, as it suggests that even individuals with high analytical capacity may remain vulnerable to misinformation when narcissism and closure motivation co-occur.

Recent research has explicitly examined the conjunction of narcissism, uniqueness, and need for closure in conspiracy beliefs and misinformation, with findings suggesting that education may fail to protect against misinformation under these conditions [35]. The mechanism underlying this interaction involves the need for uniqueness—a narcissistic trait that drives individuals to distinguish themselves from others through distinctive beliefs and attitudes. When combined with high NfCC, this creates a motivational configuration where individuals seek both certainty and distinctiveness, making conspiracy theories particularly attractive as they offer definitive explanations while simultaneously marking believers as possessing special knowledge unavailable to the mainstream.

The protective effects of cognitive ability, typically measured through instruments like the Cognitive Reflection Test, may disappear when narcissism and high NfCC are present. While high cognitive ability generally correlates with reduced susceptibility to misinformation through enhanced analytical processing, narcissistic individuals may deploy their cognitive resources defensively rather than objectively. They use their analytical capacity not to evaluate evidence impartially, but to rationalize and defend beliefs that satisfy both their closure needs and their need for uniqueness.

This dynamic helps explain why knowledge-based interventions sometimes fail among certain populations. Individuals high in both narcissism and NfCC are motivated to maintain beliefs that serve their identity needs, and their cognitive abilities become tools for belief defense rather than belief revision. The seizing and freezing processes characteristic of high NfCC—where individuals quickly latch onto initial information and resist subsequent contradictory evidence—are amplified by narcissistic defensiveness, creating a particularly rigid epistemic stance.

The interaction between narcissism and NfCC also manifests in social information processing. Narcissistic individuals with high closure needs show heightened sensitivity to perceived threats to their distinctiveness or status, leading them to reject information from sources they perceive as mainstream or authoritative. This rejection is not based on content evaluation but on source characteristics that fail to satisfy their uniqueness needs. Consequently, misinformation that positions itself as alternative or counter-establishment may be preferentially accepted, regardless of its factual accuracy.

Research on political conservatism, need for cognitive closure, and preference for aggressive actions demonstrates related interactive effects, suggesting that personality configurations involving NfCC produce consistent patterns across different domains of social cognition [25]. The narcissism-NfCC interaction follows similar logic, where the combination produces outcomes that neither variable would predict independently.

This moderator combination poses significant challenges for misinformation mitigation. Standard interventions that assume rational information processing may be ineffective when narcissistic identity needs drive belief adoption. Interventions must address the underlying motivational drivers—both the need for certainty and the need for distinctiveness—rather than focusing solely on factual correction or cognitive skill development.

### Efficacy at Fulfilling the Need for Closure (EFNC)

Efficacy at Fulfilling the Need for Closure (EFNC) represents a critical but often overlooked moderator that explains why high NfCC does not uniformly lead to rigid information processing. EFNC refers to an individual's perceived ability to achieve cognitive closure through their preferred epistemic processes, and it fundamentally influences whether high closure motivation results in adaptive or maladaptive belief formation.

The EFNC construct was developed and validated by Yoram Bar-Tal and colleagues, who demonstrated that the EFNC Scale measures a unitary construct with good psychometric properties [3]. Crucially, the scale correlates only with constructs representing the ability to use preferred epistemic processes and does not correlate with constructs representing epistemic motivations themselves. This distinction is essential: EFNC captures perceived capability rather than motivational intensity, making it a true moderator rather than a correlate of NfCC.

The mechanism through which EFNC operates involves the relationship between closure motivation and processing flexibility. When individuals high in NfCC also possess high EFNC—meaning they feel confident in their ability to achieve closure—they may engage in more complex cognition rather than defaulting to heuristic processing. Research indicates that when the need for closure promotes complex cognition, individuals with high efficacy can satisfy their closure needs through thorough information processing rather than premature judgment [26]. This occurs because high EFNC individuals trust that they can reach definitive conclusions through systematic analysis, reducing the pressure to seize quickly on available information.

Conversely, individuals with high NfCC but low EFNC experience a different psychological dynamic. They desire closure but doubt their ability to achieve it through effortful processing, creating anxiety that drives them toward rapid heuristic judgments. The uncertainty about their own epistemic capabilities compounds the discomfort with ambiguity, accelerating the freezing process where initial judgments become resistant to revision.

The intensity of the need for closure determines the method of processing information, but EFNC moderates which method becomes dominant [20]. High NfCC combined with high EFNC may produce individuals who are motivated to reach conclusions but willing to invest cognitive effort in doing so accurately. High NfCC combined with low EFNC produces individuals who are motivated to reach conclusions and compelled to do so quickly through whatever means available, typically heuristic cues or socially validated beliefs.

This moderator has important implications for understanding individual differences in misinformation susceptibility. Two individuals with identical NfCC scores may show dramatically different vulnerability to misinformation depending on their EFNC levels. The high-EFNC individual may use their closure motivation as energy for careful evaluation, while the low-EFNC individual may use the same motivation as justification for accepting the first plausible explanation encountered.

The EFNC construct also helps explain why some interventions succeed while others fail. Interventions that enhance individuals' confidence in their analytical capabilities—effectively raising EFNC—may reduce misinformation susceptibility even among those with high closure motivation. By contrast, interventions that simply present factual corrections without addressing efficacy beliefs may fail, as low-EFNC individuals lack the confidence to engage with complex corrective information.

Research on need for closure and heuristic information processing supports this interpretation, indicating that the relationship between closure motivation and processing style is not deterministic but depends on perceived capabilities [28]. EFNC represents one such capability perception that shapes whether high NfCC leads to rigid or flexible processing outcomes.

### Education and Cognitive Ability as Conditional Protections

Education and cognitive ability are frequently assumed to provide universal protection against misinformation, but evidence indicates these benefits are conditional and may disappear at high levels of NfCC and narcissism. This finding challenges standard assumptions about knowledge-based interventions and suggests that cognitive resources alone cannot overcome motivational drivers of belief formation.

Research by Ksiazkiewiz et al. found that respondents with high Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) scores were more aligned with experts at information assessment tasks, demonstrating the protective value of analytical thinking under normal conditions [1]. However, this protective effect is not uniform. The same research indicates that respondents with high Need for Cognitive Closure were less aligned with experts, suggesting that closure motivation can override the benefits of cognitive ability. When both factors are present—high cognitive ability and high NfCC—the closure motivation may direct analytical resources toward defending predetermined conclusions rather than evaluating evidence objectively.

The conditional nature of educational protection is explicitly highlighted in research titled "When education fails: Narcissism, uniqueness, and need for closure in conspiracy beliefs and misinformation" [35]. This work demonstrates that education benefits may disappear when personality factors create motivational barriers to accepting corrective information. Educated individuals with high NfCC and narcissistic traits possess the cognitive capacity to understand corrections but lack the motivational openness to accept them, as acceptance would threaten both their need for certainty and their need for distinctive identity.

The mechanism underlying this conditional protection involves the deployment of cognitive resources. Education and cognitive ability provide tools for information processing, but motivation determines how those tools are used. High-NfCC individuals may use their education selectively, engaging in motivated reasoning that leverages their knowledge to construct defenses for preferred beliefs rather than to evaluate claims impartially. This phenomenon explains why highly educated individuals sometimes exhibit strong commitment to conspiracy theories or misinformation—their education provides rhetorical resources for belief defense rather than epistemic safeguards against false claims.

Research on the correlation between need for cognitive closure and narrative creativity in secondary education reveals additional complexity in how education interacts with closure motivation [31]. While education typically promotes critical thinking, high NfCC may channel creative and analytical capacities toward constructing coherent but factually unsupported narratives. The educational investment does not disappear but is redirected in service of closure needs.

The implications for intervention design are substantial. Knowledge-based interventions that assume education provides universal protection may fail among high-NfCC populations, as these individuals can use their knowledge to rationalize rather than revise beliefs. Effective interventions must address the motivational underpinnings of belief formation alongside cognitive skill development. This may involve techniques that reduce the perceived threat of belief revision, frame corrective information in ways that satisfy closure needs, or enhance EFNC to increase confidence in analytical processing.

Comparative research on cognitive ability measures and memory accuracy for misinformation details indicates that performance-based behavioral measures of cognitive ability are required to properly assess these relationships [17]. Self-reported education levels may not capture the relevant cognitive capacities, and even objective cognitive measures may not predict misinformation susceptibility when motivational factors dominate information processing.

The conditional protection finding also has implications for understanding demographic patterns in misinformation acceptance. Groups with high average education levels may still show elevated misinformation susceptibility if closure motivation and related personality factors are prevalent. This helps explain why misinformation spreads across all educational strata and why targeting education alone cannot solve the misinformation problem. Interventions must be multifaceted, addressing cognitive, motivational, and social factors simultaneously to achieve meaningful reductions in misinformation acceptance and sharing.

## Contextual Dynamics: Social Media, Framing, and Cross-Cultural Variance

The Need for Cognitive Closure (NfCC) does not operate in a vacuum. Its effects on misinformation acceptance are profoundly shaped by contextual factors including the digital environments where information is encountered, the structural presentation of messages, and the cultural-institutional frameworks that govern trust and compliance. This section examines how NfCC interacts with these contextual dynamics to produce differential vulnerability to misinformation across platforms, message formats, and national contexts.

### Social Media Behavior and Attitudes Toward Warning Tags

The proliferation of social media as a primary news source has fundamentally altered the information landscape, creating conditions where misinformation can spread rapidly and where interventions such as warning tags and content flags have become standard practice. However, emerging evidence suggests that the effectiveness of these interventions is not uniform across individuals, with NfCC serving as a critical moderator of how users respond to platform-based corrective measures.

Research indicates that individuals with high NfCC exhibit distinct patterns of engagement with misinformation warning systems. The urgency to achieve cognitive certainty means that high-NfCC individuals may process warning tags differently than their low-NfCC counterparts. When confronted with content flags indicating potential misinformation, high-NfCC users face a tension between the desire for quick resolution and the uncertainty introduced by the warning itself. This tension can produce counterintuitive outcomes, where warnings intended to reduce misinformation acceptance may instead trigger defensive processing or selective attention to information that confirms pre-existing beliefs [1].

The evidence on warning tag effectiveness remains thin but points toward the necessity of personalized and culturally-relevant interventions. One-size-fits-all approaches to preventing misinformation uptake and spread may fail if they do not harmonize with the specific needs and motivations of particular user segments [1]. For high-NfCC individuals, warning tags that provide clear, definitive guidance about content credibility may be more effective than ambiguous flags that introduce additional uncertainty. The mechanism here aligns with the broader seizing and freezing framework: high-NfCC users seek to seize upon clear signals and freeze on judgments that reduce ambiguity, meaning that warning systems must provide unambiguous cues to be effective.

Trust characteristics further complicate the relationship between NfCC and responses to platform interventions. Trust in informational sources, including news media, elected officials, and medical scientists, underlies political differences in misinformation vulnerability, sharing behavior, and attitudes toward corrective measures [1]. When high-NfCC individuals distrust the platform or the entities issuing warnings, they may reject warning tags outright, perceiving them as attempts to impose unwanted uncertainty or as evidence of institutional bias. This dynamic suggests that warning tag efficacy is contingent not only on individual cognitive traits but also on the broader trust environment in which those traits operate.

Inoculation-based interventions represent a promising alternative to reactive warning tags. The Bad News intervention, an online "fake news" game, exposes players to weakened doses of misinformation techniques in a safe gaming environment, inducing a sense of threat and awareness of vulnerability [2]. Following inoculation theory, this prebunking approach allows players to engage in simulated resistance, building psychological antibodies against future misinformation exposure [13]. For high-NfCC individuals, such interventions may be particularly valuable because they provide structured, game-based environments where uncertainty is managed and resolution is achievable through skill development rather than ambiguous fact-checking.

However, the evidence base for how NfCC specifically moderates responses to inoculation interventions remains limited. What is clear is that cognitive closure affects perception of the social world broadly, shaping how individuals interpret platform governance, content moderation, and the credibility of digital information sources [20]. High-NfCC users may view platform interventions through the lens of their epistemic needs, accepting warnings that align with their desire for certainty while rejecting those that introduce ambiguity or challenge established beliefs.

The thin but emerging evidence on social media behavior and warning tags suggests several critical implications. First, platform designers must account for individual differences in NfCC when developing content moderation systems. Second, trust in the platform and its governance structures may be as important as the design of the warnings themselves. Third, proactive inoculation approaches may offer advantages over reactive warning systems, particularly for high-NfCC users who benefit from structured learning environments that reduce uncertainty while building resistance skills.

### Susceptibility to Framing and Message Structure

The manner in which information is framed and structured exerts a powerful influence on belief formation, and NfCC serves as a critical moderator of framing susceptibility. Individuals high in NfCC demonstrate heightened vulnerability to attribute framing effects, where the same information presented in different ways produces divergent judgments and decisions. This susceptibility stems from the core epistemic mechanisms of NfCC: the urgency to seize upon readily available information and the tendency to freeze on early judgments without full consideration of alternative interpretations.

Research specifically examining the impact of NfCC on susceptibility to framing has identified clear patterns of differential vulnerability. High-NfCC individuals are more likely to be influenced by how messages are structured, particularly when framing provides clear, unambiguous cues that satisfy the need for cognitive closure [11]. Attribute framing, which differs from risky choice and goal framing in that no risk is involved, appears to operate through encoding mechanisms that are particularly salient for high-NfCC individuals [11]. When information is framed in a manner that provides immediate cognitive structure, high-NfCC users are more likely to accept that framing without engaging in effortful processing of alternative perspectives.

The seizing and freezing processes provide the mechanistic explanation for this framing susceptibility. Seizing refers to the tendency to quickly latch onto information that provides immediate cognitive structure, while freezing denotes the reluctance to revise judgments once they have been formed [26]. In the context of message framing, high-NfCC individuals seize upon the initial frame presented and freeze on the interpretation it provides, making them resistant to subsequent information that might challenge that frame. This mechanism explains why headlines and message breaks can exert disproportionate influence on high-NfCC users: the initial exposure provides the cognitive structure they seek, and subsequent elaboration is often ignored or discounted.

Lay epistemology theory further clarifies how NfCC shapes information processing in framed contexts. According to this framework, individuals high in NfCC rely on heuristic processing rather than systematic analysis when evaluating information [18]. When information arrives that contradicts an already-formed cognitive structure, high-NfCC individuals tend to ignore it due to its incongruency with the established framework. This selective processing means that framed messages, once accepted, become resistant to correction because contradictory information is filtered out at the encoding stage.

The intensity of NfCC determines the method of processing information, with higher levels associated with greater reliance on heuristics and reduced engagement with complex or ambiguous content [20]. This has important implications for how misinformation is structured and disseminated. Misinformation that is presented in clear, simple frames with definitive conclusions is more likely to be accepted by high-NfCC individuals than information that acknowledges uncertainty or presents multiple perspectives. The preference for order and structure that characterizes high NfCC means that messy, nuanced information is less appealing than clean, framed narratives that provide immediate cognitive closure [31].

Message structure also interacts with NfCC through the manipulation of information breaks. When news is presented in segmented formats with clear headlines followed by elaboration, high-NfCC individuals may form judgments based on the headline alone, treating it as sufficient for cognitive closure without engaging with the supporting details. Research examining the manipulation of breaking up news content has found that NFC factors influence how individuals process segmented information, with high-NfCC users more likely to rely on initial segments for judgment formation [18]. This has significant implications for digital media consumption, where headline-driven scrolling is the norm and deep engagement with article content is increasingly rare.

The relationship between NfCC and framing susceptibility is not absolute but is moderated by several factors. Efficacy at Fulfilling Need for Closure (EFNC) represents one such moderator, capturing an individual's perceived ability to achieve closure through their preferred epistemic processes [3]. Individuals high in NfCC but also high in EFNC may demonstrate more flexible processing because they feel confident in their ability to achieve closure when needed, reducing the urgency to seize and freeze on initial frames. Conversely, high NfCC combined with low EFNC may produce the greatest framing susceptibility, as individuals feel both a strong need for closure and uncertainty about their ability to achieve it through effortful processing.

Political trust also moderates the relationship between NfCC and framing effects, though evidence on this interaction is mixed. Some studies find strong effects for political trust in explaining belief formation, with pre-pandemic trust and concurrent trust in political and medical institutions strongly negatively related to conspiracy beliefs [4]. However, other research finds no support for a moderating effect of political trust on the relationship between NfCC and belief outcomes [4]. This suggests that while political trust is an important predictor of misinformation acceptance in its own right, its role as a moderator of NfCC effects on framing susceptibility may be context-dependent.

The implications for misinformation intervention are clear. Messages designed to correct misinformation among high-NfCC audiences must account for framing susceptibility by providing clear, structured alternatives that satisfy the need for closure rather than simply presenting contradictory information. Inoculation approaches that pre-expose individuals to framing techniques may be particularly effective, as they build awareness of how frames operate without requiring effortful processing at the moment of exposure. Additionally, message structures that encourage engagement beyond headlines, such as interactive formats or progressive disclosure of information, may help high-NfCC users move beyond initial framing effects.

### Cross-Cultural Differences in Trust and Compliance

The expression and consequences of NfCC vary significantly across cultural contexts, with national differences in institutional trust, governance structures, and social norms shaping how cognitive closure needs translate into misinformation acceptance and compliance behavior. Comparative research between the United States and China provides critical insights into these cross-cultural dynamics, revealing that the relationship between NfCC and pandemic-related outcomes is moderated by cultural context and trust in government.

A study comparing the United States and China examined how NfCC and trust towards government predict pandemic behavior and mental health, collecting data from college students in both nations [9]. The hypothesized model linked NfCC to compliance behavior, depression, and life satisfaction via trust towards government, testing whether the pathways from cognitive closure to behavioral outcomes differ across cultural contexts. This research builds on earlier cross-cultural validation of the Need for Cognitive Closure scale, which compared its structure in Croatia, Italy, the USA, and the Netherlands, establishing that while the construct is measurable across cultures, its expression and correlates may vary [30].

The findings from the US-China comparison reveal important nuances in how NfCC operates within different trust environments:
*   **High Trust Contexts (e.g., China):** In contexts where government trust is high, such as China during the early pandemic period, high NfCC may predict greater compliance with preventive measures because government directives provide the clear, authoritative guidance that high-NfCC individuals seek. The seizing and freezing mechanisms operate in service of compliance when institutional sources are trusted, as individuals can quickly seize upon official guidance and freeze on compliance behaviors without the uncertainty that might arise from conflicting information sources.
*   **Low/Polarized Trust Contexts (e.g., United States):** Conversely, in contexts where government trust is lower or more polarized, such as the United States, high NfCC may predict variable compliance depending on which information sources individuals trust. When institutional trust is fragmented, high-NfCC individuals may seize upon alternative authoritative sources, including political leaders, media outlets, or community figures, leading to divergent compliance patterns based on which sources are deemed credible. This helps explain why NfCC alone is often a weak predictor of specific behaviors without accounting for the trust environment in which those behaviors occur.

Research on NfCC and political trust in the context of COVID-19 conspiracy theories found only small effects for individual differences in NfCC but strong effects for political trust in explaining conspiracy beliefs [4]. This suggests that while NfCC creates susceptibility to certain information processing patterns, the content of what individuals believe is more strongly determined by their trust in political and medical institutions. The interaction between NfCC and political trust may be more complex than simple moderation, with trust serving as a gatekeeper that determines which information sources high-NfCC individuals seize upon and freeze on.

Previous studies have shown that individuals with high NfCC are more likely to follow societal norms, particularly when those norms are perceived as stable and authoritative [9]. This norm-following tendency interacts with cultural context, as what constitutes a societal norm varies across nations. In collectivist cultures with strong emphasis on social harmony and governmental authority, high NfCC may predict greater conformity to official guidance. In individualist cultures with emphasis on personal autonomy and skepticism toward authority, high NfCC may predict conformity to subgroup norms rather than national directives, potentially leading to polarization along political or ideological lines.

The relationship between NfCC and conspiracy beliefs also demonstrates cross-cultural variation. Research has shown that NfCC is positively related to the endorsement of political views that are chronically salient within a given cultural context [8]. When conspiratorial explanations are made salient, NfCC positively predicts beliefs in conspiracies, but this relationship can reverse when alternative explanations are made more accessible. This suggests that cultural context determines which explanations are chronically salient, with high-NfCC individuals in different cultures seizing upon different narrative frameworks based on what is most available and authoritative in their environment.

Trust in medical scientists emerges as another critical cross-cultural variable. Respondents with high trust in medical scientists were more aligned with experts at information assessment tasks, while respondents with high NfCC and those who lean politically conservative were less aligned with experts [1]. This pattern suggests that trust in scientific authority can partially offset the effects of NfCC on misinformation acceptance, but the offsetting effect may vary by cultural context depending on the baseline level of trust in scientific institutions.

The cross-cultural evidence also highlights the importance of distinguishing between memory distortion and belief formation. While NfCC has been shown to augment retrieval-induced forgetting and magnify misinformation effects in eyewitness situations [32], the mechanisms operating in political belief formation and conspiracy endorsement may differ. Cross-cultural research on pandemic compliance focuses on belief formation and behavioral compliance rather than memory accuracy, suggesting that different intervention approaches may be needed for different types of misinformation vulnerability.

The implications for intervention design are significant. Interventions that work in high-trust cultural contexts may fail in low-trust environments, and vice versa. Inoculation approaches like the Bad News game have shown effectiveness across cultures, suggesting that prebunking techniques that build general resistance to manipulation techniques may be more culturally portable than interventions that rely on specific institutional trust [13]. However, the implementation of such interventions must still account for cultural differences in how authority, expertise, and credibility are perceived.

In summary, cross-cultural differences in trust and compliance reveal that NfCC is not a universal predictor of misinformation acceptance but rather a susceptibility factor whose expression is shaped by cultural context, institutional trust, and the availability of authoritative information sources. Effective interventions must account for these contextual dynamics, recognizing that the same cognitive trait can produce divergent outcomes depending on the trust environment in which it operates.

## Intervention Efficacy: Why Education Fails and Inoculation Succeeds

The psychological architecture of Need for Cognitive Closure (NfCC) creates systematic vulnerabilities that render conventional misinformation interventions insufficient for high-closure populations. This section evaluates the comparative efficacy of intervention strategies, distinguishing between approaches that attempt to correct established beliefs and those that build preemptive resistance. The evidence reveals a critical asymmetry: while traditional debiasing and fact-checking struggle against the cognitive mechanisms that sustain misinformation among high-NfCC individuals, inoculation-based prebunking strategies demonstrate cross-cultural efficacy by targeting the underlying epistemic processes rather than specific false claims. Furthermore, emerging research on personalized interventions suggests that accounting for individual differences in Efficacy at Fulfilling Need for Closure (EFNC) and related cognitive traits may unlock more targeted approaches for populations otherwise resistant to standard corrections.

### Limitations of Traditional Debiasing and Fact-Checking

Standard correction methods, including fact-checking and debiasing interventions, face fundamental limitations when deployed against misinformation that has already taken hold, particularly among individuals with high Need for Cognitive Closure. The core challenge stems from the continued influence effect: once people are exposed to a falsehood, it becomes difficult to correct even when the misinformation is explicitly retracted [13]. This phenomenon is not merely a matter of memory failure but reflects deeper epistemic processes wherein corrected information fails to displace the cognitive structure formed during initial exposure.

For high-NfCC individuals, these limitations are compounded by the seizing and freezing dynamics described in lay epistemology theory. When facing uncertainty combined with a conspiracy-salient environment, those high in need for cognitive closure rely on easily accessible explanations and subsequently ignore information arriving at a later stage that suggests alternative possibilities [8][18]. The mechanism is structural: information that contradicts the established cognitive structure is dismissed due to its incongruency with the frozen judgment, not because of its evidentiary weakness. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle where corrections are perceived as threats to epistemic certainty rather than opportunities for belief updating.

Empirical evidence demonstrates that belief regression occurs systematically after correction. While people initially update their beliefs extremely well following misinformation correction, this change is rarely sustained over time, with beliefs returning toward pre-correction levels [7]. For high-NfCC populations, this regression is accelerated by the preference for prototypical information over diagnostic evidence [28]. When confronted with conflicting information, high-closure individuals favor explanations that align with their existing cognitive frameworks, even when those frameworks are based on misinformation.

The interaction between NfCC and other personality traits further undermines educational interventions. Research indicates that education benefits may disappear at high levels of NfCC and narcissism [35]. This finding challenges the assumption that increased information or critical thinking training will uniformly reduce misinformation susceptibility. Instead, for individuals with both high closure needs and narcissistic traits, educational interventions may trigger reactance or be dismissed as irrelevant to their epistemic goals. The limited cognitive perspective and simplistic perception characteristic of high-NfCC individuals are related to preferences for coercive actions in inter-group conflicts, suggesting that corrections perceived as challenging group-aligned beliefs may be actively resisted [25].

Trust characteristics further moderate intervention efficacy. Respondents with high Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) scores, Conscientiousness, and Trust in Medical Scientists were more aligned with experts at information assessment tasks, while respondents with high Need for Cognitive Closure and those who lean politically conservative were less aligned with experts [1]. This divergence indicates that fact-checking from institutional sources may be ineffective or counterproductive for high-NfCC populations who distrust those same institutions. The correction itself becomes evidence of institutional bias rather than factual clarification.

The mechanism of failure is not merely informational but motivational. High-NfCC individuals are more likely to follow societal norms and adhere to perceived ingroup positions [9]. When corrections come from outgroup sources or challenge ingroup-aligned misinformation, the epistemic motivation to maintain closure overrides the accuracy motivation that corrections presume. This explains why debunking efforts often fail to penetrate high-closure echo chambers: the intervention targets the content of belief rather than the epistemic process that sustains it.

### Inoculation Theory and Prebunking Strategies

In contrast to corrective interventions that operate after misinformation exposure, inoculation theory offers a preemptive approach that builds general resistance against manipulation techniques. The psychological theory of inoculation, adapted from biological vaccination models, posits that exposing individuals to weakened doses of misinformation in a safe environment induces a sense of threat and awareness of vulnerability, followed by the opportunity to develop counterarguments [2]. This two-component process—threat induction followed by refutational preemption—creates cognitive antibodies that generalize across misinformation instances rather than targeting specific false claims.

The Bad News game exemplifies this approach as an online interactive intervention where players assume the role of misinformation creators. By exposing players to weakened doses of six common misinformation techniques, the game allows individuals to learn manipulation tactics through active engagement rather than passive correction [2][13]. This active learning component is critical for high-NfCC populations because it provides the cognitive closure of understanding "how the trick works" while simultaneously building resistance to future manipulation attempts. The game satisfies the epistemic need for definitive knowledge about misinformation mechanisms while inoculating against those same mechanisms.

Empirical evidence demonstrates that prebunking interventions based on inoculation theory can reduce susceptibility to misinformation across cultures [12]. This cross-cultural efficacy is particularly significant given that NfCC manifests differently across cultural contexts but consistently predicts susceptibility to simplistic explanations. Social impact games rooted in basic insights from social psychology can boost immunity against misinformation across a variety of cultural, linguistic, and political settings [12]. The generalizability suggests that inoculation targets universal cognitive vulnerabilities rather than culture-specific beliefs.

Harmony Square, produced by the Department of Homeland Security, extends the inoculation approach to political misinformation specifically [2]. Like Bad News, it operates on the principle that there is no better way to inoculate yourself than to walk a mile in the shoes of someone trying to dupe you. This perspective-taking component may be particularly effective for high-NfCC individuals because it provides a structured, rule-based understanding of manipulation tactics that satisfies the need for cognitive structure while undermining the tactics themselves.

However, inoculation interventions face their own limitations. Unlike biological vaccines, psychological inoculation decays over time without regular booster shots, such as retesting or replays [2]. This decay presents a practical challenge for sustained intervention efficacy, particularly for high-NfCC populations who may not voluntarily seek out booster exposures once their initial epistemic need for understanding manipulation tactics is satisfied. The intervention must be designed to maintain engagement or be integrated into recurring information environments to sustain protective effects.

Importantly, while inoculation interventions help people spot fake news, this does not automatically imply they will change sharing behavior or broader attitudes [2]. The distinction between detection and behavioral change is critical: high-NfCC individuals may recognize manipulation techniques yet continue sharing misinformation if doing so serves epistemic or social goals that outweigh accuracy concerns. Inoculation builds cognitive resistance but does not necessarily alter the motivational calculus that drives misinformation engagement.

The comparative advantage of inoculation over correction lies in its timing and target. By intervening before misinformation exposure, inoculation avoids the continued influence effect entirely. By targeting manipulation techniques rather than specific claims, it builds generalizable resistance that does not require endless fact-checking of new false claims. For high-NfCC populations specifically, inoculation provides the cognitive closure of understanding the "rules" of misinformation while simultaneously undermining the epistemic shortcuts that make those populations vulnerable.

### Personalized Interventions Based on Cognitive Traits

The heterogeneity in misinformation susceptibility across individuals necessitates personalized intervention approaches that account for cognitive traits beyond NfCC alone. One-size-fits-all approaches to prevent misinformation uptake and spread may fail if they do not harmonize with the specific needs and motivations of particular populations [1]. This recognition has spurred research into tailored interventions that match intervention type to cognitive profile, particularly distinguishing between individuals with high versus low Efficacy at Fulfilling Need for Closure (EFNC).

EFNC represents a critical moderator that has been underexplored in misinformation intervention research. The EFNC Scale measures a unitary construct that correlates with the ability to use preferred epistemic processes without correlating with constructs representing epistemic content [3]. This distinction is crucial: two individuals may have equally high NfCC, but the one with high EFNC can achieve closure through more sophisticated cognitive processes, while the one with low EFNC must rely on simplistic heuristics. Interventions that ignore this distinction risk misallocating resources or applying inappropriate strategies.

*   **Low EFNC:** For individuals with low EFNC, structured learning interventions that provide clear, rule-based frameworks for evaluating information may be most effective. These individuals lack the cognitive resources to achieve closure through complex reasoning, so interventions must supply external structure that satisfies closure needs while promoting accuracy. The correlation between Need for Cognitive Closure and Narrative Creativity in secondary education suggests that narrative-based interventions may resonate with high-NfCC populations by providing coherent story structures that satisfy epistemic needs [31]. Framing accurate information within compelling narratives may be more effective than presenting decontextualized facts.
*   **High EFNC:** In contrast, individuals with high EFNC may benefit from interventions that leverage their existing capacity for complex cognition. People who have a high need for cognition, which unlike the need for cognitive closure signifies the motivation and preference towards the thinking process, are characterized by a skeptical approach towards prejudices and tend to reflect on substantive issues [31]. For these individuals, interventions that encourage elaboration and critical analysis may be more effective than those that provide ready-made conclusions. The key is distinguishing between NfCC (the desire for closure) and NFC (the enjoyment of thinking), as these traits predict different intervention responses.

Trust characteristics provide another dimension for personalization. Respondents with high Trust in Medical Scientists were more aligned with experts at information assessment tasks, while those with high NfCC were less aligned [1]. This suggests that for high-trust, low-NfCC individuals, institutional fact-checking may be effective, while for high-NfCC, low-trust individuals, alternative credibility sources or peer-based interventions may be necessary. The intervention must match the credibility framework that the target population accepts.

Political trust emerges as a particularly strong moderator. Research finds strong effects for political trust in explaining conspiracy beliefs, with some studies finding no support for a moderating effect of political trust on the NfCC-conspiracy relationship [4]. This suggests that political trust may operate as an independent pathway to misinformation susceptibility rather than merely moderating NfCC effects. Personalized interventions must therefore assess political trust separately from NfCC and tailor credibility appeals accordingly.

Narcissism represents another critical trait for personalization. The finding that education benefits disappear at high levels of NfCC and narcissism indicates that interventions for this subgroup require fundamentally different approaches [35]. For individuals high in both traits, interventions that appeal to uniqueness or superior insight may be more effective than those that emphasize consensus or institutional authority. The intervention must satisfy the narcissistic need for distinctiveness while redirecting it toward accurate information processing.

Cognitive Reflection Test scores provide a measurable indicator for intervention targeting. High CRT scores correlate with reduced misinformation sharing and more favorable intervention attitudes [1]. Screening for CRT performance could enable triage of intervention types: high-CRT individuals may benefit from brief accuracy prompts, while low-CRT individuals may require more intensive inoculation or structured learning interventions. This stratification optimizes resource allocation by matching intervention intensity to cognitive capacity.

The imperative for personalization extends to cultural and contextual factors. Cross-cultural studies of the Need for Cognitive Closure scale reveal structural differences across Croatia, Italy, the USA, and the Netherlands, indicating that NfCC manifests differently across cultural contexts [30]. Personalized interventions must therefore account for cultural variations in how closure needs are expressed and satisfied. What constitutes satisfying cognitive closure in one cultural context may differ substantially in another.

The path forward requires intervention frameworks that assess cognitive traits at the point of delivery and adapt accordingly. Warning tag interventions that are personalized and culturally relevant show promise, as they harmonize with specific needs and motivations rather than imposing uniform solutions [1]. Future research should investigate not only whether cognitive abilities protect against misinformation distortions, but how they do so—by contributing to more detailed encoding, enhancing discrepancy detection, or improving source monitoring [27]. Understanding these mechanisms enables more precise intervention design.

In summary, the evidence supports a clear judgment on intervention efficacy: inoculation-based prebunking strategies outperform traditional education and fact-checking for high-NfCC populations, but optimal outcomes require personalization based on EFNC, trust, narcissism, and cognitive reflection. The intervention landscape must shift from universal corrections to targeted, trait-matched approaches that respect the epistemic motivations driving misinformation susceptibility.

## Synthesis and Strategic Recommendations

This section consolidates the empirical findings on Need for Cognitive Closure (NfCC) as a driver of misinformation acceptance, translating psychological mechanisms into actionable recommendations for policymakers and platform designers, and identifying critical gaps requiring future investigation. The synthesis emphasizes the distinction between memory-based distortion and belief-based adoption, the moderating roles of trust and efficacy, and the differential efficacy of intervention strategies.

### Summary of Psychological Mechanisms and Moderators

The psychological architecture underlying NfCC-driven misinformation susceptibility operates through two primary mechanisms: **seizing** and **freezing**. Individuals high in NfCC exhibit a tendency to seize upon early-encountered information and freeze on that interpretation, resisting subsequent contradictory evidence [26]. This process is rooted in lay epistemology theory, which posits that the need for closure determines the method of information processing, favoring heuristic shortcuts over systematic evaluation [18][20]. The intensity of this need drives individuals to seek clear, certain, and definitive knowledge to reduce the tension resulting from cognitive uncertainty [20].

A critical distinction must be maintained between memory distortion in eyewitness contexts and belief formation in conspiracy or political domains. In eyewitness paradigms, NfCC contributes to retrieval-induced forgetting and misinformation effects through source-monitoring failures, where individuals high in closure needs are more likely to incorporate post-event misinformation into their memory representations [30]. By contrast, in political and conspiracy belief domains, NfCC operates through motivated social cognition, where individuals endorse chronically salient political views or conspiratorial explanations that provide epistemic certainty [8][25]. This distinction matters because legal memory errors involve different corrective mechanisms than political belief adoption, with the latter being more resistant to factual correction due to worldview congruence [15].

Three moderators fundamentally alter the main effect of NfCC on misinformation acceptance:
1.  **Political Trust:** Functions as a protective factor; individuals with high trust in medical scientists and political institutions show greater alignment with expert information even when NfCC is elevated [1]. However, research indicates that pre-pandemic and concurrent trust in political and medical institutions are strongly negatively related to conspiracy beliefs, though some studies find no support for a moderating effect of political trust on the NfCC-conspiracy relationship [4][29]. This suggests trust may operate as an independent predictor rather than a pure moderator in certain contexts.
2.  **Narcissism:** Represents a critical boundary condition where standard mitigation assumptions fail. Evidence indicates that education benefits may disappear at high levels of NfCC and narcissism, as individuals high in both traits exhibit heightened susceptibility to conspiracy beliefs and misinformation regardless of informational interventions [35]. This interaction challenges the assumption that increased education or cognitive ability uniformly protects against misinformation, suggesting that motivational factors can override cognitive resources.
3.  **Efficacy at Fulfilling Need for Closure (EFNC):** Measures an individual's perceived ability to use their preferred epistemic process to achieve closure [3]. This construct correlates only with ability-related measures and does not correlate with epistemic content, distinguishing it from NfCC itself [3]. Individuals with high EFNC may be less vulnerable to misinformation because they feel capable of achieving closure through legitimate means rather than resorting to conspiratorial or heuristic shortcuts. The EFNC scale demonstrates good psychometric properties and represents a unitary construct distinct from the need itself [3].

The mechanism of susceptibility extends beyond simple correlation to explain why NfCC leads to misinformation acceptance. High-NfCC individuals engage in narrow or shallow information searches, relying on incipient cues and first-encountered apparent fit rather than comprehensive evaluation [6][26]. This predisposes them to accept misinformation that arrives early in the information sequence and resist corrections that arrive later, as the cognitive structure formed during the seizing phase becomes resistant to incongruent information [18]. The continued influence of misinformation persists because once false beliefs are formed and considered true, revision becomes a challenging task, with memory sins helping explain the underlying difficulty [15].

### Implications for Policy and Platform Design

Translating psychological insights into actionable recommendations requires distinguishing between interventions that address the mechanism of susceptibility versus those that merely provide corrective information. The evidence supports structural inoculation over reactive correction as the primary strategy for NfCC-driven misinformation vulnerability.

Inoculation theory, operationalized through interventions like the Bad News game, exposes individuals to weakened doses of misinformation in safe gaming environments, inducing a sense of threat and awareness of vulnerability [2][13]. This approach triggers the psychological immune system without overwhelming it, allowing players to engage in simulated misinformation creation and thereby develop resistance to manipulation techniques [2]. The Bad News intervention has demonstrated effectiveness across cultural and political contexts, including Sweden, the United Kingdom, and other international settings [13][12]. This cross-cultural validity is critical for platform design, as misinformation vulnerabilities transcend national boundaries.

Platform designers should integrate prebunking interventions directly into content delivery systems rather than relying on post-hoc fact-checking. Given that individuals high in NfCC freeze on early-encountered information, interventions must arrive before the seizing phase completes [26]. This suggests that warning tags or inoculation messages should appear at the point of initial exposure rather than after misinformation has been consumed and shared. The Cognitive Reflection Test correlates with misinformation sharing and intervention attitudes, indicating that platforms could use brief cognitive assessments to tailor intervention intensity [1]. However, given the narcissism moderator, such tailoring must account for motivational factors that may override cognitive ability [35].

Trust-building represents a parallel strategic priority. Since political trust and trust in medical scientists predict alignment with expert information [1], platforms and policymakers should prioritize transparency and consistency in institutional communication. The comparison between United States and China responses to COVID-19 preventive measures demonstrates that positive perceptions of government implementation predict compliance behavior [9]. This suggests that institutional trust functions as a buffer against NfCC-driven susceptibility, particularly during crisis situations where uncertainty is elevated.

Education-based interventions require careful calibration given the evidence that education benefits may disappear at high levels of NfCC and narcissism [35]. Traditional media literacy programs that assume rational processing may fail for individuals whose motivational needs override cognitive resources. Instead, education should be combined with inoculation techniques that address the emotional and motivational dimensions of closure needs. UNESCO has underscored the importance of up-to-date knowledge, skills, and attitudes in combating misinformation, but these must be delivered through methods that account for epistemic motivation [13].

Corrections are more successful when the corrected misinformation is worldview-discordant rather than worldview-congruent [15]. This has direct implications for platform design: corrections should be framed in ways that minimize identity threat for high-NfCC individuals who may perceive factual challenges as attacks on their epistemic security. Making corrections more distinctive through mental imagery or other memorable techniques can maximize long-term efficacy [15]. Repeated corrections could be an effective strategy given that memory plays a fundamental role in belief regression after misinformation exposure [7].

The explicit judgment on intervention efficacy favors inoculation over education for high-NfCC populations. Inoculation addresses the mechanism of susceptibility by building resistance before exposure, whereas education assumes post-exposure rational processing that high-NfCC individuals may not engage in [13][15]. Social impact games rooted in social psychology insights can boost immunity against misinformation across cultural, linguistic, and political settings [13]. Platforms should prioritize integrating such games into onboarding processes or as optional engagement tools that build resistance proactively.

Policy recommendations should emphasize structural changes over individual-level interventions. Since individuals with high NfCC are more likely to follow societal norms [9], establishing clear normative standards for information quality at the platform level can leverage this tendency toward norm adherence. This includes consistent labeling of misinformation sources, transparent algorithmic processes, and visible enforcement of community standards that signal what constitutes acceptable information behavior.

### Future Research Directions and Unresolved Ambiguities

Several critical gaps in the literature require systematic investigation to advance understanding of NfCC and misinformation susceptibility.

*   **Longitudinal Effects:** Current research relies heavily on cross-sectional designs that capture NfCC and misinformation acceptance at single time points [8][16]. Longitudinal studies are needed to determine whether NfCC-driven susceptibility persists over time, whether interventions like inoculation maintain their protective effects, and how NfCC interacts with repeated exposure to misinformation in digital environments. Future research should examine how NFCC relates to various outcomes beyond cross-sectional correlations, including sentencing judgments, policy preferences, and behavioral compliance over extended periods [16].
*   **Neural Mechanisms:** Neural mechanisms underlying NfCC and misinformation processing remain largely unexplored. While cognitive and neural mechanisms underlying inaccurate, distorted, or false memories have received attention in neuroscience literature [22], the specific neural correlates of NfCC-driven information processing have not been mapped. Research should investigate whether high-NfCC individuals show distinct patterns of activation in prefrontal regions associated with cognitive control and uncertainty processing. The anterior prefrontal cortex and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex are implicated in erroneous recall and false memory formation [22], but their role in NfCC-moderated misinformation acceptance requires direct examination. Functional connectivity studies could reveal whether stable psychological dispositions like NfCC are associated with dynamic changes in cortico-cortical functional connectivity during information evaluation [30].
*   **Trait vs. State in Digital Environments:** The distinction between trait and state NfCC in digital environments represents a fundamental unresolved ambiguity. Most research treats NfCC as a stable dispositional construct [23], but digital environments may induce state-like fluctuations in closure needs through time pressure, information overload, or algorithmic curation. Research should examine whether situational factors in social media platforms temporarily elevate NfCC, making otherwise low-NfCC individuals susceptible to misinformation. The NFCC and Condition scoring indicates that individuals scoring high on the Need for Cognitive Closure Scale are more likely to attempt to draw closure by relying on incipient cues [6], but whether this scoring reflects trait or state remains unclear in digital contexts. Future studies should employ experience sampling methods to capture within-person variation in NfCC across different online contexts.
*   **Emerging Misinformation Formats:** The interaction between NfCC and emerging misinformation formats requires investigation. Current research focuses primarily on text-based misinformation, but visual misinformation, deepfakes, and AI-generated content may interact differently with closure needs. Individuals high in NfCC may be particularly vulnerable to visually compelling misinformation that provides immediate epistemic certainty without requiring cognitive elaboration. Research should test whether the seizing and freezing mechanisms operate similarly across modalities or whether visual information accelerates the freezing process due to reduced cognitive load.
*   **Cross-Cultural Validity:** Cross-cultural validity of NfCC measures and interventions requires expanded testing. While the Bad News game has demonstrated effectiveness across Sweden, the United Kingdom, and other contexts [13], systematic comparison across diverse cultural settings remains limited. The comparison between United States and China on pandemic behavior suggests cultural differences in how trust and closure needs interact [9], but whether NfCC operates identically across collectivist and individualist cultures requires direct examination. Future research should replicate findings in non-Western contexts and test whether interventions require cultural adaptation to maintain efficacy.
*   **Narcissism Moderator Boundary Conditions:** The boundary conditions of the narcissism moderator require clarification. Evidence suggests education benefits disappear at high levels of NfCC and narcissism [35], but the mechanisms underlying this interaction remain unspecified. Research should determine whether narcissism amplifies NfCC effects through defensive motivation, whether it operates independently, or whether the interaction reflects measurement artifacts. Understanding this boundary condition is critical for designing interventions that work across personality profiles rather than only for individuals low in narcissistic traits.
*   **Methodological Improvements:** Methodological improvements are needed to distinguish memory distortion from belief formation in NfCC research. Studies should employ designs that separately measure eyewitness-style memory errors and political belief adoption within the same samples to determine whether NfCC affects these domains through common or distinct mechanisms [15][30]. The Washington Pre-College Test and similar aptitude measures have been investigated for association with eyewitness misinformation susceptibility [17], but parallel measures for belief formation require development and validation.
*   **Cognitive Ability Protections:** Finally, research should investigate how cognitive abilities protect against misinformation distortions, examining whether protection operates through more detailed encoding, enhanced discrepancy detection, or improved source monitoring [27]. Understanding these protective mechanisms can inform intervention design by identifying which cognitive processes to target for individuals high in NfCC. Future research should seek to replicate findings and investigate potential moderators and boundary conditions to understand the psychological predispositions that make people prone to endorse misinformation [8].
