# Cloud-Native Train Control Systems in Urban Rail: Architectures, Safety, and Market Trajectory (2024-2026)

## Executive Summary: The State of Cloud Migration in Urban Rail

The urban rail industry is undergoing a fundamental architectural transformation, shifting from proprietary hardware-based signaling systems to cloud-native, software-defined control architectures. This transition is driven by the need for cost reduction, operational scalability, and enhanced data analytics capabilities. However, the migration path is not uniform across all system functions. A critical distinction exists between Safety-Critical Control systems requiring Safety Integrity Level 4 (SIL 4) certification and Non-Safety Management systems operating at SIL 0-2 levels. Current market data indicates the Train Control and Management Systems (TCMS) market reached USD 3 Billion in 2024 and is projected to reach USD 4.52 Billion by 2032, growing at a 4.85% CAGR [23]. Regional variations show Germany leading Western Europe with an 11.3% CAGR, while Japan maintains a 7.4% growth trajectory [27]. This executive summary synthesizes the current state of cloud migration, safety feasibility assessments, and investment trends shaping the 2024-2032 period.

### Strategic Shift from Legacy Hardware to Software-Defined Control

The urban rail signaling industry is experiencing a paradigm shift from proprietary, hardware-centric architectures to software-defined, cloud-native control systems. Traditional Communications-Based Train Control (CBTC) systems relied on dedicated wayside equipment, physical interlocking hardware, and closed proprietary networks operating on technologies such as GSM-Railway (GSM-R) for voice and low-data-rate signaling or Wi-Fi-based networks in the 2.4 GHz frequency band [10][14]. These legacy systems, while proven over decades of operation, present significant limitations in scalability, maintenance costs, and integration with modern data analytics platforms.

The transition to software-defined control is fundamentally reshaping how train control functions are deployed and managed. Siemens Mobility's Train2Cloud architecture exemplifies this shift, enabling efficient CBTC system management through high-performance Commercial Off-The-Shelf (COTS) servers deployed in on-premises signaling data centers [15]. This approach facilitates easier system upgrades, reduced hardware footprint, and improved lifecycle management compared to traditional distributed wayside equipment. Similarly, Huawei's Urban Rail Cloud Solution integrates cloud computing, big data, 5G, and artificial intelligence to build a unified Horizon Digital Platform for urban rail operations [28]. Deployments in cities with existing large-scale multi-line networks—including Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Shenzhen, and Wuhan—demonstrate the scalability of cloud-native architectures across complex operational environments [28].

The primary drivers accelerating this architectural transition include:

- **Cost Reduction**: Cloud-native architectures reduce capital expenditure on proprietary hardware and enable more efficient resource utilization through virtualization and centralized processing.
- **Scalability**: Software-defined systems allow operators to scale computing resources dynamically based on operational demands without physical hardware expansion.
- **Data Analytics Integration**: Centralized cloud platforms enable advanced analytics, predictive maintenance, and operational optimization that were impractical with distributed legacy systems.
- **Interoperability**: Open standards-based cloud architectures facilitate integration across multiple vendor systems and legacy infrastructure.

Hitachi Rail's HMAX platform demonstrates an edge-to-cloud architecture where critical data is processed locally on-board via NVIDIA IGX hardware, while summary analytics are transmitted to cloud infrastructure [23]. This hybrid approach balances the latency requirements of safety-critical functions with the analytical capabilities of cloud computing. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated adoption of cloud-based command systems as on-site maintenance and physical monitoring became limited, forcing railway operators to resort to sophisticated simulation and remote-control capabilities [29].

However, the migration from legacy hardware introduces significant technical challenges. Many older networks lack foundational digital security frameworks, making upgrades difficult and requiring careful integration strategies to ensure end-to-end protection across mixed legacy and modern systems [23]. The convergence of new Operational Technology (OT) systems with existing Information Technology (IT) infrastructure requires careful network design and a clear digital resilience strategy, particularly when the same physical network is shared across multiple systems with many stakeholders [53].

### Key Findings on Safety-Critical Cloud Feasibility

The feasibility of deploying cloud infrastructure for safety-critical train control functions represents the most significant technical and regulatory challenge in the cloud migration journey. A clear maturity gap exists between cloud-based maintenance analytics (typically SIL 0-2) and cloud-based safety control systems requiring SIL 4 certification. This distinction is critical for accurate risk assessment and regulatory compliance.

**Regulatory Framework Transition: EN 50128 to EN 50716:2023**

The regulatory baseline for railway software safety underwent a fundamental change in November 2023 when EN 50128 was withdrawn and replaced by EN 50716:2023 [40]. This transition has significant implications for cloud-native system certification:

- **Basic Integrity Requirements**: Unlike EN 50128, EN 50716 requires that at least one quality assurance process is applied to all non-safety-related functions, previously categorized under SIL 0 but now considered to have "Basic Integrity" [12][24].
- **SIL 4 Testing Requirements**: At SW-SIL 4, dynamic testing with comprehensive coverage is mandatory, with Clause 6.7.4.2 stating clearly that "The selection of the tools in classes T2 used for SIL 1 to SIL 4 and T3 used for SIL 1 to SIL 4 shall be justified" [40].
- **Assessor Requirements**: EN 50716 requires that an assessor be assigned for SIL 1 to SIL 4 systems, while an assessor is no longer required for basic integrity functions [24].

**Latency Constraints for Cloud-Based Movement Authority**

Latency represents the primary technical constraint for real-time train control functions, particularly for Movement Authority (MA) calculations. CBTC systems centrally manage the movement authority of a train, and this function is highly dependent on the Train-to-Ground (T2G) communication system [44]. While specific end-to-end latency targets vary by implementation, industry analysis indicates that cloud-based Movement Authority calculations require latency in the 40-80ms range to maintain safe train separation and braking distances.

Huawei's AirFlash 5G Train-to-Ground Data Transfer Solution features high bandwidth and low latency capabilities designed to address these challenges [28]. However, the feasibility of achieving consistent sub-100ms latency in production cloud environments remains a subject of ongoing validation. Data communication in moving block systems relies on continuous bidirectional radio links between the train and the wayside control center to exchange critical information [10]. Any interruption or latency spike in this communication channel directly impacts the safety case for cloud-based control.

**SIL 4 Cloud Certification Status**

The question of whether cloud infrastructure can achieve SIL 4 certification is actively being addressed by the industry. Research indicates emerging milestones in this area, with organizations exploring whether virtualization platforms and hypervisors can meet the deterministic behavior requirements of SIL 4 systems [48]. However, the current state of deployment shows a clear differentiation:

- **Non-Safety Management Systems (SIL 0-2)**: Cloud deployment is mature and widely adopted for functions including predictive maintenance analytics, passenger information systems, energy management, and operational dashboards. These systems benefit from cloud scalability without the stringent determinism requirements of safety control.
- **Safety-Critical Control (SIL 4)**: Full cloud deployment for core safety functions remains limited. Most implementations use hybrid architectures where safety-critical processing occurs on certified on-board or wayside hardware, with cloud infrastructure supporting non-safety functions and data aggregation.

The Tolerable Functional Failure Rate for SIL 4 systems ranges from 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁶ per hour, requiring extremely high reliability that challenges traditional cloud architectures designed for availability rather than safety integrity [25]. Functional safety relies on active systems to respond to dangers—for example, if a sensor fails, the system must detect it and take actions such as halting the train to prevent accidents [12].

**Cybersecurity: Zero Trust for IT/OT Convergence**

As cloud migration increases the attack surface of rail control systems, cybersecurity architecture must evolve beyond traditional perimeter-based security. The Zero Trust framework has emerged as the standard for protecting cloud-connected rail systems, particularly given the convergence of IT and OT environments.

The Cloud Security Alliance published guidance in October 2024 examining the application of Zero Trust principles for critical infrastructure, bridging the gap between traditional information technology security methodologies and the unique requirements of operational technology [5]. Key findings include:

- **Zero Trust is Paramount**: Zero-Trust architecture is paramount to ensuring business continuity and railway safety, particularly as railway systems become more attractive targets for cyberattackers [4].
- **IT/OT Convergence Challenges**: The associated convergence of new OT systems with existing IT systems requires careful network design and a clear digital resilience strategy [53].
- **CLC/TS 50701:2021**: This technical specification provides the cybersecurity framework for railway applications, with the second edition published in August 2023 aiming to ensure that bad actors cannot compromise railway systems [12].

Zero Trust architecture helps reduce the impact of cyberattackers by performing fully passive authentication and validation of all communications to detect attempts at lateral movement and minimize the risk of privilege escalation [4]. This is particularly critical for CBTC signaling systems that exist in signaling and system-to-system communications environments [4].

## Architectural Definitions: Distinguishing Control, Management, and Maintenance

The transition to cloud-native architectures in urban rail represents a fundamental restructuring of how train control, operational management, and maintenance functions are distributed across computing environments. This architectural shift is not uniform; safety-critical control functions remain constrained by latency and certification requirements that limit full cloud migration, while operational management and maintenance analytics increasingly leverage centralized cloud infrastructure. Understanding these distinctions is essential for accurate risk assessment and realistic deployment planning. The regulatory landscape has evolved significantly, with EN 50128 withdrawn in November 2023 and replaced by EN 50716:2023 as the current baseline for software safety certification [40]. This transition reflects the industry's recognition that traditional software safety standards require adaptation for virtualized and cloud-connected environments.

### Safety-Critical Train Control (CBTC) vs. Operational Management (ATS)

The technical distinction between Communications-Based Train Control (CBTC) systems and Automatic Train Supervision (ATS) platforms defines the boundary between cloud-viable and edge-constrained functions. CBTC encompasses safety-critical subsystems including Automatic Train Protection (ATP), which must operate at Safety Integrity Level 4 (SIL 4), the highest certification tier for railway software [25]. ATP functions calculate movement authority, enforce speed restrictions, and initiate emergency braking when collision risks are detected. These operations demand deterministic response times and cannot tolerate the variable latency inherent in public cloud infrastructure.

In contrast, ATS functions operate at significantly lower safety integrity levels, typically SIL 0 to SIL 2, classified under EN 50716:2023 as having "Basic Integrity" rather than full safety-critical status [40][24]. ATS manages train scheduling, dwell time optimization, passenger information systems, and operational reporting. These functions are data-intensive but not safety-critical, making them suitable candidates for cloud deployment where scalability and analytical processing capabilities outweigh latency concerns.

The latency constraint for safety-critical movement authority calculations represents the primary technical barrier to cloud-based ATP. End-to-end communication cycles for CBTC systems typically require 40-80ms response times to maintain safe train separation at operational speeds [10]. This window encompasses radio transmission, wayside processing, movement authority computation, and confirmation back to the onboard unit. GSM-Railway (GSM-R) and Wi-Fi-based networks in the 2.4GHz band facilitate continuous bidirectional radio links between train and wayside control centers, but introducing cloud-based processing layers adds network hops that risk exceeding these thresholds [10]. Research indicates that CBTC systems centrally manage movement authority, and damage to a single communication node can cascade through the network, emphasizing the need for localized processing resilience [44].

Hitachi Rail's HMAX platform exemplifies the architectural compromise required for safety-critical functions. Deployed across over 2,000 trains and thousands of kilometers of signaling infrastructure, HMAX uses an edge-to-cloud architecture where critical data is processed locally on-board via NVIDIA IGX hardware, while summary analytics are transmitted to cloud environments for aggregation [23]. This separation ensures that ATP calculations remain within certified latency bounds while still enabling cloud-based fleet optimization and predictive maintenance.

The regulatory implications of this distinction are substantial. EN 50716:2023 requires that at least one quality assurance process be applied to all non-safety-related functions, a requirement that did not exist under EN 50128 for SIL 0 systems [12]. This change acknowledges that even non-safety functions in cloud-connected rail systems can impact overall system integrity through cascading failures or cybersecurity vulnerabilities. Independent Safety Assessors must now evaluate the entire software lifecycle, including cloud-based components that interface with safety-critical systems [9].

Maintenance analytics represent a third category distinct from both CBTC and ATS. Functions such as hot axle box detection, lateral acceleration monitoring, and predictive component failure analysis operate at SIL 2 or below but require substantial data processing capabilities [25]. These functions benefit significantly from cloud deployment where machine learning models can aggregate data across entire fleets to identify degradation patterns invisible at the individual train level. The maturity gap between cloud-based maintenance analytics and cloud-based safety control remains significant; conflating these categories leads to inaccurate risk assessments and unrealistic deployment timelines.

### The Edge-Cloud Continuum in Rail Infrastructure

Rail infrastructure architecture increasingly follows a hybrid model where latency-sensitive functions remain at the edge while data-intensive processing migrates to cloud environments. This edge-cloud continuum is not a binary choice but a spectrum of deployment options determined by function criticality, data volume, and regulatory constraints. The architectural decision matrix must account for communication bandwidth, processing determinism, cybersecurity posture, and certification requirements simultaneously.

Siemens Mobility's Train2Cloud architecture demonstrates one implementation of this continuum. The system enables efficient CBTC management through high-performance Commercial Off-The-Shelf (COTS) servers deployed in on-premises signaling data centers rather than public cloud infrastructure [15]. This approach maintains the latency characteristics required for safety-critical functions while leveraging virtualization benefits such as resource pooling, simplified maintenance, and scalable compute capacity. The Signaling X platform integrates data center capabilities directly into the wayside architecture, reducing the physical footprint of traditional interlocking equipment while preserving deterministic response characteristics.

Huawei's Urban Rail Cloud Solution represents a more centralized approach, leveraging cloud computing, big data, 5G, and artificial intelligence to build a unified Horizon Digital Platform for urban rail operations [28]. Deployed in cities including Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Shenzhen, and Wuhan, this architecture streamlines production, management, and service subsystems through a common cloud infrastructure. The Huawei AirFlash 5G Train-to-Ground Data Transfer Solution provides high bandwidth and low latency communication channels, uploading onboard CCTV videos and monitoring data to centralized analytics platforms [28]. However, safety-critical movement authority calculations remain segregated from this cloud infrastructure, processed at the edge to maintain certification compliance.

The convergence of Operational Technology (OT) and Information Technology (IT) systems introduces distinct network design challenges. When the same physical network carries both safety-critical signaling traffic and non-safety operational data, careful segmentation and digital resilience strategies become essential [53]. Ethernet/IP protocols now transmit data in on-board train control applications, but this convergence requires isolation mechanisms to prevent non-critical traffic from interfering with safety-critical communication cycles [53]. The associated convergence of new OT systems with existing IT systems requires clear architectural boundaries, particularly when multiple stakeholders share network infrastructure.

Cybersecurity considerations fundamentally shape edge-cloud architecture decisions. Traditional perimeter security models prove inadequate for cloud-connected rail systems where attack surfaces expand across distributed infrastructure. The Cloud Security Alliance has published guidance examining Zero Trust principles for critical infrastructure, bridging the gap between traditional IT security methodologies and the unique requirements of OT environments [5]. Zero Trust architecture assumes no implicit trust for any network connection, requiring continuous verification of identity, device integrity, and access permissions regardless of network location.

For railway applications, Zero Trust implementation requires specific adaptations. The NIST framework highlights core Zero Trust components including identity management, device authentication, and micro-segmentation [4]. In rail contexts, this translates to verifying every communication between onboard units, wayside equipment, and cloud services, even when originating from within the operator's network perimeter. Regulatory compliance increasingly drives Zero Trust adoption, with 47% of organizations citing alignment with cybersecurity regulations as a top strategic driver [38].

The edge-cloud continuum also affects maintenance and update procedures. Cloud-based systems enable over-the-air software updates and remote diagnostics, but safety-critical components require certified update processes that maintain SIL 4 compliance throughout the modification cycle. EN 50716:2023 Section 9.2 mandates that regression testing is not optional during software maintenance, requiring documentation of test re-execution and reuse of updated tests during re-validation [40]. Given that railway systems often operate for more than 20 years, this lifecycle view necessitates architecture that supports long-term maintainability without compromising safety certification.

### Virtualization of Wayside and Onboard Systems

The shift from physical interlocking hardware to virtualized signaling data centers represents one of the most significant architectural transformations in modern rail infrastructure. Traditional wayside equipment comprised dedicated hardware for each signaling function—interlocking controllers, track circuit processors, and communication gateways—each requiring physical installation, power, cooling, and maintenance access. Virtualization consolidates these functions onto standardized server platforms, reducing physical footprint while enabling software-defined configuration and resource allocation.

Siemens Mobility's approach utilizes on-premises signaling data centers with high-performance COTS servers to host virtualized CBTC functions [15]. This architecture enables operators to manage CBTC systems more efficiently while maintaining the deterministic characteristics required for safety certification. The virtualization layer must provide temporal isolation between functions, ensuring that non-critical processes cannot interfere with safety-critical execution timelines. Hypervisor technologies certified for railway applications provide this isolation, partitioning compute resources so that SIL 4 functions execute with guaranteed CPU cycles and memory access regardless of system load.

Onboard compute virtualization follows similar principles but faces additional constraints related to power, cooling, vibration, and space. Hitachi Rail's HMAX platform processes critical data locally on-board via NVIDIA IGX hardware, demonstrating how edge computing capabilities enable sophisticated onboard analytics without cloud dependency [23]. QNX real-time operating systems remain relevant for onboard control units where deterministic response is mandatory [23]. The combination of QNX for safety-critical functions and containerized applications for non-safety services enables mixed-criticality systems on shared hardware platforms.

Virtual interlocking systems eliminate the need for physical relay-based interlocking equipment, replacing it with software implementations running on redundant server clusters. This transition reduces maintenance requirements and enables remote configuration changes without physical site visits. However, virtual interlocking must maintain the same safety integrity as physical systems, requiring rigorous verification that virtualization layers do not introduce failure modes absent in hardware implementations. Independent Safety Assessors evaluate the entire virtualization stack, from hypervisor to application layer, ensuring that safety cases remain valid in virtualized environments [9].

The certification implications of virtualization extend beyond individual components to system-level interactions. EN 50716:2023 addresses software in virtualized and containerized environments, providing guidance on redundancy, determinism, and resilience requirements for SIL 4 systems [48]. Tool qualification becomes more complex when development and testing tools operate in cloud environments, requiring verification that tool chains do not introduce unverified code or compromise traceability requirements. Jama Software and similar Application Lifecycle Management platforms have achieved TÜV SÜD certification for developing safety-related products, demonstrating that cloud-based development tools can meet railway safety standards when properly configured [31].

Onboard virtualization also enables function consolidation, reducing the number of physical compute units required per train. Traditional architectures deployed separate controllers for traction, braking, doors, and passenger information systems. Virtualized architectures consolidate these functions onto shared compute platforms, reducing weight, power consumption, and maintenance complexity. The Multifunction Vehicle Bus (MVB) and Train Communication Network (TCN) protocols facilitate communication between virtualized functions, but network segmentation ensures that safety-critical traffic maintains priority over non-critical data flows [25].

The transition to virtualized architectures requires substantial investment in testing infrastructure. Model-based development and hardware-in-the-loop simulation enable comprehensive validation of virtualized systems before deployment, but these capabilities require significant upfront investment in simulation platforms and test automation [13]. Organizations implementing virtualization must establish competency in software quality, security, and compliance disciplines that differ substantially from traditional hardware-focused railway engineering [39].

Looking forward, the virtualization trajectory points toward greater software-defined functionality across both wayside and onboard domains. However, the pace of migration remains constrained by certification timelines, legacy system interoperability requirements, and the need to maintain operational continuity during transition periods. Systems designed with SIL compliance early face fewer redesign and recertification costs in the future, making forward-looking architecture decisions critical for long-term asset value [25]. The industry's experience with virtualization in adjacent sectors, including avionics and automotive, provides transferable lessons but railway's unique safety and regulatory requirements necessitate domain-specific validation approaches.

## Core Technology Stack: 5G, Edge Computing, and Virtualization

The transition to cloud-native train control systems represents a fundamental architectural shift in urban rail signaling, driven by three converging technology pillars: 5G communications, edge computing, and virtualization. These technologies collectively enable the migration from proprietary, hardware-bound signaling equipment to software-defined, cloud-orchestrated control systems. However, the technical feasibility of this transition hinges on meeting stringent performance metrics, particularly for safety-critical functions where latency thresholds and bandwidth requirements cannot be compromised. Research evidence indicates that end-to-end latencies in the range of 40-80ms are critical for cloud-processed movement authorities, while network slicing provides the logical isolation necessary to guarantee quality of service for critical communications [28][19]. This section examines the technical specifications, performance constraints, and integration challenges associated with each technology layer, with explicit distinction between Safety-Critical Control (SIL 4) and Non-Safety Management (SIL 0-2) applications throughout.

### 5G Network Slicing for Critical Communications

Fifth-generation (5G) wireless technology has emerged as the foundational communication layer for next-generation Communications-Based Train Control (CBTC) systems, offering capabilities that substantially exceed those of legacy WLAN and LTE networks. The critical innovation enabling 5G adoption in rail signaling is network slicing, which creates dedicated, low-latency logical networks within a shared physical infrastructure. This capability allows operators to allocate specific network resources to train-ground communication while isolating safety-critical traffic from non-essential data flows such as passenger Wi-Fi or onboard entertainment systems [19].

Network slicing addresses a fundamental limitation of previous wireless technologies in rail applications: the inability to guarantee consistent quality of service under varying load conditions. In traditional shared networks, congestion from non-critical traffic can introduce unpredictable latency spikes that compromise the deterministic behavior required for train control. 5G slicing customizes each slice for specific service requirements by devoting appropriate resources to each slice, with the service characteristics determining the slice configuration [19]. For CBTC applications, this means establishing ultra-reliable low-latency communication (URLLC) slices that prioritize movement authority updates, train position reports, and emergency stop commands over all other traffic.

The Huawei AirFlash 5G Train-to-Ground Data Transfer Solution exemplifies the practical implementation of this architecture, featuring high bandwidth and low latency characteristics specifically engineered for urban rail environments [28]. In the Shenzhen Metro deployment, this solution leverages cloud computing, big data, 5G, and artificial intelligence to build a unified Horizon Digital Platform for urban rail operations. The system supports bidirectional train-ground communications essential for CBTC functionality, where the signaling system centrally manages the movement authority (MA) of each train [44]. Damage to a single communication link in such architectures can have cascading effects, making the redundancy and isolation provided by network slicing particularly valuable.

Major signaling vendors have announced integration of CBTC with 5G communications as part of their cloud-native product roadmaps. Hitachi Rail has announced integration of Communications Based Train Control with 5G communications, positioning this capability as central to their next-generation signaling offerings [23]. Similarly, Nokia focuses on 5G-enabled, mission-critical communication in railways, working through partnerships with OEMs and infrastructure vendors to provide scalable, future-proof connectivity supporting advanced signaling [29]. These developments align with the broader industry transition toward FRMCS (Future Railway Mobile Communication System), which is enabled by 5G technology and represents the successor to GSM-R for mainline applications [29].

The cybersecurity implications of 5G-based train-ground communication require careful attention, particularly given the convergence of operational technology (OT) and information technology (IT) systems. Traditional perimeter security models are inadequate for cloud-connected rail systems where the attack surface extends beyond internal security perimeters to include external cloud services, data processing platforms, and third-party communication infrastructure [34]. The Cloud Security Alliance has examined considerations and application of Zero Trust principles for critical infrastructure, bridging the gap between traditional IT security methodologies and the unique requirements of OT/ICS/IoT environments [5]. For 5G network slicing in rail applications, Zero Trust architecture requires continuous authentication and validation of all communications to detect attempts at lateral movement and minimize privilege escalation risks [4].

It is essential to distinguish between safety-critical and non-safety applications when evaluating 5G slicing capabilities. While 5G URLLC slices can theoretically support SIL 4 functions, current certification pathways remain limited. The regulatory baseline for all software safety discussions has transitioned from EN 50128 to EN 50716:2023, with the predecessor standard withdrawn in November 2023 [40]. EN 50716 introduces basic integrity requirements for non-safety-related functions (previously SIL 0), while maintaining SIL 1 through SIL 4 classifications for safety-critical applications [24][25]. This distinction is critical because cloud-based maintenance analytics (SIL 0-2) have achieved significantly greater maturity than cloud-based safety control (SIL 4), and conflating these capability levels leads to inaccurate risk assessments.

### Latency Management and Real-Time Performance

Latency represents the primary technical constraint for real-time train control in cloud-native architectures, with end-to-end delay thresholds directly determining the feasibility of cloud-processed movement authority calculations. Research evidence identifies specific latency ranges of 40-80ms as critical benchmarks for CBTC systems where movement authorities are computed in centralized or cloud-based locations rather than on wayside equipment [28]. These thresholds are not arbitrary but derive from the fundamental physics of train braking distances, reaction times, and the continuous bidirectional radio links required between trains and control centers [10].

The relationship between latency and train headways is direct and quantifiable. In moving block CBTC systems, the minimum safe separation between trains depends on the time required to transmit position updates, compute movement authorities, and deliver braking commands. Any increase in end-to-end latency necessitates larger safety buffers, which reduces line capacity and undermines one of the primary benefits of CBTC deployment. GSM-Railway facilitates transmission of movement authority updates but operates at lower data rates compared to 5G-based solutions, creating bandwidth constraints that become increasingly problematic as train densities increase [10]. The Huawei AirFlash solution addresses these constraints through high bandwidth and low latency characteristics, enabling the transmission of larger data volumes including onboard CCTV videos and monitoring data alongside control messages [28].

Edge computing architectures have emerged as a critical mitigation strategy for latency management in cloud-native train control. Rather than processing all control functions in centralized data centers, edge computing distributes computation closer to the point of action, reducing propagation delays and network congestion. Hitachi Rail's HMAX platform exemplifies this approach, deployed on over 2,000 trains and across thousands of kilometers of signaling infrastructure using an edge-to-cloud architecture where critical data is processed locally on-board via NVIDIA IGX hardware, while summary analytics are sent to cloud platforms [23]. This hybrid model preserves the low-latency response required for safety-critical functions while leveraging cloud capabilities for non-real-time analytics and maintenance functions.

Siemens Mobility's Train2Cloud architecture takes a different approach, enabling efficient CBTC system management through high-performance COTS (Commercial Off-The-Shelf) servers in on-premises signaling data centers [15]. This architecture facilitates easy scaling and reduces the physical footprint of wayside equipment while maintaining the deterministic performance required for train control. The Signaling X platform, based on Train2Cloud, represents Siemens' next-generation CBTC system optimized for metro operations, delivering comprehensive solutions that balance cloud flexibility with real-time performance requirements [15].

The distinction between safety-critical and non-safety latency requirements becomes particularly important when evaluating cloud processing feasibility. EN 50716 governs software safety for control and protection systems, specifying techniques for coding, testing, and validation based on Safety Integrity Level [9]. For SIL 4 functions such as movement authority calculation and emergency braking, latency must be not only low but also deterministic and bounded with high confidence. Non-safety functions such as passenger information systems, energy optimization, or predictive maintenance analytics can tolerate higher and more variable latencies without compromising safety. This differentiation allows architects to deploy hybrid architectures where safety-critical processing occurs on certified edge platforms while non-safety analytics leverage centralized cloud resources.

QNX and similar real-time operating systems remain relevant for real-time operating needs in train control applications, providing the deterministic scheduling and interrupt handling required for safety-critical functions [23]. These systems can be deployed in virtualized environments using type-1 hypervisors that provide temporal and spatial isolation between safety-critical and non-safety workloads. The technical challenge lies in certifying these virtualization layers to SIL 4 standards under EN 50716, which requires demonstrating that hypervisor failures cannot propagate to safety-critical guest operating systems.

Ongoing maintenance and cybersecurity add to the economic burden of low-latency architectures, with resilient wireless networks and redundancy measures necessary to ensure ultra-reliable communication for moving block functionality, potentially increasing operational costs by 20-30% compared to traditional signaling systems [10]. These costs must be weighed against the capacity improvements and operational flexibility that cloud-native architectures enable. Regional analysis indicates that North America's market growth is driven by strong investments in automated signaling, advanced traffic management, and predictive maintenance programs, suggesting that operators view these technology investments as economically justified despite the additional complexity [23].

### Digital Twins and Predictive Maintenance Integration

Digital twin technology has emerged as a transformative capability within the cloud-native rail ecosystem, enabling operators to simulate rail operations, optimize maintenance schedules, and predict equipment failures before they impact service. In railway maintenance, digital twins serve as a platform for real-time monitoring, predictive simulations, and maintenance planning, with their strength lying in combining data with engineering knowledge to allow scenario testing and intervention optimization [34]. This capability is particularly valuable for cloud-native architectures where the separation of control functions from physical hardware creates opportunities for continuous simulation and validation without disrupting live operations.

The integration of digital twins with predictive maintenance features within Train Control and Management Systems is accelerating rapidly as networks grow more digital [23]. ## The Safety Imperative: Navigating EN 50716 and SIL 4 Certification

The migration of train control systems to cloud-native architectures introduces unprecedented regulatory complexity. As urban rail operators pursue digital transformation through centralized computing platforms, the safety certification framework governing railway software has undergone fundamental restructuring. The November 2023 withdrawal of EN 50128 and its consolidation into EN 50716:2023 represents the most significant regulatory shift in railway software safety in over two decades [40]. This transition coincides with industry efforts to deploy Safety Integrity Level 4 (SIL 4) functions on commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) cloud infrastructure, creating tension between established safety paradigms and emerging virtualization technologies. For cloud-native train control systems, compliance is no longer merely a documentation exercise but a structural constraint that shapes architectural decisions from hypervisor selection to network topology.

### Transitioning from EN 50128 to EN 50716:2023

The consolidation of EN 50128 (software for railway control and protection systems) and EN 50657 (railway applications – communication, signaling and processing systems – software for railway control and protection systems) into the unified EN 50716:2023 standard fundamentally alters the compliance landscape for cloud-native development teams [24]. Both predecessor standards were withdrawn in November 2023, establishing EN 50716 as the current regulatory baseline for all software safety discussions in railway applications [40]. While EN 50716 is not retrospective, all upgrades and maintenance activities initiated after its publication must align with its requirements, forcing organizations to balance legacy system maintenance with new development under a unified framework [40].

The structural alignment between EN 50716:2023 and its predecessor EN 50128:2011 preserves core safety principles while introducing clarifications critical for modern development methodologies. Analysis of the Swiss versions of both standards reveals that a substantial portion of text has been revised, though the fundamental safety integrity level (SIL) classification structure remains intact [24]. The abbreviation "INT" (Integrator) has been deleted from the standard, reflecting evolved understanding of role responsibilities in complex software supply chains [24]. For non-safety-related functions, EN 50716 stipulates the use of at least one quality assurance process, with "basic integrity" requirements serving as the minimum threshold for software not classified under SIL 1-4 [24].

EN 50716 governs software safety for control and protection systems, which is essential in an era where software drives signalling, diagnostics, and automation across urban rail networks [17]. The standard operates within the broader CENELEC framework alongside EN 50126 (RAMS) and EN 50129 (safety-related electronic systems for signaling), forming what industry practitioners refer to as the "CENELEC Trio" that defines railway safety certification [9]. EN 50129 specifically outlines how to develop robust Safety Cases for electronic signalling systems, forming the foundation for approvals and audits that cloud-native architectures must satisfy [16].

For railway software teams navigating the EN 50716 transition, the question has shifted from whether to automate verification and validation to whether certification deadlines can be met without automation [40]. This pressure is particularly acute for cloud-native implementations where continuous integration and deployment pipelines must demonstrate compliance with standards originally designed for waterfall development methodologies. The Software Quality Assurance Plan now governs railway software development and verification activities under EN 50716, requiring organizations to establish defined processes for development and production with detailed end-to-end traceability from high-level user needs to validation and verification [40][31].

The transition also clarifies the distinction between safety-critical control functions (SIL 4) and non-safety management functions (SIL 0-2), a differentiation critical for cloud capability assessments. Research evidence indicates a significant maturity gap between cloud-based maintenance analytics operating at SIL 0-2 and cloud-based safety control requiring SIL 4 certification [7]. Conflating these categories leads to inaccurate risk assessments, as the certification burden, development methodologies, and architectural constraints differ substantially between SIL 2 implementations (commonly used for diagnostic and monitoring systems) and SIL 4 implementations (required for movement authority and train protection functions) [7][25].

### Challenges in Achieving SIL 4 in Virtualized Environments

Deploying SIL 4 functions on commercial off-the-shelf cloud hardware presents technical and certification hurdles that distinguish safety-critical control from non-safety management workloads. The fundamental challenge lies in demonstrating that virtualized environments can meet the determinism, redundancy, and resilience requirements that SIL 4 certification demands [48]. High-integrity systems developed within virtualized and containerized environments must demonstrate compliance with CENELEC standards while operating on infrastructure not originally designed for safety-critical applications [32].

Latency represents the primary technical constraint for real-time train control in cloud-native architectures. For cloud-based Movement Authority calculations, end-to-end latency must remain within the 40-80ms window to maintain safe train separation and response times [28]. This requirement creates tension with cloud architectures that prioritize throughput and elasticity over deterministic response times. The Huawei AirFlash 5G Train-to-Ground Data Transfer Solution features high bandwidth and low latency capabilities specifically designed to address these challenges in urban rail cloud implementations [28]. However, achieving consistent sub-80ms latency across distributed cloud infrastructure requires careful network design, edge computing placement, and quality of service guarantees that traditional enterprise cloud deployments do not provide.

The CBTC system centrally manages the movement authority (MA) of a train, making it highly dependent on the train-to-ground communication system [44]. Damage or degradation of a single communication link can compromise movement authority updates, which rely on continuous bidirectional radio links between the train and the wayside control center to exchange critical information [10]. Technologies such as GSM-Railway (GSM-R) for voice and low-data-rate signaling or Wi-Fi-based networks in the 2.4 GHz band facilitate transmission of movement authority updates, but cloud-native implementations must demonstrate equivalent or superior reliability [10]. Antennas receive movement authorities, and the scale of deployment represents a fundamental transformation of how the system operates, touching nearly every aspect of train control and operations [14].

Virtualization introduces additional complexity through hypervisor certification requirements. PikeOS, a separation kernel hypervisor with hardware virtualization support on x86 architecture, represents one approach to achieving SIL 4 certification in virtualized environments [48]. The hypervisor must provide temporal and spatial partitioning that prevents faults in non-safety workloads from affecting SIL 4 functions, while maintaining the deterministic behavior required for train control applications. Linux-based virtualization solutions face additional scrutiny, as general-purpose operating systems were not designed with the fault containment requirements that SIL 4 demands [48].

The distinction between Safety-Critical Control (SIL 4) and Non-Safety Management (SIL 0-2) becomes particularly important when evaluating cloud capabilities. Train control systems ensure control of subsystems including doors, brakes, and traction, all of which require SIL 4 certification for safety-related functions [13]. In contrast, maintenance analytics, passenger information systems, and operational dashboards typically operate at SIL 0-2, where cloud deployment presents fewer certification barriers [7]. This differentiation allows operators to pursue hybrid architectures where non-safety functions leverage public or multi-tenant cloud infrastructure while safety-critical functions remain on certified, isolated platforms.

Functional safety relies on active systems to respond to dangers, and EN 50716 supercedes previous standards in defining how these systems must behave in virtualized environments [12]. For example, if a sensor fails, the system must detect it and take actions such as halting the train to prevent accidents [12]. In cloud-native architectures, this failure detection and response mechanism must operate across virtual machine boundaries, container orchestration layers, and potentially distributed data centers, creating verification challenges that traditional embedded systems do not face.

### Tool Qualification and Verification Requirements

EN 50716 mandates tool classification and tool qualification for offline software tools used throughout software development, with tools categorized into three distinct classes based on their potential to introduce errors into safety-critical code [32]. Class T3 tools contribute output directly to executable code, including compilers and code generators, requiring the highest level of qualification evidence [39]. Class T2 tools support the test or verification of the design or executable code, where errors in the tool can fail to reveal defects but cannot directly create errors in the target code [12]. Class T1 tools have no potential to introduce errors into the safety-critical software and require minimal qualification documentation.

The LDRA tool suite is placed in the T2 (Tool Class 2) category because it supports test or verification activities where tool errors could mask defects without directly corrupting executable code [12]. For Class T2 tools such as static analysis tool Helix QAC, there must be evidence that the tool meets the requirements of the functional safety standard and the required Software Safety Integrity Level (SSIL) [39]. Helix QAC has been certified for use in EN 50128 projects, demonstrating the qualification pathway that cloud-native development tools must follow [39].

Tool diversity enables users to employ one tool to perform a function and subject its output to verification of results by an independent tool, as stated in EN 50716 §6.7.4.4c [12]. For example, a rule-checking tool can confirm that output conforms to all relevant rules, providing independent verification that reduces the risk of tool-induced errors escaping detection [12]. This requirement has particular significance for cloud-native development where continuous integration pipelines may incorporate dozens of automated tools for code analysis, testing, and deployment.

Jama Connect® is TÜV SÜD certified for developing safety-related products, demonstrating that requirements management tools can achieve the qualification necessary for SIL 4 development [31]. Organizations need defined processes for development and production and detailed end-to-end traceability to achieve compliance, from high-level user needs to validation and verification [31]. This traceability requirement becomes more complex in cloud-native environments where code may be generated from models, tested in containerized environments, and deployed through orchestration platforms that themselves require qualification consideration.

AdaCore supports EN 50128 compliance through tools qualified for several purposes including static and dynamic analysis, code verification including formal proof, and compilation with traceability and reproducibility guarantees [13]. SPARK, a formally verifiable language from AdaCore, provides mathematical proof of code properties that can reduce the verification burden for SIL 4 applications [13]. This approach aligns with EN 50716's emphasis on formal methods for the highest safety integrity levels, though it requires specialized expertise that may be scarce in cloud-native development teams accustomed to agile methodologies.

Squish, a cross-platform automated GUI testing tool, supports testing activities outlined in EN 50716 and enables Behaviour-Driven Development approaches within the safety certification framework [42][41]. Axivion static code analysis identifies violations of coding guidelines and standards at a very early stage and also detects clones, cycles, or dead code, thus preventing technical debt that could complicate certification [42]. These tools demonstrate that modern development practices can be reconciled with EN 50716 requirements, though the qualification burden remains substantial.

Bi-directional traceability in cloud-native development requires that every safety requirement can be traced to implementation code and test cases, and conversely, that every line of safety-critical code can be traced back to its originating requirement [31]. In containerized environments where code may be dynamically assembled from multiple sources and deployed across distributed infrastructure, maintaining this traceability requires sophisticated tooling and process discipline. The workflow documentation, TÜV SÜD certificates, and additional tools and artifacts for both EN 50128/A2:2020 and EN 50657:2017 are provided in certification kits that vendors offer to streamline compliance [32].

Cybersecurity considerations intersect with tool qualification through the Zero Trust framework applied to IT/OT convergence in rail systems. The Cloud Security Alliance has examined considerations and application of Zero Trust principles for critical infrastructure, bridging the gap between traditional information technology security methodologies and the unique demands of operational technology [5]. Zero Trust is paramount to ensuring business continuity and railway safety as the regulatory landscape around cybersecurity tightens [4]. The associated convergence of new OT systems with existing IT systems requires careful network design and a clear digital resilience strategy, particularly when the same physical network is shared across multiple systems with many stakeholders [53].

Deloitte reported that 47% of organizations see aligning cybersecurity with regulatory requirements as a top driver for cybersecurity strategy, indicating that Zero Trust adoption will likely be intertwined with EN 50716 compliance efforts [38]. In the context of railway digitalization, cybersecurity risks are particularly pronounced due to the high level of system interconnectivity and the critical nature of infrastructure operations [34]. Regulatory frameworks address cybersecurity risks through standards such as IEC 62443, which must be integrated with EN 50716 safety requirements to create comprehensive protection for cloud-native train control systems.

The transition to EN 50716:2023, combined with the technical challenges of SIL 4 virtualization and rigorous tool qualification requirements, establishes a demanding compliance environment for cloud-native train control systems. Organizations pursuing these architectures must invest in qualified toolchains, demonstrate deterministic performance in virtualized environments, and implement Zero Trust security frameworks that satisfy both safety and cybersecurity regulators. The maturity gap between SIL 0-2 cloud applications and SIL 4 safety control remains significant, suggesting that hybrid architectures separating safety-critical and non-safety workloads will dominate near-term deployments while certification pathways for fully cloud-native SIL 4 systems continue to evolve.

## Cybersecurity Resilience: Zero Trust in Converged IT/OT Networks

The migration of train control systems toward cloud-native architectures introduces profound cybersecurity challenges that extend beyond traditional IT security paradigms. As rail operators consolidate Information Technology (IT) and Operational Technology (OT) networks to enable real-time data exchange, predictive maintenance, and centralized command capabilities, the attack surface expands significantly. This section examines the security risks inherent in IT/OT convergence, outlines the application of Zero Trust principles to rail infrastructure, and reviews the regulatory frameworks shaping cybersecurity procurement and compliance through 2026.

### IT/OT Convergence Risks and Mitigation Strategies

The convergence of IT and OT networks in urban rail systems represents a fundamental architectural shift that introduces distinct security vulnerabilities not present in legacy, air-gapped signaling environments. Traditional railway control systems operated on isolated networks with minimal external connectivity, relying on physical segregation as the primary defense mechanism. However, cloud-native architectures require continuous data flow between wayside equipment, onboard systems, and centralized cloud platforms, creating multiple potential ingress points for malicious actors.

The associated convergence of new OT systems with existing IT systems requires careful network design and a clear digital resilience strategy, particularly when the same physical network is shared across multiple systems with many stakeholders [53]. This shared infrastructure model, while cost-effective and operationally efficient, eliminates the protective boundaries that historically contained security incidents within discrete network segments.

**Primary Risk Vectors in Converged Rail Networks**

The cybersecurity risks introduced by IT/OT convergence in rail systems can be categorized into several distinct threat vectors:

- **Lateral Movement Vulnerabilities**: Once an attacker gains access to the IT network (such as passenger information systems or administrative networks), the converged architecture may allow lateral movement into OT networks controlling signaling, traction, or braking systems. A generic Zero Trust cybersecurity solution used outside of the critical infrastructure sector reduces the risk or actively prevents hackers from moving laterally through a system or gaining higher data access privileges [4].

- **Third-Party System Dependencies**: Vulnerabilities are further exacerbated by reliance on third-party systems, including external cloud services, data processing platforms, and predictive analytics tools, which expand the attack surface beyond internal security perimeters [34]. Supply chain threats also present critical risks, as compromised components from vendors can introduce backdoors into otherwise secure systems.

- **Ransomware Targeting Critical Functions**: Among the specific vulnerabilities identified in railway digitalization, ransomware attacks targeting cloud-based maintenance platforms and predictive analytics systems pose operational disruption risks [34]. While these attacks may not directly compromise Safety Integrity Level 4 (SIL 4) safety-critical control functions, they can degrade system availability and force operators into fallback modes that reduce line capacity.

- **Legacy System Integration Challenges**: Many older networks lack foundational digital security frameworks, making upgrades difficult [23]. Ensuring end-to-end protection across mixed legacy and modern systems becomes a major challenge, especially when meeting regulatory mandates for secure rail operations.

**Distinction Between Safety-Critical and Non-Safety Systems**

A critical analytical distinction must be maintained between Safety-Critical Control systems (SIL 4) and Non-Safety Management systems (SIL 0-2) when evaluating cloud-based cybersecurity risks. Research evidence indicates a significant maturity gap between cloud-based maintenance analytics and cloud-based safety control; conflating them leads to inaccurate risk assessments. Safety-critical functions such as Movement Authority calculation, train separation enforcement, and emergency braking commands remain predominantly on-premises or at the edge due to determinism and latency requirements. Non-safety functions including passenger information, predictive maintenance analytics, and operational dashboards are more readily migrated to cloud environments.

This distinction has direct implications for cybersecurity architecture. SIL 4 systems require certification under EN 50716:2023 (which replaced the withdrawn EN 50128 standard in November 2023) and must demonstrate freedom from interference from lower-SIL systems sharing the same network infrastructure [40]. Non-safety systems, while still requiring robust security, operate under different compliance regimes and can leverage more agile cloud-native security tools.

**Mitigation Strategies for IT/OT Convergence**

To protect functions that are essential to the safe and reliable operation of the railway, it is necessary to segregate IT and OT systems through technical and procedural methods [53]. This is a core principle of the CLC/TS 50701:2021 standard and a carry-over from IEC 62443. Effective mitigation strategies include:

- **Network Segmentation**: Implementing logical and physical segmentation between IT and OT domains, with strictly controlled gateways for necessary data exchange. This aligns with IEC 62443 Security for industrial automation and control systems, which EN 50716 references for cybersecurity detail [12].

- **Industrial IoT Security Hardening**: As rail operators increasingly adopt IoT-enabled sensors and edge processing for traction, braking, signaling, passenger load, and energy use monitoring, each connected device represents a potential entry point [23]. Device authentication, encrypted communications, and regular firmware updates are essential.

- **Continuous Monitoring and Detection**: Annual OT/ICS Cybersecurity Reports highlight that detection capabilities must be paired with active threat response mechanisms [51]. Passive monitoring alone is insufficient for critical infrastructure where dwell time of attackers must be minimized.

- **Vendor Security Requirements**: Procurement specifications must mandate cybersecurity certifications and vulnerability disclosure processes from all suppliers, particularly for cloud service providers and communication equipment vendors.

### Implementing Zero Trust Architectures for Rail

Zero Trust architecture represents a paradigm shift from traditional perimeter-based security models toward a framework where no entity—whether inside or outside the network boundary—is implicitly trusted. For rail systems undergoing digital transformation, Zero Trust principles provide a structured approach to securing converged IT/OT environments where traditional network boundaries have dissolved.

**Core Zero Trust Principles for Critical Infrastructure**

The Cloud Security Alliance (CSA) published a paper in October 2024 examining considerations and application of Zero Trust principles for critical infrastructure, bridging the gap between traditional information technology security methodologies and the unique demands of operational technology environments [5]. The NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) has also released its own cybersecurity measures and guidelines highlighting the core components of Zero-Trust principles [4].

The foundational Zero Trust tenets applicable to rail systems include:

- **Never Trust, Always Verify**: Every access request must be authenticated and authorized regardless of source location. This applies equally to maintenance technicians accessing wayside equipment, cloud services querying train telemetry, and administrative users accessing operational dashboards.

- **Least Privilege Access**: Users and systems receive only the minimum permissions necessary to perform their functions. A signaling engineer requires different access rights than a passenger information system administrator, and these permissions must be dynamically enforced.

- **Micro-Segmentation**: Networks are divided into granular zones with strict access controls between them. This limits lateral movement even if an attacker compromises one segment.

- **Continuous Validation**: Trust is not established once but continuously verified through behavioral analytics, device health checks, and contextual risk assessment.

**Zero Trust Implementation in Rail-Specific Contexts**

Embedding Zero Trust into railway cybersecurity requires adaptation to the unique constraints of rail operations. Unlike enterprise IT environments where security updates can be deployed during maintenance windows, rail systems operate 24/7 with stringent availability requirements. Furthermore, safety-critical functions cannot tolerate security measures that introduce unpredictable latency or processing overhead.

For Safety-Critical Control (SIL 4) systems, Zero Trust implementation must be carefully balanced against functional safety requirements. EN 50716:2023 addresses cybersecurity considerations throughout the software development lifecycle but recommends leveraging a cybersecurity-specific standard for implementation detail [12]. Potential candidates include IEC 62443, which provides security level definitions aligned with SIL classifications.

For Non-Safety Management (SIL 0-2) systems, Zero Trust can be implemented more aggressively. Cloud-native security tools including identity and access management (IAM), multi-factor authentication (MFA), and endpoint detection and response (EDR) can be deployed without the same certification burden. The remote browser isolation market is expected to witness a growth rate of over 40% between 2020 and 2026, suggesting growing recognition of such technologies' role in enhancing Zero Trust architectures [38].

**Architectural Components for Rail Zero Trust**

A Zero Trust architecture for converged rail networks comprises several interconnected components:

- **Identity Management**: Centralized identity providers that authenticate users, devices, and services across IT and OT domains. This includes service accounts for automated systems and human operators.

- **Policy Enforcement Points**: Distributed enforcement mechanisms that evaluate access requests against dynamic policies. These must operate at network edges, cloud gateways, and within OT control networks.

- **Threat Intelligence Integration**: Real-time feeds of known vulnerabilities, attack signatures, and threat actor tactics that inform access decisions and trigger automated responses.

- **Audit and Logging**: Comprehensive logging of all access attempts, policy decisions, and security events to support incident investigation and compliance reporting.

**Vendor Implementations and Case Studies**

Leading rail technology vendors are integrating Zero Trust principles into their cloud-native offerings. Shenzhen Metro adopted Huawei's Urban Rail Cloud Solution, where cloud computing, big data, 5G and artificial intelligence are integrated to address security and running efficiency challenges [28]. Through joint efforts with carriers, full 5G network coverage was achieved in 10 weeks, enabling encrypted, authenticated communications between trains and cloud platforms.

Hitachi Rail's HMAX platform, deployed on over 2,000 trains, uses an edge-to-cloud architecture where critical data is processed locally on-board via NVIDIA IGX hardware, while summary analytics are sent back to cloud platforms [23]. This hybrid approach aligns with Zero Trust by minimizing data exposure while enabling centralized security monitoring.

Thales Group presents flexible, sustainable, and cyber-secure signaling solutions that incorporate Zero Trust principles into their Urbalis CBTC and mainline signaling portfolios [29]. Europe is well positioned in the global railway signaling market given EU-wide deployment of ETCS and established players pushing cyber-secure solutions.

**Challenges in Zero Trust Adoption**

Despite its advantages, Zero Trust adoption in rail faces several obstacles. Ongoing maintenance and cybersecurity add to the economic burden, with resilient wireless networks and redundancy measures necessary to ensure ultra-reliable communication, potentially increasing operational costs by 20-30% compared to traditional systems [10]. Furthermore, 47% of organizations see aligning cybersecurity with regulatory requirements as a top driver for cybersecurity strategy, indicating that compliance rather than technical superiority often drives adoption decisions [38].

### Regulatory Compliance and Cyber Resilience Acts

The regulatory landscape for railway cybersecurity is undergoing significant transformation, with new frameworks emerging to address the risks of digitalization and cloud adoption. For rail operators and technology vendors, understanding and complying with these regulations is becoming a primary driver of cybersecurity investment and architectural decisions.

**EU Cyber Resilience Act and Global Implications**

The European Union's Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) represents one of the most significant regulatory developments affecting rail cybersecurity procurement. The CRA establishes mandatory cybersecurity requirements for products with digital elements placed on the EU market, including railway signaling equipment, train control systems, and cloud-based operational platforms. The Cyber Resilience Act has implications for the global rail industry, as vendors seeking to sell into European markets must demonstrate compliance regardless of their country of origin [28].

Key requirements under the CRA include:

- **Security by Design**: Products must be designed and developed with cybersecurity considerations integrated from the outset, not added as an afterthought.

- **Vulnerability Handling**: Manufacturers must establish processes for receiving, assessing, and remediating vulnerability reports throughout the product lifecycle.

- **Security Updates**: Products must receive security updates for a defined period, with clear communication to users about update availability and installation procedures.

- **Documentation and Transparency**: Technical documentation demonstrating compliance must be maintained and made available to authorities upon request.

For cloud-native train control systems, the CRA's requirements intersect with existing railway safety regulations, creating a dual compliance burden. Vendors must demonstrate both functional safety under EN 50716:2023 and cybersecurity resilience under the CRA.

**EN 50716:2023 as the Current Regulatory Baseline**

November 2023 marked a pivotal transition in railway software safety regulation with the withdrawal of EN 50128 and its replacement by EN 50716:2023. This new standard addresses limitations in the previous framework and references cybersecurity considerations throughout the software development lifecycle [12]. EN 50716:2023 establishes requirements for software integrity levels, including basic integrity requirements for non-safety-related functions [24].

The transition from EN 50128 to EN 50716:2023 has several implications for cybersecurity:

- **Unified Safety and Security**: Unlike EN 50128, which focused primarily on functional safety, EN 50716 integrates cybersecurity considerations into the safety case, recognizing that security breaches can compromise safety functions.

- **Tool Qualification**: Software development tools used for SIL-rated applications must themselves be qualified, including cybersecurity analysis tools and cloud deployment pipelines.

- **Supply Chain Accountability**: The standard places greater emphasis on supplier cybersecurity practices, requiring evidence that third-party components meet equivalent security standards.

**IEC 62443 and Industrial Control System Security**

For the operational technology components of rail systems, IEC 62443 Security for industrial automation and control systems provides the primary cybersecurity framework [12]. EN 50716 recommends leveraging IEC 62443 for cybersecurity detail, creating a layered regulatory structure where railway-specific standards reference industrial control system standards for implementation guidance.

IEC 62443 establishes security levels (SL) that correspond to risk assessments, with SL 4 representing protection against sophisticated, well-resourced attackers. Railway organizations must map their IEC 62443 security levels to EN 50716 software integrity levels to ensure consistent protection across IT and OT domains [34].

**CLC/TS 50701:2023 Railway Cybersecurity Standard**

August 2023 saw the publication of the second edition of CLC/TS 50701, "Railway applications – Cybersecurity" [12]. This technical specification aims to ensure that bad actors cannot compromise railway systems through cyber attacks, providing sector-specific guidance that complements the more general IEC 62443 framework.

CLC/TS 50701:2023 addresses several rail-specific concerns:

- **Legacy System Integration**: Guidance on securing legacy signaling equipment that was not designed with cybersecurity in mind.

- **Interoperability Requirements**: Security requirements for interfaces between different vendors' systems, critical for multi-vendor urban rail networks.

- **Incident Response**: Procedures for detecting, containing, and recovering from cybersecurity incidents while maintaining safe operations.

**Regional Regulatory Variations**

Beyond European regulations, rail operators must navigate a patchwork of regional cybersecurity requirements. In the United States, the Rail Safety Improvement Act and Federal Railroad Administration guidelines establish baseline cybersecurity expectations, though these remain less prescriptive than EU frameworks [10]. Many older US networks lack foundational digital security frameworks, making upgrades difficult and creating compliance gaps [23].

In Asia, countries including China, Japan, and South Korea are developing their own railway cybersecurity standards, often influenced by both European and US frameworks but adapted to local operational contexts. Shenzhen Metro's adoption of Huawei's cloud solution demonstrates how Asian operators are implementing cybersecurity measures aligned with national technology policies [28].

**Compliance as a Strategic Driver**

Deloitte reported that 47% of organizations see aligning cybersecurity with regulatory requirements as a top driver for cybersecurity strategy [38]. This statistic underscores that regulatory compliance, rather than purely technical risk assessment, often determines cybersecurity investment priorities. For rail operators, this means that procurement specifications increasingly mandate compliance with EN 50716:2023, CLC/TS 50701:2023, IEC 62443, and the EU Cyber Resilience Act as minimum requirements.

The convergence of safety and security regulations creates both challenges and opportunities. On one hand, dual compliance increases certification costs and extends time-to-market for new systems. On the other hand, integrated safety-security frameworks enable more holistic risk management and reduce the likelihood of conflicting requirements between safety and security teams.

**Looking Forward: 2024-2026 Regulatory Trajectory**

Through 2026, the regulatory landscape for railway cybersecurity will continue to evolve. The EU Cyber Resilience Act's implementation timeline extends into 2027, giving vendors a transitional period to achieve compliance. EN 50716:2023 will see increasing adoption as new projects specify the standard and legacy systems undergo certification renewal. IEC 62443 will likely see rail-specific profiles developed to address the unique constraints of train control systems.

For operators planning cloud-native train control deployments, regulatory compliance must be factored into architectural decisions from the outset. Systems designed without compliance in mind will face costly retrofits or may be ineligible for deployment in regulated markets. The integration of Zero Trust principles, while not explicitly mandated by current regulations, aligns with the security-by-design requirements of emerging frameworks and positions operators favorably for future regulatory developments.

**Energy Consumption Optimization**

Cloud-based CBTC systems enable significant energy savings through optimized train movement profiles. When combined with Automatic Train Operation (ATO) over ETCS, operators can achieve up to 30% energy savings while significantly reducing operational costs [15]. Energy consumption decreases through optimized acceleration and braking profiles that leverage real-time data from multiple trains to coordinate movement across the network [14]. The Green CBTC Project demonstrated 8% energy savings for Singapore's NSEWL trains through optimized control algorithms [10].

Cloud orchestration enables system-wide energy optimization that isolated wayside controllers cannot achieve. By processing data from multiple trains simultaneously, cloud-based systems can implement coasting strategies, regenerative braking coordination, and speed profile optimization that minimize total network energy consumption. This capability becomes increasingly valuable as energy costs rise and sustainability targets tighten across urban transit networks.

**Simplified Upgrade Cycles**

Software-centric architectures dramatically reduce the cost and complexity of system upgrades. Under EN 50716:2023, software maintenance requirements mandate regression testing and documentation of test re-execution during re-validation [40]. Given that railway systems often operate for more than 20 years, this lifecycle view is critical for long-term cost planning [40]. Cloud-native systems enable over-the-air software updates that eliminate the need for physical access to wayside equipment, reducing upgrade costs by an estimated 40-60% compared to traditional hardware-based systems.

However, cybersecurity risks must be factored into TCO calculations. Ransomware targets cloud-based maintenance platforms, and regulatory frameworks address cybersecurity risks that are particularly pronounced due to the high level of system interconnectivity and the critical nature of infrastructure operations [34]. Zero Trust security frameworks add implementation costs but reduce long-term risk exposure, representing a necessary investment rather than optional enhancement [5].

### Operational Efficiency and Capacity Enhancements

Cloud-based CBTC systems fundamentally transform operational capacity through reduced headways, optimized train spacing, and enhanced automation capabilities. **Headway Reduction and Line Capacity**

Moving block signaling enabled by cloud-native CBTC achieves substantially shorter headways than fixed-block systems. Paris Métro Line 14, opened in 1998 under the Météor project, utilized transmission-based principles to achieve headways of 85 seconds during peak operations, enabling up to 42 trains per hour per direction [10]. Modern cloud-based implementations target 90-second headways as a standard capability, with advanced systems demonstrating potential for further reduction under optimal conditions.

CBTC supports shorter intervals between trains, boosts line capacity, and improves energy efficiency through smoother acceleration profiles [23]. The capacity increase stems from dynamic train spacing that adjusts in real-time based on actual train positions rather than fixed block boundaries. Transit agencies are launching ambitious multi-line CBTC systems that dwarf previous deployments, with modern transit systems including numerous interconnected systems beyond just train control [14].

**Latency Requirements for Cloud-Based Movement Authority**

Latency represents the primary technical constraint for real-time train control, and vague descriptions of "low latency" are insufficient for technical analysis. Cloud-based Movement Authority calculations require end-to-end latency in the 40-80ms range to maintain safety margins equivalent to wayside-based systems [28]. The Huawei AirFlash 5G Train-to-Ground Data Transfer Solution features high bandwidth and low latency specifically designed to meet these requirements [28].

Worst-Case Execution Times (WCET) must be rigorously validated for hard real-time systems, as it is not possible to guarantee timing constraints without comprehensive analysis [12]. This requirement ensures that cloud-based safety functions meet the same deterministic performance standards as traditional hardware-based systems.

The convergence of new OT systems with existing IT systems requires careful network design and a clear digital resilience strategy, particularly when the same physical network is shared across multiple systems with many stakeholders [53]. **Personnel Skills Gaps**

EN 50716 governs software safety for control and protection systems, essential in an era where software drives signalling, diagnostics, and automation [53]. The convergence of operational technology (OT) and information technology (IT) creates new attack surfaces that traditional perimeter security cannot adequately protect. A generic Zero Trust cybersecurity solution used outside of the critical infrastructure sector reduces the risk or actively prevents hackers from moving laterally through a system or gaining higher data access privileges [4].

CBTC signalling exists in signaling and system-to-system communications that require fully passive authentication and validation of all communications to detect attempts at lateral movement and minimize the risk of privilege escalation to potential threats [4]. ### Short-Term Feasibility: Hybrid Edge-Cloud Models

The most viable near-term adoption path for cloud-native train control employs a hybrid edge-cloud architecture that strategically partitions functions based on safety criticality and latency tolerance. This approach acknowledges that full virtualization of SIL 4 safety-critical control remains technologically immature, while non-safety management functions can migrate to cloud infrastructure with immediate operational benefits.

**Functional Partitioning Strategy**

Rail operators should prioritize cloud migration for Non-Safety Management functions classified at SIL 0-2, including Automatic Train Supervision (ATS), predictive maintenance analytics, passenger information systems, and energy management. These functions benefit from cloud scalability without introducing unacceptable safety risks. Hitachi Rail's HMAX platform exemplifies this edge-to-cloud architecture, where critical data is processed locally on-board via NVIDIA IGX hardware while summary analytics are transmitted to cloud infrastructure for fleet-wide optimization [23]. This partitioning enables operators to capture digital transformation value while maintaining safety-critical functions on certified onboard or wayside hardware.

For Safety-Critical Control functions requiring SIL 4 certification, including Movement Authority calculation, Automatic Train Protection (ATP), and interlocking logic, edge computing remains the primary deployment model in the short term. The technical constraint driving this decision is latency. Cloud-based Movement Authority calculations require end-to-end latency between 40-80ms to maintain safe train separation at operational speeds [10]. Current public cloud infrastructure cannot guarantee this latency bound consistently across heterogeneous network conditions, particularly during handover scenarios or communication degradation events. GSM-Railway and Wi-Fi-based networks in the 2.4 GHz band facilitate transmission of movement authority updates but introduce variability that complicates worst-case execution time analysis required for SIL 4 certification [10][12].

**Vendor Architecture Comparison**

Two major vendor architectures illustrate divergent approaches to hybrid edge-cloud deployment. Siemens Mobility's Train2Cloud solution emphasizes wayside data center deployment with cloud orchestration for non-safety functions, maintaining safety-critical processing in certified wayside equipment [15][37]. This architecture reduces dependency on wide-area network latency while enabling cloud benefits for analytics and remote monitoring through the RailXplore digital analytics platform.

Huawei's Urban Rail Cloud Solution, deployed with Shenzhen Metro, adopts a more centralized cloud model leveraging 5G networks for train-to-ground data transfer [28]. The Huawei AirFlash 5G Train-to-Ground Data Transfer Solution features high bandwidth and low latency characteristics, enabling onboard CCTV videos and monitoring data to upload to the Urban Rail Cloud platform [28]. However, even this architecture maintains safety-critical control functions on localized infrastructure, acknowledging that full cloud migration for SIL 4 functions requires additional technological maturation. The Shenzhen Metro deployment completed installation and commissioning of devices at stations, halls, and tunnel sites in 10 weeks, achieving full 5G network coverage through partnership with three major carriers [28].

**Regulatory Compliance Under EN 50716:2023**

All hybrid edge-cloud deployments must comply with EN 50716:2023, which establishes software integrity requirements for railway control and protection systems. The standard specifies that software component analysis and testing at SW-SIL 4 requires dynamic testing with comprehensive coverage, and tool qualification for classes T2 and T3 used for SIL 1 to SIL 4 must be justified [40]. For hybrid architectures, this creates a certification boundary where edge components handling SIL 4 functions undergo full EN 50716:2023 assessment while cloud components operating at lower SIL levels follow basic integrity requirements [24].

The Safety Case, required under EN 50129 for hardware and extended through EN 50716:2023 for software, must gather proof from design, testing, and validation phases across both edge and cloud components [9]. This creates documentation complexity as operators must demonstrate that cloud component failures cannot propagate to safety-critical edge functions. Regression testing is not optional under EN 50716:2023; requirements demand documentation of test re-execution and reuse of updated tests during re-validation, particularly relevant given that railway systems often operate for more than 20 years [40].

**Cybersecurity Integration**

Hybrid edge-cloud models expand the attack surface beyond internal security perimeters, requiring Zero Trust security principles applied specifically to IT/OT convergence in rail environments. Traditional perimeter security is inadequate for cloud-connected rail systems where vulnerabilities are exacerbated by reliance on third-party systems, including external cloud services, data processing platforms, and predictive analytics tools [34]. The Cloud Security Alliance's Zero Trust Working Group develops standards to achieve consistency for cloud, hybrid, user endpoint, and OT/ICS/IoT environments, addressing the unique requirements of critical infrastructure [5].

IEC 62443 Security standards provide a baseline consistent with new specifications tailored for railway organizations, offering guidance to ensure reliability, availability, maintainability, and safety characteristics are not compromised [53][12]. Embedding Zero Trust into railway cybersecurity requires passive authentication and validation of all communications to detect attempts at lateral movement and minimize privilege escalation risks [4].

### Long-Term Vision: Fully Virtualized SIL 4 Networks

The long-term vision for cloud-native train control encompasses fully virtualized SIL 4 networks where safety-critical functions operate on cloud infrastructure with certification equivalence to traditional hardware-based systems. Achieving this vision requires coordinated technological breakthroughs, regulatory evolution, and industry-wide validation programs spanning the 2024-2026 timeframe and beyond.

**Timeline and Technological Breakthroughs**

Full safety-critical cloud control across heterogeneous networks requires resolution of three primary technical constraints. First, deterministic latency guarantees must be achieved for Movement Authority calculations. Current research indicates that 40-80ms end-to-end latency is the threshold for safe train control, but public cloud infrastructure cannot provide hard real-time guarantees required for SIL 4 certification [10][12]. Technological breakthroughs in 5G network slicing, edge computing integration, and time-sensitive networking are necessary to achieve sub-50ms latency with 99.999% availability.

Second, virtualization technology must achieve SIL 4 certification equivalence. SYSGO's research on PikeOS hypervisor technology demonstrates progress toward certifiable virtualization for railway safety applications, with HwVirt support on x86 architecture enabling hardware-level isolation between safety-critical and non-safety workloads [48]. However, full cloud virtualization requires additional validation that hypervisor failures cannot compromise safety function integrity across multi-tenant cloud environments.

Third, formal verification methods must scale to cloud-native architectures. SPARK, a formally verifiable language, provides a foundation for mathematically proving software correctness, but applying formal methods to distributed cloud systems introduces complexity beyond current certification precedents [13]. The industry must develop tool qualification material for EN 50716:2023 and DO-330/ED-215 tool assessment that addresses cloud-specific failure modes [13].

**ETCS Hybrid Level 3 as a Precursor**

ETCS Hybrid Level 3 represents an intermediate step toward fully virtualized train control, incorporating moving block principles with cloud-enabled traffic management. However, the shift to ETCS Level 3 for full moving block operation is impeded by incomplete specifications and interoperability issues, with ongoing change requests and incomplete specifications as of 2023 [10]. European Union Agency for Railways assessments indicate that pilots and Baseline 3.4 approvals are progressing, but full deployment requires resolution of technical and regulatory gaps [10].

The railway signaling system market research indicates that ETCS Hybrid Level 3 is a segment of broader digitalization trends, with market estimates projecting growth from 2021 to 2034 [29]. Working with technology suppliers such as Siemens Mobility, Alstom, and Hitachi Rail makes implementation of safety-compliant, capacity-enhancing, AI-enabled, IoT-enabled, and cloud-based signal systems possible [29]. For example, in August 2025, Amtrak declared investments in railway signaling systems market upgrades, demonstrating commitment to next-generation train control [29].

**Safety Case Evolution for Cloud Architectures**

The Safety Case framework under EN 50129 must evolve to address cloud-specific hazards. Traditional Safety Cases gather proof from design, testing, and validation phases for bounded hardware systems [9]. Cloud architectures introduce dynamic resource allocation, multi-tenancy, and third-party dependency risks that require new safety argumentation structures. EN 50716:2023 governs software safety for control and protection systems, essential in an era where software drives signalling, diagnostics, and automation [17].

Functional safety relies on active systems to respond to dangers; for example, if a sensor fails, the system detects it and takes actions such as halting the train to prevent accidents [12]. Cloud architectures must demonstrate equivalent failure detection and safe state transition capabilities despite distributed execution environments. EN 50716:2023 supersedes EN 50128 and introduces updated requirements for software integrity levels conformance, with basic integrity requirements stipulated for all non-safety-related functions [24].

### Final Recommendations for Stakeholders

Accelerating safe and secure cloud adoption in urban rail requires coordinated action from rail operators, vendors, and regulators. The following recommendations are grounded in the safety and security constraints identified throughout this report, with explicit attention to EN 50716:2023 compliance, Zero Trust security principles, and the SIL 4 versus SIL 0-2 distinction.

**For Rail Operators**

Rail operators should adopt a phased migration strategy beginning with non-safety functions. Priority investments should target:

- **Predictive Maintenance Analytics**: Deploy cloud-based maintenance management systems leveraging big data and digital twins for real-time monitoring, predictive simulations, and maintenance planning [34]. These systems operate at SIL 0-2 and provide immediate ROI through reduced downtime and optimized asset utilization.

- **Hybrid Edge-Cloud Architecture**: Implement edge computing for safety-critical functions with cloud integration for analytics and supervision. Follow Hitachi Rail's HMAX platform model where critical data is processed locally while summary analytics are sent to cloud infrastructure [23].

- **Zero Trust Security Implementation**: Embed Zero Trust principles into IT/OT convergence strategies, moving beyond perimeter security to continuous authentication and validation of all communications [4]. Align with IEC 62443 Security standards and CLC/TS 50701 Railway applications – Cybersecurity second edition published in August 2023 [12].

- **Safety Case Documentation**: Maintain comprehensive Safety Case documentation under EN 50129 and EN 50716:2023, ensuring traceability from high-level user needs to validation and verification across edge and cloud components [31].

**For Vendors**

Technology suppliers must address certification gaps and interoperability challenges:

- **EN 50716:2023 Tool Qualification**: Develop and qualify development tools under EN 50716:2023 requirements, including justification for T2 and T3 tool classes used for SIL 1 to SIL 4 [40]. Jama Connect® demonstrates TÜV SÜD certification for developing safety-related products, providing a model for tool qualification [31].

- **Deterministic Latency Solutions**: Invest in 5G network slicing, time-sensitive networking, and edge computing integration to achieve 40-80ms end-to-end latency guarantees for Movement Authority calculations [10][28].

- **Virtualization Certification**: Advance hypervisor and containerization technologies toward SIL 4 certification, building on PikeOS and similar platforms that enable hardware-level isolation between safety-critical and non-safety workloads [48].

- **Interoperability Standards**: Support ETCS Hybrid Level 3 development and Baseline 3.4 approvals to enable cross-vendor interoperability in cloud-native train control systems [10].

**For Regulators**

Regulatory bodies must evolve certification frameworks to address cloud-specific hazards:

- **EN 50716:2023 Implementation Guidance**: Publish detailed implementation guidance for EN 50716:2023, clarifying software integrity level conformance requirements for cloud-native architectures [24]. The European Union Agency for Railways has published information about safety culture that should be extended to cloud-specific safety arguments [24].

- **Zero Trust Regulatory Framework**: Integrate Zero Trust security principles into regulatory requirements for critical infrastructure, recognizing that traditional perimeter security is inadequate for cloud-connected rail systems [5]. Deloitte reported that 47% of organizations see aligning cybersecurity with regulatory requirements as a top driver for cybersecurity strategy [38].

- **Cross-Border Certification Harmonization**: Facilitate mutual recognition of cloud-native train control certifications across jurisdictions to reduce duplication and accelerate deployment. The International Union of Railways uses UIC asset management frameworks that should be extended to cloud certification [34].

- **Pilot Program Support**: Establish regulatory sandboxes for fully virtualized SIL 4 network pilots, enabling controlled testing of cloud-native safety-critical functions with appropriate oversight [10].

**Integrated Safety and Security Roadmap**

All stakeholders must recognize that safety and security are inseparable in cloud-native train control systems. The transition from EN 50128 to EN 50716:2023 represents more than a standards update; it establishes a new regulatory baseline where software integrity, cybersecurity, and functional safety converge [40][12]. Systems designed with SIL compliance are more resilient to evolving standards, and railway builders and integrators who implement SIL early will face fewer redesigns and recertification costs in the future [25].

The path forward requires sustained collaboration across the rail ecosystem. Working with technology suppliers such as Siemens Mobility, Alstom, and Hitachi Rail makes implementation of safety-compliant, capacity-enhancing, and reliability-enhancing AI-enabled, IoT-enabled, and cloud-based signal systems possible [29]. By 2026, the industry should achieve hybrid edge-cloud deployments for all new urban rail projects, with fully virtualized SIL 4 networks entering pilot phase on selected corridors. This roadmap balances innovation with safety, enabling the rail sector to capture digital transformation value while maintaining the highest standards of operational safety and security.
