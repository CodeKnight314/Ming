## Strategic Context: The Transition from SSA to Space Domain Awareness in Cislunar Space

The strategic landscape of space operations is transforming as activities extend beyond traditional near-Earth orbits into the cislunar regime. This transition demands an evolution in awareness capabilities, moving from conventional Space Situational Awareness (SSA) focused on object cataloging toward comprehensive Space Domain Awareness (SDA). SDA encompasses threat assessment, intent analysis, and operational context. The cislunar environment presents unique challenges that render legacy SSA approaches insufficient for national security and commercial objectives. This section examines the strategic imperatives driving this transition, the evolving mission requirements, and the policy frameworks governing interagency coordination.

### Defining the Cislunar Operational Volume and Security Imperatives

The cislunar operational volume encompasses the vast region of space between Earth and the Moon, extending from geosynchronous Earth orbit (GEO) outward to lunar vicinity. This domain represents a volume of space **three orders of magnitude greater** than that in the near-Earth regime out to GEO, creating unprecedented challenges for detection, tracking, and characterization of space objects [28]. The term "cislunar" specifically refers to the area of space between the Earth and the Moon, including critical orbital regimes such as GEO, lunar transfer orbits, and various Lagrange point orbits [12].

The strategic drivers necessitating enhanced cislunar awareness stem from converging national security imperatives and emerging commercial opportunities:

*   **National Security:** Considerations require the ability to monitor potential adversarial activities in a domain where traditional terrestrial and near-Earth surveillance architectures prove inadequate. The military's growing need to increase the number and variety of space assets has made cislunar SSA an emergent issue, largely attributed to potential anti-satellite threats and the strategic value of positions in this regime [16].
*   **Commercial Imperatives:** The National Cislunar Science & Technology Strategy, released by the White House, identifies cislunar detection and tracking capabilities as a key national priority [22]. NASA's mission to return humans to the Moon and establish sustained lunar presence creates additional requirements for comprehensive domain awareness to ensure mission safety and operational success [31].
*   **Timeline Urgency:** Chief of Space Operations Gen. John W. "Jay" Raymond estimated the need for cislunar space domain awareness within the **next five to 10 years**, reflecting the urgency of developing these capabilities before commercial and international activities saturate the domain [31].

The security imperatives extend beyond mere object tracking to encompass understanding the operational environment in its entirety. Cislunar space presents unique physical challenges, including perturbations from the Moon and the Sun that constantly pull spacecraft off course, requiring frequent station-keeping maneuvers [14]. These dynamical complexities, governed by Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem (CR3BP) mechanics rather than simple Keplerian orbits, create detection corridors and observational challenges that demand specialized awareness architectures.

The commercial lunar economy introduces additional stakeholders and objects requiring monitoring. As private entities develop lunar transportation, resource utilization, and infrastructure capabilities, the cislunar regime will host an increasing diversity of activities requiring coordination and deconfliction. This convergence of national security and commercial interests creates a strategic imperative for awareness systems that can serve both classified defense requirements and open commercial space traffic management functions.

### Evolution of Mission Requirements: From Cataloging to Operational Intelligence

The distinction between **Space Situational Awareness (SSA)** and **Space Domain Awareness (SDA)** represents a fundamental shift in mission requirements and operational philosophy (see Data Sources & Definitions). SSA typically refers to the cataloging of objects and their locations—a passive function focused on maintaining a registry of space objects and their orbital parameters [14]. This traditional approach emphasizes finding, tracking, identifying, and maintaining custody of space objects through observational data and orbital propagation [10].

SDA, by contrast, is a broader concept that includes understanding the intent, context, and operational environment of those objects [14]. This evolution from passive cataloging to active intelligence represents a qualitative leap in capability requirements. SDA encompasses not only where objects are and where they will be, but why they are there, what they might do, and how their activities affect broader operational objectives.

The operational intelligence requirement has been driven to a higher level of fidelity and demand for the warfighter as SSA becomes enveloped into the broader SDA concept [10]. Military operations cannot be effectively carried out without a full understanding of the threat environment. This necessitates capabilities that can:
*   Predict and differentiate between attacks and inherent anomalies on or from satellites.
*   Anticipate space weather and space system irregularities and their potential impact.
*   Prepare regular status of forces [16].

SSA traditionally maintains four core objectives: search, discover, and track satellites; track interactions to discern between different types of objects and their intent; characterize the space environment; and support collision avoidance [16]. However, the cislunar regime exposes limitations in these traditional objectives. The volume expansion, combined with complex orbital dynamics and increased object velocities, requires enhanced detection sensitivity, more frequent observation cycles, and sophisticated data fusion capabilities.

The evolution toward SDA demands active threat assessment capabilities that transcend simple custody maintenance. This includes the ability to discern between cooperative and non-cooperative objects, identify maneuvering behavior indicative of hostile intent, and assess the operational context of activities in the cislunar domain. Recent research emphasizes adaptive sensor tasking strategies for tracking non-cooperative cislunar space objects, highlighting the shift toward intelligence-driven awareness rather than passive observation [26].

This mission evolution also requires integration of multiple data sources and phenomenologies. Single electro-optical or infrared (EO/IR) sensor systems prove infeasible for comprehensive cislunar SDA, necessitating distributed space-based networks and multi-phenomenology observation architectures [23]. The optimization of these distributed networks must account for operational cost metrics while maintaining the fidelity required for threat assessment and intent analysis [47].

The transition from cataloging to operational intelligence also implies a shift in the temporal characteristics of awareness requirements. Traditional SSA operates on relatively long update cycles suitable for stable near-Earth orbits. Cislunar SDA demands more frequent updates and predictive capabilities to account for the faster dynamics and greater uncertainty inherent in three-body orbital regimes. This creates requirements for advanced propagation models, uncertainty quantification, and reachability analysis that can support operational decision-making on compressed timelines.

### Policy Frameworks and Interagency Coordination Mechanisms

The policy framework governing cislunar awareness and space traffic management has undergone significant restructuring in recent years, reflecting the transition from predominantly military space operations to a mixed civil-commercial-security domain. **Space Policy Directive-3 (SPD-3)**, signed on **June 18, 2018**, established the foundational policy architecture for this transition [35]. SPD-3 shifts responsibility for providing space situational awareness data to satellite operators from the Department of Defense (DoD) to the Department of Commerce (DoC), fundamentally altering the interagency coordination landscape [35].

The **Traffic Coordination System for Space (TraCSS)** represents the operational implementation of this policy shift. The Department of Commerce plans to roll out **TraCSS Phase 1.0** as an initial version of its nascent system for warning space operators of potential collisions [4][36]. TraCSS is designed to deliver conjunction data messages and provide basic space traffic coordination, including conjunction and reentry notifications, free of direct user fees while supporting new opportunities for commercial and non-profit products and services [19][37].

The delineation of responsibilities between Defense, Commerce, and commercial entities remains an active area of policy development and interagency negotiation:
*   **Responsibility Shift:** The DoD and Commerce departments signed a memorandum of agreement to begin shifting responsibilities for managing space traffic, with DoD taking responsibility for managing space object warning and tracking while Commerce develops the civil traffic coordination infrastructure [33].
*   **Industry Engagement:** Assistant Secretary of Defense for Space Policy John Plumb stated that DoD looks to broaden its relationship with industry, allies, and partners to help achieve the objectives of SPD-3 [33].

However, significant challenges persist in DoD-Commerce data sharing. Concerns about data-sharing between the departments have been explicitly addressed in policy discussions, reflecting the tension between national security classification requirements and the need for open commercial space traffic coordination [36]. The Space Force has opposed cutting the TraCSS program from the Commerce budget, emphasizing that the system allows operational and sustainment squadrons to focus more fully on core missions of exploiting opportunities and mitigating vulnerabilities in the national security space terrain [32].

Industry coordination mechanisms have emerged to facilitate public-private collaboration on SSA and SDA capabilities:
*   **Commercial SSA Coalition:** Represents a broad industry coalition advocating for commercial participation in space awareness functions [32].
*   **Industry Associations:** The Satellite Industry Association and Aerospace Industries Association have also engaged in policy discussions, with industry representatives noting that civilian space situational awareness needs differ significantly from military requirements [32].

The National Space Policy further elaborates on interagency coordination requirements, directing multiple departments including State, Defense, Commerce, Transportation, and Energy to cooperate on space situational awareness and traffic management functions [37]. The policy emphasizes making basic SSA data available while supporting commercial opportunities, establishing a presumption of approval for space activities consistent with national security and public safety interests [37].

Updated TraCSS data sharing policies continue to evolve through public listening sessions and stakeholder feedback mechanisms. The NOAA Office of Space Commerce has conducted sessions on updated data sharing policies, providing opportunities for public comment on the frameworks governing information exchange between government and commercial entities [20]. These ongoing policy refinements reflect the recognition that effective cislunar SDA requires seamless integration of government and commercial capabilities, with clear delineation of responsibilities and robust data sharing protocols that balance security and transparency requirements.

The policy framework for cislunar operations specifically remains less mature than near-Earth space traffic management policies. The National Cislunar Science & Technology Strategy identifies detection and tracking as priorities, but operational coordination mechanisms for the cislunar regime are still under development. This policy gap creates uncertainty for commercial operators and complicates interagency planning for cislunar SDA architectures. As activities in the cislunar domain accelerate, policy frameworks must evolve to address the unique coordination challenges of this expanded operational volume while maintaining the security-intelligence functions essential to national defense.

## Orbital Dynamics and Uncertainty Propagation in the Three-Body Problem

The transition from near-Earth orbital mechanics to cislunar space represents a fundamental shift in the mathematical frameworks required for accurate SSA and SDA. While traditional SSA focuses on cataloging and tracking objects in Low Earth Orbit (LEO) and Geosynchronous Earth Orbit (GEO) using well-established two-body dynamics, cislunar SDA demands active intelligence about intent and threat assessment in a regime where gravitational perturbations from multiple bodies create chaotic, non-Keplerian trajectories. This section provides deep technical analysis of the mathematical shift from Keplerian to Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem (CR3BP) models, the computational trade-offs of multi-fidelity propagation methods, and the covariance analysis techniques required for maintaining custody of objects on periodic orbits such as Near Rectilinear Halo Orbits (NRHO) and Halo orbits.

### Limitations of Keplerian Mechanics in Cislunar Regimes

Traditional two-body orbital mechanics, which assume a spacecraft orbits a single dominant gravitational body, fail catastrophically in the cislunar regime due to the comparable gravitational influence of both Earth and Moon. In Low Earth Orbit, atmospheric drag serves as the primary perturbation force, and models like SGP4 provide adequate prediction accuracy for short-term tracking. However, SGP4-XP and similar two-body propagators introduce key sources of error when applied to cislunar trajectories, where the perturbations from the Moon and the Sun constantly pull a spacecraft off course in ways that Keplerian models cannot capture [8][14].

The mathematical foundation of cislunar dynamics rests on the **Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem (CR3BP)**, which models the motion of a negligible-mass spacecraft under the gravitational influence of two primary bodies (Earth and Moon) rotating about their common barycenter. Unlike Keplerian orbits characterized by six classical orbital elements that remain constant in the absence of perturbations, CR3BP trajectories exhibit chaotic dynamics that become difficult to predict over long time horizons using classical Initial Orbit Determination (IOD) methods [25]. The CR3BP introduces five equilibrium points known as Lagrange points (L1 through L5), around which families of periodic orbits exist, including Halo orbits and Near Rectilinear Halo Orbits (NRHO) that are critical for cislunar infrastructure and observation platforms.

The failure of two-body models in cislunar space manifests in several specific ways:
1.  **Central Field Assumption:** The assumption of a central gravitational field breaks down when a spacecraft operates in regions where Earth and Moon gravitational accelerations are comparable in magnitude.
2.  **Jacobi Constant:** The Jacobi constant, a conserved quantity in the CR3BP that defines zero-velocity surfaces and admissible regions of motion, has no equivalent in Keplerian mechanics.
3.  **Invariant Manifolds:** The existence of invariant manifolds—tubular structures in phase space that guide natural transport between orbital regimes—creates persistent detection corridors (PDCs) that govern feasible cislunar trajectories but remain invisible to two-body analysis [28].

Research demonstrates that many potential cislunar paths are infeasible within CR3BP dynamics, requiring enforcement of transcription constraints between trajectory nodes to identify viable routes [28]. This constraint structure fundamentally differs from the conic section geometry of Keplerian orbits, where any position-velocity pair defines a valid trajectory. The neighborhood of CR3BP periodic orbits exhibits time-varying perturbation characteristics that require specialized model identification techniques, such as normal form methods to define admissible control regions [5].

The operational implications for SSA versus SDA are significant. While SSA traditionally focuses on precise tracking and characterization of man-made objects in orbit using predictive techniques based on two-body dynamics with perturbation corrections, cislunar SDA requires understanding the intent and capability of objects operating in a regime where station-keeping maneuvers are frequently required to maintain position [16][14]. In LEO, atmospheric drag is the primary enemy; in cislunar space, the third-body perturbations demand continuous delta-v expenditure for orbit maintenance, making maneuver detection and intent assessment critical components of domain awareness rather than mere catalog maintenance.

Linear state-space representations for cislunar tracking must be based on Restricted Three Body Dynamics rather than Keplerian elements, as the latter fail to capture the coupled gravitational effects that dominate cislunar motion [47]. This mathematical shift necessitates new approaches to orbit determination, uncertainty propagation, and sensor tasking that account for the inherently non-linear and chaotic nature of cislunar trajectories.

### Adaptive Multi-Fidelity Propagation for Computational Efficiency

The computational demands of cislunar Space Domain Awareness require balancing high-fidelity accuracy with low-fidelity speed, particularly when tracking multiple targets across the vast cislunar volume. Multi-fidelity propagation architectures address this challenge by dynamically selecting the appropriate dynamical model complexity based on tracking requirements, object characteristics, and available computational resources. This approach is essential for cislunar multi-target tracking scenarios where the number of Resident Space Objects (RSOs) may grow significantly as lunar infrastructure develops.

**Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)** estimation filters have emerged as a critical tool for processing observations of periodic orbits in the cislunar regime. Research on cislunar orbit determination behavior demonstrates that GMM-based filters can effectively handle the non-Gaussian uncertainty distributions that arise from CR3BP dynamics, where linearized covariance propagation fails to capture the true uncertainty evolution [25]. The Ensemble Gaussian Mixture Filter (EnGMF) approach extends this capability by combining ensemble methods with Gaussian mixture representations, enabling accurate uncertainty quantification while maintaining computational tractability for multi-target scenarios.

Multi-fidelity architectures operate on the principle that not all objects require the same propagation fidelity at all times:
*   **High-Fidelity:** CR3BP or N-body propagation is computationally expensive but necessary for objects on sensitive periodic orbits or those exhibiting maneuver behavior.
*   **Low-Fidelity:** Simplified two-body propagators with empirical corrections provide adequate accuracy for objects in stable regions or during periods of low observational activity.
*   **Adaptive Switching:** The key innovation lies in adaptive switching mechanisms that transition between fidelity levels based on uncertainty thresholds, observation availability, and mission-critical status.

Enhanced heuristic algorithms for optimal cislunar SSA architecture design incorporate multi-fidelity considerations into sensor placement and tasking optimization [1]. These algorithms evaluate trade-offs between SSA capability, station-keeping costs, and the number of satellites in a given constellation, generating Pareto-optimal solutions that balance competing resource constraints [27]. The optimization problem seeks to analyze these trade-offs systematically, recognizing that computational resources for propagation and estimation are finite and must be allocated efficiently across the cislunar domain.

The application of multi-fidelity propagation extends to initial orbit determination challenges specific to cislunar space. Adaptive approaches to the IOD problem using machine learning techniques can rapidly generate initial state estimates that are then refined through multi-fidelity filtering [1]. These methods approach the limitations of classical IOD by approximating solutions of nonlinear system trajectories using numerical integration, though chaotic dynamics such as CR3BP remain difficult to predict over long time horizons without frequent observation updates [25].

For multi-target tracking, the computational cost of maintaining custody grows exponentially with the number of objects if high-fidelity propagation is applied uniformly. Multi-fidelity architectures mitigate this by applying CR3BP or higher-fidelity N-body dynamics only to objects in dynamically sensitive regions (near Lagrange points, on manifold trajectories, or exhibiting maneuver signatures) while using simplified models for objects in more stable orbital families. This selective fidelity allocation enables scalable cislunar SDA systems that can track dozens to hundreds of objects without prohibitive computational overhead.

The distinction between SSA and SDA becomes operationally relevant in multi-fidelity propagation design. SSA-focused systems prioritize catalog maintenance and collision avoidance, favoring consistent moderate-fidelity propagation across all objects. SDA-focused systems, which include intent and threat assessment, allocate high-fidelity resources to objects of interest based on behavioral anomalies, orbital characteristics suggestive of military or dual-use capability, or proximity to critical cislunar infrastructure. This intelligence-driven fidelity allocation represents a fundamental shift from passive cataloging to active domain awareness.

Research on distributed space-based networks for cislunar SDA emphasizes the need for optimized sensor distribution strategies that account for multi-fidelity propagation requirements [47]. Sensor tasking must consider not only observation geometry but also the fidelity requirements of objects in the tracking queue, ensuring that high-priority targets receive the observation frequency necessary to support high-fidelity propagation and uncertainty reduction.

### Covariance Analysis and Observability Metrics for Periodic Orbits

Maintaining custody of objects on cislunar periodic orbits requires sophisticated covariance analysis techniques that account for the unique observability characteristics of CR3BP trajectories. Visibility-informed covariance analysis has emerged as a critical methodology for quantifying state uncertainty across lunar phases and observer geometries, particularly for objects on NRHO, Halo orbits, and other periodic orbit families near Lagrange points [3].

The fundamental challenge in cislunar covariance analysis stems from the angle-only observation geometry typical of optical sensors. Ground-based and space-based optical observers collect azimuth and elevation measurements that provide bearing information but no direct range data. For targets on periodic orbits defined by the CR3BP, this creates observability challenges that vary dramatically with the Sun-Target-Observer angle, which determines the amount of light reflected toward the observer and thus the signal-to-noise ratio of detections [41][3].

Research on visibility-informed covariance analysis for cislunar periodic orbits simulates month-long observation campaigns from equatorial ground stations collecting angle-only optical measurements of targets on CR3BP-defined periodic orbits [3]. The orbits analyzed typically include L1 NRHO 9:2 resonant orbits and L1/L2 Halo orbits, which inhabit three-dimensional periodic trajectories near collinear Lagrange points [3][41]. These simulations reveal that covariance ellipsoid evolution is highly sensitive to observer geometry, with certain lunar phases and observer positions providing significantly better observability than others.

Optimized sensor distribution strategies for cislunar SDA must maintain custody of objects of interest in L2 Lyapunov and Near Rectilinear Halo Orbits that are able to perform maneuvers at unknown times [30]. The sensor distribution problem becomes a multi-objective optimization that balances coverage of critical orbital regimes against the cost of deploying and operating sensors in cislunar space. Halo orbits are particularly valuable for observation platforms because they are three-dimensional periodic orbits near Lagrange points that provide a stable line of sight to Earth and the Moon, allowing continuous communication and observation without being blocked by either body [14].

Observability metrics for space-based cislunar domain awareness quantify the information content of potential observations before they are collected, enabling proactive sensor tasking that maximizes uncertainty reduction [28]. These metrics incorporate factors including:
*   Observer-target geometry.
*   Expected signal-to-noise ratio based on illumination conditions.
*   Orbital dynamics sensitivity to measurement updates.
*   The current uncertainty state of the target.

Advanced observability analysis recognizes that not all observations provide equal information value; a measurement taken when the target is near a dynamically sensitive region of its orbit may reduce uncertainty more than multiple measurements taken during stable orbital phases.

The covariance analysis challenge is compounded by the need to distinguish between SSA and SDA requirements. SSA-focused covariance analysis emphasizes position uncertainty for collision avoidance and catalog maintenance, typically requiring three-sigma position uncertainties below operational thresholds. SDA-focused covariance analysis must also characterize velocity uncertainty and maneuver capability, as intent assessment requires understanding not just where an object is but where it can go and how quickly it can change its trajectory. This expanded uncertainty characterization demands higher observation frequency and more sophisticated filtering techniques.

Research on cislunar orbit determination behavior demonstrates that processing observations of periodic orbits with Gaussian Mixture Model estimation filters can effectively handle the non-linear uncertainty evolution characteristic of CR3BP dynamics [25]. The GMM approach represents the probability density function as a weighted sum of Gaussian components, enabling accurate representation of multi-modal and skewed uncertainty distributions that arise from non-linear dynamics and sparse observations.

Initial Orbit Determination remains critical for cislunar SDA, as angle-only observations in cislunar space present unique challenges for establishing initial tracks on newly detected objects [45]. State transition tensors and uncertainty analysis techniques specific to cislunar space enable robust IOD despite the limited observability inherent in angle-only measurement geometries. The combination of IOD with subsequent covariance propagation and observability-informed sensor tasking creates a closed-loop tracking architecture that maintains custody even for objects on complex periodic orbits.

The operational implementation of covariance analysis and observability metrics requires integration with sensor tasking systems that can dynamically allocate observation resources based on uncertainty growth rates and mission priorities. Research on sensor tasking strategies for space-based observers examines the feasibility and efficacy of Moon-based optical observers for sensor tasking, as well as the selection of space-based observer orbits and the benefits of coupling space and ground-based sensors [18]. This multi-platform approach leverages the complementary observability characteristics of different observer locations to maintain continuous custody of cislunar objects across all lunar phases and orbital configurations.

## Architectural Design for Distributed Cislunar Sensor Networks

The architectural design of distributed sensor networks for cislunar Space Domain Awareness (SDA) represents a fundamental departure from traditional Space Situational Awareness (SSA) paradigms. While SSA focuses primarily on passive cataloging and tracking of Resident Space Objects (RSOs), SDA encompasses intent assessment, threat characterization, and operational intelligence within the cislunar regime. This distinction is critical as the cislunar volume spans approximately **three orders of magnitude greater space** than the near-Earth regime out to Geosynchronous Earth Orbit (GEO), demanding fundamentally different architectural approaches [28]. The design challenge involves optimizing sensor placement across specific orbital families including Near Rectilinear Halo Orbits (NRHO) and Lyapunov orbits while balancing coverage performance against station-keeping costs within the Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem (CR3BP) dynamical framework.

### Ground-Based Limitations and the Necessity of Space-Based Observers

Ground-based surveillance systems face inherent physical and operational constraints that render them insufficient for comprehensive cislunar SDA. The Space Surveillance Network (SSN) sensors currently employ predictive techniques to monitor objects, but these systems are fundamentally limited by the number of available sensors, the range in capability and availability of each sensor, and their geographic distribution around the world [16]. These limitations create significant coverage gaps in the cislunar volume that cannot be addressed through ground-based infrastructure alone.

**Primary Constraints on Ground-Based Surveillance:**
*   **Weather Conditions:** Atmospheric distortion, cloud cover, and precipitation introduce observation gaps that can extend for hours or days, during which cislunar objects may execute maneuvers undetected. Space-based sensors provide independence from weather conditions, offering unobstructed views of RSOs without the disruption due to weather or atmospheric distortion conditions that often plague ground-based systems [16][44].
*   **Day-Night Cycles:** Observations are restricted to nighttime hours when solar illumination conditions permit detection of faint objects against the dark sky background. For cislunar objects at lunar distances, detection becomes comparable to spotting a firefly in front of a searchlight, requiring precise Sun-Target-Observer angle management that ground-based systems cannot continuously maintain [14].
*   **Lunar Phase:** Bright lunar illumination can overwhelm sensor dynamic range and obscure faint RSOs in proximity to the Moon.

Current ground-based surveillance architectures create coverage gaps throughout the cislunar volume that compromise custody maintenance. Research indicates that cislunar space's growing importance necessitates robust SDA for safe and sustainable utilization, yet current ground-based surveillance cannot provide the persistent monitoring required [30]. The geometric constraints of Earth-based observation mean that significant portions of the cislunar regime remain unobservable for extended periods, particularly objects in orbits with high inclination relative to the Earth-Moon plane or those positioned behind the Moon from Earth's perspective.

The transition from ground-based to space-based observers represents a shift from passive, Earth-bound activity to an active, space-based discipline [14]. Space-Based Space Surveillance (SBSS) frameworks utilizing multiple Electro-Optical Sensors (EOS) demonstrate superior tracking accuracy through simulation case studies, validating the architectural necessity of space-based observers for cislunar SDA [44]. This architectural shift enables continuous observation不受 Earth's rotation, weather, and lunar phase constraints, providing the persistent coverage required for comprehensive cislunar domain awareness.

### Optimization of Constellation Placement Using Heuristic Algorithms

Determining optimal sensor locations within the cislunar regime requires sophisticated optimization techniques that account for the complex orbital dynamics governed by the Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem (CR3BP). Unlike Keplerian orbital mechanics that dominate near-Earth regimes, cislunar trajectories require multi-objective optimization algorithms to find Pareto-optimal placement of observers that maximizes coverage while minimizing operational costs [28]. Genetic Algorithms and related metaheuristic optimization techniques have emerged as primary tools for solving this architecture design problem.

The optimization problem seeks to analyze trade-offs between SDA capability, station-keeping costs, and the number of satellites in a given constellation. These objectives compete for resources within the optimization problem, requiring a Pareto front of optimal solutions to depict the feasible design space [27]. Hidden-Genes Genetic Algorithms have been specifically proposed to tackle the challenge of optimizing multi-spacecraft cislunar SDA systems, enabling exploration of complex design spaces that traditional optimization methods cannot efficiently navigate [47].

Specific orbital families serve as candidate locations for sensor placement, with L1 and L2 halo orbits, NRHO, and Distant Retrograde Orbits (DRO) representing primary options. Optimized sensor distribution strategies for Cislunar SDA focus on maintaining custody of objects of interest in the L2 Lyapunov and Near Rectilinear Halo Orbits (NRHO), which are able to perform maneuvers at unknown times [30]. The L1/L2 halo orbits inhabit families of periodic orbits defined by the CR3BP, offering stable observation platforms with predictable dynamics that reduce station-keeping requirements compared to alternative orbital configurations [41].

Station-keeping costs represent a critical constraint in constellation optimization. Operating in cislunar space requires vehicles that can perform frequent station-keeping maneuvers due to perturbations from the Moon and the Sun that constantly pull spacecraft off course [14]. In Low Earth Orbit (LEO), atmospheric drag is the primary perturbation source; in cislunar space, the gravitational perturbations from both the Earth-Moon system and the Sun create complex disturbance environments that demand continuous orbit maintenance. The optimization algorithms must account for these non-linear costs when evaluating candidate constellation architectures.

Multi-objective optimization algorithms have been used to find Pareto-optimal placement of observers within cislunar space that maximized coverage of trans-Lunar injection trajectories as well as varying volumes of the region, while minimizing the total non-linear cost associated with station-keeping and deployment [28]. The optimization metrics integrate sensor detection performance and orbit maintenance costs into a library that can assess the performance of distributed sensor networks consisting of observers spanning the cislunar volume [23]. This integrated approach enables architects to evaluate trade-offs between coverage completeness, revisit rates, detection sensitivity, and lifecycle costs across different constellation configurations.

The optimization process must also account for the detectability of the target deck, which varies based on sensor placement relative to common cislunar transit corridors and operational orbits. Research has examined formulations of sensor scheduling for SSA with solutions for both ground-based and space-based sensors, though relatively few in-depth studies have tested these approaches in operational cislunar environments [29]. The enhanced heuristic algorithms for optimal cislunar SSA architecture continue to evolve, incorporating multi-fidelity modeling and multi-target tracking capabilities to improve optimization accuracy [1].

### Emerging Capabilities: In-Space Assembly and Large Aperture Sensors

In-Space Assembly and Manufacturing (ISAM) capabilities represent a transformative technology for cislunar SDA architectures, enabling the deployment of sensor systems that exceed traditional launch vehicle fairing constraints. Studies investigating access to large optical and RF sensors through technology maturation in ISAM materials and designs indicate that diameters **greater than or equal to 100 meters** could enable persistent, accurate, and timely Cislunar SSA capability [39][38]. These large aperture sensors fundamentally alter the detection range and scan time characteristics for faint objects in the cislunar regime.

The detection challenge for cislunar objects requires sensors capable of high dynamic range imaging. Detecting a satellite at lunar distances is comparable to spotting a firefly in front of a searchlight, requiring sensors that can block out the brightness of the Earth and Moon while maintaining sensitivity to faint RSOs [14]. Large aperture optical sensors improve the signal-to-noise ratio proportionally to the aperture diameter squared, enabling detection of smaller objects at greater ranges or reducing integration times for objects of a given size. For a 5-degree field of view with a 10 cm aperture system, feasible detection parameters have been established, though ISAM-enabled apertures would provide orders of magnitude improvement in detection performance [41].

Radio frequency sensors complement optical systems by providing all-weather, day-night observation capability. Popular space-based Cislunar SSA sensors include both radio frequency (RF) and electro-optical (EO) sensors for providing search and detection capability across the cislunar volume [39]. ISAM-enabled large RF aperture arrays could provide enhanced sensitivity for passive detection of RSO emissions or active radar illumination for precise ranging and characterization. The combination of large optical and RF apertures through ISAM creates a multi-phenomenology observation network that improves detection confidence and characterization accuracy.

The Oracle Program sensor payload exemplifies the optical sensor requirements for cislunar operations. The optical suite consists of specialized sensors that must handle high dynamic range imaging conditions unique to the cislunar environment [14]. ISAM capabilities would enable deployment of aperture sizes that cannot be launched in a single launch vehicle, instead assembling large structures on-orbit from modular components. This approach reduces launch costs while enabling sensor performance that would otherwise be infeasible.

Research indicates that Cislunar SSA capability provides search and detection functionality that current architectures cannot achieve with conventional sensor sizes. The results from ISAM-focused studies indicate that Cislunar SSA through large aperture sensors could transform future space operations by enabling persistent monitoring of the cislunar volume [39]. Wide Field of View (WFOV) sensors complement narrow-field high-resolution systems, providing initial detection and cueing for follow-on characterization observations [14].

Electro-Optical Sensors utilized in Space Based Space Surveillance frameworks demonstrate the technology readiness of space-based optical observation systems [44]. ISAM maturation extends this capability to aperture sizes that dramatically improve detection ranges, enabling custody maintenance of smaller objects and objects at greater distances within the cislunar volume. The technology maturation pathway for ISAM materials and designs continues to advance, with ongoing research exploring the feasibility of both ground and space-based sensors for effectively surveying the cislunar volume [39].

The integration of ISAM-enabled large aperture sensors into distributed cislunar sensor networks represents a capability multiplier that addresses the fundamental detection challenges of the cislunar regime. By enabling apertures exceeding 100 meters in diameter, ISAM transforms the architectural design space, allowing fewer sensors to achieve equivalent or superior coverage compared to conventional small-aperture constellations. This capability shift must be incorporated into optimization algorithms when evaluating future cislunar SDA architectures, as the cost-performance trade-offs fundamentally change when large aperture sensors become feasible through in-space assembly.

## Autonomous Sensor Tasking and Resource Allocation Strategies

The transition from passive Space Situational Awareness (SSA) to active Space Domain Awareness (SDA) fundamentally alters the requirements for sensor tasking and resource allocation in the cislunar regime. While SSA focuses on cataloging and tracking known objects, SDA encompasses intent assessment and threat characterization, demanding autonomous systems capable of adaptive decision-making under uncertainty. The cislunar environment presents unique challenges: vast volumes, complex Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem (CR3BP) dynamics, sparse sensor coverage, and the potential for non-cooperative objects executing unanticipated maneuvers. Traditional rule-based scheduling algorithms, effective in the congested but predictable Low Earth Orbit (LEO) environment, prove inadequate for these high-dimensional, partially observable decision spaces. This section examines the mechanistic advantages of Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) for dynamic sensor scheduling, the mathematical foundations of multi-target tracking and observation association, and adaptive strategies for handling maneuvering objects that conceal their capabilities.

### Deep Reinforcement Learning for Dynamic Sensor Scheduling

Deep Reinforcement Learning has emerged as the predominant methodology for autonomous sensor tasking in cislunar Space Domain Awareness, offering mechanistic advantages over traditional rule-based and greedy scheduling approaches. The fundamental distinction lies in how DRL agents handle complex, high-dimensional action spaces that characterize cislunar sensor networks. Traditional myopic policies select actions based on immediate information gain or uncertainty reduction at each time-step, evaluating each possible action independently and choosing the locally optimal option [29]. This greedy approach fails to account for the temporal dependencies inherent in sensor tasking, where an observation decision at time t affects the value of observations at time t+1 through covariance propagation and target state evolution.

DRL agents, particularly those implementing Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithms, learn policies that optimize cumulative reward over extended horizons rather than immediate gains. Research from Massachusetts Institute of Technology demonstrates that DRL-based sensor tasking agents can manage the combinatorial explosion of possible sensor-target assignments that arises when multiple space-based observers must coordinate observations across the cislunar volume [2][9]. The action space complexity scales exponentially with the number of sensors and potential targets, creating a decision space that rule-based heuristics cannot efficiently navigate. DRL agents learn value functions that approximate the long-term consequences of actions, enabling them to sacrifice short-term information gain for strategically superior positioning that yields greater cumulative awareness.

The mechanistic superiority of DRL stems from its ability to learn representations of the underlying state space through deep neural network function approximation. Unlike traditional optimization methods that require explicit modeling of all state transitions and reward structures, DRL agents learn these relationships implicitly through interaction with simulated environments. This is critical for cislunar applications where the true dynamics involve CR3BP perturbations, solar radiation pressure, and third-body gravitational effects that are computationally expensive to model analytically in real-time scheduling decisions [16]. The neural network policy learns to recognize patterns in orbital geometry, sensor visibility windows, and uncertainty growth rates that would require extensive domain expertise to encode in rule-based systems.

Empirical comparisons between DRL policies and greedy baselines reveal significant performance advantages. When evaluated against myopic policies that select the best one-step-ahead action at each time-step, DRL agents demonstrate superior custody maintenance for objects in L2 Lyapunov and Near Rectilinear Halo Orbits (NRHO) [30]. The learned policies generalize across targets with different orbital characteristics, with deployment times for trained policies being substantially shorter than recomputing optimal schedules for new scenarios [29]. This generalization capability is essential for SDA applications where the object population may include newly detected uncorrelated tracks or objects that have executed unannounced maneuvers.

The sensor tasking problem is formally structured as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) or, more accurately for partially observable cislunar environments, a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) [16]. The state space includes the estimated orbital states and covariance matrices for all tracked objects, sensor positions and pointing constraints, and environmental conditions affecting observation quality. The action space comprises discrete sensor pointing commands and integration time selections. The reward function typically incorporates uncertainty reduction metrics, observation quality scores, and penalties for lost custody. DRL agents learn to balance exploration (observing poorly characterized objects to reduce uncertainty) against exploitation (maintaining custody of high-priority targets), a trade-off that rule-based systems struggle to optimize dynamically.

Recent implementations demonstrate that 8-bit and 4-bit quantized variants of Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (DCNNs) can execute onboard sensor tasking policies given reasonable integration times, enabling autonomous decision-making without ground-in-the-loop latency [41]. This onboard capability is critical for cislunar operations where communication delays between Earth and lunar vicinity sensors can exceed several seconds, rendering ground-based scheduling impractical for time-sensitive observation opportunities.

### Multi-Target Tracking and Observation Association Techniques

The foundation of effective sensor tasking lies in the ability to correctly associate new observations with existing tracks, a problem that becomes exponentially more challenging in the cislunar regime due to sparse observations, large state uncertainties, and the potential for multiple objects occupying similar orbital volumes. Observation association techniques must account for the unique characteristics of cislunar dynamics, where objects may follow complex trajectories governed by CR3BP mechanics rather than simple Keplerian orbits.

**Covariance-Based Track Association (CBTA)** represents the standard methodology for correlating uncorrelated observations to established tracks in both LEO and cislunar orbits [15]. The technique employs statistical gating based on the Mahalanobis distance between predicted and observed states, weighted by the combined covariance of the track state and measurement uncertainty. However, cislunar applications require modifications to account for the non-linear uncertainty propagation inherent in multi-body dynamics. Standard linearized covariance propagation, adequate for near-Earth orbits, fails to capture the rapid uncertainty growth that occurs near Lagrange points and along unstable manifold trajectories.

**Kinematic gating** provides the first filter in the association process, eliminating observation-track pairs that are physically implausible given the time interval between observations and the maximum plausible delta-v capability of the object. For cislunar objects, kinematic gates must account for the fact that objects near Lagrange points can exhibit significant apparent motion with minimal propulsive expenditure due to the underlying dynamical structure [1]. The gating thresholds must be widened compared to LEO applications to avoid false negatives, but this increases the risk of false associations when multiple objects occupy the same gate volume.

Non-kinematic heuristics supplement kinematic gating by incorporating additional discriminators such as optical brightness signatures, radar cross-section estimates, and observation geometry consistency. Research by Worthy III, Holzinger, and Scheeres developed optimization approaches for observation association that explicitly account for systemic uncertainty in electro-optical sensor measurements [25]. Their work demonstrates that association accuracy improves when the optimization framework incorporates uncertainty in the sensor calibration and atmospheric conditions (for ground-based sensors) alongside the target state uncertainty.

For maneuvering objects, traditional association techniques face significant challenges because the predicted state based on ballistic propagation diverges from the true state following an impulsive maneuver. Advanced methods employ multiple model filters that maintain parallel hypotheses about the object's dynamical behavior, including the possibility of recent maneuvers [1]. Each hypothesis generates a predicted state and covariance, and observations are associated with the hypothesis that minimizes the statistical distance metric. This approach increases computational load but provides robustness against unannounced maneuvers that would otherwise cause track fragmentation or loss of custody.

**Random Finite Set (RFS) theory**, particularly the Poisson Labeled Multi-Bernoulli (PLMB) filter, offers a mathematically rigorous framework for multi-target tracking that naturally handles the birth, death, and detection uncertainty inherent in cislunar surveillance [2]. Unlike traditional track-oriented methods that require explicit logic for track initiation and termination, RFS filters treat the set of targets as a random variable and estimate its posterior distribution directly from the observation set. This formulation is particularly well-suited to cislunar SDA where the object population may include newly launched spacecraft, debris from fragmentation events, and objects transitioning from Earth-bound orbits to cislunar trajectories.

The association problem becomes more tractable when sensors provide multiple observations within a single pass, enabling angles-only Initial Orbit Determination (IOD) that can establish new tracks without requiring correlation to existing catalog entries [25]. However, the computational cost of IOD for all uncorrelated observations scales poorly with observation volume, necessitating intelligent pre-screening based on observation quality and geometric diversity.

### Handling Non-Cooperative Objects and Unanticipated Maneuvers

The cislunar regime introduces heightened potential for non-cooperative objects that may execute impulsive maneuvers or employ concealment capabilities to evade tracking. Unlike the relatively transparent LEO environment where most objects are cataloged and their operators known, cislunar space may host objects with undisclosed missions, dual-use capabilities, or adversarial intent. Autonomous sensor tasking systems must therefore incorporate adaptive strategies that account for the possibility of unanticipated maneuvers and deliberate tracking evasion.

Adaptive sensor tasking strategies for non-cooperative objects differ fundamentally from standard custody maintenance approaches. Rather than optimizing for minimum uncertainty on known orbital elements, these strategies prioritize rapid detection of state deviations from predicted ballistic trajectories. Research by Ware, Correa, Akella, and Jones addresses this challenge through adaptive sensor tasking strategies specifically designed for tracking non-cooperative cislunar space objects [26]. Their work emphasizes the need for SDA capabilities that extend beyond passive cataloging to include active intelligence gathering about object behavior and potential intent.

**Reachability-based approaches** provide a mathematical framework for searching and detecting maneuvering cislunar objects by computing the set of all states reachable from a known initial condition within a specified time window and delta-v budget [5]. This reachable set defines a volume in state space where the object must reside if it has executed a maneuver within the assumed capability constraints. Sensor tasking can then prioritize observations that maximally reduce the volume of this reachable set, effectively constraining the possible maneuvers the object could have executed. The approach is particularly valuable when the timing and magnitude of maneuvers are unknown, as is typical for non-cooperative objects.

Maneuver detection algorithms employ statistical hypothesis testing to identify deviations from ballistic propagation that exceed the expected uncertainty growth. Traditional methods compare the innovation sequence (the difference between predicted and observed states) against thresholds derived from the predicted covariance [1]. However, these methods suffer from detection latency, as several observations may be required to establish statistical significance. Advanced techniques incorporate control distance metrics that solve uncertain two-point boundary value problems to estimate the minimum control effort required to explain the observed state transition [25]. This approach can detect maneuvers with fewer observations but requires accurate models of the object's dynamical environment.

The Air Force Research Laboratory's **Oracle Program** exemplifies the shift toward active cislunar SDA with capabilities designed to detect objects that do not correlate with known catalogues [14]. The Sentinel Function of Oracle continuously scans the cislunar region, looking for anomalies and uncorrelated tracks rather than maintaining custody of known objects. This paradigm shift from catalog maintenance to anomaly detection reflects the recognition that cislunar SDA must account for objects that may never have been cataloged or that may deliberately avoid detection. The system employs wide field of view sensors for initial detection followed by narrow field of view sensors for characterization, enabling rapid transition from search to track modes when potential threats are identified.

Cislunar adaptive filtering techniques incorporate maneuver reconstruction capabilities that estimate the timing, magnitude, and direction of impulsive maneuvers from observation data [1]. These filters maintain multiple dynamical models in parallel, including ballistic propagation and various maneuver hypotheses, weighting each model based on its consistency with incoming observations. When a maneuver is detected, the filter can rapidly converge on the post-maneuver state, enabling continued custody despite the discontinuity in the object's trajectory. This capability is essential for maintaining awareness of objects that may execute multiple maneuvers over extended periods.

The policy environment surrounding cislunar SDA introduces additional complexity for autonomous sensor tasking. The Traffic Coordination System for Space (TraCSS), managed by the Department of Commerce's Office of Space Commerce, is designed to provide basic space traffic coordination including conjunction and reentry notifications [20][37]. However, the transition of SSA data provision from the Department of Defense to the Department of Commerce under Space Policy Directive 3 has created data sharing challenges that affect sensor tasking coordination [35][33]. The DoD retains concerns about disclosing sensitive capabilities and methodologies, while commercial operators require timely, accurate data for collision avoidance. Updated TraCSS data sharing policies continue to evolve through public listening sessions, but the friction between national security requirements and commercial transparency needs remains a barrier to comprehensive cislunar awareness [20].

For non-cooperative objects, these policy frameworks provide limited utility, as adversarial actors have no incentive to share trajectory data or maneuver plans. Autonomous sensor tasking systems must therefore operate under the assumption that a subset of cislunar objects will not participate in data sharing arrangements, requiring independent detection and tracking capabilities that do not rely on cooperative data exchanges. This reality reinforces the need for the DRL-based adaptive tasking strategies described in subsection 4.1, which can dynamically reallocate sensor resources in response to detected anomalies without requiring external coordination or data sharing agreements.

## Data Fusion Methodologies for Enhanced Tracking Accuracy

Accurate tracking of Resident Space Objects (RSOs) in the cislunar regime demands sophisticated data fusion methodologies that transcend traditional near-Earth approaches. The transition from Space Situational Awareness (SSA)—focused on passive cataloging and tracking—to Space Domain Awareness (SDA), which incorporates intent assessment and threat characterization, necessitates fusion architectures capable of integrating heterogeneous sensor modalities while managing the computational constraints of deep-space operations. This section examines the technical foundations of data fusion for cislunar tracking, addressing sensor integration strategies, fusion-level trade-offs, and robust initial orbit determination methods suited to non-Keplerian dynamics.

### Integration of Heterogeneous Data Sources (EO, RF, Radar)

The cislunar volume, extending from geosynchronous orbit to the lunar sphere of influence, presents observation challenges that no single sensor modality can address comprehensively. Popular space-based Cislunar Space Situational Awareness (CSSA) sensors include radio frequency (RF) and electro-optical (EO) systems for providing search and detection capabilities across the expansive domain [39]. Each sensor type contributes distinct measurement characteristics that, when fused appropriately, yield orbit determination solutions with reduced uncertainty and improved custody maintenance.

*   **Electro-Optical Sensors:** Deployed in Space-Based Space Surveillance (SBSS) missions, these provide superior sensor resolution and tracking accuracy while maintaining independence from weather conditions that constrain ground-based observations [44]. However, single EO or infrared (EO/IR) sensor systems prove infeasible for comprehensive cislunar coverage due to geometric limitations and observation gaps inherent to angles-only measurements [23].
*   **RF Sensors:** The integration of RF sensors complements optical systems by providing range and range-rate measurements that directly constrain the radial component of the state vector, which angles-only observations cannot resolve without extended observation arcs.
*   **Radar Systems:** Contribute additional measurement diversity through direct range measurements and radar cross-section (RCS) characterization. Research into CSSA viability explores the feasibility of both ground and space-based sensors for effectively surveying the cislunar volume, with RSOs corresponding to radar cross sections that inform detection probability models [38]. The Marshall Space Flight Center has demonstrated fusion architectures that combine radar, optical, and passive RF measurements to improve SSA outcomes through complementary error characteristics [34].

The mechanistic advantage of heterogeneous fusion lies in the orthogonality of measurement sensitivities. Optical sensors provide high-precision angular measurements (azimuth and elevation) but suffer from range unobservability without multiple observation geometries or extended arcs. RF sensors, particularly passive systems detecting spacecraft telemetry, offer range-rate information through Doppler shift analysis. Radar provides direct range measurements but faces signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) degradation at cislunar distances. When fused within a unified estimation framework, these modalities produce covariance ellipsoids with reduced volume compared to single-sensor solutions.

Orbit determination forms a critical component of cislunar SSA metrics, requiring precise tracking and characterization of man-made objects in orbit [16]. The limitations on the number of sensors in the Space Surveillance Network (SSN), combined with the range in capability and availability of each sensor and their geographic distribution, necessitate predictive techniques that leverage multi-sensor fusion to monitor objects per observation cycle [16]. Accurate and continuous orbit determination of RSOs is critical for developing robust custody maintenance in the cislunar regime [44].

Sensor tasking strategies for space-based observers must account for the cislunar environment's unique constraints, including the examination of Moon-based optical observers and the selection of space-based observer orbits that couple space and ground assets effectively [18]. The integration of heterogeneous data sources enables distributed satellite systems to implement data fusion frameworks that maximize observation opportunities while minimizing redundancy [44].

### Measurement Fusion vs. Track-to-Track Fusion Trade-offs

Fusion architecture selection fundamentally determines the balance between tracking accuracy and computational efficiency in cislunar SSA systems. Two primary fusion levels dominate the literature: **Measurement Fusion (MF)**, where raw sensor measurements are combined before state estimation, and **Track-to-Track (T2T) fusion**, where locally generated tracks are combined at a central node. Within measurement fusion, MF-1 (centralized fusion of all measurements) and MF-2 (distributed measurement preprocessing before fusion) represent distinct implementation strategies with different resource requirements.

Simulation case studies demonstrate that **MF-1 delivers superior tracking accuracy** compared to T2T approaches, as the centralized processor maintains access to the complete measurement set and can optimally weight observations based on their instantaneous information content [44]. The accuracy advantage stems from MF-1's ability to process measurement correlations directly, avoiding the information loss inherent in compressing measurements into track states before fusion. However, this accuracy comes at the cost of increased communication bandwidth requirements and centralized computational load.

**Track-to-Track fusion** offers computational efficiency advantages in constrained environments where communication bandwidth between sensor nodes and the fusion center is limited. T2T architectures transmit compact track state vectors and covariance matrices rather than raw measurement streams, reducing data transmission requirements by orders of magnitude. This efficiency makes T2T attractive for cislunar operations where communication latency and bandwidth constraints are pronounced. The trade-off manifests as slightly degraded tracking accuracy due to the loss of measurement-level correlation information during the local track generation process.

Kalman Filter implementations process measurement data through time update (predictor) equations that propagate the state and covariance forward in time, resulting in a priori estimates of the current state variables and their uncertainties for the next time step [16]. The measurement update (corrector) equations then integrate new measurements into the state estimate. In MF architectures, this update occurs with all available measurements simultaneously, while T2T architectures perform sequential updates with track states from multiple sources.

*   **Extended Kalman Filters (EKF):** Process angles-only measurements extracted from simulated observing spacecraft with optical sensors to estimate target spacecraft position [17].
*   **Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF):** Inspects changes in target uncertainty through sigma-point propagation, providing improved handling of the nonlinear measurement models prevalent in cislunar tracking [29].
*   ** RMS Metric:** Assesses convergence before the state is updated, ensuring filter stability in the presence of observation noise [16].

Space-Based Space Surveillance frameworks utilizing multiple Electro-Optical Sensors demonstrate through simulation that MF-1 delivers superior tracking accuracy, while T2T provides computational efficiency suitable for resource-constrained implementations [44]. The selection between fusion levels depends on mission requirements: high-value target tracking in support of SDA objectives (including intent assessment) favors MF-1 for maximum accuracy, while routine catalog maintenance for SSA purposes may accept T2T's efficiency advantages.

Covariance-based track association (CBTA) strategies for cislunar orbits require careful consideration of fusion level impacts on association performance [15]. The traces of position and velocity covariance components serve as heuristic metrics to quantify spacecraft state uncertainty, with nodes excluded from analysis if state uncertainties exceed acceptable thresholds [3]. This uncertainty quantification directly informs fusion architecture selection, as MF architectures typically produce smaller covariance volumes than T2T equivalents.

### Robust Initial Orbit Determination for Non-Keplerian Trajectories

Initial Orbit Determination (IOD) in the cislunar regime faces challenges fundamentally distinct from near-Earth applications due to complex, non-Keplerian dynamics dominated by the Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem (CR3BP) [45]. Classical IOD methods, which assume two-body Keplerian dynamics, fail to converge reliably when applied to cislunar trajectories where gravitational perturbations from the Earth, Moon, and Sun create highly nonlinear state propagation. The chaotic dynamics inherent to CR3BP become difficult to predict over long observation arcs, necessitating advanced IOD methodologies that explicitly account for multi-body gravitational effects.

**State transition tensors (STT)** represent a critical advancement for robust cislunar IOD, enabling the approximation of nonlinear system trajectories through higher-order sensitivity analysis [45]. A state transition tensors-enhanced two-sided shooting approach provides robust angle-only initial orbit determination in cislunar space by iteratively correcting initial state estimates to minimize observation residuals across the entire observation arc [45]. This two-sided shooting methodology improves convergence characteristics compared to single-sided approaches by distributing correction authority across the observation interval.

The assumptions underlying advanced IOD methods do not preclude applications in traditional two-body dynamics but remain general enough to allow for rapid IOD with complex multi-body systems and maneuvering targets [25]. Optional inclusion of admissible regions further constrains the solution space, improving convergence probability when initial guesses are poor. Preliminary results show significant promise for reconstructing large maneuvers of cislunar spacecraft with limited information, such as angles-only measurements across observation gaps of days or weeks [25].

Machine learning techniques assist the complicated cislunar IOD process through adaptive approaches that learn mapping functions from observation sequences to state estimates [1]. Neural networks trained on synthetic cislunar trajectory data can generalize to real sensor observations when simulation parameters accurately capture the relevant dynamics [41]. This data-driven approach complements physics-based methods by providing rapid initial estimates that serve as seeds for iterative refinement through numerical integration.

IOD forms a part of the Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem framework, with algorithms developed to address the limitations of classical methods by approximating solutions of nonlinear system trajectories using numerical integration [1][25]. Linear state-space representations based on restricted three-body dynamics provide the foundation for covariance propagation in these estimation frameworks [47].

The algorithm produces accurate results without requiring a close initial guess for the spacecraft state, quantifying the accuracy and consistency of IOD estimates through statistical metrics [25]. This robustness to poor initial conditions is essential for cislunar operations where prior state information may be unavailable or unreliable. IOD focuses on improvements to accuracy and reliability, addressing the fundamental challenge that initial orbit determination faces due to complex, non-Keplerian attribute characteristics of cislunar trajectories [25][45].

Angular rates relative to the sidereal frame, measured in microradians per second (µrad/sec), inform detection scenario characterization for cislunar trajectories projected onto the lunar orbital plane [41]. These angular rate constraints directly impact IOD feasibility, as slower angular motion reduces observation geometry diversity and degrades state observability. Reachability-based approaches for search and detection of maneuvering cislunar objects complement IOD methods by defining admissible state regions consistent with observation constraints [5].

The integration of state transition tensors, two-sided shooting correction schemes, and machine learning-assisted initialization represents the current state-of-the-art for robust cislunar IOD. These methods collectively address the convergence challenges posed by CR3BP dynamics while maintaining computational tractability for operational deployment. As cislunar traffic increases with Artemis Program activities and commercial lunar operations, robust IOD capabilities become increasingly critical for maintaining custody of objects in the Earth-Moon system and supporting the broader SDA mission of characterizing object intent and behavior.

*   **Architecture:** The spacecraft architecture incorporates a network of subcontractors and vendors supplying the satellite bus, propulsion systems, and sensor payloads necessary for sustained cislunar operations [14].
*   **Strategy:** This acquisition approach leverages commercial space industry capabilities while maintaining military oversight of critical SDA functions.

Complementing Oracle, the **Silent Barker** satellite system represents another classified Space Force acquisition program focused on space-based surveillance capabilities [7]. While specific technical details remain restricted, Silent Barker contributes to the broader SDA architecture by providing additional sensor coverage and tracking capacity for objects operating in high-altitude orbits. The program exemplifies the Space Force's strategy of deploying multiple complementary systems rather than relying on single-point solutions for cislunar monitoring.

Contract awards demonstrate significant financial commitment to cislunar SDA infrastructure. The acquisition strategy explicitly distinguishes between Space Situational Awareness (SSA) and Space Domain Awareness (SDA), recognizing that cislunar operations demand more than passive cataloging of Resident Space Objects (RSOs). ### Standardization and Interoperability Protocols

Effective multi-national cislunar operations require standardized communication and navigation protocols that enable interoperability across diverse spacecraft systems and national boundaries. *   **Interoperability:** This layering approach maximizes flexibility and interoperability with commercial protocols such as TCP/IP, enabling integration of space-based systems with terrestrial network infrastructure [6].

This multi-lateral coordination is essential for cislunar operations where spacecraft from different nations will operate in proximity, requiring compatible communication systems for collision avoidance, emergency coordination, and potential cooperative missions.

CCSDS-approved publications covering Space Link Services are accessible through established distribution channels, with the Space Link Coding and Synchronization Working Group and Space Link Protocols Working Group maintaining separate but complementary standard portfolios [6]. Navigation standardization presents particular challenges for cislunar operations, where traditional Earth-centered reference frames prove inadequate. International cooperation frameworks extend beyond technical standards to encompass operational agreements for cislunar activities. Data format standardization remains critical for interoperability, with CCSDS Orbit Data Messages providing a common structure for ephemeris and tracking data exchange [6]. Implementation of these frameworks requires agreement on data quality metrics, uncertainty reporting standards, and update frequencies appropriate for cislunar dynamics where orbital prediction errors accumulate more rapidly than in low Earth orbit.

Security considerations complicate standardization efforts, as authentication and encryption requirements must balance operational security with the transparency needed for collision avoidance. CCSDS security protocols specify requirements for both uplink and downlink protection while maintaining compatibility across different national cryptographic systems [6]. This flexibility enables participation by nations with varying security policies while ensuring baseline protection against unauthorized command injection or data spoofing attacks that could compromise cislunar operations.

## Comparative Synthesis of Tracking Methodologies and Architectures

The cislunar regime presents unique challenges for Space Situational Awareness (SSA) and Space Domain Awareness (SDA) that demand rigorous comparison of tracking methodologies and system architectures. While SSA traditionally refers to the cataloging of objects and their locations, SDA encompasses a broader concept that includes understanding the intent, context, and operational environment of those objects [14]. This distinction is critical for military operations, as enveloping SSA into a broader SDA concept has driven the operational intelligence requirement to a higher level of fidelity and demand for the warfighter [10]. This section synthesizes evidence across three critical dimensions: propagation model fidelity, sensor network architecture, and tasking algorithm effectiveness.

### Accuracy vs. Computational Cost Across Propagation Models

Orbital dynamics in the cislunar regime fundamentally differ from near-Earth environments, rendering traditional two-body Keplerian assumptions inadequate for accurate tracking. The Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem (CR3BP) has emerged as the foundational dynamical model for cislunar operations, accounting for the gravitational influences of both Earth and Moon simultaneously [1]. Classical Initial Orbit Determination (IOD) methods approach limitations when approximating solutions of nonlinear system trajectories using numerical integration, particularly because chaotic dynamics such as the CR3BP become difficult to predict over long periods [25].

The trade-off between propagation model fidelity and computational cost represents a central design decision for cislunar SSA systems. High-fidelity models incorporating full n-body dynamics, solar radiation pressure, and third-body perturbations from the Sun provide superior accuracy but impose significant computational burdens that may preclude real-time applications or onboard implementation. Conversely, simplified models enable rapid computation but accumulate tracking error at rates that can compromise custody maintenance over operationally relevant timescales.

Multi-fidelity propagation approaches have emerged as a pragmatic compromise, dynamically selecting model complexity based on tracking phase and uncertainty thresholds. Research demonstrates that optional inclusion of admissible regions allows for rapid IOD with complex multi-body systems and maneuvering targets while maintaining computational tractability [25]. The SGP4-XP model, an extension of the standard SGP4 propagator, has been evaluated for cislunar applications but exhibits key sources of error when applied beyond its designed near-Earth regime [8].

Visibility-informed covariance analysis provides heuristic metrics to quantify spacecraft state uncertainty across different propagation models. The traces of position and velocity covariance components serve as metrics to assess whether state uncertainties exceed acceptable thresholds, informing decisions about model selection and observation scheduling [3]. This approach enables adaptive fidelity management, where computational resources are allocated to high-uncertainty objects requiring high-fidelity propagation while low-uncertainty tracks maintain custody with simplified models.

**Table 1: Comparative Analysis of Propagation Models for Cislunar Tracking**

| Model Type | Accuracy (Position Error Growth) | Computational Cost | Applicable Regime | Key Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Keplerian (Two-Body)** | High error (>10 km/day in cislunar) | Very Low | Near-Earth LEO/MEO | Fails to capture Earth-Moon gravitational interactions [45] |
| **SGP4-XP** | Moderate error (5-10 km/day) | Low | LEO to GEO | Not designed for cislunar dynamics; accumulating errors [8] |
| **CR3BP** | Low error (<1 km/day for periodic orbits) | Moderate | Cislunar periodic orbits (L1/L2 Halo, NRHO) | Assumes circular Earth-Moon orbit; ignores solar perturbations [3] |
| **Multi-Fidelity Adaptive** | Variable (optimized per object) | Variable (adaptive) | Full cislunar volume | Requires uncertainty threshold management logic [25] |
| **Full N-Body Numerical** | Very Low error (<100 m/day) | Very High | All cislunar trajectories | Computationally prohibitive for large catalogs [5] |

The evidence indicates that CR3BP-based models are essential for objects operating near Lagrange points or in periodic orbits such as Near-Rectilinear Halo Orbits (NRHO), where perturbations from the Moon and Sun constantly pull spacecraft off course [14]. For trans-lunar injection trajectories or objects in highly elliptical cislunar orbits, full n-body numerical integration remains necessary despite computational cost. The multi-fidelity approach enables systems to balance these competing demands, reserving high-fidelity propagation for objects with elevated uncertainty or those operating in dynamically sensitive regions near collinear Lagrange points [3].

### Centralized vs. Distributed Sensor Network Performance

Architecture selection for cislunar SSA/SDA systems involves fundamental trade-offs between centralized ground-based networks and distributed space-based sensor constellations. The cislunar volume spans approximately **three orders of magnitude greater space** than the near-Earth regime out to GEO, creating coverage challenges that single-sensor or ground-only architectures cannot adequately address [28].

Ground-based sensor networks, while benefiting from established infrastructure and maintenance accessibility, face inherent limitations for cislunar tracking. Atmospheric distortion, weather conditions, and daylight obscuration create observation gaps that compromise continuous custody maintenance. Additionally, the geometric constraints of Earth-based viewing angles limit observability of objects in certain cislunar regions, particularly those near the Moon or in orbits with high inclination relative to the ecliptic plane. Ground-based systems remain valuable for initial detection and coarse tracking but cannot provide the persistent coverage required for comprehensive SDA.

Space-Based Space Surveillance (SBSS) presents a viable solution for tracking Resident Space Objects (RSOs) in cislunar space, providing superior sensor resolution, tracking accuracy, and independence from weather conditions [44]. The Geosynchronous Space Situational Awareness Program (GSSAP) satellites demonstrate the advantages of space-based platforms, operating from near-geosynchronous orbits with unobstructed views of RSOs without disruption due to weather or atmospheric distortion conditions that often plague ground-based systems [16]. However, GSSAP communication flows through Air Force Satellite Control Network (AFSCN) ground stations, introducing latency and single-point dependencies that distributed architectures seek to eliminate.

Distributed space-based networks offer enhanced scalability and resilience compared to centralized architectures. Research on optimizing distributed space-based networks for cislunar SDA demonstrates that metrics for sensor detection performance and orbit maintenance costs can be integrated into optimization frameworks that assess observer placement spanning the cislunar volume [23]. The evidence indicates that comprehensive cislunar SDA is infeasible with a single Electro-Optical or Infrared (EO/IR) sensor system, necessitating networked approaches [23].

The integration of SBSS and Ground-Based Space Surveillance (GBSS) data surpasses the performance of GBSS alone across all evaluated data fusion methodologies [44]. This hybrid approach leverages the persistent coverage of space-based sensors with the aperture advantages of ground-based telescopes, creating a multi-phenomenology observation network. Multi-objective optimization algorithms have been used to find Pareto-optimal placement of observers within cislunar space that maximized coverage of trans-Lunar injection trajectories and varying volumes of the region while minimizing total non-linear cost [28].

Scalability considerations favor distributed architectures for several reasons:
1.  **Incremental Improvement:** Adding sensors to a distributed network incrementally improves coverage without creating single-point failure modes.
2.  **Onboard Processing:** Distributed processing enables onboard data reduction, reducing downlink requirements and latency. Research indicates that 8-bit and 4-bit variants of Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (DCNNs) would be capable of running onboard in Ultrascale-class hardware given a 60-second integration time, enabling edge processing for detection and characterization [41].
3.  **Dynamic Adaptation:** Distributed networks can adapt to dynamic threat environments by re-tasking sensors without central coordination bottlenecks.

Resilience analysis reveals that distributed architectures maintain functionality despite individual sensor failures, whereas centralized systems experience catastrophic coverage gaps when critical nodes become unavailable. The Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL) Oracle Program exemplifies a space-based sensor approach, utilizing Wide Field of View (WFOV) sensors to scan large swathes of sky for anomalies, then employing Narrow Field of View (NFOV) sensors for higher-resolution characterization once targets are identified [14]. This "search and track" method allows Oracle to maintain custody through a modular architecture that could scale across multiple platforms.

Policy and interoperability considerations significantly impact architecture selection. The Traffic Coordination System for Space (TraCSS), managed by the Department of Commerce's Office of Space Commerce, represents an effort to shift routine tracking responsibility from the Department of Defense to civil authorities [35]. However, DoD concerns about data-sharing have created friction in this transition, with the Department of Commerce addressing concerns about data-sharing protocols while planning to deliver conjunction data messages through TraCSS [36][19]. The Space Force has opposed cutting the TraCSS program from the Commerce budget, reflecting ongoing tension between military SDA requirements and civil space traffic management [32]. These policy dynamics necessitate architectures that can operate across classification boundaries while maintaining data integrity and operational security.

### Rule-Based vs. AI-Driven Sensor Tasking Effectiveness

Sensor tasking represents the operational layer where tracking methodologies and network architectures converge into actionable observation schedules. Traditional rule-based and greedy algorithms have dominated sensor resource management but exhibit fundamental limitations when applied to the complex, dynamic cislunar environment. Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) agents have emerged as superior alternatives, offering mechanistic advantages in handling complex action spaces, adapting to non-stationary environments, and optimizing long-term information gain rather than immediate myopic rewards.

Rule-based sensor tasking relies on predefined heuristics such as priority rankings, fixed revisit intervals, or simple geometric constraints. Greedy algorithms select observations that maximize immediate information gain without considering downstream consequences. While computationally efficient, these approaches fail to account for the temporal dependencies inherent in cislunar tracking, where observation timing affects future observability windows and uncertainty evolution. The myopic nature of greedy scheduling becomes particularly problematic for objects in chaotic CR3BP dynamics, where small timing variations produce large divergence in observation geometry and information content.

DRL agents overcome these limitations through several mechanistic advantages:
*   **Complex Action Spaces:** DRL handles complex action spaces by learning policies that map high-dimensional state representations (including object ephemerides, sensor constraints, uncertainty covariances, and environmental conditions) to optimal tasking decisions. Research from MIT and Georgia Tech has demonstrated DRL applications to space-based sensor tasking for SSA, with agents trained to optimize long-term tracking performance rather than single-step rewards [2][29].
*   **Exploration vs. Exploitation:** DRL agents learn to balance exploration (gathering information on uncertain objects) with exploitation (maintaining custody on known tracks), a trade-off that rule-based systems struggle to optimize dynamically.

Empirical evidence supports DRL superiority for cislunar sensor tasking. Studies on cislunar space situational awareness sensor tasking using DRL agents demonstrate that learned policies generalize across targets with different orbits, with the time to deploy a learned policy being very short [29]. These policies can be adapted to consider advanced scenarios involving unexpected target maneuvers or collision events, capabilities that exceed the adaptability of static rule sets. The University of Arizona has produced research on autonomous sensor tasking for SSA using DRL, validating the approach through electronic dissertation research that compares DRL implementations against traditional methods [16].

The mechanistic explanation for DRL superiority centers on three factors:
1.  **Value Functions:** DRL agents learn value functions that approximate long-term expected rewards, enabling non-myopic decision-making that accounts for how current observations affect future tracking quality.
2.  **Conditional Behaviors:** DRL policies can encode complex conditional behaviors that would require extensive hand-coding in rule-based systems, such as adapting tasking priorities based on detected maneuver patterns or threat indicators relevant to SDA intent assessment.
3.  **Simulation Transfer:** DRL agents can be trained on simulated environments that capture cislunar dynamics complexity, then transferred to operational systems with minimal fine-tuning, as evidenced by models trained on synthetic data generalizing to real sensor data when simulation parameters are accurate [41].

Sensor tasking strategies for space-based observers in the cislunar environment require consideration of observer orbit selection and the benefits of coupling space and ground-based assets [18]. DRL agents can optimize across heterogeneous sensor networks, learning when to task space-based sensors for continuous coverage versus ground-based sensors for high-resolution characterization. Research on adaptive sensor tasking strategies for tracking non-cooperative cislunar space objects highlights the need for approaches that extend SDA capabilities beyond passive cataloging to active intent assessment [26].

The computational cost of DRL training represents a barrier to adoption, but inference-time execution is comparable to rule-based systems once policies are trained. Furthermore, the ability to deploy learned policies rapidly across different operational scenarios offsets initial training investment. Others have considered formulations of sensor scheduling for SSA with solutions for both ground-based and space-based sensors, though relatively few in-depth studies have tested RL applicability across the full SSA problem space [29]. This gap indicates opportunity for continued research validating DRL advantages across diverse cislunar tracking scenarios.

The transition from rule-based to AI-driven tasking aligns with the broader shift from SSA to SDA. As SDA requires understanding intent and threat context rather than merely cataloging positions, sensor tasking must prioritize observations that reveal behavioral patterns, maneuver capabilities, and operational postures. DRL agents can learn to identify anomalous behaviors that warrant increased observation priority, supporting the intelligence requirements that distinguish SDA from traditional SSA [10]. This capability becomes critical as cislunar space hosts increasing numbers of objects from multiple nations, requiring awareness systems that can discern between routine operations and potential threats.

## Recommendations for Short-Term Tracking and Future Capability Development

This section provides actionable recommendations grounded in the analytical findings of previous sections, addressing the user's intent for short-term effectiveness in cislunar Space Domain Awareness (SDA). It is critical to distinguish between Space Situational Awareness (SSA), which typically refers to the cataloging of objects and their locations, and SDA, which is a broader concept that includes understanding the intent, context, and operational environment of those objects [14]. Enveloping SSA into a broader SDA concept has driven the operational intelligence requirement to a higher level of fidelity and demand for the warfighter [10]. The recommendations below prioritize immediate accuracy improvements, strategic infrastructure investments, and policy adjustments necessary for comprehensive awareness.

### Prioritized Methodologies for Immediate Accuracy Improvements

For near-term deployment to enhance tracking precision in the cislunar regime, specific algorithms must be prioritized based on their demonstrated accuracy and computational feasibility. The fundamental challenge in cislunar tracking stems from orbital dynamics that cannot be accurately modeled using traditional Keplerian assumptions. Chaotic dynamics such as the Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem (CR3BP) become difficult to predict over long durations using classical methods [25]. Consequently, all references to orbital dynamics in cislunar trajectories must explicitly account for CR3BP rather than generic two-body approximations.

**State Transition Tensors for Initial Orbit Determination**
State Transition Tensors represent a critical methodology for Immediate Orbit Determination (IOD) in cislunar space, particularly when working with angle-only observations [45]. These tensors enable rapid IOD with complex multi-body systems and maneuvering targets by approximating solutions of nonlinear system trajectories using numerical integration [25]. The optional inclusion of admissible regions further refines uncertainty bounds, making this approach suitable for operational deployment where computational resources are constrained but accuracy requirements are stringent.

**Adaptive Multi-Fidelity Tracking Algorithms**
Multi-Fidelity approaches deliver superior tracking accuracy compared to single-fidelity methods when integrating heterogeneous sensor data. Research demonstrates that the integration of Space-Based Space Surveillance (SBSS) and Ground-Based Space Surveillance (GBSS) data surpasses the performance of GBSS across all evaluated data fusion methodologies [44]. Adaptive Multi-Fidelity algorithms should be prioritized because they dynamically adjust model complexity based on available computational resources and required accuracy thresholds. This adaptability is essential for cislunar operations where sensor availability varies significantly based on orbital geometry and line-of-sight constraints.

**Comparative Assessment of Propagation Models**
The following comparison illustrates the trade-offs between propagation models for cislunar tracking applications:

| Propagation Model | Accuracy in Cislunar Regime | Computational Cost | Recommended Application |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Keplerian/SGP4** | Low (fails in cislunar) | Low | Near-Earth orbits only |
| **CR3BP Linearized** | Medium | Medium | Short-term cislunar prediction |
| **Multi-Fidelity Adaptive** | High | Variable (optimized) | Operational cislunar tracking |
| **Full N-Body Numerical** | Highest | High | Mission planning and analysis |

This comparison underscores that Keplerian models are explicitly noted in evidence as failing in cislunar regimes, while Multi-Fidelity approaches offer the best trade-off for short-term tracking [44].

**Uncertainty Management Through Covariance Analysis**
Cislunar SSA metrics are defined as finding, tracking, identifying, and maintaining custody of space objects [10]. Due to limitations on the number of sensors in the Space Surveillance Network (SSN), as well as the range in capability and availability of each sensor and their geographic distribution around the world, current SSN sensors use predictive techniques that require enhanced uncertainty propagation [16]. Visibility-informed covariance analysis for cislunar periodic orbits should be implemented to maintain custody of objects of interest in L1 and L2 Lyapunov orbits, where traditional covariance propagation fails to capture the true uncertainty evolution [3].

### Strategic Investments in Sensor Infrastructure and AI Integration

Closing current capability gaps in cislunar SDA requires strategic investments in both physical sensor infrastructure and artificial intelligence integration. The evidence indicates that single Electro-Optical or Infrared (EO/IR) sensor systems are infeasible for comprehensive cislunar coverage [23]. Distributed architectures combined with AI-enabled autonomy represent the most viable path forward.

**Space-Based Optical Networks**
Space-Based Space Surveillance provides superior sensor resolution, tracking accuracy, and independence from weather conditions compared to ground-based alternatives [44]. Investment priorities should include:
*   **Distributed Observer Constellations:** Optimizing distributed space-based networks for cislunar SDA requires multi-spacecraft monitoring to tackle the challenges of coverage and revisit rates [47]. Research proposes optimization problem formulations using Hidden-Genes Genetic Algorithms to determine optimal spacecraft placement.
*   **Oracle Program (formerly CHPS):** The Air Force Research Laboratory's Oracle Program, originally named Cislunar Highway Patrol System (CHPS), was rebranded to better reflect its mission of providing vision and awareness rather than just enforcement [14]. This space-based sensor initiative should receive continued funding as a cornerstone of cislunar infrastructure.
*   **Moon-Based Optical Observers:** Examination of the feasibility and efficacy of Moon-based optical observers for sensor-tasking should be pursued, as this offers unique vantage points for cislunar surveillance that complement Earth-based and Earth-orbiting sensors [18].

**Deep Reinforcement Learning for Sensor Tasking**
Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) offers mechanistic advantages over rule-based algorithms for sensor tasking that justify investment in training environments and deployment infrastructure. The superiority of DRL stems from several specific mechanisms:
*   **Handling Complex Action Spaces:** RL is widely applicable to the SSA problem because it can navigate action spaces that are too complex for rule-based systems to enumerate explicitly [29]. In cislunar space, where the number of potential observation targets, sensor configurations, and timing constraints creates a combinatorial explosion of possibilities, DRL agents learn policies that generalize across scenarios rather than relying on pre-programmed rules.
*   **Adaptability to Unexpected Maneuvers:** DRL policies can be adapted to consider advanced scenarios where one is interested in unexpected target maneuvers or collision events [29]. Rule-based systems require explicit programming for each contingency, whereas DRL agents learn to respond to novel situations through their training on diverse scenarios.
*   **Short Deployment Time:** The time to deploy a learned policy is very short once training is complete, enabling rapid adaptation to changing operational requirements [29]. This is critical for cislunar operations where threat environments evolve quickly.

Research from MIT and other institutions has demonstrated DRL applications to space-based sensor tasking for SSA, with work specifically addressing cislunar space situational awareness sensor tasking using deep reinforcement learning agents [29][9]. Investment should prioritize creating DRL training environments that accurately simulate cislunar dynamics and sensor characteristics.

**Onboard Processing Capabilities**
Enabling autonomous operation requires investment in onboard processing hardware and software. Evidence indicates that 8-bit and 4-bit variants of Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (DCNNs) would be capable of running onboard in Ultrascale-class hardware given a 60-second integration time [41]. FPGA toolchains for deep learning-based solutions to image processing problems should be developed to enable real-time detection and tracking without reliance on ground-based processing [41]. Models trained on synthetic data were able to generalize to data from real sensors as long as the simulation parameters were accurate, reducing the need for extensive on-orbit calibration [41].

### Policy Adjustments to Enable Robust Data Fusion and Sharing

Operational interoperability requires policy adjustments to facilitate seamless military-commercial data integration. Current policy friction represents a critical barrier to comprehensive awareness, particularly in the transition of SSA responsibilities from the Department of Defense to the Department of Commerce.

**Traffic Coordination System for Space (TraCSS) Status and Funding**
The Traffic Coordination System for Space (TraCSS) is being developed by the Department of Commerce's Office of Space Commerce to provide basic space traffic coordination, including conjunction and reentry notifications [20][37]. However, significant challenges exist:
*   **Industry Opposition to Program Cuts:** A broad industry coalition of seven trade groups representing some 450 space companies appealed to Congress to reverse the decision to cut the TraCSS program from the Commerce budget [32]. Space advocates argue that eliminating the program will increase the chance of collisions in orbit and undermine America's leadership role in international space.
*   **Phase 1.0 Rollout:** The Commerce Department planned to roll out TraCSS Phase 1.0, addressing concerns about data-sharing between departments [36]. Continued funding and accelerated deployment are necessary to realize the system's potential.
*   **Conjunction Data Messages:** TraCSS plans to deliver conjunction data messages as part of its core functionality, with listening sessions held on space data standards and formats [19]. Standardization of these messages is essential for interoperability.

**DoD-Commerce Data Sharing Challenges**
Space Policy Directive 3 (SPD-3) shifts responsibility for providing SSA data to satellite operators from the Department of Defense (DoD) to the Department of Commerce (DoC) [35]. This transition creates several challenges that require policy adjustments:
*   **Memorandum of Agreement Implementation:** A Memorandum of Agreement between Defense and Commerce departments represents a first step in shifting the responsibility for routine tracking of space objects to a civil authority [33]. However, operational details regarding data access, classification levels, and update frequencies remain unresolved.
*   **Unified Data Library Access:** The Government Accountability Office (GAO) recommended that the Space Force create a plan to determine how the Unified Data Library can have access and manage all of the data for the service's SSA systems [7]. The GAO also recommended that the Space Force establish a process to regularly identify and evaluate commercial SSA capabilities and how they could be operationalized.
*   **Commercial Data Operationalization:** The Space Force must better operationalize commercial data for SSA, as current processes do not adequately integrate commercial capabilities into military operations [7]. A catalyst campus in Colorado Springs was established to bring in commercial partners to try out capabilities against data and operators in the DEVOPS environment [10].

**Regulatory Framework for Data Fusion**
Policy adjustments should establish a regulatory framework that enables robust data fusion while protecting sensitive information:
*   **Updated Data Sharing Policy:** The NOAA Office of Space Commerce held listening sessions on updated TraCSS data sharing policy, indicating ongoing refinement of data access rules [20]. These policies should be finalized and implemented expeditiously.
*   **Human-System Interface Considerations:** Design of space data fusion and display methods must consider human-system interfaces to ensure threat analysts can effectively interpret fused data from multiple sources [40]. What may first appear as a routine system glitch may, in fact, be something much more serious, and providing the ability to detect and predict events via multiple data sources can be critical to mission success.
*   **International Coordination:** The United States should continue to make available basic SSA data and provide for basic space traffic coordination free of direct user fees while supporting new opportunities for commercial and non-profit products and services [37]. International coordination through bodies such as the Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems (CCSDS) should be leveraged to establish common standards [6].

In summary, achieving accurate cislunar SDA requires coordinated action across technical, infrastructure, and policy domains. Prioritizing State Transition Tensors and Adaptive Multi-Fidelity algorithms will deliver immediate accuracy improvements. Strategic investments in space-based optical networks and DRL training environments will close capability gaps over the medium term. Finally, resolving TraCSS funding and DoD-Commerce data sharing challenges through policy adjustments will enable the robust data fusion necessary for comprehensive awareness. These recommendations are grounded in current evidence and aligned with the strategic shift from passive SSA cataloging to active SDA that includes intent and threat assessment.
