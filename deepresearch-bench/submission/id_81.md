## Conceptualizing the Intersection of History and Collective Memory

The relationship between history as an academic discipline and memory as a collective social construct forms the theoretical foundation for understanding how societies engage with their past. This distinction represents fundamentally different modes of relating to temporal experience. History, practiced within institutional frameworks of universities, archives, and peer-reviewed scholarship, claims methodological rigor through source criticism and evidentiary standards. Collective memory, by contrast, operates through social frameworks that preserve, transmit, and transform understandings of the past according to the needs and identities of living communities. Maurice Halbwachs, the pioneering theorist of collective memory, established that memory is continually preserved through social groups rather than existing as individual psychological phenomena [7]. This foundational insight reveals that what societies remember, how they remember it, and what they forget are all socially determined processes serving present-day functions of identity formation, political legitimation, and group solidarity.

The tension between these two modes has intensified in contemporary public discourse. Museums, textbooks, commemorative practices, and digital media have become battlegrounds where competing claims about historical truth collide with competing claims about memorial justice. Understanding this intersection requires careful theoretical work that neither collapses history into memory nor treats them as entirely separate domains. The following subsections develop this conceptual framework by establishing the theoretical boundary between objective historiography and collective memory, tracing the evolution of historiographical methods, and analyzing how collective memory functions to define social groups.

### Distinguishing Historical Fact from Constructed Memory

The theoretical boundary between history and collective memory rests on their different relationships to evidence, truth claims, and social function. Academic history operates within what Collini describes as disciplines that "attempt to understand, across barriers of time and culture, the actions and creations of other human beings considered as bearers of meaning, where the emphasis tends to fall on matters to do with individual or cultural distinctiveness" [26]. This disciplinary commitment requires historians to subject claims to evidentiary scrutiny, acknowledge uncertainty, and revise interpretations in light of new sources. Collective memory, by contrast, serves the psychological and social needs of groups in the present. As Halbwachs theorized, collective memory plays a central role in how a society understands itself, which is why authoritarian regimes seek to align mass memory with the system's ideological demands [7].

This distinction does not render history objective and memory subjective in any simple sense. Historical scholarship operates within paradigms, institutional constraints, and cultural contexts that shape which questions are asked. However, the disciplinary infrastructure of history—including peer review, archival access, and methodological training—creates mechanisms for accountability that collective memory lacks. Memory operates through what scholars describe as "collective processes of remembering and of forgetting" that serve commemorative and identity-forming functions rather than truth-seeking ones [21]. When societies commemorate, they select elements of the past that reinforce group cohesion, often simplifying or mythologizing complex historical realities.

The Spadina Museum in Toronto provides a concrete example of how this distinction operates in institutional practice (见数据来源框). Research on the museum's decolonization efforts reveals that visitors showed deep interest in diversifying interpretations, yet communication gaps emerged between institutional intentions and public reception [5]. The museum's transformation from a preserved Victorian house representing elite domestic life to a site acknowledging enslaved and Indigenous presence required navigating between historical evidence and collective memory frameworks that had long excluded these narratives. This case demonstrates that museums occupy an ambiguous position between history and memory: they house material evidence and employ curatorial expertise, yet they also function as sites of public commemoration where collective identity is performed.

The stakes of distinguishing history from memory become particularly acute when national myths are examined. National narratives often celebrate heroes and triumphs while glossing over darker chapters such as colonialism, slavery, or internal conflicts [8]. This selective memory serves to create a sense of national unity but obscures historical complexity. The United States, for instance, has been described as built on narratives that have not fully reckoned with colonial and imperial history, visible in the lack of education covering these dimensions [15]. When historical scholarship challenges these selective memories, the conflict is not merely about facts but about the identity functions that those memories serve.

Yesomi Umolu's analysis of museums illuminates the ideological foundations underlying these institutions. She states that "museums are built on the ideological foundation of being repositories of knowledge and spaces of care" but their history is tied to "the colonial impulse to collect and amass objects (and therefore cultural knowledge) from the world over" [3]. The conditions of collecting upon which museums were founded are inextricably linked to colonial violence enacted on non-western bodies, spaces, and societies. Yet museums have long positioned their values and activities as apolitical acts of civic benevolence without probing their own proximity to power. This critique reveals how institutions that present themselves as neutral preservers of history actually participate in constructing collective memory that serves particular ideological functions.

### The Evolution of Historiographical Methods

The development of historical methodology reflects an ongoing negotiation between claims to objectivity and acknowledgment of partial perspectives. Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*, originally published in 1962, introduced the concept of paradigm to describe the set of practices that unites a group of practitioners within a scientific community [25]. While Kuhn focused on natural sciences, his framework has proven influential for understanding historiographical change. Historical scholarship operates within paradigms that determine which questions are legitimate, which sources count as evidence, and which interpretive frameworks are acceptable. Paradigm shifts occur when accumulated anomalies or new methodological approaches render existing frameworks inadequate.

The presentism debate exemplifies contemporary tensions within historiographical methodology (见数据来源框). Presentism is defined as an approach that evaluates historical texts and ideas through the lens of contemporary values and concerns, which the Cambridge School rejects in favor of historical contextualization [25]. James H. Sweet, president of the American Historical Association, bemoaned the trend toward presentism in an essay titled "Is History History?" arguing that defying neutrality defies the very essence of the profession [9]. This position reflects a commitment to historical contextualization that treats the past as fundamentally different from the present, requiring scholars to suspend contemporary moral judgments.

However, this critique of presentism has been challenged by scholars who argue that claims to neutrality often mask particular ideological commitments. Black historians have taken an active role in confronting political abuses of the past, exemplified by Du Bois's response to the Dunning School and the efforts of Anna Julia Cooper and Carter G. Woodson [9]. Du Bois's pioneering book, published against the backdrop of Black radical movements during the Great Depression, directly refuted the false narratives emerging from leading white scholars of the Dunning School. His work demonstrated that what appeared as neutral scholarship actually served to legitimize racial hierarchy and obscure the achievements of Reconstruction. This historical example reveals that claims to objectivity can themselves be ideological positions that protect established power structures.

The 1619 Project, created by Nicole Hannah-Jones for the *New York Times Magazine*, became a flash point in discussions of race during the Black Lives Matter movement (见数据来源框). The project centered United States history around the African-American experience, challenging dominant narratives that marginalized slavery's foundational role. The controversy surrounding the 1619 Project illustrates how historiographical debates become public battles over collective memory. Critics charged the project with presentism and factual errors, while defenders argued that it corrected longstanding omissions in American historical narrative. This debate demonstrates that historiographical methodology cannot be separated from questions about whose experiences count as historically significant.

Engaged pluralism represents one response to these tensions, acknowledging that multiple partial perspectives can contribute to more comprehensive historical understanding. James D. Sidaway is associated with engaged pluralism approaches that recognize the value of diverse methodological and interpretive frameworks [25]. This approach rejects the notion that there is one correct way to practice history, instead embracing methodological diversity while maintaining disciplinary standards of evidence. Scholarship engaging Indigenous perspectives exemplifies this shift, as public history practice increasingly questions colonial bias and processes at stake in institutions such as archives, museums, and universities [19]. The self-reflective approach of public history asks who is doing history, for whom, with whom, and for what purpose.

The evolution toward engaged pluralism also reflects changes in how historians communicate with public audiences. Historians writing for other academics make complex arguments with complex language that sounds like jargon to the uninitiated, yet scholarly debate is intended to further understanding of the past in ways that should inform public discourse [6]. Proposals for a Journal of Public Humanities envision a big-tent initiative featuring humanities scholarship for public readers, creative writing by humanities scholars, and articles about humanities practice [13]. This reflects recognition that historical knowledge must circulate beyond academic institutions to fulfill its democratic functions, even as this circulation creates new vulnerabilities to political pressure and censorship.

### Memory as a Vehicle for Group Identity and Solidarity

Collective memory functions as a mechanism through which social groups define themselves, establish boundaries, and foster solidarity through shared narratives of the past. Halbwachs's theory highlights that memory is continually preserved through social groups, and as people are generally ready to accept the past without questioning it, they also tend to show loyalty to those who present themselves as the heirs of that past [7]. This dynamic creates powerful incentives for political actors to shape collective memory in ways that legitimize their authority and marginalize competitors. In democratic contexts, multiple groups compete to establish their narratives as dominant, while authoritarian regimes seek to align mass memory with state ideology.

The relationship between collective memory and group identity operates through what scholars describe as shared recollection of past events that are relevant for the identity of a group [21]. These shared recollections are not simply aggregations of individual memories but emerge from social frameworks that determine what is memorable, how it should be remembered, and what it means for the present. Collective memory is constructed through narratives that influence understanding of political responsibility for historical injustices, as demonstrated in research on soldier victims in the Colombian Armed Conflict [20]. These narratives do not merely describe the past but assign responsibility, establish victimhood, and justify claims for recognition or reparations.

The Baldwin Hall Controversy at the University of Georgia provides a concrete example of how collective memory functions in institutional contexts surrounding reinterment of human remains [27]. The controversy involved remains of enslaved African Americans discovered during construction, raising questions about how institutions should acknowledge and memorialize those whose labor built them. As one dissertation on the subject states: "May this dissertation serve, in part, as an act of remembrance. May it stand as witness to the Black and Indigenous lives who shaped this place long before we arrived—and who still call on us to carry their memory forward." This framing reveals how memory work functions as ethical practice, calling present generations to acknowledge historical debts.

Vine Deloria Jr.'s work, particularly *Custer Died for Your Sins*, exemplifies how Indigenous scholars have challenged dominant collective memory frameworks that erased or distorted Native American experiences [20]. His analysis amends the politics of memory with a framework that attends to the complex relationship between narrative modes by which historical wrongs are represented and present attributions of collective responsibility. By viewing memory of historical wrongs not just as objects of historical study but as active forces shaping contemporary political relationships, Deloria demonstrated how collective memory functions as a vehicle for asserting Indigenous sovereignty.

The Vietnam War provides another example of how collective memory operates through competing narratives that serve different group identities. William E. Schlenger's work on *Veteran Narratives and the Collective Memory of the Vietnam War* examines how civilians grappled with questions of both U.S. involvement in Vietnam and the war's results in the years that followed [2]. Public memory and narrative methodologies of remembering and retelling Vietnam reveal how different groups—veterans, anti-war activists, policymakers, and subsequent generations—constructed competing memories that served different identity functions. Some narratives emphasized soldier victimhood, others focused on civilian suffering in Vietnam, and still others framed the war as a necessary containment of communism.

Memory politics varies significantly across political contexts. In Azerbaijan and Armenia, memory approaches are primarily built around war narratives and the cult of leadership, while Georgia, despite longer democratic experience, has not fully escaped the grip of political struggle and security dilemmas in its memory politics [7]. After the Velvet Revolution of 2018 in Armenia, although the legacy of former elites and issues of corruption sparked widespread public debate, no serious institutional reforms concerning memory were implemented. This pattern reveals that transitions to democracy do not automatically produce more pluralistic memory cultures.

The function of collective memory in fostering group solidarity carries both constructive and dangerous potentials. Shared narratives can create bonds of mutual recognition and support among marginalized groups, as demonstrated by Black historians' efforts to construct counter-narratives against the Dunning School's distortions [9]. Marion Thompson Wright, Dorothy Porter Wesley, and others joined Du Bois, Cooper, and Woodson in building institutional alternatives that preserved and transmitted African American historical knowledge. Yet the same mechanisms that enable marginalized groups to construct empowering memories also enable authoritarian regimes to construct nationalist myths that justify exclusion and violence.

## The Presentism Debate: Memory vs. Agendas in Historical Analysis

The debate over presentism—the practice of interpreting historical events through contemporary values and concerns—has intensified significantly between 2002 and 2024 (见数据来源框). This section examines the tension between memory as a collective social construct and history as an academic discipline, tracing how presentist approaches have been alternately condemned as methodological failure and defended as ethical necessity. The controversy reveals fundamental disagreements about whether historical scholarship can achieve neutrality, whether the past should be judged by present standards, and whose interests are served by competing approaches.

### The AHA Controversy and the Critique of Objectivity

The institutional crystallization of anti-presentist sentiment within professional history occurred through the American Historical Association. In 2002, Lynn Hunt, then president of the AHA, published "Against Presentism," arguing that presentism "besets us in two different ways: (1) the tendency to interpret the past in presentist terms; and (2) the tendency to focus only on those aspects of the past that are relevant to the present" [13]. This framing established presentism as a pejorative term within the discipline, defining it as "a bias towards the present or present-day attitudes, esp. in the interpretation of history"—a definition that originated in 1916 but gained renewed urgency in the early twenty-first century [13].

The controversy reached its apex in 2022 when James H. Sweet, president of the American Historical Association, published an essay titled "Is History History?" in the September issue of *Perspectives* (见数据来源框). Sweet bemoaned the trend toward presentism, arguing that to judge historical actors by contemporary moral standards is "to defy the very essence of the profession, one supposedly based on neutrality" [9]. This position reflected a broader concern within the profession that historical scholarship was becoming increasingly instrumentalized for contemporary political purposes.

The AHA's institutional response to external pressures further illuminated this tension. In March 2024, the AHA and the Organization of American Historians issued a joint statement on federal censorship of American history (见数据来源框). They reaffirmed their commitment to "historical accuracy, critical inquiry, and public service" while objecting to restrictions on terms like "diversity," "equity," and "inclusion" in government-funded historical work [4]. This statement revealed the paradox at the heart of the presentism debate: the AHA simultaneously defended professional standards against presentist distortion while resisting political efforts to sanitize historical narratives.

The Cambridge School of intellectual history exemplifies the methodological alternative to presentism, rejecting approaches that evaluate historical texts and ideas through the lens of contemporary values in favor of rigorous historical contextualization [25]. This school emphasizes perspective recognition as a core historical thinking skill, requiring scholars to reconstruct the decisions of historical actors by taking into account their specific historical context. The underlying assumption is that historical empathy requires suspending present-day judgments to understand past actors on their own terms.

However, this commitment to contextual neutrality faces practical challenges in public history institutions. The Spadina Museum in Toronto underwent a transformation beginning in 2023 under the City of Toronto's strategic plan (见数据来源框). Visitors to the museum expressed expectations of "truthful and real historical narration," yet the museum's efforts to diversify interpretations challenged traditional notions of authenticity by incorporating previously silenced perspectives [5]. This case illustrates how institutional mandates for inclusive history inevitably introduce presentist concerns about representation and equity into historical interpretation.

### Black Historians and the Rejection of Neutrality

The critique of presentism from the AHA provoked a powerful counter-argument from Black historians and scholars of color, who contended that the supposed neutrality of traditional historiography had itself served ideological functions. James Baldwin's observation that "History is not the past. History is the present. We carry our history with us. To think otherwise is criminal" became a foundational text for this position [9]. From this perspective, the claim to objectivity masked the ways in which supposedly neutral scholarship had long promoted racist narratives while systematically excluding Black historical experience.

The Dunning School of Reconstruction historiography provided the clearest example of how claims to scholarly objectivity served reactionary political ends. Named after William Archibald Dunning of Columbia University, this school portrayed Reconstruction as a tragic period in which Southern whites were victimized by Black political participation [9]. W.E.B. Du Bois's pioneering work, published against the backdrop of Black radical movements during the Great Depression, directly refuted these false narratives, demonstrating that the Dunning School's supposedly objective scholarship actually advanced a white supremacist political agenda [9]. Du Bois's response, along with the efforts of Anna Julia Cooper and Carter G. Woodson, exemplified how Black historians took an active role in confronting political abuses of the past.

Anna Julia Cooper's 1924 dissertation in history at the Sorbonne University in Paris directly condemned the institution of slavery at a time when European nations maintained colonial rule over millions of people of color across the globe [9]. Cooper wielded her scholarship as a tool against colonial exploitation, refusing to separate historical analysis from contemporary moral judgment. Similarly, Carter G. Woodson's work on Black history emerged from the conviction that the systematic exclusion of African Americans from historical narratives served to justify their continued subordination in the present. These scholars understood that the claim to neutrality often functioned as a mechanism for maintaining existing power structures.

The 1619 Project, initiated by Nicole Hannah-Jones with the New York Times Magazine, became a flash point in this debate during the Black Lives Matter movement (见数据来源框). Hannah-Jones won a Pulitzer Prize in 2020 for work that centered United States history around the African-American experience, fundamentally challenging traditional periodization and narrative frameworks [6]. The project's approach was explicitly presentist in the sense that it asked contemporary readers to reconsider the foundations of American society through the lens of slavery's enduring legacy. Conservative critics condemned the project as presentist distortion, while its defenders argued that it corrected centuries of historiographical neglect.

The long-term dangers of enforcing neutrality became evident in contemporary museum censorship. When curatorial choices are dictated not by evidence, scholarship, and historical truth but by political decree, curation slides into censorship [22]. This dynamic particularly affects the history of Black Americans, as political pressures increasingly demand the silencing or softening of narratives about racial injustice [22]. The Smithsonian Institution became a particular target, with critics arguing that what disappears from its exhibitions matters profoundly because of the institution's role in shaping national historical consciousness.

Black historians' rejection of neutrality thus rests on a historical materialist insight: the production of historical knowledge always occurs within specific social and political contexts, and claims to objectivity often mask the interests served by particular narratives. From this perspective, presentism is not a methodological failure but an honest acknowledgment that all historical writing serves some purpose in the present. The question is not whether history serves contemporary concerns, but whose concerns it serves and whether those concerns advance equity or reinforce existing hierarchies.

### Presentism as Political Tool versus Historical Necessity

The presentism debate ultimately divides into two distinct camps: those who view presentism as a political tool that distorts historical understanding, and those who view it as a historical necessity that acknowledges the inescapable situatedness of all knowledge production. This division maps roughly onto conservative critiques versus perspectives from literary studies and critical theory, though the boundaries are not absolute.

Conservative critiques of presentism in history education have gained significant political traction since 2022, particularly in Florida and Texas where curriculum laws explicitly restrict how certain historical topics can be taught. These legislative efforts frame presentism as an ideological imposition that corrupts students' understanding of the past. The American Historical Association and Organization of American Historians jointly opposed these restrictions, arguing that they deny the American people access to whole history [4]. However, the conservative position maintains that professional historians should resist the temptation to make the past serve present political agendas.

Literary studies and critical theory offer a fundamentally different understanding of presentism. In 1999, Bruce Robbins brought presentist analysis to Victorian studies in an essay titled "Presentism, Pastism, Professionalism," observing that a fetishistic pastism—the obsessive commitment to historical context for its own sake—could be as ideologically loaded as presentism [13]. The V21 Collective, which promotes Victorian studies for the twenty-first century, explicitly embraces the relevance of historical study to contemporary concerns, arguing that strategic, analytical, theoretical, and historical presentism are not problematic from the perspective of professional correctness when they serve pure research purposes [13].

This disciplinary divergence reflects deeper epistemological commitments. History as a discipline has traditionally claimed access to the past through rigorous archival methods and contextual analysis, positioning itself as capable of recovering what actually happened independent of present concerns. Literary studies, by contrast, has long acknowledged that texts mean differently in different historical moments, and that interpretation is always conditioned by the reader's historical position. From this perspective, presentism is not a distortion but a condition of being: we cannot escape our present when we read the past.

The museum sector illustrates how this theoretical debate plays out in institutional practice. Museums face pressure from multiple sources—visitors, donors, board members, and leaders of school groups—with nearly half of respondents in recent surveys reporting pressure to not publicly display art that might generate controversy (见数据来源框). The American Association of Museums issued a statement in 2025 on the growing threats of censorship against U.S. museums, noting that international museum sectors were watching events in the U.S. with increasing concern (见数据来源框). Museums are caught between professional commitments to historical accuracy and political pressures that demand either the sanitization of difficult histories or, conversely, their amplification for contemporary political purposes.

The Burke Museum in Washington state exemplifies one approach to this tension, seeking to become a space for healing and joy rather than merely a repository of objects [15]. This model creates space for Black, Indigenous, and People of Color artists to research and create within the museum, demonstrating that Indigenous art is alive and in constant creation rather than something of the past [15]. This approach explicitly rejects the notion that museums should preserve the past unchanged, instead embracing a presentist understanding that historical institutions must serve living communities.

The tension between presentism as political tool and historical necessity ultimately cannot be resolved through methodological refinement alone. It reflects a deeper disagreement about the purpose of historical knowledge: whether it exists to recover the past on its own terms or to serve the needs of the present. The evidence suggests that both positions contain partial truths. Presentism can indeed be instrumentalized for narrow political agendas that distort historical evidence, as conservative critics charge. But the claim to neutrality has also been instrumentalized to maintain existing power structures by treating contingent historical arrangements as natural and inevitable, as Black historians have demonstrated. The productive path forward may lie not in choosing between presentism and objectivity, but in acknowledging that all historical work serves some purpose in the present, and making those purposes explicit.

## Mechanisms of Ideologisation in Public Historiography

The transformation of historical inquiry into public historiography is rarely a neutral process of transmission. Instead, it operates through specific mechanisms of **ideologisation**, wherein the academic discipline of history is converted into a social construct of **memory** designed to serve contemporary political and cultural ends. While history functions as a critical discipline dedicated to evidence-based reconstruction and analysis of the past, collective memory operates as a selective social framework that validates present-day identities and power structures. This section analyzes the operational mechanisms through which public historiography is ideologised.

### Constructing National Identity through Selective Narratives

The primary mechanism of ideologisation in public historiography is the construction of national identity through the curation of selective narratives. States and cultural institutions utilize history not to reveal the past in its complexity, but to confirm present importance and legitimacy. This process relies on the distinction between history as an academic pursuit and memory as a collective social construct; where the former seeks accuracy through critique, the latter seeks cohesion through affirmation. National myths are frequently built on idealized versions of the past, celebrating national heroes and triumphs while glossing over darker chapters of history, such as colonialism, slavery, or internal conflicts [8]. This selective memory serves to create a sense of national unity, but it does so by excising the contradictions that might undermine the state's moral authority.

Theoretical frameworks surrounding collective memory, such as those pioneered by Maurice Halbwachs, suggest that memory is not stored individually but is reconstructed within social frameworks that prioritize group cohesion over factual completeness [21]. In the context of national identity, this reconstruction often defaults to Eurocentric or settler-colonial perspectives that validate the status quo. For example, the United States exemplifies a selective historical narrative where national identity is built upon contradictory elements that are smoothed over in public memory. The narrative of "American Exceptionalism" often requires the suppression of indigenous dispossession and racial violence to maintain the coherence of the national myth [14]. This **instrumentalisation** of history ensures that the past serves the future needs of the state, transforming historical events into tools for civic education that prioritize loyalty over critical understanding.

The mechanism of selection is particularly visible in how heroes of the nation's history are drawn from all periods to create an image intended to convince contemporaries of the might of the nation [26]. This process involves a deliberate narrowing of the historical lens. When museums and public institutions engage in this construction, they often mediate identity by utilizing art and narration that aligns with established power dynamics. For instance, the transformational strategy of institutions like the Spadina Museum involves including truths about racialized history, yet this often occurs within a framework that must first acknowledge the colonial perspective it seeks to dismantle (见数据来源框). The tension here highlights the difficulty of constructing identity without reproducing the very hierarchies that defined the past.

Furthermore, the construction of identity is not static; it is a site of contestation where **silenced narratives** threaten the stability of the dominant myth. Post-2023 discourse in public history has showcased diverse international perspectives, particularly from countries like South Africa and Brazil, advocating for participatory methodologies that challenge the monolithic national story [19]. However, the default mechanism remains one of exclusion. By framing the nation as a singular entity with a unified destiny, public historiography often renders marginalized experiences as peripheral or anomalous. This is not an accidental omission but a structural feature of how national identity is ideologised.

### Textbooks as Active Distorters of Public Memory

If national identity construction is the strategic goal of ideologisation, secondary education curricula serve as the primary tactical vehicle for its implementation. Textbooks function as active distorters of public memory, codifying selective narratives into the foundational knowledge of future citizens. The mechanism here is one of standardization; by presenting a specific version of events as objective fact, textbooks reinforce settler-colonial narratives and exclude competing interpretations. This is particularly evident in the treatment of conflict and expansion, where the violence of state-building is often reframed as inevitable progress or defensive necessity.

A critical case study in this distortion is the coverage of the Plains Wars in United States history textbooks. Research indicates that teaching materials often engage in a process of "teaching us to forget," where the systemic violence against Indigenous peoples is minimized or contextualized within a narrative of inevitable westward expansion [14]. This narrative choice is not merely a matter of space constraints but a deliberate ideological positioning. By omitting the perspectives of Indigenous communities or framing their resistance as obstructionist, textbooks validate the legitimacy of the settler state. The mechanism operates by establishing a "schematic narrative template" that shapes how students collectively remember the past, ensuring that the moral ambiguity of colonization is resolved in favor of the victor [21].

Similarly, the historiography of the Vietnam War in educational materials demonstrates how textbooks manage public memory to align with national self-perception. Textbooks discussing America's involvement in Vietnam often navigate the tension between acknowledging the conflict's controversy and maintaining a narrative of national purpose. For instance, analyses of textbook narratives reveal that while the war is acknowledged, the deeper questions regarding U.S. involvement and the nature of the conflict are often sanitized [2]. This sanitization serves to protect the national identity from the corrosive effects of "The Vietnam Syndrome," a term used to describe the public's reluctance to engage in military intervention following the war's unpopularity.

The role of textbooks extends beyond mere omission; they actively shape the interpretive frameworks students use to analyze history. When curricula prioritize Eurocentric narratives, they challenge the validity of alternative knowledge systems. Scholarship that attempts to decolonize higher education curricula notes that factoring in archives through public programming initiatives is necessary to counteract these distortions [19]. However, the standard secondary curriculum often lags behind academic scholarship, perpetuating outdated or biased interpretations. This lag creates a disconnect between the "history" taught in universities and the "memory" instilled in schools. The textbook becomes a site where the **ideologisation** of history is most visible, as it translates complex historical debates into simplified moral lessons that serve the needs of the state.

Moreover, the mechanism of distortion is reinforced by the authority granted to the textbook as an object of truth. Students are conditioned to view the textbook narrative as definitive, which makes the exclusion of marginalized voices particularly damaging. When the Plains Wars are framed solely through the lens of military campaigns rather than indigenous survival, or when the Vietnam War is framed through the lens of soldier sacrifice rather than geopolitical critique, the public memory is skewed. This skewing is not passive; it is an active process of **instrumentalisation** where education is used to produce citizens who accept the legitimacy of state actions without critical interrogation.

### The Role of Dominant Narratives in Perpetuating Inequity

The ultimate consequence of these mechanisms is the perpetuation of systemic inequity through the maintenance of dominant narratives. Dominant stories function to exclude marginalized experiences, often framing colonialism as a civilizing mission rather than a system of exploitation. This framing is central to the mechanism of **ideologisation**, as it converts historical oppression into a narrative of benevolence or necessity. By controlling the narrative, dominant groups can obscure the material realities of exploitation and present the current social hierarchy as the natural outcome of historical progress.

A stark example of this dynamic is found in the historiography of slavery and Reconstruction in the United States. For decades, the "Dunning School" of history dominated academic and public discourse, framing Reconstruction as a failure caused by Black incapacity and corruption. This narrative served to justify the Jim Crow era and the disenfranchisement of Black citizens. However, this dominant narrative was contested by Black historians who utilized history as a tool for political resistance. Du Bois's response to the Dunning School, as well as the efforts of Anna Julia Cooper and Carter G. Woodson, exemplify how Black historians have taken an active role in confronting political abuses of the past [9]. Woodson's work, specifically *The Miseducation of the Negro*, argued that the exclusion of Black history from curricula was a form of psychological domination.

This conflict brings into sharp relief the debate over "Presentism," a critical concept in understanding how narratives are policed. Presentism is defined as an approach that evaluates historical texts and ideas through the lens of contemporary values and concerns [25]. The critique of presentism, notably articulated by James H. Sweet, president of the American Historical Association, argues that defying neutrality to address contemporary political concerns undermines the essence of the historical profession (见数据来源框). From this perspective, history must remain distinct from memory to maintain its integrity. However, the defense of presentism, often advanced by Black historians and literary scholars, posits that neutrality is itself a political stance that favors the dominant narrative.

The framing of colonialism further illustrates this mechanism. Walter Mignolo's analysis of museums in the colonial horizon of modernity explores how institutions have been complicit in the colonization of power, knowledge, and being [3]. Acts such as rampant slavery were not only about economic exploitation but also about imposing a specific epistemic order. When public historiography frames these acts as part of a "civilizing mission," it erases the violence of that imposition. This erasure is a form of **silenced narrative** that maintains inequity by denying the legitimacy of the oppressed group's experience. For example, narrative modes such as romance, tragedy, and satire engender distinct political understandings, affecting how collectivities perceive their obligations to address historical wrongs [20].

Furthermore, the perpetuation of inequity is sustained by the exclusion of non-Indigenous narratives of healing and closure that do not align with state interests. Truth and reconciliation processes, for instance, are often critiqued for being influenced by narratives that prioritize closure over justice. Tony Bennett highlights that museums must navigate social divisions marked by race, class, and gender while adapting to a globally changing public sphere [5]. When they fail to do so, they reinforce the colonial modernity that produced the inequity in the first place. The mechanism here is one of containment; marginalized experiences are allowed into the public sphere only insofar as they do not threaten the foundational myths of the state.

In conclusion, the mechanisms of **ideologisation** in public historiography are multifaceted, operating through the construction of identity, the standardization of education, and the policing of narrative boundaries. By distinguishing between history as a discipline and memory as a construct, it becomes possible to see how the past is **instrumentalised** to serve present power structures. The selective narratives that build national identity, the textbooks that distort public memory, and the dominant stories that perpetuate inequity are all interconnected components of a system that seeks to maintain hegemony.

## State Instrumentalisation of Collective Memory

The instrumentalisation of collective memory represents one of the most potent mechanisms through which states shape political legitimacy, national identity, and social cohesion. Unlike history as an academic discipline grounded in evidentiary standards and methodological rigour, memory functions as a collective social construct that can be mobilised, manipulated, and deployed for political ends. This section examines how states across different regime types instrumentalise collective memory.

### Memory Control in Post-Soviet Authoritarian Regimes

Post-Soviet authoritarian regimes demonstrate some of the most explicit forms of state instrumentalisation of collective memory, employing what researchers describe as "historical engineering" to legitimise power structures and consolidate national identity around carefully curated narratives. The collapse of the USSR created an ideological vacuum that newly emerged states sought to fill, often through artificially constructed historical continuities that served immediate political purposes rather than evidentiary accuracy. Where actual historical foundations proved insufficient, regimes systematically filled the vacuum with invented traditions and mythologised leadership narratives presented as "historical continuity." [7]

The "savior leader" narrative represents a central mechanism in this process of memory control. In Azerbaijan, Heydar Aliyev's return in 1993 is presented as the historical moment when the state was saved from collapse, despite the complexity of actual historical circumstances. Large-scale annual events, state-produced documentaries, and school textbook narratives all serve the same purpose: to promote Aliyev's rule as the sole protector of national statehood. [7] After the victory in the 2020 war, the narrative that Azerbaijan's successes are solely linked to the Aliyev family was further deepened, with official discourse emphasising concepts of "territorial integrity" and "restoration of historical justice." [7]

Russia under Vladimir Putin offers another prominent example of authoritarian memory control centred on the myth of the "Great Patriotic War." Victory Day on May 9th has been elevated into a foundational national ritual that serves multiple political functions: legitimising current leadership, fostering national unity, and justifying contemporary foreign policy positions. The appropriation of this memory transforms historical commemoration into a vehicle for present-day political mobilisation. [7] This exemplifies how authoritarian regimes convert collective memory into what scholars term "victory memory"—a selectively curated narrative that emphasises triumph while silencing narratives of suffering, complexity, or moral ambiguity.

Turkmenistan under Saparmurat Niyazov demonstrates an even more extreme form of memory instrumentalisation through the cult of personality embedded in national commemorative practices. Traditions formed under Niyazov—such as the "Ruhnama Commemoration Ceremony" and the official celebration of the leader's birthday—were not based on actual historical facts but were presented as "foundations of national culture." [7] The aim was explicit: to further develop the cult of personality and embed the leader's authority into the very fabric of national identity.

The comparative picture across the Post-Soviet space reveals important variations even within authoritarian contexts. While Azerbaijan and Armenia built their memory politics primarily around war narratives and the cult of leadership, Georgia's experience demonstrates that even states with longer democratic exposure have not fully escaped the grip of political struggle over memory. Georgia's memory politics has not fully escaped security dilemmas and political contestation, suggesting that the instrumentalisation of memory persists even in hybrid regimes. [7] This indicates that the boundary between authoritarian and democratic memory politics is not absolute but exists on a continuum of state control.

### Democratic Memory Politics and Partisan Culture Wars

In liberal democracies, the instrumentalisation of collective memory operates through fundamentally different mechanisms than in authoritarian regimes, yet the underlying political dynamics reveal striking parallels. Rather than overt state control, democratic memory politics manifests through partisan culture wars, curriculum debates, and institutional pressures that shape which narratives gain prominence in public discourse.

The 1619 Project, initiated by Nicole Hannah-Jones with the New York Times Magazine, exemplifies how memory politics operates in contemporary American democracy (见数据来源框). The project centred United States history around the African-American experience and slavery's foundational role, winning a Pulitzer Prize in 2020. [6] However, it simultaneously became a flashpoint in discussion of race during the Black Lives Matter movement, triggering intense partisan debate over curriculum content and historical interpretation. This case demonstrates how democratic memory politics functions through public contestation rather than state decree.

The partisan dimension of American memory politics intensified during the Trump administration, which issued executive orders criticising institutions such as the Smithsonian Institute for portraying America's "unpatriotic" narratives (见数据来源框). [16] This represents a form of democratic instrumentalisation that approaches authoritarian methods—using executive power to pressure cultural institutions into conforming with preferred historical narratives. The administration's framing positioned certain historical interpretations as threats to national identity.

Textbook controversies provide another window into democratic memory politics. Research on United States history textbooks reveals how selective historical narratives perpetuate inequity by celebrating national heroes and triumphs while glossing over darker chapters such as colonialism, slavery, or internal conflicts. [8] The case of Plains Wars representation in textbooks demonstrates how public memory can be shaped through educational materials that teach students to forget certain historical realities. [14]

Museum censorship surveys from 2024-2025 provide contemporary evidence of the pressures facing cultural institutions in democratic contexts (见数据来源框). PEN America's reporting found that the nation's most aggressive book banning proposals appear in the legislation of states with Republican control of both the legislature and executive, particularly in states like West Virginia. [12] The American Alliance of Museums (AAM) issued statements on the growing threats of censorship against U.S. museums, calling on all who value shared heritage to support the museum field in resisting censorship. [18]

The incentives driving memory politics in democratic contexts differ markedly from authoritarian regimes but produce similar outcomes in terms of narrative control. Jason Steinhauer noted that the incentives of the social web can be very different from the incentives of the museum or classroom, creating new dynamics in how historical knowledge circulates. [6] Social media platforms amplify partisan interpretations of history, rewarding simplified narratives that confirm existing political identities rather than complex historical analysis. This creates what scholars term "hybrid journalism"—the overlap in public discourse between history and contemporary political commentary—where historical claims become weapons in culture wars. [6]

### Policy Implications of Historical Memory in Liberal Democracies

The instrumentalisation of collective memory in liberal democracies carries significant policy implications, particularly regarding how societies address historical wrongs and assign political responsibility. Memory and justice are intricately linked: in order to adequately address historical wrongs, liberal democracies must develop frameworks that connect collective memory to contemporary notions of political responsibility. [20] This connection shapes policy debates over reparations, truth commissions, and institutional reform.

The reparations discourse in the United States exemplifies how memory of historical wrongs shapes contemporary policy debates. The framework for addressing historical injustice requires attending to the complex relationship between the narrative modes by which historical wrongs are represented and present attributions of collective responsibility. [20] By viewing memory of historical wrongs not just as objects of historical study but as active forces shaping political claims, policymakers can develop more nuanced approaches to historical justice. The Case for Reparations, discussed in public humanities discourse, represents one attempt to connect historical memory to contemporary policy proposals. [13]

Public memory holds potential to facilitate reconciliation with the past, but this potential depends on how narratives of historical wrongs are constructed and deployed. [14] When public memory acknowledges silenced narratives and incorporates multiple perspectives, it can serve as a foundation for policy approaches that address historical inequities. However, when public memory remains selective—celebrating triumphs while obscuring injustices—it perpetuates the very inequities that policy seeks to address.

The policy implications extend beyond reparations to encompass broader questions of institutional reform and curricular standards. Museums and educational institutions face growing pressure to diversify storytelling and incorporate previously silenced narratives. Research on decolonizing museums emphasizes moving away from narratives told by oppressors toward frameworks that honor a plurality of perspectives. [15] This shift carries policy implications for funding, governance, and curatorial practices across the cultural sector.

The tension between academic freedom and political pressure creates additional policy challenges. When museums face censorship threats, the gray zone between curation and censorship becomes most leveraged and exploited by political actors. [12] Policy recommendations emerging from recent surveys suggest that museums create written censorship policies to guide their response to challenges and complaints, including under what conditions they might take action or alter exhibitions. [1] Such procedures can serve as vital protections for institutional independence.

The comparative analysis between authoritarian and democratic memory politics reveals important lessons for policy development. Authoritarian regimes demonstrate the dangers of complete state control over historical narrative, where memory becomes purely instrumental to regime survival. Democratic contexts, while offering greater space for contestation, face their own challenges as partisan actors seek to instrumentalise memory for electoral advantage. Effective policy must navigate between these extremes—protecting institutional independence while remaining responsive to legitimate public concerns about historical representation.

The long-term dangers of memory instrumentalisation include the silencing or softening of the history of marginalized groups, which perpetuates inequity across generations. [22] Policy approaches that prioritise evidentiary standards, multiple perspectives, and institutional independence offer the most promising path forward. As the AAM statement emphasised, museums educate, connect, and help societies understand one another—functions that depend on protecting cultural institutions from political instrumentalisation while remaining engaged with public debates over historical meaning. [18]

## Silenced Narratives and Strategies of Narrative Reclamation

### Defining Historical Silence as Active Suppression

Historical silence operates not as a passive absence of documentation but as an active mechanism of erasure systematically deployed against marginalized populations. This process of suppression targets women, Indigenous peoples, and colonized groups through institutional practices that privilege dominant narratives while rendering alternative experiences invisible. The intersectional nature of historical silencing means that voices marginalized along multiple dimensions face compounded erasure, as evidenced by how the history of Black women in the Civil Rights Movement is often overshadowed by narratives focusing primarily on male leaders [8]. The Austin Family case exemplifies this dynamic, where archival records and public commemorations reflect intersectional silence that obscures the contributions of women and people of color within historically significant households [8].

Museums and archives, traditionally positioned as neutral repositories of knowledge, are built upon ideological foundations inextricably linked to colonial violence enacted on non-Western bodies, spaces, and societies. Yesomi Umolu articulates that museums have long positioned their values and activities as apolitical acts of civic benevolence without probing their own proximity to power, yet their history is tied to the colonial impulse to collect and amass objects and cultural knowledge from the world over [3]. This colonial infrastructure of knowledge production actively suppresses narratives that challenge established power structures, creating what scholars term "historical silences" that perpetuate inequity across generations.

Authoritarian regimes demonstrate the most explicit forms of active suppression, where historical memory is shaped to serve political continuity rather than factual accuracy. In post-Soviet contexts, if there is no actual historical foundation, the vacuum is artificially filled and eventually presented as historical continuity, a trend clearly visible in regimes that seek to fill ideological gaps with manufactured narratives [7]. The United States has not fully reckoned with its colonial past, as evidenced by the lack of education covering the colonial and imperial history of the nation, suggesting that silence operates even in democratic contexts where suppression is less overt but equally effective [15].

Contemporary censorship pressures reveal the ongoing nature of active suppression in cultural institutions. Recent surveys from PEN America and the Association of Art Museum Directors document rising censorship threats, with nearly half of museum respondents reporting pressure to not publicly display certain works or narratives (见数据来源框). These pressures originate from museum visitors, donors, board members, and leaders of school groups, demonstrating how silencing operates through multiple channels of institutional influence. One curator reported experiencing considerable pressure from donors and some members of the public not to exhibit the work of Palestinian artists, especially if it addressed the Israeli-Palestinian conflict or the history of Israel [12]. The American Alliance of Museums issued statements on growing threats of censorship against U.S. museums in 2025, calling on all who value shared heritage to support the museum field in resisting censorship so institutions can continue to educate, connect, and inspire (见数据来源框).

### Post-Colonial Resistance and Counter-Archives

Post-colonial societies have developed sophisticated mechanisms of resistance through counter-archives that challenge dominant historical narratives and reconstruct silenced histories. These counter-archives utilize oral traditions, literature, and digital tools to affirm identity and restore agency to communities whose histories were systematically erased during colonial occupation. The self-reflective approach of public history questions the colonial bias and processes at stake in institutions such as archives, museums, and universities, asking fundamentally who is doing history, for whom, with whom, and for what purpose [19].

South Africa exemplifies how post-colonial societies contribute to public history through decolonization initiatives that factor in archives through public programming. Decolonizing higher education curricula requires transforming institutional structures that perpetuate colonial knowledge hierarchies, with scholarship increasingly engaging Indigenous perspectives that were previously excluded from academic discourse [19]. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission model, while critiqued for non-Indigenous narratives of healing, nevertheless created institutional space for previously silenced voices to enter public memory.

Digital platforms have emerged as crucial tools for counter-archive construction, enabling communities to bypass traditional gatekeepers of historical knowledge. The Open Journal Systems and PKP publishing infrastructure provides platforms and workflows that allow marginalized scholars to disseminate research outside conventional academic channels [17]. In post-colonial societies, historical narratives of resistance analyze the role of memory in reconstructing national identity, with digital tools enabling communities to document oral histories that would otherwise remain vulnerable to erasure.

The Spadina Museum in Toronto demonstrates how house museums can mediate colonial narratives by utilizing art and narration to diversify storytelling (见数据来源框). The museum's transformational strategy includes real and imagined accounts of marginalized people's experiences, dismantling colonial perspectives by incorporating truths about racialized history and even incorporating an imagined Afrofuturism lens [5]. This approach acknowledges Tony Bennett's observation that museums must navigate social divisions marked by race, class, and gender while adapting to a globally changing public sphere, confronting their historical associations with colonial modernity and adopting interactive and multi-sensory approaches [5].

Indigenous communities have pioneered counter-archive methodologies that center resilience and ongoing presence rather than narratives of disappearance. By critically interrogating the colonial roots of museum practices, institutions can begin to imagine frameworks that honor a plurality of perspectives and foster genuine understanding [3]. Rose B. Simpson's work upends narratives of supposed finality, challenging the tired colonial narrative of erasure that continues to shape institutional practices. The Walker Art Center Archives and similar institutions are increasingly pressured to acknowledge how their collections were acquired through colonial violence, creating space for repatriation and narrative reclamation [3].

Oral history projects function as living counter-archives that resist the fixity of written colonial records. Abir-Am and Elliott note that museums, archives, oral histories, and biographical dictionaries all serve as sites where collective memory is constructed and contested [26]. These oral traditions carry cultural truths implicated in audience group membership, meaning that a narrative does not have to appeal to broader societal truth but rather to the cultural truths of specific communities [2]. This epistemological shift validates Indigenous knowledge systems that were previously dismissed as non-historical.

Digital resistance extends to social media platforms where communities create alternative archives outside institutional control. Jason Steinhauer notes that incentives of the social web can be very different from the incentives of the museum or classroom, enabling rapid dissemination of counter-narratives that challenge official histories [6]. Journalistic venues like The New Yorker and The Atlantic, along with born-digital ventures like The Los Angeles Review of Books and Zócalo Public Square, draw heavily from academia while reaching broader publics, creating hybrid spaces where scholarly counter-archives meet popular discourse [13].

### Afro-Latina and Indigenous Acts of Narrative Healing

Afro-Latina and Indigenous communities employ historiographic metafiction and digital resistance as specific strategies for confronting cultural trauma and reclaiming spiritual epistemologies. These acts of narrative healing move beyond victimhood to demonstrate agency in reconstructing identity through literary and digital interventions that restore severed connections to ancestral knowledge. Contemporary Afro-Latinx literature, including works like Um Defeito de Cor, anchors literary analysis that offers a foundation for theorizing Afro-Latinx identity, memory, and resistance [11].

The works of Ana Maria Gonçalves and Dahlma Llanos-Figueroa exemplify how historiographic metafiction operates as a tool for historical resistance and sacred archive reconstruction. Their literary projects frame Afro-Latinidad through spiritual memory and the digital frontier, approaching both printed novels and digital media as interconnected rather than treating one as precursor to the other [11]. Um Defeito de Cor becomes more than a novel; it functions as a Carnaval samba plot that reimagines Afro-diasporic memory through narrative forms that center Black women's experiences previously excluded from Brazilian national historiography.

Afro-Latinx healing constitutes part of broader Afro-Latinx cultural and spiritual resilience, where narrative acts serve as mechanisms for processing intergenerational trauma. Afro-Puerto Rican women resist colonial violence through literary and digital practices that anchor identity in spiritual epistemologies rather than colonial archives [11]. This resistance operates through what scholars term the sacred archive, where spiritual memory functions as an alternative repository of historical knowledge that colonial institutions could not access or destroy.

Indigenous acts of narrative healing similarly employ digital resistance to confront cultural trauma while reclaiming spiritual epistemologies. At the 2024 Venice Biennale, America was represented by Choctaw and Cherokee artists whose work invited viewers to reconsider how narratives about American history are shaped and contested [16]. This representation marked a significant shift from historical exclusion to institutional recognition, though the tension between institutional validation and community autonomy remains unresolved.

The Nasher Museum at Duke University exemplifies how institutional spaces can facilitate narrative healing through strategic gallery pairings. A 2025 exhibition paired Pobre Diabla (2022) by Miami-based Nicaraguan artist Joel Gaitan with a 1000-year-old Quimbaya Seated Male Figure, creating dialogue between contemporary Afro-Latina artistic expression and pre-colonial Indigenous material culture [3]. Such curatorial decisions acknowledge the continuity of Indigenous and Afro-diasporic presence rather than treating these cultures as historical artifacts.

Digital platforms enable Afro-Latina and Indigenous communities to create networked archives of healing that transcend geographic boundaries. Afro-Latinx digital connections facilitate community-building across national borders, allowing diasporic populations to share narrative strategies and spiritual practices that support collective healing. Black Twitter and similar platforms function as digital counter-archives where communities document experiences of anti-Blackness and colonial violence while simultaneously creating spaces for joy, resilience, and cultural affirmation.

The tension between presentism and historical accuracy becomes particularly salient in narrative healing work. James Baldwin observed that history is not the past but the present, arguing that we carry our history with us and to think otherwise is criminal [9]. Black historians know there is no such thing as objective history, and recent critiques of presentism fail to see that the past cannot be divorced from the present. supposedly objective scholarship has long promoted racist narratives and suppressed Black history, as exemplified by W.E.B. Du Bois's response to the Dunning School and the efforts of Anna Julia Cooper and Carter G. Woodson [9].

Woodson explained in The Miseducation of the Negro that societal change is impossible when we fail to interrogate standard accounts of history, arguing that telling neutral historical accounts of egregious practices such as slavery and lynching serves a fundamental purpose to excuse injustices of the past [9]. Anna Julia Cooper's 1924 dissertation in history at the Sorbonne University in Paris directly condemned the institution of slavery, wielding scholarship as a weapon against colonial exploitation while European nations maintained colonial rule over millions of people of color across the globe [9].

Narrative healing through historiographic metafiction acknowledges that memory of historical wrongs involves complex relationships between narrative modes by which historical injustices are represented and present attributions of collective responsibility [20]. This framework attends to how Afro-Latina and Indigenous communities use literary and digital forms not merely to document trauma but to transform the conditions of remembering itself, creating epistemological spaces where spiritual knowledge and historical analysis converge.

The Vietnam War Stories project demonstrates how veteran narratives and collective memory function in post-conflict healing, with civilians grappling with questions of U.S. involvement and war results through narrative reconstruction [2]. While focused on a different community, this model informs understanding of how Afro-Latina and Indigenous communities similarly use narrative to process cultural trauma and reclaim agency over historical interpretation. Public memory and narrative methodologies of remembering and retelling enable communities to move from passive recipients of historical accounts to active producers of counter-narratives that affirm identity and demand accountability.

## Commemorative Practices as Sites of Contestation

Commemorative practices function as critical sites where collective memory is constructed, contested, and renegotiated. Unlike academic history, which operates within disciplinary frameworks of evidence and interpretation, memory functions as a collective social construct that shapes public understanding of the past through material culture, ritual, and institutional narrative. This section examines how museums, monuments, and historic sites become arenas of ideological struggle, where established narratives face challenges from marginalized communities seeking recognition and repatriation.

### Museum Decolonization and the Redefinition of Narratives

The decolonization of museums represents one of the most significant shifts in commemorative practice over the past decade, marking a transition from institutions that functioned as cabinets of curiosity to participatory spaces that center Indigenous voices and repatriation claims. Museums, as Martha Macintyre observed, carry colonial origins that ensure they can be variously interpreted as the patrimony of victorious nations, the lost cultural heritage of the colonized, or more neutrally, as evidence of human diversity and achievement [3]. This interpretive flexibility has become a site of contestation, as Indigenous communities and descendant populations demand that museums acknowledge their role in colonial projects of classification and extraction.

The nineteenth-century museum emerged alongside European colonial expansion, driven by a need to identify and classify objects, plants, and people from colonized territories [3]. According to Yesomi Umolu, museums are built on the ideological foundation of being repositories of knowledge that served colonial administrative and scientific purposes [3]. Museum boards remain tied to this colonial impulse, creating structural barriers to meaningful decolonization even when institutional leadership expresses commitment to change [3]. The material consequences of this history include the pillaging of cultural objects under doctrines like Manifest Destiny, which justified the extraction of Indigenous cultural heritage for display in metropolitan institutions [10].

Decolonization efforts have focused on repatriation and the inclusion of Indigenous voices in curatorial decision-making. The Burke Museum in Seattle exemplifies this shift, seeking to become a space for healing and joy rather than a repository of colonial trophies (见数据来源框). The museum incorporates Indigenous and Native languages into its exhibitions, emphasizing Indigenous and cultural survival and self-determination rather than Western expansion and imperialism [15]. However, the meaning of decolonization remains contested. Some scholars argue that decolonization can bring to light the histories of the colonized, while others caution that white guilt leaves little room for genuine deconstruction, reflection, and relationship-building [15].

Scholarship increasingly challenges Eurocentric narratives that have dominated museum interpretation. Decolonization entails a transformation of institutional practices, not merely the addition of diverse voices to existing frameworks [19]. Debates evolved significantly after the 2023 National Council on Public History conference, showcasing diverse international perspectives from countries like South Africa and Brazil [19]. Authors advocate for participatory methodologies and inclusion of Indigenous voices, as seen in the work of scholars like Krista McCracken, who emphasize community-led interpretation over institutional authority [19].

The practical difficulties of changing established narratives become apparent when considering the material constraints museums face. Objects acquired through colonial extraction carry provenance histories that institutions are increasingly compelled to disclose, yet legal frameworks often prevent repatriation. Museum directors report receiving pressure from donors, board members, and political actors regarding exhibition content, with nearly half of respondents in recent surveys reporting pressure to not publicly display certain artworks or historical interpretations (见数据来源框). This pressure originates from multiple sources across the political spectrum, demonstrating that commemorative practices remain sites of ongoing contestation.

### The Politics of Monuments and Public Rituals

Physical monuments and public rituals transform historical knowledge into collective memory, often sparking conflict over removal or preservation. Unlike museum exhibitions, which can be modified through curatorial decisions, monuments occupy public space in ways that make their removal or reinterpretation inherently political. The theory of collective memory, developed by Maurice Halbwachs, provides a framework for understanding how commemorative practices shape public understanding of the past through social frameworks rather than individual recollection [21]. Halbwachs argued that memory is constructed through interactions between government and citizens, focusing on the role of experts and institutions in shaping public policy around commemoration [21].

Monument removal has become a flashpoint in memory politics, particularly in contexts where authoritarian regimes have used commemorative practices to construct national identity. In Georgia, during the Saakashvili era, a large-scale process of de-Sovietization was initiated through the removal of Soviet symbols [7]. However, these actions took on a selective character under the influence of political struggle, with some monuments targeted purely for political purposes while others remained untouched [7]. The term "national heroes" constitutes the basis of the state's identity, and after the Velvet Revolution of 2018, although the legacy of former elites and issues of corruption sparked widespread public debate, no serious institutional reforms concerning memory were implemented [7].

In the United States, the erection of Confederate monuments preceded Vietnam War Stories in the timeline of commemorative conflict, establishing patterns of memorialization that would recur in later debates [8]. National myths are often built on idealized versions of the past, celebrating national heroes and triumphs while glossing over darker chapters of history, such as colonialism, slavery, or internal conflicts [8]. This selective memory serves to create a sense of national identity that excludes marginalized populations and their historical experiences.

The Baldwin Hall Controversy at the University of Georgia illustrates the practical difficulties of addressing historical injustice through commemorative practice. The controversy surrounds reinterment of remains of enslaved African Americans and Indigenous peoples whose bodies were held in university collections [27]. As one scholar noted, such work serves as an act of remembrance and witness to the Black and Indigenous lives who shaped places long before current institutions arrived, and who still call on us to carry their memory forward [27]. The controversy demonstrates how material remains become sites of contestation when institutions attempt to address historical wrongs.

Public rituals generate cultural memory through embodied practices, not merely through symbolic representation. Studies show that rituals are not just cultural memory but also generate cultural memory through embodied practices, creating lasting impressions that transcend individual participation [23]. William E. Schlenger's work on Veteran Narratives and the Collective Memory of the Vietnam War demonstrates how civilians grappled with questions of U.S. involvement in Vietnam and the results of the war itself through public commemoration [2]. Public memory and narrative methodologies of remembering and retelling Vietnam became sites where competing interpretations of historical events were negotiated through monument dedication, anniversary ceremonies, and veteran testimony [2].

The United States identity contains contradictory elements that manifest in commemorative conflict. National narratives often celebrate freedom and democracy while obscuring histories of dispossession and enslavement, creating tensions that erupt during moments of commemorative contestation [14]. Teaching Us to Forget, a study of United States history textbooks and the Plains Wars, demonstrates how public memory is shaped through educational materials that selectively emphasize certain historical events while minimizing others [14]. This selective memory perpetuates historical silences that operate in intersectional ways, meaning that voices and experiences of those marginalized along multiple dimensions are even more likely to be silenced or ignored [8].

### Case Study: The Spadina Museum and Diversifying Storytelling

The Spadina Museum in Toronto provides a concrete case study of the practical challenges involved in decolonizing a house museum (见数据来源框). Originally owned by the Austin Family, the museum underwent transformation efforts beginning in 2023 to diversify storytelling and address historical silences in its interpretation [5]. The Ethnography Lab at the University of Toronto conducted research using participant observation and ethnographic interviews to investigate visitors' interaction with this transformation [5].

The Austin Family history reflects intersectional silence, where narratives focus primarily on male leaders while obscuring the experiences of women, servants, and marginalized populations who inhabited the same spaces [8]. Historical silences often operate in intersectional ways, meaning that the voices and experiences of those marginalized along multiple dimensions are even more likely to be silenced or ignored [8]. For instance, the history of Black women in the Civil Rights Movement is often overshadowed by narratives that focus primarily on male leaders, a pattern that repeats in house museum interpretation where domestic workers and enslaved peoples remain invisible despite their central role in maintaining the household [8].

Research on the Spadina Museum transformation revealed that visitors showed deep interest in the museum's efforts to diversify interpretations, including Indigenous voices sharing stories from multiple perspectives [5]. However, the research also identified a gap of communication between institutional intentions and visitor understanding. While the museum followed the City of Toronto's divisional strategic directions from 2022 that aim to promote Toronto's economy, cultural vibrancy, and global competitiveness, these municipal goals sometimes conflicted with decolonization objectives that require slowing down interpretive processes to center community voices [5].

The practical challenges of decolonizing a house museum include the material constraints of the building itself. House museums preserve architectural features, furnishings, and spatial arrangements that embody the social hierarchies of their original inhabitants. Changing interpretation without changing the physical space creates dissonance for visitors who encounter decolonial labels in rooms that still display the material culture of colonial elites. The Austin Family's ownership history is embedded in the building's structure, making it difficult to center alternative narratives without appearing to erase the very history that makes the site historically significant.

Communication gaps between institutions and visitors emerged as a significant barrier to effective decolonization. Visitors expressed interest in diversified storytelling but often lacked the contextual knowledge to understand why certain interpretive changes were made. The museum's efforts to include Indigenous voices and address colonial histories required visitors to engage with uncomfortable historical truths that conflicted with their expectations of house museums as sites of domestic nostalgia rather than colonial critique. This gap demonstrates that decolonization requires not only institutional change but also public education about why commemorative practices matter.

The Spadina Museum case illustrates broader patterns in museum decolonization where institutional commitment to change encounters practical obstacles in implementation. Museum professionals face pressure from multiple stakeholders, including donors, board members, community groups, and political actors, each with different expectations for how history should be presented (见数据来源框). The American Association of Museums and the American Association for State and Local History have issued statements on growing threats of censorship against U.S. museums, calling on all who value shared heritage to support the museum field in resisting censorship so museums can continue to educate, connect, and inspire (见数据来源框). However, these professional standards provide limited guidance on navigating the specific challenges of house museum decolonization.

The Spadina Museum experience demonstrates that decolonization is not a one-time intervention but an ongoing process that requires sustained commitment to community engagement, interpretive experimentation, and institutional reflection. The gap between institutional intentions and visitor understanding reveals that commemorative practices remain sites of contestation even when institutions express commitment to change. Material constraints, communication challenges, and stakeholder pressures create practical difficulties that slow the pace of narrative transformation.

## Contemporary Threats to Public Historiography and Academic Freedom

The landscape of public historiography in the United States has undergone significant transformation in 2024-2025, marked by escalating legislative pressures, institutional censorship, and the systematic erosion of academic freedom. This section examines the contemporary threats facing museums, historians, and curatorial professionals. The evidence from recent surveys and documented cases reveals a coordinated effort to reshape public historical narratives through federal executive action, state legislation, and funding mechanisms.

### Legislative Pressures and Censorship in US Museums

The period from 2024 to 2025 witnessed an unprecedented convergence of federal and state-level legislative actions targeting museums and historical institutions. At the federal level, the Trump administration issued an executive order in March 2024 criticizing institutions such as the Smithsonian Institute for portraying America's "unpatriotic" narratives, resulting in the loss of federal funding, grants, and contracts for targeted institutions (见数据来源框). [16] This executive action represented a direct intervention into the curatorial independence of national museums, transforming federal funding into a mechanism of ideological compliance.

The American Historical Association (AHA) and Organization of American Historians (OAH) issued a joint statement on March 7, 2024, addressing federal censorship of American history and objecting specifically to restrictions on the use of terms like "diversity," "equity," and "inclusion" across government platforms (见数据来源框). [4] These professional associations argued that such actions "deny the American public access to accurate, comprehensive historical scholarship" and undermine the foundational principles of historical inquiry.

State-level legislation complemented federal pressures through what PEN America has termed "Educational Intimidation Bills." Wyoming's HB 68 exemplifies this trend, targeting museum programming and educational content related to race, gender, and immigration history [12]. Tennessee witnessed objections by multiple Republican legislators to an exhibition at East Tennessee State University's Reece Museum, which forced the temporary closure of the exhibition and prompted the museum to require viewers to sign waivers before accessing contested content [12]. This case demonstrates how state legislative pressure translates into direct institutional censorship.

The American Alliance of Museums (AAM) issued a statement on August 15, 2025, addressing the growing threats of censorship against U.S. museums, calling on all who value shared heritage to support the museum field in resisting censorship (见数据来源框). [18] The AAM emphasized that museums "educate, connect, and help us understand one another," positioning institutional independence as essential to democratic discourse. Project 2025 documentation further revealed proposals to treat librarians and museum professionals as potential "sex criminals" for providing access to certain historical materials, indicating the extremity of ideological pressures facing cultural institutions [12].

The National Park Service (NPS) became a focal point of these pressures, with the AHA and AASLH launching a "Defending Whole History at NPS and Historic Sites" initiative to counter federal censorship efforts [4]. This initiative recognized that federal historic sites serve as critical nodes in the public history ecosystem, where legislative pressures directly impact visitor access to comprehensive historical narratives.

### The Chilling Effect on Curatorial Independence

Survey data from 2024-2025 reveals the profound impact of legislative and funding pressures on curatorial decision-making, documenting what researchers term the "chilling effect" on museum professionals (见数据来源框). The Association of Art Museum Directors (AAMD), in collaboration with the Artists at Risk Connection, commissioned a survey of art museum directors released on January 14, 2025, which found worsening threats of censorship affecting curatorial independence across the sector [1]. The survey results demonstrated that censorship in museums "not only undermines artistic freedom, it also denies visitors the opportunity to form their own opinions and perspectives about the work they encounter."

PEN America's research, conducted in collaboration with Slover Linett at NORC, administered a questionnaire to AAMD membership from June 24 to July 11, 2024, receiving 95 responses out of 220 individuals (见数据来源框). [12] The survey results demonstrated "increasing trepidation concerning political pressure and the threat of state action against the sector," leading to self-censoring behavior amongst museum directors. This finding reveals a critical distinction between formal censorship (direct removal of content by external actors) and self-censorship (preemptive avoidance of contested content by institutional actors).

The research identified a gray zone "between curation and censorship" that could be most leveraged and exploited by censors [12]. Many directors' decisions about exhibition content, programming, and acquisitions now seep into decision-making processes influenced by anticipated political reactions rather than curatorial judgment alone. This represents a fundamental shift in institutional governance, where the threat of funding cuts or legislative scrutiny becomes a de facto curatorial criterion. The survey found that threats of censorship worsened from previous years, indicating an accelerating trend rather than isolated incidents.

Funding threats emerged as a primary mechanism of pressure, with the Trump administration's executive order against the Smithsonian Institute establishing a precedent for using federal appropriations as leverage over institutional content (见数据来源框). [16] Art museum directors reported that the conundrum of responding to political pressure has raised alarms on censorship issues, with news outlets and museum directors rushing to define the role of art museums in politics. This defensive positioning consumes institutional resources that would otherwise support curatorial research, exhibition development, and public programming.

The 2024 Venice Biennale provided a contrasting example of curatorial resistance, where America was represented by Choctaw and Cherokee artists who upended narratives of supposed finality [16]. However, such instances of curatorial independence remain exceptional rather than normative, as survey data indicates most institutions adopt risk-averse strategies to preserve funding and avoid legislative scrutiny.

### Regional Museums as Guardians of Historical Record

As national institutions face intensified political pressure, regional and heritage museums have emerged as critical guardians of contested local memory, preserving historical narratives that federal and state authorities seek to suppress. The Northeast Louisiana Delta African-American Heritage Museum exemplifies this role, operating under the principle that "when curation becomes censorship, regional museums matter more than ever" [22]. This summer, the Smithsonian was placed under direct political pressure unlike any previous administration, creating a vacuum in national historical leadership that regional institutions have begun to fill.

The East Tennessee State University's Reece Museum case demonstrates both the vulnerability and resilience of regional institutions. When multiple Republican legislators objected to an exhibition, the museum faced temporary closure but ultimately preserved the contested content through modified access protocols [12]. This compromise reflects the precarious position of regional museums: they possess greater flexibility than federal institutions but remain vulnerable to local political pressures.

The Spadina Museum in Toronto provides an international comparative case, where the 2023 strategic plan emphasized decolonizing Toronto History Museums through diversifying storytelling and addressing the gap of communication between institutional narratives and community memory (见数据来源框). [5] Ethnographic research at Spadina Museum provided an opportunity to observe the postcolonial mechanism around the museum's decolonization mandate, revealing how regional institutions can serve as laboratories for historical reinterpretation when national institutions face constraints.

The Burke Museum at the University of Washington demonstrates another model of regional institutional resistance, incorporating Indigenous and Native languages into exhibition interpretation to emphasize Indigenous and cultural survival and self-determination (见数据来源框). [15] Rather than showing only the consequences of Western expansion, colonialism, and imperialism, the Burke Museum emphasizes Indigenous persistence, positioning regional institutions as sites where silenced narratives can be recovered and centered.

The American Association for State and Local History (AASLH) has supported regional institutions through its "Defending Whole History" initiative, recognizing that state and local museums face distinct pressures from federal institutions [4]. Regional museums often serve communities directly affected by historical injustices, making their interpretive work both more urgent and more vulnerable to political challenge. The National Museum of African American History and Culture, while federally funded, has been singled out for excessive focus by political actors, demonstrating how even national institutions serving specific communities face disproportionate scrutiny [22].

Post-2023 National Council on Public History conferences have showcased diverse international perspectives from countries like South Africa and Brazil, with authors advocating for participatory methodologies and inclusion of Indigenous voices [19]. This international dimension reveals that threats to public historiography are not uniquely American but part of a global pattern where authoritarian and semi-authoritarian regimes employ memory methods to ensure domestic legitimacy. Regional museums, by virtue of their community embeddedness, possess unique capacity to resist these pressures through localized historical practice.

The evidence from 2024-2025 indicates that regional museums have become essential infrastructure for preserving historical record when national institutions face political pressure. Their smaller scale, community connections, and interpretive flexibility allow them to maintain contested narratives that federal institutions must abandon to preserve funding. However, this guardian role comes with significant risks, as regional institutions lack the legal and financial resources of national museums to resist legislative pressure.

## Synthesis and Future Directions for Ethical Historiography

The preceding sections have documented the mechanisms through which memory becomes politicised, history becomes instrumentalised, and narratives become contested terrain. This final section moves from diagnosis to prescription, offering frameworks for ethical historiography that can withstand political pressure while maintaining scholarly integrity. The challenge is not merely academic: as museums face funding cuts, educators confront legislative restrictions, and public discourse fragments along partisan lines, the stakes for how societies remember and interpret the past have never been higher.

### Balancing Academic Rigor with Social Relevance

The tension between academic rigor and social relevance crystallises in the debate over presentism—the practice of evaluating historical events through contemporary values and concerns. Critics argue that presentism compromises scholarly objectivity by imposing modern moral frameworks on past contexts. James H. Sweet and others within the American Historical Association have cautioned against allowing present-day political commitments to distort historical interpretation, warning that such approaches risk replacing evidence-based analysis with ideological advocacy (见数据来源框). This critique rests on the premise that history, as an academic discipline, must maintain methodological distance from the collective memory it studies.

However, this position has been robustly challenged by Black historians and scholars from marginalised communities who demonstrate that claims of objectivity have historically served to legitimise oppressive narratives. W.E.B. Du Bois's response to the Dunning School exemplifies this counter-tradition: where Dunning School historians produced scholarship that reinforced segregation and racial discrimination in the early twentieth century, Du Bois wielded historical research as a tool of confrontation against political abuses of the past [9]. Anna Julia Cooper's 1924 dissertation at the Sorbonne directly condemned slavery while European nations maintained colonial rule over millions of people of colour, demonstrating that historically grounded scholarship need not be politically neutral to be academically rigorous [9]. Carter G. Woodson's establishment of Negro History Week similarly recognised that the exclusion of Black experiences from mainstream historiography was itself a political act requiring corrective intervention.

James Baldwin's observation captures the impossibility of divorcing past from present: "History is not the past. History is the present. We carry our history with us. To think otherwise is criminal" [9]. This perspective does not abandon evidence-based scholarship but rather acknowledges that all historiography emerges from particular social positions. The persistence of racism, white supremacy, and racial inequality throughout American society makes it impossible for historians to write about the past as though divorced from present concerns [9].

A framework for balancing rigor with relevance must therefore reject the false dichotomy between objectivity and engagement. Instead, it should emphasise transparency about interpretive frameworks while maintaining fidelity to archival evidence. The 1619 Project illustrates both the potential and the pitfalls of this approach (见数据来源框). Nicole Hannah-Jones's work, which centred United States history around the African-American experience and won a Pulitzer Prize in 2020, became a flashpoint in discussions of race during the Black Lives Matter movement [6]. Critics attacked its historical claims while supporters defended its reframing of national narrative. The controversy demonstrates that socially relevant historiography will inevitably face political scrutiny, but this scrutiny should not deter scholars from pursuing evidence-based arguments.

The Spadina Museum in Toronto provides a concrete example of how institutions can navigate this balance (见数据来源框). Previously owned by the Austin Family, the house museum has undertaken decolonisation efforts that diversify storytelling while maintaining historical accuracy [5]. Visitors enter through the servants' path rather than the main entrance, physically reorienting their experience to acknowledge labour histories traditionally marginalised in domestic museum narratives. Historic interpreters introduce themselves after an introductory video featuring archival photos of the Austin Family and Old Toronto, creating space for multiple perspectives within a single interpretive framework [5]. This approach demonstrates that acknowledging presentist concerns about representation need not compromise scholarly standards.

Ethical historiography requires what might be termed "reflexive rigor": maintaining methodological standards while explicitly acknowledging the social positions from which historical questions emerge. This means citing evidence transparently, engaging with counter-arguments substantively, and distinguishing between documented facts and interpretive claims. It also means recognising that the Dunning School's supposedly objective scholarship served to legitimise segregation, demonstrating that claims of neutrality can themselves be ideological positions [9]. The goal is not to eliminate presentism—an impossible task—but to make its operations visible and subject to scholarly scrutiny.

### Recommendations for Institutions Facing Political Pressure

Museums and educational institutions operate in an increasingly hostile environment where political pressure manifests as funding threats, legislative restrictions, and organised campaigns against specific exhibitions or interpretive approaches. Recent survey data reveals the scope of this challenge (见数据来源框). According to PEN America's research on museum censorship, nearly half of respondents (45%) reported receiving pressure to not publicly display certain artworks or content, with complaints originating from museum visitors, donors, board members, and leaders of school groups [12]. The Artists at Risk Connection and AAMD's 2025 survey of art museum directors confirms that pressures to censor are spreading across the American cultural landscape [1].

The Smithsonian Institution's experience illustrates the stakes involved. In March of 2025, the Trump administration issued an executive order criticising institutions such as the Smithsonian for portraying America's "unpatriotic" histories, resulting in lost federal funding, grants, and contracts (见数据来源框). [16] This demonstrates how executive power can be weaponised against institutions that maintain interpretive independence. The long-term dangers extend beyond individual institutions: when curation becomes dictated by political decree rather than evidence and scholarship, the history of Black Americans and other marginalised groups faces silencing or softening [22].

Institutions facing such pressure require actionable strategies that balance principle with pragmatism. The following recommendations emerge from recent sector responses:

| Strategy | Implementation Action | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Develop Written Censorship Policies** | Create policies to guide response to challenges, including conditions for altering exhibitions [1]. | Provides a "vital" framework for consistent decision-making that can withstand external pressure. |
| **Review Interpretive Plans** | Ensure plans, online content, and programs are grounded in evidence-based scholarship, especially for charged topics [4]. | Documentation of scholarly sources creates defensible positions when interpretations face challenge. |
| **Build Sector-Wide Solidarity** | Support statements from AASLH, OAH, and AAM regarding censorship pressure [4][18]. | Individual institutions are more vulnerable than coordinated sector responses. |
| **Diversify Funding Sources** | Cultivate private donations, membership programs, and earned income to reduce susceptibility to funding coercion [12]. | Dependence on government funding creates vulnerability (e.g., NEA budget drop 1994-1996). |
| **Maintain Transparency** | Be transparent about the nature of pressure and reasoning behind decisions to maintain public trust [12]. | Concealing censorship risks long-term credibility damage; acknowledging pressures can mobilise supporters. |
| **Engage Communities** | Involve community stakeholders in developing exhibitions to build constituencies that defend interpretive choices [5]. | When communities see their histories represented authentically, they become allies against external pressure. |
| **Document Censorship Attempts** | Maintain records of pressure campaigns, sources, and outcomes to contribute to broader understanding [12]. | Systematic documentation creates evidence for advocacy and sector learning (e.g., PEN America reports). |

The Culture Wars of the 1990s offer historical precedent for current challenges. During that period, Robert Mapplethorpe's work was censured, and the NEA faced sustained political attack [12]. Museums that maintained principled positions while building coalitions ultimately preserved interpretive independence. Contemporary institutions can learn from these precedents while recognising that current pressures operate through different mechanisms, including social media campaigns and state-level legislation.

### The Future of Collective Memory in a Polarized Landscape

The future of collective memory in polarised societies depends on how institutions, scholars, and citizens navigate the tension between competing narratives and shared factual foundations. Maurice Halbwachs's theorisation of collective memory remains foundational: collective memory plays a central role in how a society understands itself, which is why authoritarian regimes seek alignment of mass memory with the system's ideological demands [7]. This dynamic operates in democratic contexts as well, though through different mechanisms.

Authoritarian regimes provide cautionary examples of memory manipulation. In Turkmenistan, traditions formed under Saparmurat Niyazov—such as the "Ruhnama Commemoration Ceremony" and official celebration of the leader's birthday—were not based on actual historical facts but were presented as "foundations of national culture" (见数据来源框). [7] The aim was obvious: to construct a national identity centred on the leader's supposed wisdom and benevolence. Russia's involvement in the 2008 Russia-Georgia war and subsequent memory politics demonstrates how military conflict becomes terrain for narrative contestation [7]. After the "Velvet Revolution" of 2018 in Armenia, although the legacy of former elites and issues of corruption sparked widespread public debate, no serious institutional reforms concerning memory were implemented, showing how memory politics can persist despite regime change [7].

Democratic societies face different but related challenges. Jason Steinhauer notes that the incentives of the social web can be very different from the incentives of the museum or classroom [6]. Digital platforms reward engagement over accuracy, creating conditions where simplified or emotionally charged historical narratives spread more rapidly than nuanced scholarship. This environment complicates the work of institutions committed to evidence-based interpretation.

The cross-disciplinary nature of memory studies offers both opportunities and challenges for addressing these conditions. Collective memory has been taken up across numerous disciplines including History, Anthropology, Sociology, Cultural Studies, and Heritage and Museum Studies [21][21]. This interdisciplinary engagement enriches understanding but also fragments authority over memory interpretation. No single discipline can claim exclusive jurisdiction over how societies remember.

Several implications follow for the future of collective memory:

*   **Memory Will Remain Contested**: The polarization of information ecosystems means that consensus on historical interpretation will become harder to achieve, not easier. Institutions should prepare for sustained contestation rather than expecting eventual agreement.
*   **Digital Literacy Becomes Essential**: As historical knowledge increasingly flows through digital channels, the ability to evaluate sources and recognise manipulation becomes a core civic competency. Educational institutions must integrate memory literacy into curricula.
*   **Transnational Memory Networks Matter**: Memory politics increasingly operates transnationally, as diaspora communities maintain connections to homeland histories. The work of Afro-Latinx scholars demonstrates how diasporic memory connects experiences across national boundaries [11].
*   **Institutional Independence Requires Active Defense**: The assumption that cultural institutions will maintain autonomy cannot be taken for granted. The Smithsonian's experience demonstrates how quickly federal support can become conditional on interpretive compliance (见数据来源框). [16]
*   **Memory and Justice Remain Linked**: The Colombian Armed Conflict's soldier victims demonstrate how collective memory is constructed through narratives that influence understanding of political responsibility for historical injustices [20]. Where societies pursue transitional justice, memory work becomes inseparable from accountability.

The future of ethical historiography depends on recognising that history and memory, while distinct, operate in continuous interaction. History as an academic discipline provides methodological tools for evaluating claims about the past. Memory as a collective social construct shapes how communities understand their identities and obligations. Neither can function healthily in isolation from the other. Ethical historiography requires maintaining this distinction while acknowledging their interdependence.

As societies navigate increasingly polarised information landscapes, the work of museums, educators, and historians becomes both more difficult and more essential. The recommendations offered in this section provide starting points, but each institution must adapt them to local conditions. What remains constant is the need for courage: the courage to pursue evidence wherever it leads, the courage to acknowledge the limitations of one's own perspective, and the courage to defend interpretive independence against those who would instrumentalise the past for present political ends. The alternative—allowing memory to become solely a tool of power—threatens not only scholarly integrity but the democratic capacity for collective self-reflection upon which just societies depend.
