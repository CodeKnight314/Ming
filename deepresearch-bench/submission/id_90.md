## Executive Synthesis: The Liability Crisis in Shared Control

The proliferation of Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) has created a fundamental tension between technological capability and legal accountability. As vehicles increasingly automate portions of the driving task, the traditional framework for assigning fault in motor vehicle accidents has become inadequate. This section synthesizes the core liability ambiguity arising from shared human-machine control, examining how automation levels collide with established tort doctrine and how courts are beginning to allocate responsibility between drivers and manufacturers.

### The Collision of Automation Levels and Legal Doctrine

The central source of legal ambiguity in ADAS-related accidents stems from the misalignment between SAE International's technical classification of automation levels and existing tort law frameworks. SAE J3016 defines five levels of driving automation, but the legal implications differ dramatically between Level 2 and Level 3 (see **Key Data & Definitions**). This distinction is not merely technical; it determines whether the driver or the manufacturer bears primary responsibility when a crash occurs.

At SAE Level 2, systems such as Tesla's Autopilot, Enhanced Autopilot, and similar offerings from Ford, GM, and other manufacturers can simultaneously control vehicle lane position, speed, and following distance. However, the human driver remains legally responsible for continuous supervision and must be prepared to intervene at any moment [39]. Tort law traditionally treats Level 2 vehicles under a negligence framework, where the driver owes a duty of care to operate the vehicle safely regardless of automation engagement. The NHTSA is explicit that these are driver assistance technologies, not self-driving systems, yet marketing language and user interface design often create confusion about the extent of automation capability [13].

Level 3 automation introduces a fundamentally different liability structure. When a Level 3 system is engaged within its Operational Design Domain (ODD), the vehicle assumes responsibility for the Dynamic Driving Task, and the human becomes a fallback-ready user rather than an active supervisor. Mercedes-Benz, the first manufacturer to deploy a commercially available Level 3 system (Drive Pilot), has accepted liability for accidents occurring while the system is properly engaged [9]. This represents a significant shift from the Level 2 model, where liability presumptively rests with the driver.

The critical legal question emerges during the transition period between automated and manual control. When a Level 3 system issues a takeover request due to exiting its ODD or detecting a system limitation, liability allocation becomes uncertain. Legal scholars have identified the "thorny question" of whether the operator has liability for accidents occurring immediately following a takeover request, or only after a grace period such as 10 seconds has elapsed [35]. This transition mechanism is where Human-Machine Interface (HMI) design becomes legally consequential. If alerts are insufficiently clear, delayed, or fail to provide adequate time for the driver to regain situational awareness, the manufacturer may bear responsibility even though the driver technically resumed control.

Existing tort doctrine struggles to accommodate this handover dynamic. Traditional negligence analysis requires establishing duty, breach, causation, and damages. In Level 2 contexts, the driver's duty is continuous; in Level 3 contexts, the duty shifts based on system status. However, most jurisdictions have not enacted legislation clarifying how this shift operates in practice. The result is a patchwork of case law where courts apply traditional products liability and negligence theories to fact patterns those theories were not designed to address [17].

The collision between automation levels and legal doctrine is further complicated by federal preemption questions. The Supreme Court has addressed the degree to which federal motor vehicle safety law may preempt state product liability actions based upon defective automobile design, but comprehensive federal legislation establishing performance standards and liability allocation for automated systems has not passed Congress [16][9]. States have filled this gap with varying approaches, creating jurisdictional inconsistency that undermines predictability for manufacturers, insurers, and accident victims alike.

### Key Findings on Driver vs. Manufacturer Fault

Recent case law reveals an evolving landscape where liability is increasingly apportioned between drivers and manufacturers based on specific factual circumstances rather than categorical rules. Two landmark cases illustrate the current boundaries of liability allocation: *Benavides v. Tesla* and *Berkoski v. Honda* (see **Key Data & Definitions** for verdict specifics).

**Benavides v. Tesla** represents a watershed moment in ADAS liability jurisprudence. The case involved a 2018 Tesla Model 3 operating with Enhanced Autopilot engaged at 69 miles per hour on US 441 in Florida. The vehicle collided with a semi-trailer truck that had turned into its path, resulting in fatalities and severe injuries [45]. The jury's verdict allocated fault significantly between the driver and the manufacturer, awarding substantial compensatory and punitive damages (see **Key Data & Definitions**).

The legal reasoning in *Benavides* focused on Tesla's misrepresentations of Autopilot's capabilities. The court upheld the jury's finding that Tesla was liable for the integral role Autopilot and the company's marketing claims played in the crash [5]. This establishes that even in Level 2 systems where the driver retains ultimate responsibility, manufacturers can face substantial liability if their communications create reasonable reliance on automation beyond its actual capabilities. The punitive damages component signals judicial willingness to penalize manufacturers whose marketing obscures the supervision requirements inherent in Level 2 automation.

**Berkoski v. Honda** presents a contrasting outcome that defines the outer limits of manufacturer liability. The Superior Court of New Jersey Appellate Division ruled that the absence of a driver-assistance system in a 2016 Honda CR-V was not a violation of the state's Products Liability Act [43]. This holding establishes that manufacturers cannot be held liable for failing to equip vehicles with ADAS technology that was not standard or required at the time of manufacture. The legal reasoning distinguishes between defects in existing systems (which can create liability, as in *Benavides*) and the absence of systems altogether (which generally does not create liability under current doctrine).

These cases reveal several high-level trends in liability allocation:

*   **Shift from Pure Driver Negligence to Comparative Fault:** Traditional accident litigation presumed driver negligence as the primary cause. ADAS cases increasingly employ comparative fault frameworks where responsibility is divided between human error and system limitations. The *Benavides* verdict's split exemplifies this approach, acknowledging that both the driver's inattention and the system's limitations contributed to the harm [25].
*   **Product Liability Emergence in Specific Contexts:** When ADAS systems malfunction, provide inadequate warnings, or are marketed deceptively, plaintiffs increasingly pursue products liability claims alongside traditional negligence theories. Manufacturers, sellers, and those in the "chain of commerce" can be held liable for injuries caused by faulty ADAS systems under products liability theories [32]. This shift is particularly pronounced when Event Data Recorder (EDR) evidence demonstrates system errors or when HMI alerts failed to provide adequate takeover time.
*   **Mechanism of Liability Shift Through HMI:** The transfer of duty from manufacturer to driver operates through specific technical mechanisms. When a Level 2 or Level 3 system issues a takeover request, liability begins shifting based on the adequacy of the alert and the time provided for driver response. EDR data now captures speed, braking, steering input, and driver attention metrics, providing objective evidence of whether the driver received adequate warning and had sufficient time to respond [46][32]. Under 49 C.F.R. Part 563, EDRs must record specific data elements including vehicle speed, engine throttle, brake status, and safety belt usage, creating an evidentiary foundation for fault determination (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [30].
*   **Jurisdictional Variances in Liability Standards:** State law continues to govern most ADAS liability questions, resulting in significant variation across jurisdictions. (See **Legal Frameworks** section for detailed jurisdictional table).
*   **EDR as Core Evidentiary Pillar:** Event Data Recorders have become indispensable for proving fault in ADAS accidents. EDR technology captures pre-crash data including vehicle dynamics, system status, and driver inputs in the seconds before impact [40]. This data can establish whether ADAS was engaged, whether takeover requests were issued, whether the driver responded, and whether system malfunctions occurred. Insurers and litigants increasingly rely on EDR data to support or deny claims, making data preservation and interpretation critical to liability determination [2][31].

The emerging pattern suggests that liability allocation in ADAS accidents will continue to depend on granular factual analysis rather than bright-line rules. Drivers cannot assume automation absolves them of responsibility, particularly in Level 2 contexts where continuous supervision is required. Manufacturers cannot assume Level 2 classification shields them from liability when marketing, design, or warning systems create reasonable reliance on automation capabilities. The crisis in shared control liability stems from this fundamental ambiguity: when both human and machine contribute to vehicle operation, both may contribute to fault, and existing legal frameworks are only beginning to articulate how that contribution should be measured and apportioned.

## Technical Principles Defining Duty of Care

The allocation of liability in Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) accidents hinges on understanding how technical specifications translate into legal duties. As vehicles transition from human-operated machines to shared human-machine control environments, the duty of care becomes fragmented between manufacturers, drivers, and system designers. This section examines the technical frameworks that define these duties, focusing on automation classification standards, human-machine interface design, and system limitations that shape foreseeable misuse claims.

### SAE J3016 Classification as a Legal Boundary

The SAE J3016 standard serves as the foundational taxonomy for vehicle automation, yet its technical definitions carry profound legal implications for duty of care allocation. The distinction between Level 2 (partial automation with driver supervision) and Level 3 (conditional automation) represents the primary source of legal ambiguity and user confusion in current ADAS litigation [34][1].

| Automation Level | Definition & Threshold | Liability Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Level 2** | Partial automation. System controls longitudinal and lateral motion. **Driver must continuously supervise.** | Liability predominantly rests with the driver. System is an assistance tool, not a replacement for human judgment [3][20][9]. |
| **Level 3** | Conditional automation. Vehicle monitors environment within ODD. **Driver may disengage active monitoring.** | Liability shifts to manufacturer during automated operation. Driver retains duty during takeover requests and outside ODD [37][35]. |

The legal significance of this classification became evident in *Benavides v. Tesla*, where the jury's allocation of fault reflected the Level 2 supervision requirement. Jurors placed the majority of the fault on the driver, who admitted he took his eyes off the road, and a significant portion on Tesla for Autopilot's failure to detect the stopped vehicle (see **Key Data & Definitions**). The legal reasoning centered on Tesla's misrepresentation of Autopilot's capabilities and inadequate warnings about the system's limitations, despite the Level 2 classification requiring constant driver attention [28][5].

In contrast, *Berkoski v. Honda* established that the absence of ADAS features does not constitute a design defect under products liability law. The Superior Court of New Jersey Appellate Division ruled that a 2016 Honda CR-V without driver-assistance systems was not unreasonably dangerous simply because it lacked technology that became more common in later model years [43]. This decision reinforces that manufacturers' duties are defined by the technical specifications of the vehicle as sold, not by subsequent technological advances. The case demonstrates how SAE classification creates a boundary: manufacturers must ensure their systems perform as specified within their designated automation level, but they are not liable for failing to include higher-level automation features.

The mechanism of liability shift between Level 2 and Level 3 operates through the allocation of monitoring responsibility. In Level 2, the driver's duty of care remains continuous and non-delegable. In Level 3, the duty shifts to the manufacturer during automated operation within the ODD, then shifts back to the driver upon takeover request [3]. This creates evidentiary challenges, as determining the exact moment of liability transfer requires precise data on system status, driver attention, and environmental conditions at the time of the incident.

### HMI Design and the "Handoff" Challenge

Human-Machine Interface (HMI) design serves as the critical mechanism through which duty of care transfers between manufacturer and driver. The effectiveness of alerts, takeover requests, and driver monitoring systems directly establishes whether the manufacturer has fulfilled its duty to ensure driver awareness before control reverts to the human operator [11][32].

Takeover requests (TORs) represent the most legally significant HMI element in conditional automation systems. When a Level 3 system encounters a situation outside its ODD, it must alert the driver and request intervention. The legal adequacy of this handoff depends on multiple factors:
*   The advance warning time provided.
*   The clarity and urgency of the alert.
*   Whether the system verifies driver readiness before transferring control [35].

Research indicates drivers may need several seconds to regain situational awareness after disengaging from non-driving tasks, creating a dangerous gap between the takeover request and effective human control [37].

Driver monitoring systems (DMS) have emerged as a key component in establishing whether manufacturers have taken reasonable steps to ensure driver attention. These systems use cameras, steering input sensors, or capacitive touch detection to verify the driver remains engaged. In Level 2 systems, adequate DMS design is essential to prevent driver over-reliance and complacency. When DMS fails to detect driver inattention or provides insufficient escalation before a crash, manufacturers face heightened liability exposure [13]. The *Benavides* case highlighted this issue, as plaintiffs argued Tesla's manual warnings and clickwrap agreements were inadequate given the system's known failure modes [28][8].

The mechanism of liability transfer through HMI operates on a notice-and-response framework. When a system issues a takeover request, it creates a temporal window during which the driver's duty of care intensifies. If the alert is clear, timely, and appropriately escalated, liability shifts toward the driver for failing to respond. However, if the HMI design is deficient—providing insufficient warning time, using ambiguous alerts, or failing to verify driver availability—the manufacturer retains liability for the handoff failure [3]. This creates a technical-legal integration point where engineering specifications directly determine fault allocation.

Visual, auditory, and haptic alerts must be designed to overcome driver complacency, particularly in systems that function reliably most of the time. The "cry wolf" effect occurs when frequent false alerts cause drivers to ignore or disable warnings, undermining the HMI's legal sufficiency. Manufacturers must balance alert frequency with credibility to maintain the system's legal defensibility [4]. Education on system features and limitations becomes essential, as drivers need to understand when ADAS might not perform well in construction zones or inclement weather [4].

The adequacy of HMI design is increasingly evaluated through Event Data Recorder (EDR) evidence. Under 49 C.F.R. Part 563, EDRs must capture pre-crash data including system status, driver inputs, and alert activations (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [30]. This data provides objective evidence of whether the HMI functioned as designed and whether the driver received adequate notice before the incident. Recorded EDR data may only be retrieved for safety research purposes, with vehicle owner consent, or through court order, creating privacy protections while preserving evidentiary value [30].

### System Limitations and Foreseeable Misuse

Technical constraints of ADAS systems create boundaries for manufacturer liability when accidents occur under conditions the system was not designed to handle. Understanding these limitations—and whether manufacturers adequately communicated them—forms the basis for defect claims and foreseeable misuse defenses [32][10].

*   **Weather Conditions:** Heavy rain, snow, fog, or glare can degrade sensor performance, causing lane-keeping assist, automatic emergency braking, or adaptive cruise control to malfunction or disengage. When manufacturers fail to warn drivers about weather-related limitations or design systems that provide false confidence in adverse conditions, they face liability for inadequate warnings or design defects [4].
*   **Lane Marking Quality:** Lane-Keeping Assist (LKA) systems depend on visible, well-maintained lane markings to function properly. In construction zones, faded markings, or temporary lane configurations, these systems may fail to detect lane boundaries or provide incorrect steering corrections [43][36]. Manufacturers must define their Operational Design Domain (ODD) to exclude conditions where sensor limitations create unacceptable risk, and they must communicate these exclusions clearly to drivers.

The concept of foreseeable misuse bridges technical limitations and legal duty. Manufacturers must anticipate how drivers might reasonably misuse their systems and design safeguards accordingly. If a system can be easily overridden or if warnings are insufficient to prevent dangerous behavior, the manufacturer may be liable for failing to anticipate foreseeable misuse [10]. This includes designing systems that resist circumvention, implementing escalating alerts for sustained inattention, and ensuring marketing materials do not overstate capabilities in ways that encourage risky behavior.

The *Benavides* verdict illustrates how system limitations factor into defect claims. The jury found Tesla liable despite the driver's primary fault because Autopilot failed in its duty to detect a stopped vehicle at a stop sign—a scenario within the system's intended operational domain [8]. The plaintiffs successfully argued that Tesla's manuals and warnings were inadequate given the company's awareness of Autopilot's failure to detect stationary objects [28]. This established that manufacturers cannot rely solely on driver supervision requirements when their systems have known failure modes that should be addressed through design improvements or more prominent warnings.

Product liability theories applicable to ADAS limitations include design defect, manufacturing defect, and failure to warn.
*   **Design Defect:** Alleges the system was not reasonably safe for its intended purpose, such as when sensors cannot reliably detect obstacles in common driving scenarios [32].
*   **Manufacturing Defect:** Involves deviations from intended design, such as miscalibrated sensors or software bugs.
*   **Failure to Warn:** Focuses on inadequate communication of system limitations, particularly when manufacturers know of risks but do not sufficiently alert drivers [19].

The defense of foreseeable misuse requires manufacturers to demonstrate they took reasonable steps to prevent or mitigate predictable misuse. This includes implementing driver monitoring, designing systems that require periodic driver input, and providing clear documentation of limitations. However, this defense fails when the misuse was itself foreseeable and the manufacturer did not design adequate safeguards [17]. The legal standard evaluates whether the manufacturer anticipated the misuse and whether the design reasonably addressed that risk.

System limitations also intersect with regulatory requirements. NHTSA's Standing General Order on crash reporting requires manufacturers to report incidents involving Level 2 ADAS and automated driving systems, creating a data repository that informs both regulatory oversight and litigation (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [39]. This data helps establish whether certain limitations are industry-wide or specific to particular manufacturers, influencing defect determinations and liability allocation.

The integration of technical specifications into legal duty creates a complex liability landscape where manufacturers must balance innovation with safety, and drivers must maintain vigilance despite increasing automation. As ADAS technology evolves, the technical principles defining duty of care will continue to shape how courts allocate responsibility in shared human-machine control environments.

## Legal Frameworks: Federal Oversight vs. State Tort Law

The allocation of liability in ADAS-related accidents operates within a complex jurisdictional landscape where federal regulatory authority intersects with state tort law. This tension creates significant legal ambiguity, particularly when distinguishing between SAE Level 2 systems requiring continuous driver supervision and SAE Level 3 conditional automation where the vehicle assumes primary responsibility within its operational design domain. The following analysis examines the federal regulatory framework, state-level statutory variances, and the critical preemption questions that define current liability boundaries.

### NHTSA Regulatory Authority and Crash Reporting

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) exercises federal oversight through Standing General Orders (SGO) that mandate crash reporting for vehicles equipped with automated driving systems and advanced driver assistance systems. These orders establish a foundational evidence-gathering mechanism critical for liability determinations in ADAS accidents. Under the SGO framework, manufacturers must report crashes involving Level 2 ADAS and Level 3+ ADS within specific timeframes, creating a centralized database that plaintiffs and defendants alike rely upon for establishing fault (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [39].

The distinction between Level 2 and Level 3 automation is explicitly recognized in NHTSA's reporting requirements. Level 2 systems, which can simultaneously support vehicle lane position, speed, and following distance, require constant driver supervision and do not transfer liability from the operator to the manufacturer upon engagement [39]. In contrast, Level 3 conditional automation allows the driver to disengage from active monitoring within specific operational conditions, creating a fundamentally different liability structure when the system encounters scenarios beyond its design parameters. This regulatory distinction directly impacts how courts assess whether the driver or manufacturer bears responsibility when a crash occurs.

The Automated Vehicle Safety and Transparency and Evaluation Program (AV STEP) further enhances NHTSA's oversight capacity by promoting awareness of motor vehicle safety matters through structured information programs [42]. AV STEP's impact on liability evidence is substantial, as it creates additional documentation requirements that can be subpoenaed in litigation. The program's transparency provisions mean that manufacturers cannot easily conceal system limitations or failure modes that contributed to an accident. This evidentiary trail becomes particularly important when plaintiffs allege that HMI alerts were insufficient to transfer duty back to the driver during a system handover scenario.

Event Data Recorder (EDR) capabilities serve as a core evidentiary pillar under 49 C.F.R. Part 563, which mandates specific data elements be recorded before, during, and after a crash event (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [30]. The regulation requires recording of vehicle speed, driver steering inputs, brake application, and ADAS engagement status in the seconds preceding a collision. This data provides objective evidence of whether the driver was supervising the system appropriately and whether the ADAS functioned as designed. NHTSA has supplied EDR data to the Volpe Center for validation studies, confirming the accuracy of these recorders in full-scale crash tests [40].

The mechanism of liability shift in ADAS accidents hinges on how HMI alerts transfer duty back to the driver. When a Level 2 system encounters conditions beyond its operational capacity, it issues visual, auditory, or haptic warnings requiring immediate driver intervention. The legal question becomes whether these alerts were sufficient given the time available for the driver to regain situational awareness. EDR data can establish the timeline of alerts versus driver response, creating a factual record that courts use to determine whether the manufacturer's handover protocol was reasonably designed or whether the driver failed in their supervision obligation. This evidentiary framework makes EDR data indispensable for proving fault in ADAS accidents, as it captures the precise moment when control should have transferred between human and machine [31].

NHTSA's authority under Section 30111 of the Safety Act provides the statutory foundation for prescribing motor vehicle safety standards and carrying out safety research [42]. This authority extends to oversight, rulemaking, and exemptions related to emerging vehicle technologies. However, NHTSA has been clear that current Level 2 systems constitute driver assistance technologies, not self-driving systems, reinforcing that liability remains with the human operator unless a manufacturing defect can be proven [13]. This regulatory position shapes how courts interpret manufacturer warnings and user agreements in ADAS litigation.

The mechanism of liability shift varies significantly across these jurisdictions. In Florida, the focus remains on whether the manufacturer's HMI design adequately communicated system limitations and whether marketing materials created unreasonable expectations about Level 2 capabilities. Michigan's no-fault structure means that even when liability shifts to the manufacturer through a successful product defect claim, the plaintiff's recovery may be limited by statutory caps and threshold requirements. Washington D.C.'s proximity to federal regulators creates a unique environment where NHTSA's interpretation of Level 2 versus Level 3 distinctions carries substantial weight in judicial analysis.

A contrasting precedent emerged in New Jersey, where the Superior Court Appellate Division ruled that the absence of a driver-assistance system in a 2016 Honda CR-V was not a violation of the state's Products Liability Act (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [43]. This *Berkoski v. Honda* reasoning established that manufacturers cannot be held liable for failing to include ADAS features that were not standard equipment at the time of manufacture. The court required plaintiffs to demonstrate that a product was not reasonably fit, suitable, or safe for its intended purpose, and that the product's risks outweighed its utility [43]. This creates an important boundary: liability attaches to defective implementation of ADAS, not to the decision not to include such systems in a particular vehicle trim or model year.

### Federal Preemption of State Product Liability

The question of federal preemption represents the most significant unresolved tension in ADAS liability law. The core issue centers on whether compliance with Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards (FMVSS) shields manufacturers from state-level defect claims when an ADAS-equipped vehicle causes harm. The Supreme Court has addressed the degree to which federal motor vehicle safety law may preempt state product liability actions based upon defective automobile design, but the application to modern ADAS technology remains unsettled [16].

Section 30111 restricts the activities of vehicles that do not comply with applicable vehicle standards, establishing federal minimum safety requirements [42]. However, the Safety Act and related statutes provide NHTSA with authority relating to oversight, rulemaking, research, transparency, and exemptions without explicitly preempting state tort actions [42]. This statutory silence creates ambiguity about whether a manufacturer that meets all FMVSS requirements can still face liability under state law for design defects that the federal standards did not specifically address.

The preemption analysis becomes particularly complex when examining conflicts between federal safety standards and state-level defect claims. In *Geier v. American Honda Motor Co., Inc.*, the Supreme Court held that federal regulations could implicitly preempt state tort claims when those claims would stand as an obstacle to the accomplishment of federal regulatory objectives [16]. However, subsequent decisions have narrowed this interpretation, requiring clear evidence of congressional intent to preempt before state law claims are barred. For ADAS technology, where federal standards have not kept pace with technological deployment, courts face the question of whether FMVSS compliance should immunize manufacturers from liability for system failures that federal regulations did not specifically contemplate.

The distinction between SAE Level 2 and Level 3 automation carries significant preemption implications. Level 2 systems, which require constant driver supervision, operate within a regulatory framework that assumes human oversight as the primary safety mechanism. When a Level 2 system fails, the manufacturer may argue that federal standards contemplated driver intervention as the fallback, and therefore state law claims alleging inadequate system design are preempted. However, plaintiffs counter that marketing representations and HMI design created reasonable reliance on system capabilities that exceeded the supervision model contemplated by federal regulations. This argument gained traction in *Benavides v. Tesla*, where the jury found that Tesla's representations about Autopilot capabilities created liability exposure despite FMVSS compliance [26].

For Level 3 conditional automation, the preemption analysis shifts because the regulatory framework contemplates periods where the driver is not actively supervising the vehicle. When a Level 3 system requests a handover and the driver fails to respond in time, the liability question becomes whether the handover protocol met federal expectations for safe transition of control. NHTSA's AV STEP program and Standing General Orders create documentation requirements that can be used to establish whether the manufacturer's handover design met regulatory expectations [42]. If the manufacturer complied with all federal handover requirements, a preemption defense becomes more viable. However, if federal standards are silent on specific handover timing or alert modalities, state law claims alleging inadequate design remain available.

Congress has not passed comprehensive legislation establishing performance standards, testing requirements, and liability allocation for automated vehicles, leaving states to fill the gap with a patchwork of regulations [9]. This legislative silence strengthens the argument against preemption, as courts typically require clear congressional intent before finding that federal law displaces state tort remedies. The absence of specific ADAS liability provisions in federal statute means that traditional products liability doctrines continue to govern most ADAS accident litigation, subject to case-by-case preemption analysis.

The practical effect of this preemption uncertainty is that manufacturers face liability exposure in multiple forums simultaneously. A single ADAS accident may trigger NHTSA investigation under the Standing General Order, state tort litigation under products liability or negligence theories, and potentially federal court action if diversity jurisdiction exists. Each forum applies different standards for evaluating whether the ADAS system was defectively designed, whether warnings were adequate, and whether the driver's conduct breaks the chain of causation. This multi-forum environment creates substantial litigation risk for manufacturers and complicates settlement negotiations, as outcomes in one jurisdiction may not predict results in another.

EDR data under 49 C.F.R. Part 563 serves as a critical evidentiary bridge between federal regulatory compliance and state tort claims. The standardized data elements required by federal regulation provide objective evidence that courts in any jurisdiction can evaluate when assessing liability (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [30]. This federal data standard creates a common factual foundation even when legal standards diverge across jurisdictions. Plaintiffs can use EDR data to establish that the ADAS system was engaged at the time of crash, that alerts were issued, and that the driver did or did not respond within the available timeframe. Manufacturers can use the same data to demonstrate system performance met design specifications and that the driver failed to maintain required supervision. The universality of EDR data partially mitigates the jurisdictional fragmentation created by varying state liability standards.

The preemption question ultimately turns on whether federal safety standards occupy the entire field of ADAS regulation or whether they establish minimum requirements that state tort law can supplement. Current judicial trends favor the latter interpretation, allowing state law claims to proceed unless they directly conflict with specific federal requirements. This means that FMVSS compliance provides evidence of reasonable care but does not automatically immunize manufacturers from liability. The *Benavides* verdict demonstrates that juries remain willing to find liability when marketing representations and system performance create a gap that federal standards did not specifically address. As ADAS technology continues to evolve faster than federal rulemaking, this gap-based liability theory is likely to remain the dominant framework for allocating responsibility in shared human-machine control accidents.

## Responsibility Boundaries: Driver Negligence vs. Manufacturer Defect

The allocation of liability in accidents involving Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) represents one of the most complex challenges in contemporary tort law. As vehicles increasingly incorporate semi-autonomous capabilities, courts must navigate the ambiguous boundary between human error and technological failure. This section examines the legal frameworks governing responsibility when control is shared between driver and machine, with particular attention to the critical distinction between SAE Level 2 systems, which require continuous driver supervision, and Level 3 systems, which permit conditional automation with manufacturer liability acceptance during automated operation.

### The Persistence of Driver Supervision Duties

Despite marketing language that suggests otherwise, SAE Level 2 ADAS technologies do not relieve drivers of their fundamental duty to maintain control of the vehicle. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) has been unequivocal on this point: these are driver assistance technologies, not self-driving systems [13]. This regulatory clarity establishes the foundation for negligence analysis in Level 2 contexts, where the human operator remains legally responsible for the dynamic driving task regardless of system engagement.

Systems such as Tesla's Autopilot, General Motors' Super Cruise, and Ford's BlueCruise operate at Level 2 automation, meaning they assist the driver but do not replace the driver [9]. Courts have consistently held that Level 2 system engagement does not transfer liability from the driver to the manufacturer, even when the system is actively controlling steering and acceleration [9]. This legal principle rests on the understanding that Level 2 automation requires constant driver supervision, with the human operator expected to monitor the environment and intervene when necessary.

The mechanism of liability shift in Level 2 contexts operates through Human-Machine Interface (HMI) alerts and Driver Monitoring Systems (DMS). When a vehicle's DMS detects driver inattention, it issues escalating warnings—visual, auditory, and haptic—that transfer the duty back to the driver. If the driver fails to respond to these takeover requests, negligence principles apply. The legal theory is straightforward: the manufacturer fulfilled its duty by providing adequate warnings, and the driver breached their duty of care by ignoring them [11]. This framework places primary liability on the driver when inattention overrides system engagement.

Driver over-reliance constitutes a primary source of potential negligence in ADAS-related accidents [13]. When drivers treat Level 2 systems as fully autonomous, they engage in behavior that falls below the standard of care expected of a reasonable operator. This negligence persists even when manufacturers use terminology like "Autopilot" or "Full Self-Driving" that may create consumer confusion about system capabilities. Courts have recognized this tension but generally maintain that the driver's manual and warning systems provide sufficient notice of supervision requirements.

The landmark case of *Benavides v. Tesla* illustrates the complexities of driver supervision duties in Level 2 contexts. In this Florida case, a driver operating a 2018 Tesla Model 3 engaged the Enhanced Autopilot system before colliding with a semi-trailer truck [45]. The jury returned a significant verdict against Tesla, finding the company liable for the integral role Autopilot and the company's misrepresentations of its capabilities played in the crash (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [5][8]. The legal reasoning centered on Tesla's marketing and CEO messaging that led consumers to believe Autopilot was safer to use without constant hands-on-wheel attention, despite the Level 2 classification requiring supervision.

This verdict demonstrates that while driver supervision duties persist at Level 2, manufacturers cannot escape liability through disclaimers alone when their representations encourage unsafe system use. The court upheld the jury's finding that Tesla's misrepresentations contributed to driver over-reliance, creating a shared responsibility scenario where both parties bore fault. This outcome signals to manufacturers that Level 2 classification does not provide blanket immunity from product liability claims when marketing undermines supervision requirements.

In contrast to Level 2, SAE Level 3 systems permit the driver to disengage from active monitoring during specific operational conditions. Unlike Level 2 systems, which require constant driver supervision, Level 3 lets the driver disengage—reading, watching a movie, or relaxing [37]. However, when the system encounters a situation it cannot handle, it must re-engage the driver, who may need several seconds to regain situational awareness. Mercedes-Benz, the first manufacturer to deploy a commercially available Level 3 system, has accepted liability for accidents occurring during automated operation [9]. This represents a fundamental shift in the liability framework: during Level 3 engagement, the manufacturer assumes responsibility, whereas Level 2始终保持 driver liability.

The critical legal question at Level 3 concerns the transition period—when the system requests human takeover and the human has not yet resumed control [9]. Does the operator potentially have liability for any accident immediately following a takeover request, or only for accidents occurring after a grace period? This ambiguity creates significant litigation risk and underscores why the Level 2 versus Level 3 distinction remains the primary source of legal confusion in ADAS liability cases.

### Product Liability Theories in ADAS Failures

When accidents involve alleged ADAS malfunctions rather than driver inattention, product liability theories provide the legal framework for holding manufacturers accountable. Three primary theories apply to ADAS failures: design defect, manufacturing flaw, and failure-to-warn claims, each with distinct evidentiary requirements and legal standards specific to software and sensor systems [41].

*   **Design Defect Claims:** Allege that the ADAS system was inherently unsafe due to flaws in its engineering or software architecture. To establish a design defect, a plaintiff must demonstrate that the product was not reasonably fit, suitable, or safe for its intended purpose, and that the product's risks outweighed its utility [43]. In ADAS contexts, this often involves showing that sensor configurations, algorithmic decision-making, or system limitations created foreseeable hazards. For example, if a vehicle's automatic emergency braking system fails to detect stationary objects under specific conditions that should have been anticipated during design, a design defect claim may succeed.
    *   *Complexity:* Requires expert testimony on software engineering, sensor fusion, and machine learning systems. The objective complexity of a driving task will likely be captured via engineering design of the Operational Design Domain (ODD) and automated driving system of the vehicle, making products liability resulting from design choices highly fact-specific [20].
*   **Manufacturing Flaw Claims:** Allege that a specific vehicle deviated from its intended design due to errors in production, assembly, or calibration. These claims are generally easier to prove than design defects because they do not require showing the entire product line is defective—only that the individual vehicle suffered from a production error. In ADAS contexts, manufacturing flaws may involve misaligned sensors, faulty wiring, improper software installation, or calibration errors that cause the system to malfunction [27]. When any part of the intricate ADAS network fails due to defective sensors, calibration errors, or flawed software integration, drivers lose critical safety protections [27].
    *   *Liability Chain:* Manufacturers, sellers, or those in the "chain of commerce" could be held liable for injuries caused by faulty ADAS systems under manufacturing defect theories [32].
    *   *Evidence:* Event Data Recorder information becomes critical here, as it can capture system status, sensor readings, and error codes at the time of the accident.
*   **Failure-to-Warn Claims:** Allege that the manufacturer did not provide adequate instructions or warnings about feature limits, creating unreasonable risks for users. From a manufacturer's perspective, failure-to-warn and inadequate instructions cases are often easier to pursue than defective design cases [8]. A manufacturer has certain informational obligations to users and consumers of its products; thus, a product is reasonably safe only if it carries sufficient information to permit consumers to use it with reasonable safety [8].
    *   *Application:* In ADAS litigation, failure-to-warn claims frequently target the adequacy of owner's manual disclosures, in-vehicle warnings, and marketing materials. The *Benavides v. Tesla* verdict turned significantly on this theory, with plaintiffs successfully arguing that Tesla's manuals and clickwrap agreement warnings were inadequate given the company's public representations about Autopilot capabilities [28]. Court filings referred to internal Tesla emails discussing how executives and engineers were aware of Autopilot failing to detect certain obstacles, yet marketing continued to emphasize system capabilities without corresponding warnings about limitations [28].

The *Berkoski v. Honda* case presents a contrasting outcome on product liability theory. The Superior Court of New Jersey Appellate Division ruled that the absence of a driver-assistance system in a 2016 Honda CR-V was not a violation of the New Jersey Products Liability Act (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [43]. The court held that manufacturers cannot be held liable for failing to include safety technology that was not standard equipment at the time of manufacture. This ruling establishes an important boundary: product liability applies to defects in existing systems, not to the absence of optional safety features. Plaintiffs must prove that a product's risks outweighed its utility, and the mere absence of ADAS does not render a vehicle defective under current law [43].

This distinction matters significantly for liability allocation. When ADAS is present but malfunctions, product liability theories apply. When ADAS is absent, negligence principles govern, and manufacturers generally face no duty to equip vehicles with advanced safety systems beyond federal minimum standards. This creates a paradoxical incentive structure where manufacturers may face greater liability exposure by offering ADAS than by omitting it entirely.

Software-specific liability claims introduce additional complexity because over-the-air (OTA) updates can alter system behavior after sale. If a manufacturer pushes an update that degrades system performance or introduces new failure modes, liability may attach to the software modification rather than the original design. This dynamic liability environment challenges traditional product liability frameworks that assume product characteristics remain fixed after purchase.

### Shared Fault and Comparative Negligence Models

When accidents involve both driver error and system malfunction, courts must allocate percentages of fault between human and machine. Comparative negligence models provide the legal mechanism for this allocation, allowing liability to be apportioned based on the relative contribution of each party's negligence to the accident [17].

Current negligence and product liability tort doctrines struggle to allocate liability in semi-autonomous scenarios due to issues of foreseeability, the unpredictability of AI decision-making, and shared human-machine control [11]. Traditional comparative negligence frameworks assume clear causation chains, but ADAS accidents often involve complex interactions where driver inattention and system limitations combine to produce harm. Courts must determine what percentage of the accident resulted from the driver's failure to monitor versus the system's failure to perform as advertised.

In a 2019 Tesla accident, shared liability was evident: the driver was traveling 13 miles per hour over the speed limit, and experts noted the driver had plenty of time to prevent the collision, while Tesla's Autopilot system—which did not detect cross-traffic—also came under fire [4]. This illustrates the comparative negligence scenario where both parties bear responsibility. The driver's speeding constituted negligence, while the system's failure to detect cross-traffic potentially constituted a design or warning defect. A court applying comparative negligence would assign fault percentages to each party, reducing the plaintiff's recovery by their percentage of fault.

Because ADAS is used to assist an individual operating a motor vehicle to make the driving experience safer, those involved in the production and operation of these systems could face liability if they too were negligent under the circumstances [32]. The growing use and reliance on ADAS technology creates shared fault scenarios where several actors may bear responsibility resulting from an accident involving a motor vehicle [32].

Event Data Recorders (EDR) serve as the core evidentiary pillar for establishing fault percentages in comparative negligence analysis. Under 49 C.F.R. Part 563, EDRs must capture specific data elements including vehicle speed, brake application, throttle position, and safety system status in the seconds preceding a crash (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [40][30]. This data allows investigators to reconstruct whether the driver had hands on the wheel, whether the system issued warnings, whether the driver responded to alerts, and whether the system malfunctioned. EDR data may be used as evidence to prove fault or, conversely, to deny claims if it proves policy violations such as driving under the influence or excessive speeding [2].

Vehicle telematics systems store data such as vehicle speed, driver steering, and brake application, providing critical evidence for proving claims under products liability or general negligence theories [32]. When used correctly, EDR evidence can be a powerful tool in securing compensation and holding responsible parties accountable [31]. However, not every vehicle has an EDR, though they are common in newer models and becoming more widespread [31].

Insurance products are evolving to include hybrid policies that cover both the driver and the manufacturer, products liability for autonomous system malfunctions, and potential cyber liability for technology errors [24]. Ultimately, comparative negligence models in ADAS cases require courts to balance competing policy objectives: holding drivers accountable for maintaining supervision while ensuring manufacturers cannot escape liability through inadequate warnings or misleading marketing. As Level 3 systems become more prevalent, this balance will shift, with manufacturers accepting greater liability during automated operation. Until then, the Level 2 framework maintains driver supervision duties while allowing product liability claims when system defects or inadequate warnings contribute to accidents.

## Case Law Precedents: Shifting Liability in ADAS Accidents

The emergence of Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) has fundamentally altered the legal landscape surrounding vehicular accidents, creating unprecedented questions about liability allocation between human operators and vehicle manufacturers. As vehicles transition from SAE Level 2 (driver supervision required) to Level 3 (conditional automation where the system monitors the environment), the traditional framework of driver negligence versus product defect becomes increasingly complex. Current case law reveals a judiciary grappling with how to assign responsibility when control is shared between human and machine, with verdicts varying significantly based on jurisdiction, automation level, and the specific mechanism of liability transfer.

### Benavides v. Tesla: The Verdict Analysis

The landmark Florida case of *Benavides v. Tesla* represents a watershed moment in ADAS liability jurisprudence, establishing critical precedents for how courts evaluate manufacturer responsibility in SAE Level 2 accidents. In this wrongful death action, a jury awarded significant damages against Tesla, with the verdict structure revealing nuanced fault allocation that will influence future litigation involving automated driving systems (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [47]. The jury ultimately placed the majority of the fault on the driver, McGee, who admitted he took his eyes off the road after dropping his phone, while assigning a substantial portion of the blame to Tesla [25].

The damages breakdown demonstrates the severity with which courts may treat marketing misrepresentation in the ADAS context. The jury awarded compensatory damages to the decedent's parents and an injured passenger, and notably, significant punitive damages (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [25]. This punitive component signals judicial willingness to penalize manufacturers whose representations about self-driving technology exceed the actual capabilities of their SAE Level 2 systems. The verdict suggests that juries may be increasingly skeptical of Tesla's representations about its self-driving technology, particularly when marketing creates consumer expectations that conflict with the legal requirement for constant driver supervision [25].

The legal reasoning underlying this verdict hinges on the mechanism of liability shift inherent in Level 2 systems. Under SAE J3016 standards, Level 2 automation requires the driver to continuously supervise the driving environment and remain prepared to take immediate control. However, the *Benavides* case illustrates how Human-Machine Interface (HMI) alerts and the design of the takeover request (TOR) system can transfer duty back to the driver in ways that may be legally insufficient. Tesla presented arguments that a pre-trial agreement would cap punitive damages at three times compensatory damages, but the court found no new arguments warranting overturning the jury's decision [5]. Tesla has indicated it will appeal the verdict to a higher court, and filed a motion to appeal the verdict [26][5].

The punitive damages component draws comparison to historical product liability cases involving known dangers. Legal analysts note parallels to the 1978 Ford Pinto case, where a California jury awarded $125 million in punitive damages (later reduced to $3.5 million) against Ford for knowingly selling a vehicle with a dangerously placed gas tank [8]. Both cases rest on a common legal principle: manufacturers may face enhanced liability when internal knowledge of safety defects conflicts with public representations about product safety. In the Tesla context, court filings referred to internal Tesla emails discussing how executives and engineers were aware of Autopilot limitations while marketing suggested broader capabilities [28].

Florida's strict standard for punitive damages adds significance to this verdict. Florida Statutes §768.72 requires plaintiffs to make a prima facie showing of intentional misconduct or gross negligence before a punitive damages claim can proceed [47]. The trial court's decision to allow the punitive damages claim to proceed meant the case could continue with the possibility of additional damages meant to punish Tesla, reinforcing that Florida courts may find the threshold for gross negligence met when marketing misrepresentation intersects with fatal accidents involving Level 2 systems [45].

The *Benavides* verdict carries implications for how liability shifts between driver and manufacturer in Level 2 contexts. While the driver retained majority fault, the substantial manufacturer liability demonstrates that SAE Level 2 classification does not immunize manufacturers from responsibility. The mechanism of liability shift operates through the HMI design: when visual, auditory, or haptic alerts fail to effectively recapture driver attention, or when marketing creates reasonable reliance on system capabilities beyond their designed Operational Design Domain (ODD), manufacturers may bear partial responsibility even when the legal duty of supervision remains with the driver.

### Berkoski v. Honda: Liability for Absence of ADAS

In stark contrast to *Benavides*, the New Jersey case of *Berkoski v. Honda* establishes that the absence of ADAS technology does not constitute a design defect under state products liability law. The Superior Court of New Jersey Appellate Division ruled that the absence of a driver-assistance system in a 2016 Honda CR-V was not a violation of the state's Products Liability Act (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [43]. This ruling creates a critical boundary in ADAS liability jurisprudence: while manufacturers may be liable for defects in systems they choose to include, they are not currently required to equip vehicles with ADAS technology to avoid design defect claims.

The *Berkoski* decision addresses a fundamental question emerging as ADAS becomes increasingly common: does the industry standard of care now require collision avoidance technology as standard equipment? The New Jersey court's negative answer preserves manufacturer discretion in safety feature deployment, at least for the time period relevant to the 2016 model year vehicle at issue. This stands in contrast to claims brought against trucking manufacturers for failing to include collision avoidance technology in new builds, where Connecticut saw a case in August 2024 involving a 2016 Freightliner that was not equipped with crash avoidance technology [14].

The legal reasoning in *Berkoski* distinguishes between defects in existing systems versus the absence of optional systems. Under products liability theory, manufacturers, sellers, or those in the "chain of commerce" could be held liable for injuries a faulty ADAS system caused, but this liability framework does not extend to systems the manufacturer chose not to include [32]. The first theory of liability—that the product was defectively designed—requires showing that the omission of an alternative design renders the product not reasonably safe, a threshold the *Berkoski* plaintiff failed to meet for ADAS absence [20].

This ruling has significant implications for the liability mechanism in shared control scenarios. In SAE Level 2 systems, the driver maintains continuous supervision responsibility, and the ADAS functions as an assistive layer. The *Berkoski* decision reinforces that this assistive layer remains optional from a design defect perspective, meaning liability allocation in accidents involving vehicles without ADAS follows traditional negligence frameworks rather than products liability theories. The absence of automated braking, lane-keeping assist, or blind spot monitoring does not shift liability toward the manufacturer; instead, the full burden of safe operation remains with the human driver.

The jurisdictional variance between Florida (*Benavides*) and New Jersey (*Berkoski*) highlights how state products liability statutes create different liability landscapes for ADAS-related accidents. New Jersey's Products Liability Act provided the statutory framework for the *Berkoski* ruling, while Florida's common law approach to punitive damages enabled the substantial verdict in *Benavides* [16]. This divergence means that identical accidents involving identical vehicles could produce dramatically different liability outcomes based solely on the forum state, creating uncertainty for manufacturers, insurers, and plaintiffs alike.

The *Berkoski* precedent also interacts with federal preemption questions. Congress has introduced bills which would have generally federalized most product liability law by preempting most forms of state statutory and common law pertaining to product liability actions, though such federalization has not yet occurred [16]. Until federal standards emerge, state courts will continue developing divergent approaches to ADAS liability, with some jurisdictions potentially moving toward requiring ADAS as standard equipment while others maintain the *Berkoski* principle that absence does not equal defect.

### Criminal Liability in Automated Driving Contexts

While *Benavides* and *Berkoski* address civil liability, the emergence of automated driving systems has also created unprecedented questions about criminal liability for operators of semi-autonomous vehicles. Current events focus a spotlight on potential criminal liability for operation of an automated vehicle, with the Arizona Uber robotaxi case establishing that safety drivers in semi-autonomous scenarios can face criminal charges [35]. In that case, the safety driver in an Uber robotaxi pled guilty to an undesignated felony in response to a charge of negligent homicide, demonstrating that the presence of automation does not immunize human operators from criminal responsibility.

The criminal liability framework for automated driving differs fundamentally from civil products liability. While civil cases allocate fault between driver and manufacturer through comparative negligence or strict liability theories, criminal cases require proving individual culpability beyond a reasonable doubt. For SAE Level 2 systems, where the driver must continuously supervise the driving environment, criminal negligence charges remain viable when the driver fails to maintain adequate attention or fails to intervene when the system requests takeover. The mechanism of liability shift in criminal contexts operates through the driver's awareness or indifference with respect to the risks created by their inattention [17].

Before an accident occurs, the driver of an automated vehicle might be criminally negligent by failing to carefully watch the road, failing to take control of the steering wheel, or failing to apply the brakes when the system issues a takeover request [17]. Alternatively, the driver might fail to perceive a warning that the system is approaching its operational limits. The growing use and reliance on ADAS technology creates over-reliance risks where drivers may engage in non-driving related tasks (NDRTs) that render them unable to respond to takeover requests within the available time budget [32].

The distinction between SAE Level 2 and Level 3 becomes particularly critical in criminal liability analysis. At Level 2, the driver bears continuous responsibility for monitoring the driving environment, making criminal negligence charges straightforward when attention lapses contribute to accidents. At Level 3, the system monitors the environment during automated operation, and the driver may disengage from active supervision until the system requests takeover [34]. This shift in monitoring responsibility creates ambiguity about when criminal liability attaches: does it attach at the moment of takeover request, at the moment the driver should have recognized the need to intervene, or only after the driver fails to respond within the allocated time?

Event Data Recorder (EDR) capabilities serve as a core evidentiary pillar in establishing criminal liability for automated driving accidents. EDRs include speed, braking, steering input, and even driver attention metrics that can reconstruct the sequence of events leading to a crash (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [46]. Under 49 C.F.R. Part 563, EDR data may only be retrieved for the purpose of improving motor vehicle safety and vehicle safety research, or through the vehicle owners' consent or a court or administrative order [30]. In criminal proceedings, prosecutors may obtain EDR data through court orders to establish whether the driver was attentive, whether the system issued appropriate warnings, and whether the driver had sufficient time to respond.

The Uber safety driver prosecution illustrates how criminal liability can attach even when automation was engaged. The safety driver's guilty plea to negligent homicide demonstrates that courts view the human operator as the ultimate responsible party in Level 2 and transitional Level 3 scenarios, regardless of system engagement status. This precedent creates significant exposure for professional drivers operating semi-autonomous vehicles in commercial contexts, including trucking, ride-hailing, and delivery services where ADAS or ADS features are increasingly standard.

Looking forward, criminal liability in automated driving contexts will likely expand as more jurisdictions prosecute cases involving semi-autonomous vehicle accidents. The legal framework remains unsettled regarding how to balance system capabilities against human supervision requirements, particularly as marketing and user interfaces create expectations that may conflict with the legal duty of care. Courts currently remain free to decide that an operator of a Level 3 vehicle has liability for automation failure, though the precise boundaries of that liability remain subject to ongoing litigation and legislative development [35].

The intersection of criminal and civil liability creates complex exposure for both drivers and manufacturers. A single accident may generate criminal charges against the driver, civil products liability claims against the manufacturer, and insurance coverage disputes involving both parties. The *Benavides* verdict demonstrates that civil juries may allocate substantial fault to manufacturers even when drivers bear majority responsibility, while the Uber prosecution shows that criminal liability remains firmly with the human operator. This bifurcated liability landscape requires careful navigation by all parties involved in automated vehicle deployment, with EDR data serving as the critical evidentiary foundation for establishing the mechanism of liability shift in each case.

## The Level 3 Transition: Handoff Protocols and Liability Gaps

Level 3 conditional automation represents the most legally ambiguous tier in the SAE International framework for driving automation. Unlike Level 2 systems, which require continuous driver supervision, Level 3 permits the driver to disengage from active monitoring under specific operational conditions. However, when the system encounters scenarios beyond its capabilities, it must request human takeover—a transition period that creates significant liability uncertainty. This section examines the manufacturer accountability structures during conditional automation, the cognitive demands placed on drivers during handoff scenarios, and the regulatory voids that complicate liability allocation in Level 3 deployments.

### Manufacturer Accountability in Conditional Automation

The deployment of Level 3 conditional automation introduces a fundamental shift in liability allocation compared to Level 2 supervised automation. Mercedes-Benz became the first manufacturer to commercially deploy a Level 3 system with its Drive Pilot technology, and critically, the company has accepted liability for accidents occurring during active Level 3 engagement [9]. This represents a notable departure from Level 2 systems like Tesla's Autopilot, GM's Super Cruise, and Ford's BlueCruise, where courts have consistently held that system engagement does not transfer liability from the driver to the manufacturer [9].

The mechanism of liability shift in Level 3 systems operates through the Human-Machine Interface (HMI) alerts that signal when the Automated Driving System (ADS) can no longer safely manage the Dynamic Driving Task (DDT). During active Level 3 engagement, the vehicle itself monitors the environment rather than the driver, and the system makes decisions without driver supervision [34]. When the system reaches its Operational Design Domain (ODD) limits—such as exiting slow-moving motorway traffic conditions where Drive Pilot operates—it issues a takeover request that transfers duty back to the driver [9].

The critical legal question centers on what happens during the transition period when the system requests human takeover and the human has not yet resumed control [9]. Mercedes-Benz's liability acceptance applies during active Level 3 engagement, but the company does not dictate the parameters of criminal law via owner's manual provisions [35]. Courts remain free to determine whether an operator of a Level 3 vehicle has liability for automation failures while the automation is engaged, creating jurisdictional variability in how manufacturer accountability is enforced.

This contrasts sharply with Level 2 liability frameworks established in landmark cases. In *Benavides v. Tesla*, a Florida jury awarded significant damages following a fatal crash involving a 2018 Tesla Model 3 with Enhanced Autopilot engaged (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [25][8]. However, jurors placed the majority of the fault on the driver, who admitted taking his eyes off the road, and a smaller portion on Tesla [25]. This verdict reinforces that Level 2 system engagement does not absolve drivers of supervision duties, even when manufacturers face product liability claims for inadequate warnings about system limitations.

The *Berkoski v. Honda* case further clarifies manufacturer accountability boundaries. The Superior Court of New Jersey Appellate Division ruled that the absence of a driver-assistance system in a 2016 Honda CR-V was not a violation of the state's Products Liability Act (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [43]. This establishes that manufacturers cannot be held liable for failing to equip vehicles with ADAS technologies, distinguishing between defects in existing systems versus the absence of optional safety features.

Manufacturer accountability in Level 3 also intersects with insurance frameworks. The allocation of liability depends on the level of automation, the role of the driver, the existence of system defects, and insurance coverage [10]. As autonomy increases, the relevance of manufacturer and insurer liability grows while driver liability diminishes. Munich Re and other insurers are actively assessing risk models for Level 3 deployments, recognizing the shift from individual driver liability toward vehicle manufacturers and ADS developers [24].

### The "Supervisor" Role and Cognitive Readiness

Level 3 conditional automation creates a unique "supervisor" role that differs fundamentally from both Level 2 driver supervision and Level 4 unsupervised operation. During Level 3 engagement, the driver is not required to monitor the driving environment continuously, but must remain cognitively ready to resume control when the system issues a takeover request [11]. This creates legal implications regarding whether drivers can realistically regain situational awareness within the timeframe provided by takeover requests.

The cognitive readiness challenge stems from the human factors involved in transitioning from a passive monitoring state to active vehicle control. Research indicates that 40 percent of crashes occur because of "recognition error," where drivers fail to perceive hazards in time to respond appropriately [17]. When a Level 3 system disengages, the driver must rapidly process the traffic environment, assess risks, and execute appropriate control inputs—a cognitive load that may exceed human capabilities in emergency scenarios.

The legal implications of requiring drivers to regain situational awareness during takeover requests remain unresolved. Courts must determine whether operators of Level 3 vehicles potentially have liability for accidents immediately following a takeover request, or only for accidents occurring after a grace period [35]. Some regulatory frameworks reference a 10-second grace period following takeover requests, but this standard has not been uniformly adopted across jurisdictions [35].

Driver Monitoring Systems (DMS) serve as a critical mechanism for assessing cognitive readiness during Level 3 operation. These systems mitigate fatigue and distraction by tracking driver attention metrics, but their effectiveness in ensuring safe handoffs remains subject to debate [14]. DMS technology can include speed, braking, steering input, and driver attention metrics that become evidentiary pillars in accident investigations [46].

The "supervisor" role concept proposes defining liability dependent on whether the human driver provided adequate readiness to resume control [11]. This framework distinguishes Level 3 from Level 2, where the driver must continuously supervise the system, and from Level 4, where no human supervisor is required within the ODD. However, the legal standards for what constitutes "adequate readiness" remain undefined in most jurisdictions.

Event Data Recorders (EDR) play a crucial role in documenting driver cognitive readiness during takeover scenarios. Under 49 C.F.R. Part 563, EDRs must capture pre-crash data including vehicle speed, braking status, and driver inputs in the seconds preceding a collision (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [40]. This data becomes critical evidence for determining whether a driver had sufficient time to respond to takeover requests and whether the HMI alerts provided adequate warning. EDR data and dash cam footage can be admissible in car accident claims, but only if they meet certain evidentiary standards [6].

The *Benavides v. Tesla* verdict demonstrates how EDR data influences liability determinations even in Level 2 cases. The defense argued that the crash was caused solely by driver distraction, but plaintiffs successfully demonstrated that Tesla's manuals and clickwrap agreement warnings were inadequate regarding Autopilot's limitations [28]. For Level 3 systems, where drivers are explicitly permitted to disengage from monitoring, the adequacy of takeover warnings and the reasonableness of response timeframes will face even greater legal scrutiny.

Product liability claims can emerge against car manufacturers or software developers for defective systems that fail to adequately monitor driver readiness or provide insufficient takeover warnings [19]. Manufacturers, sellers, or those in the "chain of commerce" could be held liable for injuries caused by faulty ADAS systems under products liability theories [32]. The failure-to-warn theory becomes particularly relevant when HMI alerts do not provide drivers with adequate time or information to safely resume control.

The absence of uniform federal standards means that identical Level 3 deployments may face different liability exposures depending on the jurisdiction where an accident occurs.

Missing standards for handoff timing represent a critical regulatory gap. There is no federally mandated minimum timeframe for takeover requests, leaving manufacturers to determine appropriate warning intervals based on their own safety assessments [48]. The question of whether liability attaches immediately upon takeover request or after a grace period remains unresolved in most jurisdictions [35]. This ambiguity creates legal risk for both manufacturers and drivers, as neither party has clear guidance on when liability transfers during the handoff sequence.

Driver monitoring system requirements also lack standardization. While DMS technology can track driver attention and readiness, there are no federal performance standards specifying what metrics must be monitored or how the data should be used to determine driver availability [14]. Requirements for data storage will become critical for liability claims in Level 3 automated driving, as the transfer of vehicle control from driver to ADS has implications for liability determination [48].

The NHTSA Standing General Order on crash reporting requires ADS developers to report crashes involving SAE Levels 3-5 driving automation technologies, but Level 2 systems, which have been installed on millions of vehicles, face different reporting requirements (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [39]. This creates a data gap in understanding how Level 3 handoff scenarios compare to Level 2 supervision failures in terms of crash frequency and severity.

Regulation No. 79, which governs steering equipment, has been amended to accommodate Level 3 conditional automation, but consumers remain confused about the limits of driver assistance systems [48]. The automotive industry is transitioning from supervised (SAE Level 0-2 or ADAS) to unsupervised driving (Level 4-5), which sparks debate about the need and benefit of an intermediate Level 3 [48]. This transitional status contributes to regulatory uncertainty, as Level 3 occupies a gray zone between driver-supervised and fully autonomous operation.

The 2015 Fixing America's Surface Transportation Act added provisions to Section 30111 regarding vehicle safety standards, but the FMVSS do not currently set performance standards specifically for ADS, and compliant vehicles can generally be equipped with ADS technologies without NHTSA approval [42]. This regulatory permissiveness allows Level 3 deployment but provides minimal guidance on liability allocation when systems fail during handoff scenarios.

Event Data Recorder regulations under 49 C.F.R. Part 563 require certain data elements to be captured, but the NPRM process for updating EDR requirements continues to evolve (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [30]. Safety advocates, insurance institutes, and industry associations have commented on proposed EDR updates, but consensus on what data should be mandatory for Level 3 handoff documentation remains elusive [30].

The regulatory gaps in Level 3 deployment create a liability environment where manufacturers must navigate uncertain legal terrain while drivers face unclear responsibilities during takeover scenarios. Until federal standards establish uniform handoff timing requirements, driver monitoring specifications, and liability transfer mechanisms, Level 3 conditional automation will remain the most legally complex tier in the SAE automation framework.

## Evidentiary Mechanisms: EDR Data and Forensic Analysis

The determination of liability in Advanced Driver-Assistance Systems (ADAS) accidents hinges fundamentally on the availability, authenticity, and interpretability of vehicle data. As vehicles transition from purely mechanical systems to software-defined platforms, the evidentiary landscape has shifted accordingly. Event Data Recorders (EDRs), telemetry logs, and sensor data now serve as the primary mechanisms for reconstructing pre-crash conditions, establishing system engagement status, and allocating fault between human operators and manufacturers. This section examines the technical-legal bridge that transforms raw vehicle data into admissible evidence, addressing regulatory standards, forensic methodologies, and the procedural hurdles that govern data access in litigation.

### Event Data Recorder (EDR) Standards and Accessibility

The regulatory framework governing Event Data Recorders in the United States is anchored in 49 C.F.R. Part 563, which establishes minimum requirements for the data elements, format, and durability of EDR systems in light-duty vehicles. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) maintains authority over these standards, with recent rulemaking activities focused on expanding data capture capabilities to accommodate emerging automated driving technologies (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [30]. Under current regulations, EDRs must record specific parameters during the pre-crash, crash, and post-crash phases, including vehicle speed, engine throttle position, brake status, steering input, and safety system activation. This standardized data set provides a foundational evidentiary baseline for accident reconstruction.

However, the regulatory landscape reveals significant gaps when applied to ADAS-specific functionality. While 49 C.F.R. Part 563 mandates recording of traditional vehicle dynamics, it does not comprehensively require capture of automation system status, Human-Machine Interface (HMI) alerts, or driver monitoring system data. This limitation creates evidentiary challenges in cases where the central question is whether the driver was appropriately engaged with a Level 2 system or whether a Level 3 system failed to issue a proper Takeover Request (TOR). SAE International has advocated for harmonization with UN ECE Regulation No. 160, which includes more robust requirements for Data Storage Systems for Automated Driving (DSSAD), particularly for Level 3 and above [30]. The divergence between U.S. and international standards complicates cross-jurisdictional litigation and creates uncertainty regarding what data manufacturers are obligated to preserve.

Data retrieval for litigation purposes presents additional practical obstacles. Federal regulations recognize that vehicle owners generally control access to EDR data, requiring either owner consent or a court order for download in most circumstances [31]. This ownership framework, while protecting privacy, can delay critical evidence collection in time-sensitive litigation. Furthermore, not all vehicles are equipped with EDRs, and among those that are, data retention periods vary significantly by manufacturer. Some systems overwrite data within seconds of a non-deployment event, while others maintain records for extended periods. This inconsistency means that identical accidents involving different vehicle models may yield dramatically different evidentiary records.

The technical requirements for EDR retrieval also introduce potential points of failure in the evidentiary chain. Proper download requires specialized equipment, often manufacturer-specific, and must be performed by trained technicians to avoid data corruption. Improper access can compromise data integrity, rendering otherwise critical evidence inadmissible [7]. Courts increasingly require expert testimony to establish the authenticity and reliability of EDR data, adding cost and complexity to litigation. The correlation between EDR readings and physical evidence—such as skid marks, vehicle damage patterns, and roadway conditions—must be demonstrated to establish that the recorded data accurately reflects actual pre-crash dynamics rather than sensor anomalies or post-impact artifacts.

NHTSA's ongoing rulemaking activities signal potential expansion of EDR requirements to address these gaps. The agency's regulatory agenda includes consideration of minimum performance standards for automated driving systems that would mandate more comprehensive data recording [15]. However, until these regulations are finalized and implemented, litigants must navigate a patchwork of manufacturer-specific data architectures and varying state evidentiary standards. This regulatory uncertainty particularly affects cases involving the distinction between SAE Level 2 systems, where the driver retains continuous supervision responsibility, and Level 3 systems, where the manufacturer assumes liability during automated operation within the Operational Design Domain (ODD).

### Using Telemetry to Prove System Engagement

Establishing whether an ADAS was active at the time of impact represents the critical forensic question in shared-control accident litigation. Software logs, sensor data, and telemetry records serve as the primary mechanisms for proving system engagement status, but the interpretive challenges vary significantly between SAE Level 2 and Level 3 architectures. For Level 2 systems, including Tesla's Autopilot, Enhanced Autopilot, GM's Super Cruise, and Ford's BlueCruise, the fundamental legal principle remains that system engagement does not transfer liability from the driver to the manufacturer [9]. Telemetry must therefore demonstrate not only that the system was active, but also whether the driver maintained appropriate supervision and responded to HMI alerts.

The landmark case of *Benavides v. Tesla* illustrates the evidentiary complexities inherent in Level 2 liability determinations. In this Florida federal trial, a 2018 Tesla Model 3 operating with Enhanced Autopilot engaged at 69 miles per hour collided with a semi-trailer truck that had turned into its path [8][45]. Telemetry data established that Autopilot was engaged at the time of impact, but the critical forensic question centered on driver attention and response to system limitations. The jury allocated the majority of fault to the driver, McGee, who admitted to looking away from the road after dropping his phone, and a significant portion to Tesla for design defects and misrepresentations of Autopilot capabilities (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [25]. The verdict totaled a significant amount, including compensatory damages to the decedent's parents and another injured party, and punitive damages [25][5].

The *Benavides* verdict demonstrates the mechanism of liability shift in Level 2 contexts: telemetry proving system engagement establishes manufacturer responsibility only for defects in the system itself, while driver monitoring data and HMI alert logs determine whether the driver fulfilled their continuous supervision obligation. Tesla's defense emphasized that the owner's manual required constant driver attention, and telemetry showed no hands-on-wheel input during critical pre-crash seconds. However, plaintiffs successfully argued that Tesla's marketing and HMI design created reasonable reliance on system capabilities beyond actual performance envelope, establishing the manufacturer fault allocation. This proportional liability framework reflects the shared-control reality of Level 2 systems, where both human and machine contribute to the driving task.

In contrast, *Berkoski v. Honda* addressed a fundamentally different question regarding ADAS availability rather than engagement. The Superior Court of New Jersey Appellate Division ruled that the absence of a driver-assistance system in a 2016 Honda CR-V did not constitute a violation of the state's Products Liability Act (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [43]. This holding establishes that manufacturers cannot be held liable for failing to equip vehicles with ADAS features that were optional or unavailable at the time of manufacture. The legal reasoning distinguishes between defects in existing systems (which may create liability) and the non-existence of systems (which generally does not). This precedent limits plaintiff strategies that might otherwise argue manufacturers should have equipped vehicles with collision avoidance technology that could have prevented the accident.

For Level 3 systems, the telemetry requirements and liability implications differ substantially. When a Level 3 system is engaged within its ODD, the manufacturer assumes responsibility for the Dynamic Driving Task (DDT), and liability shifts accordingly unless the driver fails to respond to a TOR [20]. Telemetry must therefore capture not only system engagement status but also ODD boundary conditions, system confidence levels, and the timing and adequacy of takeover requests. Mercedes-Benz's Drive Pilot system, for example, includes comprehensive logging of these parameters to support liability determinations [48]. The mechanism of liability shift in Level 3 contexts turns on whether the system remained within its validated ODD at the time of the incident and whether adequate warning was provided before requesting driver intervention.

NHTSA's Standing General Order on crash reporting requires manufacturers to submit incident reports for ADS and Level 2 ADAS within one day of learning of a crash, including data on the type of automation system engaged at the time of the incident (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [39]. This regulatory requirement creates an additional evidentiary source beyond vehicle-based telemetry, though the publicly available summary data often lacks the granularity needed for detailed liability analysis. Vehicle Identification Number (VIN) data is included in these reports, enabling correlation with specific vehicle configurations and software versions [39]. However, the distinction between ADS and Level 2 ADAS in these reports is not always definitive, creating potential ambiguity in system classification for litigation purposes.

The forensic analysis of telemetry data requires correlation with multiple evidence streams to establish reliability. A high-speed reading from EDR data may reflect impact speed rather than travel speed before evasive actions, necessitating correlation with physical evidence such as skid marks or vehicle damage patterns [7]. Similarly, system engagement logs must be validated against driver monitoring system data, HMI alert records, and roadway conditions to establish whether the driver had adequate opportunity to intervene. This multi-source validation approach is essential for overcoming potential challenges to data authenticity or interpretive accuracy in litigation.

### Privacy and Chain of Custody Concerns

The legal hurdles surrounding vehicle data access create significant procedural barriers to establishing liability in ADAS accidents. Federal regulations stipulate that recorded EDR data may only be retrieved for the purpose of improving motor vehicle safety and vehicle safety research (provided the data are not personally identifiable), or through the vehicle owner's consent or a court or administrative order (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [30]. This privacy framework, while protecting individual rights, introduces substantial friction into the litigation process. Plaintiffs seeking data from vehicles they do not own—such as in multi-vehicle collisions or cases involving deceased drivers—must navigate subpoena processes, potential privacy objections, and varying state laws governing data access.

The chain of custody requirements for vehicle data mirror those for other digital evidence but present unique challenges due to the technical complexity of retrieval processes. Courts require demonstration that data was authentic, relevant, properly preserved, and legally obtained before admitting it as evidence [6]. This means documenting every step from initial data extraction through forensic analysis, including identification of the technician performing the download, the equipment used, the software version, and any potential points of data alteration. Gaps in this documentation can provide grounds for opposing counsel to challenge admissibility, potentially excluding critical evidence from trial.

Federal regulations generally recognize that the vehicle owner controls access to the recorder, creating disputes especially in multi-car crashes where multiple parties may claim ownership interests in the data [31]. Insurance companies may also assert rights to data under policy provisions, creating additional layers of negotiation before plaintiffs can access evidence. In some jurisdictions, police departments may download EDR data during accident investigation, but this data is not automatically preserved for civil litigation and may be subject to different retention policies than those governing criminal proceedings.

Privacy concerns extend beyond EDR data to encompass the broader ecosystem of vehicle telematics, cloud-stored data, and manufacturer databases. Modern vehicles transmit data to manufacturer servers for diagnostic purposes, over-the-air updates, and connected services, creating potential evidence sources beyond the vehicle itself. However, accessing this data requires navigating manufacturer privacy policies, potential Fourth Amendment considerations, and varying state privacy statutes. California's requirement that autonomous vehicles be equipped with an "autonomous vehicle data recorder" represents one approach to addressing these concerns, but this requirement does not extend uniformly across all jurisdictions or vehicle types [20].

The Privacy Act and related statutes provide NHTSA with authority relating to oversight and rulemaking, but these protections also limit data sharing for litigation purposes [42]. NHTSA's crash investigation databases, including the National Automotive Sampling System and Crash Injury Research & Engineering Network, contain valuable data but are primarily designed for safety research rather than individual litigation support [40]. Plaintiffs seeking to leverage these resources must demonstrate that data requests align with permitted uses or obtain data through formal discovery processes that may be subject to protective orders.

International considerations add further complexity to cross-border litigation involving vehicles with global data architectures. GDPR and similar privacy frameworks in other jurisdictions may restrict data transfer or require additional consent mechanisms before vehicle data can be used in U.S. litigation [20]. Manufacturers operating globally may maintain different data retention policies by region, creating asymmetries in evidence availability depending on where the vehicle was manufactured, registered, or operated. These jurisdictional variances require careful coordination in multi-district litigation or cases involving foreign-manufactured vehicles.

The intersection of privacy protections and evidentiary needs creates a tension that courts continue to navigate on a case-by-case basis. Some courts have recognized that privacy interests must be balanced against the need for accurate liability determination in accidents involving sophisticated vehicle technologies. However, the absence of uniform standards means that identical data requests may succeed in one jurisdiction while failing in another. This uncertainty complicates litigation strategy and may incentivize forum shopping or settlement before evidentiary disputes are resolved. As vehicle data becomes increasingly central to liability determinations, the legal framework governing access, preservation, and admissibility will require continued refinement to balance privacy rights with the demands of justice in ADAS accident litigation.

## Regulatory Recommendations and Future Risk Allocation

The evolution of Advanced Driver-Assistance Systems (ADAS) has outpaced the development of coherent liability frameworks, creating significant uncertainty for manufacturers, insurers, regulators, and consumers. As vehicles transition from supervised automation (SAE Level 2) to conditional automation (SAE Level 3), the legal mechanisms for allocating responsibility between human drivers and automated systems require fundamental restructuring. This section synthesizes emerging regulatory proposals, insurance innovations, and strategic recommendations grounded in current case law and evidentiary standards.

### Proposed Frameworks for Liability Clarity

The primary source of legal ambiguity in ADAS accidents stems from the distinction between SAE Level 2 systems, which require continuous driver supervision, and SAE Level 3 systems, which permit conditional automation with takeover requests. Current tort doctrines struggle to allocate liability in semi-autonomous scenarios due to issues of foreseeability, the unpredictability of AI decision-making, and shared human-machine control [11]. To address these gaps, academic proposals and industry guidelines converge on the need for a new legal "supervisor" role that clarifies driver obligations during automated operation.

The American Association of Motor Vehicle Administrators (AAMVA) has developed Guidelines for Regulating Vehicles with Automated Driving Systems, representing every motor vehicle administration and state and provincial police agency in North America [12]. These guidelines emphasize that liability frameworks must explicitly distinguish between automation levels, as the driver's duty of care differs fundamentally between Level 2 (continuous supervision) and Level 3 (monitoring with takeover capability). Academic literature proposes defining this supervisor role with liability dependent on providing adequate attention and responding appropriately to takeover requests, rather than holding drivers strictly liable for all system failures [11].

The mechanism of liability shift operates through Human-Machine Interface (HMI) alerts that transfer duty back to the driver. In Level 3 systems, the transfer of vehicle control from the driver to the Automated Driving System has direct implications for liability determination since the driver is not continuously engaged in the Dynamic Driving Task [3]. When the system issues a Takeover Request (TOR), the driver's legal obligation to resume control begins, and failure to respond within the specified timeframe may constitute negligence. However, if the HMI fails to provide adequate warning or the timeframe is unreasonably short, liability may shift toward the manufacturer for defective design or inadequate warnings.

Landmark case law illustrates how courts currently navigate this ambiguity. In *Benavides v. Tesla*, a Florida jury awarded significant damages in a wrongful death verdict involving Tesla's Autopilot system, with jurors placing the majority of the fault on the driver McGee, who admitted he took his eyes off the road after dropping his phone, and a portion of the blame on Tesla (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [25][26]. This comparative negligence approach reflects the shared control reality of Level 2 systems, where both driver inattention and system limitations contributed to the fatal collision. The verdict demonstrates that even when manufacturers bear partial responsibility, drivers remain legally accountable for maintaining supervision during ADAS engagement.

Conversely, *Berkoski v. Honda* established important boundaries on manufacturer liability. The Superior Court of New Jersey Appellate Division ruled that the absence of a driver-assistance system in a 2016 Honda CR-V was not a violation of the New Jersey Products Liability Act (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [43]. This reasoning limits manufacturer liability to defects in existing systems rather than imposing affirmative duties to equip vehicles with ADAS technologies. The legal distinction matters: manufacturers are liable for how their systems perform when engaged, but not for failing to provide automation features that could have prevented accidents.

Jurisdictional variances further complicate liability allocation.

| Jurisdiction | Regulatory Approach | Liability Framework | Key Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Federal (NHTSA)** | Standing General Order on crash reporting; 49 C.F.R. Part 563 for EDR | Preemption of state safety standards; products liability remains state-level | Requires ADS and Level 2 ADAS crash reporting; EDR data standards [39] |
| **Florida** | Allows AV operation without driver if safety standards met | Comparative negligence; product liability claims permitted | Led autonomous vehicle regulations; Tesla Autopilot cases adjudicated here [4] |
| **Michigan** | Legislation regulating automated vehicles | Assessing liability in ADAS accidents through traditional tort | LKA and ACC systems evaluated under negligence standards [18] |

The Supreme Court has addressed the degree to which federal motor vehicle safety law may preempt state product liability actions based upon defective automobile design, though ultimate holdings have varied based on specific factual circumstances [16]. This federalism tension means that while NHTSA sets minimum performance standards, state courts retain authority over liability allocation in individual accidents, creating a patchwork of precedents that manufacturers must navigate.

### Insurance Models for Shared Control Vehicles

The insurance industry is evolving to accommodate the shift in liability from individual drivers toward vehicle manufacturers and ADS developers. Traditional auto insurance models, which assume human driver fault as the primary risk factor, are inadequate for semi-autonomous vehicles where responsibility is distributed between human operators and automated systems. Insurance products are evolving to include hybrid policies that cover both the driver and the manufacturer, products liability for autonomous system malfunctions, and potential cyber liability for technology errors [24].

Munich Re has partnered with Mobileye to assess AV risk, representing the type of collaboration between reinsurers and technology developers necessary to price emerging risks accurately [24][24]. These partnerships enable insurers to access technical data on system performance, failure modes, and operational design domains, which are essential for underwriting policies that cover both driver negligence and product defects. The NHTSA stresses that states are responsible for crafting insurance frameworks and allocating liability for AVs, meaning insurance innovation must occur within state regulatory boundaries while accommodating federal safety standards [24].

Hybrid insurance products address the coverage gaps created by shared control vehicles through several mechanisms:

*   **Split Coverage Policies:** Separate deductibles and limits for driver-caused accidents versus system-caused accidents, with clear triggers based on EDR data showing whether ADAS was engaged at the time of collision.
*   **Manufacturer-Backed Warranties:** Automakers offering to compensate for damages caused by defective technology, effectively functioning as first-party insurance for system failures [17].
*   **Cyber Liability Endorsements:** Coverage for technology errors, software vulnerabilities, and data breaches that could compromise vehicle control systems.
*   **Usage-Based Premiums:** Pricing models that adjust based on the percentage of miles driven with ADAS engaged, recognizing that risk profiles differ between manual and automated operation.

The allocation of liability depends on the level of automation, the role of the driver, the existence of system defects, and insurance coverage [10]. In general, the greater the autonomy of the vehicle, the greater the relevance of the manufacturer and the insurer, and the less relevance of the driver's personal insurance. This gradient means that Level 2 vehicles will continue to rely primarily on driver insurance with manufacturer products liability as secondary coverage, while Level 3 and above may shift toward manufacturer-primary coverage with driver insurance as backup.

Event Data Recorder capabilities serve as a core evidentiary pillar for insurance claims involving ADAS. EDR technology stores data such as vehicle speed, driver steering, brake application, and even driver attention metrics in the seconds before a crash (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [32][46]. Under 49 C.F.R. Part 563, NHTSA regulates EDR requirements, ensuring standardized data collection that insurers can rely upon for claims adjudication [30]. When used correctly, EDR evidence can be a powerful tool in securing compensation and holding responsible parties accountable, as it provides objective data on whether the driver was attentive, whether the system was engaged, and whether the HMI issued appropriate warnings [31].

Improved subrogation outcomes benefit insurers looking to recover costs from third-party liability when EDR data clarifies fault allocation [7]. For example, if a claimant states they were at a full stop when hit, but the EDR shows acceleration prior to impact, that can raise a red flag and justify further investigation. This evidentiary clarity reduces litigation costs and enables faster settlement of claims where liability is objectively determinable from vehicle data.

No-fault insurance schemes face hurdles in determining coverage and premiums for semi-autonomous risks, as traditional fault attribution becomes ambiguous when control is shared [11]. States with no-fault regimes may need to adapt their personal injury protection frameworks to account for manufacturer liability, potentially creating hybrid systems where medical expenses are covered under no-fault provisions but property damage and system-defect claims proceed through traditional liability channels.

### Strategic Recommendations for Stakeholders

Based on the evidence gathered from case law, regulatory frameworks, and insurance innovations, the following actionable recommendations address liability risk mitigation for manufacturers, regulators, and legal practitioners.

**For Manufacturers:**
*   **Enhance HMI Design for Clear Takeover Requests:** Manufacturers must ensure that Takeover Requests provide adequate warning time and use multi-modal alerts (visual, auditory, haptic) to maximize driver response. The mechanism of liability shift depends on whether the driver received reasonable notice to resume control, and inadequate HMI design exposes manufacturers to products liability claims [3].
*   **Implement Robust Driver Monitoring Systems:** Driver Availability Recognition Systems that monitor the driver's capability to take back control of the vehicle should be standard in Level 2 and Level 3 vehicles [3]. These systems provide evidentiary support for demonstrating that the manufacturer fulfilled its duty to ensure driver engagement, shifting liability toward the driver if monitoring data shows inattention.
*   **Document Operational Design Domains Clearly:** Manufacturers should provide explicit documentation of the conditions under which ADAS functions reliably, including weather, road types, and traffic scenarios. Failure to warn about feature limits constitutes a products liability exposure, as seen in cases where drivers engaged Autopilot in construction zones or inclement weather where the system was not designed to operate [4][41].
*   **Preserve EDR Data Integrity:** Ensure that Event Data Recorders comply with 49 C.F.R. Part 563 requirements and that data is accessible for legitimate legal and insurance purposes. Vehicle data may provide clearer evidence in liability disputes, but manufacturers must balance data preservation with privacy obligations [44].

**For Regulators:**
*   **Standardize Liability Frameworks Across Jurisdictions:** The current patchwork of state regulations creates uncertainty for manufacturers operating nationally and for consumers traveling across state lines. NHTSA should work with AAMVA to develop model legislation that clarifies the supervisor role and liability allocation for each SAE automation level [12].
*   **Expand EDR Requirements for ADAS Equipped Vehicles:** Current EDR regulations under 49 C.F.R. Part 563 should be updated to require recording of ADAS engagement status, HMI alert timing, and driver monitoring system data. This information is critical for proving fault in ADAS accidents and enables more accurate liability allocation (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [40].
*   **Clarify Federal Preemption Boundaries:** The Supreme Court has addressed the degree to which federal motor vehicle safety law may preempt state product liability actions, but ongoing rulemaking should provide clearer guidance on which aspects of ADAS liability fall under federal versus state authority [16]. This clarity reduces litigation costs and enables manufacturers to design compliance programs with greater certainty.
*   **Require Transparency in ADAS Performance Data:** NHTSA's Standing General Order on crash reporting should be expanded to require manufacturers to publish aggregate data on ADAS performance, including disengagement rates, false alert frequencies, and accident involvement when systems are engaged (see **Key Data & Definitions**) [39]. This transparency enables regulators to identify systemic safety issues and informs consumer purchasing decisions.

**For Legal Practitioners:**
*   **Secure EDR Evidence Early in Claims Process:** Victims should consider discussing EDR evidence with their attorney early in the claims process, as this data can be overwritten or lost if not preserved promptly [31]. Expert analysis of EDR information is essential for legal and insurance cases, as raw data requires interpretation to establish causation and fault [7].
*   **Distinguish Between Level 2 and Level 3 in Pleadings:** Legal arguments must explicitly distinguish between SAE Level 2 (driver supervision) and Level 3 (conditional automation), as the duty of care differs fundamentally between these categories. Courts have consistently held that Level 2 system engagement does not transfer liability from the driver to the manufacturer, even when the system is active [9].
*   **Evaluate Both Negligence and Products Liability Theories:** ADAS accidents may support multiple legal theories, including driver negligence for failure to supervise, manufacturer negligence for inadequate warnings, and strict products liability for defective design [17]. Practitioners should plead alternative theories to maximize recovery options, as demonstrated in *Benavides v. Tesla* where both driver fault and manufacturer defect contributed to the verdict.
*   **Understand Jurisdictional Variations in Liability Standards:** Practitioners must account for jurisdictional differences when filing claims, as Florida, Michigan, DC, and federal regulations each impose different requirements on ADAS liability [4][18]. Venue selection may significantly impact case outcomes based on local precedents and statutory frameworks.

The transition to shared human-machine control requires coordinated action across all stakeholders. Manufacturers must design systems that clearly communicate their limitations and monitor driver engagement. Regulators must establish consistent frameworks that balance innovation with accountability. Legal practitioners must develop expertise in the technical and evidentiary aspects of ADAS litigation. Only through this coordinated approach can liability allocation evolve to match the technological reality of semi-autonomous vehicles.
