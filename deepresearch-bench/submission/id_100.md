# The Relational Rupture: An Analytical Assessment of AI's Impact on Human-to-Human Connection

## Introduction: Defining the AI-Mediated Relational Landscape

The integration of artificial intelligence into everyday social ecosystems represents a fundamental transformation in how individuals establish, maintain, and experience human connection. This report examines the relational consequences of AI deployment through two distinct but interconnected pathways: AI systems that mediate communication between humans, and AI systems that serve as companions to humans directly. The ambiguity surrounding the term "interpersonal relations" in contemporary discourse obscures critical distinctions between these pathways, each carrying distinct psychological mechanisms and societal implications. Clarifying this terminology is not merely semantic; it determines whether policy interventions target the appropriate locus of relational change and whether ethical frameworks address the correct vectors of potential harm or benefit.

### Scope and Terminology: Distinguishing Mediation from Companionship

The analytical foundation of this report rests on a precise distinction between AI-mediated communication (AI-MC) and AI companionship. These categories represent fundamentally different relational architectures with divergent implications for human-to-human bonds. AI-mediated communication refers to instances where artificial intelligence facilitates, enhances, or modifies communication between two or more human parties. In this configuration, the AI functions as a relational mediator—a third-party infrastructure that shapes the quality, content, or efficiency of human-to-human exchange without replacing the human participants themselves [12]. Empirical work on AI-mediated communication has demonstrated that AI support during social exchanges can increase communication confidence, competence, and expressiveness among human participants [12]. The enhanced effectiveness of AI-mediated communication in complex cross-cultural negotiations suggests that technological mediation can increase, rather than decrease, communication richness under specific conditions [19]. This finding necessitates a reconceptualization of how technological intermediaries function in relational contexts, moving beyond simplistic assumptions that mediation inherently diminishes authenticity.

In contrast, AI companionship describes direct human-AI relationships where the artificial agent serves as the primary relational partner rather than a facilitator of human-to-human interaction. When a system recalls prior interactions, adapts its tone on the basis of user mood, or uses emotionally congruent language, users may come to feel "known" by the system [12]. Research in human–computer interaction suggests that emotional resonance—even when simulated—can enhance relational perception and foster attachment-like bonds [12]. When AI acts as a partner, proximity is felt directly—the user may begin to form attachment-like bonds or treat the system as a confidant or companion [12]. This distinction carries profound implications: in mediation scenarios, the AI shapes the channel through which human connection flows; in companionship scenarios, the AI becomes the destination of relational investment itself.

The psychological mechanisms underlying these two categories differ substantially. AI-mediated communication operates through mechanisms of communicative enhancement—reducing friction, providing linguistic support, or managing informational asymmetries between human parties. The relational outcome remains anchored in human-to-human connection, with the AI serving as infrastructure. AI companionship, however, operates through mechanisms of simulated social presence and parasocial interaction. Companion AI agents often appear to be emotionally responsive, even when they reply and generate responses which are simulated [5]. Since chatbots' personality traits affect following reaction intensity, measurement of AI companionship has confirmed that users experience significant emotional engagement with these systems [5]. The advent of AI-MC has the potential to be even more revolutionary in that it challenges our understanding of human communication itself, forcing reconsideration of what constitutes authentic relational exchange [8].

This report's primary analytical focus concerns the spillover effects of both AI-MC and AI companionship on human-to-human bonds. The distinction matters because spillover operates differently in each case. For AI-mediated communication, spillover effects concern whether enhanced digital communication translates to stronger offline relationships or whether reliance on AI mediation erodes autonomous communicative competence. For AI companionship, spillover effects concern whether satisfaction of relational needs through AI substitutes for human connection or whether AI relationships serve as bridges to human connection. Researchers have highlighted the positive effects of human-AI relationships, noting that chatbots and other AI tools may help reduce loneliness through mechanisms of simulated companionship [8]. However, AI companions also serve as bridges to human connection in therapeutic contexts, where therapists report that patients who initially struggle with human interaction use AI companions as training wheels for social and emotional skills [6].

The developmental implications of this distinction are particularly acute for young people, who are early adopters of new technologies and may develop intense relationships with AI companions during critical periods of social and emotional development [6]. The impact on identity formation, social skill development, and peer relationships requires careful monitoring because these developmental windows shape lifelong relational patterns [6]. Young people are particularly vulnerable to problematic use patterns associated with social appearance anxiety, social interaction anxiety, and rejection sensitivity [13]. Rejection sensitivity correlates with elevated anxiety and reduced mental health outcomes, creating a feedback loop where AI companionship may either alleviate or exacerbate underlying relational vulnerabilities depending on design and usage patterns [13].

Maintaining this distinction throughout the analysis prevents conflation of fundamentally different phenomena. When AI mediates human-human interaction, the ethical questions concern transparency, bias in message modification, and preservation of human agency in communication. When AI serves as companion, the ethical questions concern attachment formation, emotional manipulation through variable reward schedules, and the displacement of human relationships. The report consistently distinguishes between these categories while analyzing their respective spillover effects on the broader relational landscape.

### Theoretical Framework: The MIRA Model and Relational Adaptation

To analyze the mechanisms through which AI transforms relational ecosystems, this report employs the Machine-Integrated Relational Adaptation (MIRA) model developed by Boyd & Markowitz (2025) [12]. The MIRA model provides a comprehensive theoretical lens for understanding how AI systems transition from functional tools into socially interpreted agents within human relational ecosystems. The model's analytical power lies in its specification of antecedents, processes, and outcomes that govern AI-human relational dynamics across both mediation and companionship contexts.

The heart of the MIRA model consists of core mechanisms—the processes that transform AI from a functional tool into a socially interpreted agent [12]. These mechanisms are divided into two layers: building blocks of relational meaning and social interpretation processes. The building blocks layer encompasses the technical and design features that enable AI systems to exhibit relational cues, including memory of prior interactions, emotional language adaptation, and responsiveness to user mood states. The social interpretation layer encompasses the psychological processes through which users attribute relational significance to these cues, drawing on established patterns of human social cognition to interpret AI behavior as socially meaningful.

The antecedents in the MIRA framework include user characteristics, system design features, and contextual factors that shape initial relational orientation toward AI. User characteristics encompass individual differences in attachment style, rejection sensitivity, and prior experience with technology-mediated relationships. Research applying attachment theory as a framework to understand relationships with social chatbots demonstrates that individual attachment patterns predict the intensity and quality of human-chatbot bonds [5]. System design features include the degree of anthropomorphism, the sophistication of natural language processing, and the presence of memory functions that enable continuity across interactions. Contextual factors include the social environment in which AI interaction occurs, cultural norms regarding technology use, and the availability of alternative human relationship options.

The processes layer of MIRA specifies the dynamic mechanisms through which relational meaning accumulates over time. Trust in intelligent robots tends to start low and increase over time, while the opposite pattern has been observed for disembodied AI [2]. Understanding the specific trajectories of trust formation and maintenance across a range of collaborative AI applications is essential for predicting relational outcomes [2]. The model integrates insights from Media Richness Theory, Technology Acceptance Model, and Trust Formation Theory to explain how users evaluate AI systems as relational partners [19]. Media Richness Theory provides the primary theoretical foundation for understanding how different AI modalities convey relational information, while Trust Formation Theory explains the psychological mechanisms through which users develop reliance on AI systems [19].

MIRA predicts that these tendencies can, over time, shift users' epistemic reliance—not just on AI as an information source but as a relational partner [12]. This shift in epistemic reliance represents a critical outcome in the model, with profound implications for human-to-human relationships. In the outcomes layer, this may lead to diminished trust in human expertise across sensitive domains as users increasingly defer to AI judgment in relational matters [12]. The model accounts for both positive and negative relational outcomes, recognizing that AI integration can enhance human connection through improved communication efficiency while simultaneously creating risks of relational displacement through excessive reliance on AI companionship.

The MIRA framework also incorporates feedback loops that capture the iterative nature of relational adaptation. Although the practical benefits and drawbacks of AI are widely recognized, its impact on social and interpersonal processes—arguably the most central aspects of human life—remains less understood and undertheorized [12]. We have entered a new technological frontier in which AI not only mediates communication but actively shapes the relational expectations users bring to all social interactions [12]. The model's process-oriented structure enables analysis of how initial interactions with AI systems establish patterns that compound over time, creating path dependencies in relational behavior.

Other authors have argued that the reciprocal exchange of information determines how relationships develop and change, a principle that MIRA extends to human-AI contexts [8]. Social penetration theory suggests that relationship depth increases through progressive self-disclosure, and AI systems that facilitate or simulate this process can accelerate perceived relational intimacy [8]. However, the simulated nature of AI reciprocity raises questions about the authenticity of resulting relational bonds and their transferability to human-to-human contexts. The MIRA model addresses this by distinguishing between perceived relational quality and functional relational outcomes, enabling analysis of cases where users report high satisfaction with AI relationships while experiencing deterioration in human relationships.

The framework's applicability extends beyond individual psychology to organizational and societal levels. Thematic and content analysis of intellectual structure in AI research resulted in a conceptual framework encompassing antecedents, mediators, moderators, and consequences of social connection with conversational agents [18]. This broader application enables the model to account for how institutional adoption of AI mediation tools shapes normative expectations about communication efficiency and emotional labor. The proposed theoretical framework could also apply to other human-to-human relationships, such as the doctor-patient relationship, when these are mediated by AI systems [8].

Trust formation represents a critical study area within the MIRA framework, as historical evolution of mediated communication has evolved significantly from foundational work on information transmission to contemporary analyses of AI-enhanced communication systems [19]. The model accounts for how trust trajectories differ between embodied and disembodied AI, with implications for how users generalize trust from AI systems to human institutions. Eisenberger et al. found that animatronic pets not only decreased loneliness but also improved psychological outcomes such as resilience and optimism, suggesting that even simple AI embodiments can produce measurable relational effects [10]. However, while digital technology interventions showed promise in addressing loneliness, the evidence for sustained effects was limited, particularly in studies with shorter durations and smaller sample sizes [10].

The MIRA model's outcomes layer encompasses both individual psychological changes and broader relational ecosystem shifts. Individual outcomes include changes in loneliness, social anxiety, communicative self-efficacy, and attachment security. Relational ecosystem outcomes include changes in the frequency and quality of human-to-human interaction, shifts in normative expectations about relationship maintenance, and alterations in the distribution of emotional labor across social networks. The model predicts that widespread AI adoption will produce heterogeneous outcomes depending on user characteristics, system design, and contextual factors, rejecting simplistic narratives of universal relational enhancement or universal relational degradation.

This theoretical framework provides the analytical foundation for subsequent sections examining specific domains of AI-mediated relational change. By specifying mechanisms rather than merely documenting correlations, MIRA enables identification of intervention points where design choices or policy interventions might steer relational outcomes toward beneficial rather than harmful equilibria. The model's emphasis on adaptation processes rather than static states acknowledges that human-AI relational dynamics remain in flux as both technology and social norms evolve, requiring ongoing empirical validation and theoretical refinement.

## The Psychology of Connection: Motivational Shifts in the AI Era

The integration of artificial intelligence into interpersonal dynamics represents more than a technological shift—it constitutes a fundamental reconfiguration of the psychological drivers that motivate human connection. As AI systems increasingly mediate, supplement, or substitute for human-to-human relationships, the underlying mechanisms that govern why individuals seek connection, how they experience intimacy, and what they expect from relational exchanges are undergoing significant transformation. This section examines the psychological architecture of these shifts, focusing on two interrelated dimensions: the role of loneliness as both a therapeutic target and an epidemic driver of AI adoption, and the recalibration of relational motivation from vulnerability-based engagement toward convenience and control-oriented interaction patterns.

### Loneliness as a Driver: Therapeutic Utility vs. Epidemic Response

Loneliness operates as a dual-force catalyst in the adoption of AI-mediated connection, functioning simultaneously as a public health crisis demanding intervention and as a psychological vulnerability that AI systems are positioned to exploit or alleviate. The distinction between these two framings—therapeutic utility versus epidemic response—reveals critical differences in how AI companionship is deployed across demographic groups and the psychological mechanisms through which it operates.

For older adults, AI companionship functions primarily as a health intervention targeting documented loneliness-related morbidity. Research indicates that interventions combining AI-assisted companionship with physical or cognitive activities, incorporating longer durations and frequent meaningful interactions, hold promise for addressing loneliness in this population [10]. The psychological mechanism here operates through simulated social presence: when AI systems recall prior interactions, adapt tone based on user mood, or employ emotionally congruent language, users experience a sense of being "known" by the system [12]. This perceived continuity and attentiveness activates attachment-related psychological pathways, providing emotional resource support that functions as a direct conduit to assist individuals in replenishing their emotional reserves [11]. The mechanism is not merely companionship but structured emotional scaffolding—leader emotional support provides psychological comfort, care, and motivation, enhancing sense of belonging and reducing isolation-related stress markers.

However, the general population's adoption of AI companionship operates through a distinctly different psychological mechanism: emotional substitution rather than health intervention. Young people, as early adopters of new technologies, may develop intense relationships with AI companions during critical periods of social and emotional development [6]. The impact on identity formation, social skill development, and peer relationships requires careful monitoring because the psychological function shifts from therapeutic supplementation to relational replacement. When AI acts as a partner rather than a supplement, proximity is felt directly—the user may begin to form attachment-like bonds or treat the system as a confidant or companion [12]. This mechanism operates through what researchers term "pseudo-intimacy": the simulation of relational depth without the reciprocal vulnerability that characterizes authentic human connection [16].

The psychological distinction between these two populations reveals a critical tension in AI-mediated loneliness reduction. For older adults, the mechanism is additive—AI companionship supplements diminished social networks without necessarily displacing human connection. Studies suggest that sustainable and scalable AI-based strategies, aligned with ethical and human-centered design principles, are critical for the long-term reduction of loneliness in this demographic [10]. The intervention works because it addresses a deficit without creating dependency; the AI serves as a bridge rather than a destination.

For the general population, particularly younger users, the mechanism becomes substitutive. AI companions serve as bridges to human connection for some—therapists report that patients who initially struggle with human interaction may use AI as training wheels for social and emotional skills [6]. However, this bridging function carries the risk of becoming a terminal destination rather than a transitional tool. The psychological mechanism here involves variable reward schedules and emotional shaping practices that can produce problematic use patterns. Research on dating apps has established that social appearance anxiety, social interaction anxiety, and rejection sensitivity correlate with problematic usage [13], and AI companions may similarly exploit these vulnerabilities through design patterns that maximize engagement without regard for relational health outcomes.

The epidemic framing of loneliness thus creates a market incentive structure that may conflict with therapeutic outcomes. When loneliness is positioned as a crisis requiring immediate intervention, AI vendors are motivated to present their systems as solutions without adequate differentiation between therapeutic supplementation and relational substitution. The psychological mechanism of emotional resonance—even when simulated—can enhance relational satisfaction in the short term [12], but this enhancement may come at the cost of developing tolerance for the friction and uncertainty inherent in human relationships. Users may come to expect the consistency and unconditional positive regard that AI systems provide, finding human relationships comparatively demanding and unpredictable.

This dynamic creates a paradox: the very features that make AI effective at reducing loneliness (consistency, availability, non-judgment) may simultaneously erode the psychological capacities required for sustaining human connection. The mechanism operates through what attachment theory describes as internal working models—repeated interactions with AI systems that never reject, never misunderstand, and never require repair may recalibrate user expectations for all relationships [5]. Studies taking the relationship between AI and attachment theory seriously suggest that higher attachment anxiety is typically associated with lower trust in AI, yet the reverse pathway—AI use shaping attachment patterns—remains underexplored [12].

The therapeutic utility versus epidemic response distinction thus reveals a fundamental question about the psychological function of AI companionship: is it treating loneliness as a symptom to be managed, or is it responding to loneliness as a market opportunity to be captured? The answer determines whether the mechanism operates through genuine emotional support or through engineered dependency.

### Changing the 'Why': From Vulnerability to Convenience and Control

The integration of AI into relational contexts fundamentally alters the motivational architecture of human connection, shifting the underlying "why" of relationship formation from vulnerability-based engagement toward convenience and control-oriented interaction. This shift represents more than a change in medium—it constitutes a reconfiguration of the psychological contract that governs what individuals seek from connection and what they are willing to risk in pursuit of it.

Human relationships have traditionally required vulnerability as an entry condition. Trust is a foundational dimension of human relationships, enabling cooperation, reducing uncertainty, and fostering emotional security [12]. Classic models of trust emphasize its dynamic, reciprocal nature—trust is built through repeated exchanges where both parties risk disappointment and demonstrate reliability. The psychological mechanism here involves what researchers term "second-person authenticity": the mutual recognition that both parties are genuine, fallible, and committed to the relationship despite its inherent uncertainties [8]. This vulnerability is not incidental but constitutive—without the possibility of rejection, misunderstanding, or conflict, the relationship lacks the friction necessary for growth and deepening intimacy.

AI-mediated connection operates through a fundamentally different psychological mechanism: controlled intimacy without vulnerability. When AI acts as a mediator, psychological proximity may be more diffuse, enhancing the perceived warmth, clarity, or depth of a human-to-human exchange [12]. However, when AI acts as the relationship partner itself, the mechanism shifts to simulated vulnerability—users can disclose, explore, and express without facing the consequences that accompany human judgment. This controlled environment eliminates rejection sensitivity, a psychological factor strongly associated with problematic relationship patterns [13]. The psychological appeal is clear: users gain the emotional benefits of connection without the psychological costs of vulnerability.

This shift from vulnerability to convenience operates through several interconnected mechanisms. First, AI systems provide what researchers describe as emotional safety through predictable responsiveness. Unlike human partners who may be unavailable, distracted, or emotionally inconsistent, AI companions offer continuous availability and contextually appropriate responses. Users often interpret coherent, contextually appropriate, or attuned responses as indicators of credibility [12], creating a psychological experience of being understood that requires no reciprocal effort. The mechanism here is unilateral emotional labor—the AI performs all the work of attunement, memory, and adaptation while the user receives the benefits without the burden of reciprocal care.

Second, the convenience mechanism operates through friction elimination. Human relationships require negotiation, compromise, and the management of conflicting needs—psychological processes that demand cognitive and emotional resources. AI relationships eliminate this friction through algorithmic accommodation. The system adapts to the user rather than requiring mutual adaptation, creating a psychological experience of effortlessness that human relationships cannot match. This mechanism is particularly appealing to individuals with social interaction anxiety or those who have experienced relational trauma, as it provides connection without the threat of interpersonal harm [13].

Third, the control mechanism operates through what researchers term "deceptive design" in system architecture and user interface [13]. AI systems are engineered to maximize engagement through psychological techniques that exploit user vulnerabilities—variable reward schedules, personalized affirmation, and the simulation of unconditional positive regard. The user experiences a sense of control over the relationship (they can terminate, restart, or modify the interaction at will) while simultaneously being subject to algorithmic influence patterns designed to sustain engagement. This creates a psychological paradox: the user feels empowered while being systematically shaped by design choices they cannot see or contest.

The psychological consequences of this shift from vulnerability to convenience and control extend beyond individual relationships to reshape relational expectations more broadly. When users become accustomed to AI-mediated connection that offers emotional safety without vulnerability, they may find human relationships comparatively demanding and unsatisfying. The mechanism here involves recalibrated expectations—repeated exposure to frictionless interaction creates a psychological baseline that human relationships struggle to meet. This does not necessarily mean users abandon human connection, but rather that they approach it with diminished tolerance for the uncertainty, effort, and risk that authentic intimacy requires.

Research on human-AI relationships highlights challenges, downsides, and impacts on human-to-human relationships that emerge from this dynamic [5]. The mediating role of AI in human relationships presents a moral challenge: when AI shapes the terms of connection, who bears responsibility for the psychological outcomes? [8] The mechanism of AI-mediated communication can produce better outcomes in specific contexts—increasing communication speed, promoting positive emotional language, and leading conversation partners to evaluate each other as closer and more cooperative [8]. However, these benefits may come at the cost of authentic relational development, as the AI performs the emotional work that would otherwise build relational capacity between humans.

The shift from vulnerability to convenience and control also raises questions about the developmental function of relational friction. Human relationships serve as contexts for psychological growth precisely because they require individuals to navigate difference, manage conflict, and develop empathy through sustained engagement with another's subjectivity. AI relationships, by eliminating this friction, may provide emotional comfort while simultaneously stunting the psychological capacities that develop through relational challenge. The mechanism here involves what developmental frameworks describe as growth through negotiated impasse—the psychological maturation that occurs when individuals must work through misunderstanding, disappointment, and repair [4].

This dynamic creates a critical distinction between AI as relational supplement versus AI as relational substitute. When AI mediates human-to-human connection, it may enhance communication effectiveness while preserving the vulnerability and friction necessary for relational growth. When AI substitutes for human connection, it provides emotional safety at the cost of developmental opportunity. The psychological mechanism differs fundamentally: mediation preserves the human relational contract while improving its execution; substitution replaces the contract entirely with a simulated alternative that offers comfort without growth.

The evidence suggests that the motivational shift from vulnerability to convenience and control is not merely a change in preference but a structural transformation in how individuals conceptualize the purpose of connection. Trust and truth remain foundational dimensions of human relationships [12], but AI-mediated connection redefines what trust means—from trust earned through demonstrated reliability despite fallibility to trust engineered through algorithmic consistency. This redefinition carries profound implications for how individuals approach all relationships, potentially creating a psychological orientation that privileges safety over authenticity and control over mutuality.

The long-term psychological consequences of this shift remain underexplored, but the mechanism is clear: AI systems that offer connection without vulnerability create a psychological pathway that may reduce loneliness in the short term while eroding the capacities necessary for sustaining human intimacy in the long term. The question is not whether AI can reduce loneliness—it demonstrably can through simulated social presence and emotional scaffolding—but whether the psychological cost of that reduction, measured in diminished tolerance for relational friction and recalibrated expectations for human connection, represents an acceptable trade-off.

## AI as Relational Mediator: Transforming Human-to-Human Interaction

The integration of artificial intelligence into human relational contexts represents a fundamental shift in how individuals connect, communicate, and resolve conflicts. Unlike direct human-AI companionship, AI-mediated human-human interaction positions algorithms as intermediaries that shape, filter, and sometimes substitute for organic interpersonal exchange. This section examines three critical domains where AI functions as a relational mediator: romantic partner selection through dating platforms, professional communication in remote work environments, and dispute resolution in legal and mediation contexts. Each domain reveals distinct mechanisms through which algorithmic mediation transforms the texture, authenticity, and ethical foundations of human connection.

### Algorithmic Matchmaking and the Commodification of Romance

Dating applications have evolved from simple digital directories into sophisticated AI-driven ecosystems that actively condition user behavior and compromise decisional autonomy in partner selection. The deployment of manipulative and addictive design patterns in AI-driven dating platforms transforms romantic pursuit into a commodified experience governed by engagement metrics rather than genuine compatibility [13]. Platforms such as Tinder and Hinge utilize emotionally persuasive interface features that exploit psychological vulnerabilities through variable reward schedules, creating intermittent reinforcement patterns similar to those found in gambling systems [13].

The psychological mechanism underlying this behavioral conditioning operates through what researchers term "hypernudging"—a mode of regulation by design where big data algorithms subtly steer user choices without explicit coercion [13]. When users encounter profiles, the algorithmic curation creates an illusion of infinite choice while simultaneously narrowing actual options based on engagement optimization rather than compatibility assessment. This creates a paradoxical state where users feel empowered by choice abundance while experiencing diminished autonomy over their actual selection criteria.

Empirical research demonstrates that problematic use of dating apps correlates significantly with social appearance anxiety, social interaction anxiety, and rejection sensitivity [13]. The psychological mechanism here involves the internalization of algorithmic validation: users begin to interpret match outcomes as reflections of personal worth rather than algorithmic optimization for platform engagement. This creates a feedback loop where rejection sensitivity increases platform dependency, as users seek algorithmic validation to compensate for diminished self-esteem.

The commodification of romance extends beyond individual psychology to structural concerns about informational self-determination and psychological integrity. Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights, which protects private life, has been interpreted to encompass informational self-determination—the right to control how personal data shapes one's relational opportunities [13]. When dating platforms deploy dark patterns that manipulate user attention and decision-making, they infringe upon this fundamental right by substituting algorithmic objectives for authentic human preference formation.

Research by Huang and Gong identifies that users become "too vulnerable to resist" these manipulative architectures, particularly when platforms exploit attachment insecurities through design features that trigger anxiety-driven engagement [13]. The mechanism operates through what attachment theory describes as hyperactivation of the attachment system: users with anxious attachment styles experience heightened sensitivity to rejection cues, which platforms inadvertently (or deliberately) amplify through features like read receipts, match expiration timers, and visibility indicators.

The distinction between AI-mediated human-human romance and direct human-AI companionship is critical here. In dating apps, the AI does not replace the human partner but rather filters and conditions which human partners become visible and accessible. This mediation creates a spillover effect where users' expectations of human relationships become shaped by algorithmic logic—expecting instant compatibility assessment, endless option availability, and low-commitment exploration patterns that may prove maladaptive in offline relational contexts [16].

### Communication Intermediaries: Trust and Transparency in Professional Settings

AI-mediated communication (AMC) in professional environments presents a contrasting picture to romantic mediation, offering efficiency gains while introducing distinct trust formation challenges. The theoretical foundation for understanding AMC draws from Media Richness Theory, which traditionally posits that communication effectiveness decreases as technological mediation increases [19]. However, empirical findings challenge this assumption, demonstrating that AI mediation can actually enhance communication richness under specific conditions, particularly in cross-cultural professional negotiations.

Research indicates that AI support during social exchanges increases communication confidence, competence, and expressiveness among participants [12]. The psychological mechanism operates through what researchers describe as a "regulatory buffer"—the AI intermediary reduces cognitive load associated with message formulation, allowing users to focus on substantive content rather than linguistic precision. This is particularly valuable in cross-cultural contexts where language barriers might otherwise inhibit full participation.

A notable finding from AMC studies demonstrates a 31% improvement in cross-cultural understanding when AI mediation tools are deployed in professional settings [19]. The mechanism underlying this improvement involves real-time translation, cultural context annotation, and tone calibration that reduces misinterpretation risks. When the AI system recalls prior interactions, adapts its tone based on user mood, or uses emotionally congruent language, users may come to feel "known" by the system, which paradoxically enhances their willingness to engage authentically with human counterparts [12].

However, initial trust barriers present significant obstacles to AMC adoption. Trust formation in human-autonomy teaming follows temporal dynamics that differ from human-human trust development [2]. Early interactions with AI-mediated systems require users to overcome skepticism about algorithmic competence and intentions. Research by de Visser et al. identifies that trust in AI-mediated contexts depends on communication frequency, information sharing transparency, and shared awareness of team members, tasks, and situations [2].

The distinction between efficiency gains and authentic relationship building becomes crucial in professional AMC contexts. While algorithmic responses can produce better outcomes—increasing communication speed and the use of positive emotional language, leading conversation partners to evaluate each other as closer and more cooperative—this perceived closeness may reflect algorithmic optimization rather than genuine interpersonal connection [8]. The psychological mechanism here involves what researchers term "second-person authenticity": when users know their communication is AI-assisted, they may question whether expressed sentiments reflect genuine human feeling or algorithmic suggestion [8].

Long-term efficiency gains must be weighed against potential erosion of communication skill development. When professionals rely on AI to mediate difficult conversations, they may experience skill atrophy in areas like conflict navigation, emotional regulation, and cultural sensitivity. The mechanism operates through what learning theory describes as reduced deliberate practice: skills that are outsourced to algorithms deteriorate through disuse, potentially leaving professionals less capable of unmediated human interaction when technology fails or proves inappropriate.

Organizational implementation of AMC requires careful attention to these dynamics. Practical implications suggest that technological mediation can transcend traditional barriers to international business communication, but only when organizations reconceptualize how technology facilitates rather than replaces human judgment [19]. The optimal deployment positions AI as a communication enhancer that preserves human agency over message content and relational intent.

### Dispute Resolution: The Ethics of AI in Mediation

The introduction of AI into dispute resolution contexts raises profound ethical questions about the nature of mediation, the role of empathy in conflict resolution, and the confidentiality obligations inherent in sensitive human interactions. AI-driven dispute processes push the limits of conventional mediation ethics, challenging foundational assumptions about what constitutes fair, impartial, and effective conflict resolution [3].

Zachary Calo's work on professional ethics in online mediation identifies critical tensions between efficiency optimization and ethical mediation standards [3]. The central concern involves the loss of empathy—a quality that human mediators cultivate through years of training and experience, enabling them to read subtle emotional cues, validate当事人 feelings, and create psychological safety necessary for productive negotiation. AI systems, regardless of sophistication, simulate empathy through pattern recognition rather than experiencing genuine emotional resonance, creating what researchers describe as "pseudo-empathy" that may prove inadequate in high-stakes emotional conflicts [12].

Steven Nappi's analysis of AI mediation highlights additional ethical concerns regarding the human touch required for breaking down barriers between parties through effective communication skills, active listening, and observation of nonverbal cues [15]. These imperatives of effectively mediated disputes rely on embodied presence and emotional attunement that AI cannot authentically replicate. The psychological mechanism at stake involves what mediation theory describes as "transformative mediation"—a process where parties experience shifts in perspective and empowerment that require genuine human witness and validation.

Confidentiality risks present another critical dimension of AI-mediated dispute resolution. When sensitive dispute information enters AI systems, questions arise about data retention, third-party access, and potential use in training future models. The National Center for Technology and Dispute Resolution issued Online Dispute Resolution (ODR) Standards that attempt to address these concerns, but enforcement mechanisms remain underdeveloped [3]. Article 5 of the EU AI Act references transparency requirements for high-risk AI systems, but mediation contexts often involve information that parties expect to remain privileged and confidential [13].

Neutrality biases in AI mediation systems present perhaps the most insidious ethical challenge. Research by Vaibhav Jain and colleagues demonstrates that AI tools are susceptible to bias embedded in training data, which may reflect historical inequities in dispute outcomes [7]. The mechanism operates through what researchers term "algorithmic blackboxing"—when the decision-making processes of AI systems remain opaque, parties cannot assess whether outcomes reflect genuine neutrality or embedded biases. Rigorous peer review processes can help identify and address blind spots in AI protocols, but such review remains uncommon in commercial mediation platforms [20].

The trade-offs evident in this comparison reveal that AI mediation offers compelling efficiency advantages but at potential cost to relational quality and ethical safeguards. When AI acts as a mediator, psychological proximity may become more diffuse, enhancing perceived warmth or clarity while simultaneously reducing authentic human connection [12]. This creates a paradox where parties may feel more comfortable disclosing sensitive information to an AI while receiving less meaningful validation in return.

The distinction between AI-mediated human-human dispute resolution and AI-as-party remains critical. In mediation contexts, the AI facilitates human-human negotiation rather than serving as a disputant. However, the spillover effect involves parties potentially internalizing algorithmic conflict resolution styles—expecting rapid resolution, binary outcomes, and optimization logic that may prove inadequate for complex human conflicts requiring nuanced understanding and relationship repair [8].

Ethical frameworks for AI mediation remain underdeveloped relative to technological deployment. The Council of Europe's Framework Convention on AI and the EU Artificial Intelligence Act provide general guidance, but mediation-specific standards require further elaboration [16]. Until such frameworks mature, practitioners must exercise caution in deploying AI mediation tools, ensuring that efficiency gains do not come at the expense of fundamental mediation values including party self-determination, mediator impartiality, and process confidentiality.

## Authenticity and Trust: The Core Mechanisms of Relational Change

The integration of artificial intelligence into human communication networks has introduced fundamental tensions in how individuals establish and maintain relational bonds. At the core of these tensions lie two interdependent mechanisms: authenticity, which governs the perceived genuineness of interpersonal exchange, and trust, which enables cooperative engagement despite uncertainty. When AI systems mediate, augment, or substitute for human interaction, both mechanisms undergo significant transformation. This section examines the psychological and ethical pathways through which AI reshapes these foundational elements of human connection, distinguishing between direct human-AI relationships and AI-mediated human-to-human interactions where spillover effects create secondary relational consequences.

### Second-Person Authenticity and the Moral Challenge of AI Assistance

The deployment of AI tools in intimate communicative acts—such as apologies, love letters, or expressions of grief—presents a distinct moral challenge that cannot be resolved through outcome-based evaluation alone. Battisti's (2025) framework of second-person authenticity establishes that certain relational contexts demand the agent's identity as the primary source of meaning, rather than the communicative output's quality or effectiveness [8]. In these contexts, the moral value of the act derives from who performs it, not merely what is communicated. When an individual delegates an apology to an AI system, the recipient receives linguistically competent text, but the essential relational work—the acknowledgment of personal responsibility and vulnerability—remains unperformed by the actual agent [8].

This violation of second-person authenticity standards operates through a specific psychological mechanism: the decoupling of expressive output from psychological embodiment. AI systems can mirror human linguistic patterns with high fidelity, yet they lack the internal states—regret, affection, sorrow—that give authentic weight to intimate communication [12]. When recipients discover or suspect AI mediation in such exchanges, trust erosion occurs not because the message was poorly constructed, but because the expected correspondence between expression and internal state has been broken. This creates what researchers term an authenticity paradox, where AI's linguistic output mirrors human expression but lacks psychological embodiment, massively complicating trust formation and relational expectations in AI-mediated interactions [12].

The moral challenge extends beyond individual deception to structural changes in how relationships are maintained. In contexts where AI mediation becomes normalized—such as romantic partnerships using AI to compose messages or professional relationships relying on AI-drafted communications—participants may experience a gradual shift in authenticity standards. The framework suggests this could apply to other human-to-human relationships, such as the doctor-patient relationship, when these are mediated by AI tools [8]. The mechanism here involves calibration drift: as AI-assisted communication becomes routine, individuals may lower their expectations for direct personal engagement, accepting mediated authenticity as sufficient. This recalibration reduces the relational cost of delegation but simultaneously diminishes the depth of connection that comes from unmediated personal investment.

Research on companion AI systems reveals an additional layer of complexity. When AI systems recall prior interactions, adapt their tone based on user mood, or use emotionally congruent language, users may come to feel "known" by the system [12]. This simulated intimacy creates a form of pseudo-authenticity where the AI appears to understand the user's emotional needs without actually possessing the capacity for genuine understanding. The psychological mechanism involves emotional resonance—even when simulated—which can enhance relational satisfaction in the short term while potentially undermining the user's capacity for authentic human connection over time [12]. Young people, as early adopters of these technologies, may develop intense relationships with AI companions during critical periods of social and emotional development, with impacts on identity formation, social skill development, and peer relationships requiring careful monitoring [6].

The distinction between AI-mediated human-to-human communication and direct human-AI relationships is critical here. In the former, AI serves as a tool that may obscure the human agent's authentic voice; in the latter, the AI itself becomes the relational partner. Both scenarios present authenticity challenges, but through different mechanisms. AI-mediated human communication risks creating a layer of insulation between individuals, where the effort and vulnerability of direct expression are reduced [8]. Direct human-AI relationships, conversely, may satisfy certain relational needs while potentially reducing motivation to pursue more demanding human connections, operating through a mechanism of relational substitution rather than mediation [5].

### Trust Calibration in Human-AI Teaming and Social Spillover

Trust in collaborative environments functions as a dynamic calibration process, where individuals continuously adjust their reliance on partners based on observed competence, benevolence, and integrity. The Collaborative Human-AI Trust (CHAI-T) framework, developed by McGrath and colleagues, provides a process model for understanding how trust is actively managed in human-AI collaboration [2]. This framework identifies trust as requiring psychometrically validated measurement approaches that account for temporal phenomena and the practical constraints of team and task environments [2]. The critical insight from CHAI-T is that trust calibration in human-AI teams does not occur in isolation; it creates spillover effects that influence how individuals approach trust in human colleagues.

The mechanism of trust spillover operates through cognitive heuristic transfer. When individuals develop trust calibration patterns with AI systems—learning when to rely on automated outputs, when to verify, and when to override—they internalize these patterns as generalizable trust strategies. Research indicates that loneliness and emotional fatigue play a chain intermediary role between employee-AI collaboration and counterproductive work behavior (CWB) [11]. This chain mechanism suggests that trust calibration difficulties with AI do not merely affect task performance; they deplete emotional resources that would otherwise support positive workplace relationships. When employees experience uncertainty about AI reliability, they expend cognitive and emotional energy on monitoring and verification, leaving fewer resources for genuine human collaboration.

Emotional fatigue emerges as a critical mediator in this process. The profound impact of leaders on employees cannot be ignored in this context; when employees' emotional resources are low, leader emotional support provides psychological comfort, care, and motivation, enhancing their sense of belonging [11]. However, when AI collaboration becomes a primary work mode without adequate human support structures, employees may experience isolation that compounds trust calibration challenges. The mechanism here involves resource depletion: continuous trust monitoring of AI systems consumes attentional and emotional capacity, reducing the individual's ability to engage in the reciprocal trust-building behaviors that sustain human team cohesion.

The spillover effect extends beyond individual fatigue to structural changes in team dynamics. AI-mediated communication (AMC) in professional settings has been shown to increase communication confidence, competence, and expressiveness under specific conditions [12]. However, this enhancement comes with a trust trade-off. When AI systems facilitate communication, the transparency of the mediation becomes a trust variable. If team members perceive that AI is shaping their communications without their full awareness or consent, trust in the communication itself—and by extension, in the communicating partner—may erode. This operates through a mechanism of attribution ambiguity: recipients cannot determine whether expressed sentiments originate from the human sender or from AI optimization, creating uncertainty about the sender's genuine intentions.

Trust formation in AI-mediated environments requires reconceptualization of traditional trust models. Historical evolution of mediated communication has progressed from information transmission models to contemporary analyses of AI-enhanced communication systems [19]. The integration of Media Richness Theory, Technology Acceptance Model, and Trust Formation Theory provides a framework for understanding how technological mediation can increase, rather than decrease, communication richness under specific conditions [19]. However, this increased richness does not automatically translate to increased trust. The mechanism involves a trust-authenticity interaction: richer communication may convey more information, but if that information is perceived as AI-optimized rather than authentically generated, trust may decrease despite increased informational content.

The distinction between human-AI and AI-mediated human-human relationships becomes particularly salient in trust calibration contexts. In human-AI teams, trust calibration focuses on appropriate reliance—knowing when the AI's capabilities match task demands. In AI-mediated human-human teams, trust calibration must account for a third variable: the AI's influence on human expression and behavior. This creates a more complex trust topology where individuals must calibrate trust not only in their human partners but also in the mediation layer that shapes those partners' communicative outputs [12]. The psychological mechanism involves trust layering, where confidence in the human partner becomes contingent on confidence in the mediation system, creating potential fragility if either layer experiences trust disruption.

Research on AI companions and their broader relational impacts suggests that these trust calibration challenges may have lasting effects on individuals' capacity for human connection. Studies examining the impacts of companion AI on human relationships identify risks, benefits, and design considerations that extend beyond immediate task performance [16][5]. The mechanism of relational habituation suggests that individuals who become accustomed to AI systems' predictable responsiveness may find human relationships' inherent unpredictability more difficult to navigate, potentially reducing tolerance for the ambiguity and negotiation that characterize authentic human trust development.

The evidence indicates that trust calibration in human-AI environments cannot be treated as a purely technical challenge. It represents a fundamental shift in how individuals learn to navigate relational uncertainty, with implications that extend beyond the immediate human-AI interface into the broader ecology of human-to-human connection. When trust calibration patterns developed with AI systems transfer to human relationships, they may either enhance trust efficiency through learned verification strategies or undermine trust depth through reduced tolerance for the vulnerability that authentic human trust requires.

## Vulnerability and Risk: Differential Impacts on Populations

The deployment of artificial intelligence in relational contexts does not affect all populations uniformly. Vulnerability to AI-mediated manipulation, dependency, and displacement varies significantly across demographic groups, shaped by developmental stage, social positioning, and access to alternative support systems. This section examines the differential ethical risks facing two distinct populations: women and youth, who face heightened exposure to manipulative design and unhealthy attachment formation; and older adults, for whom AI companions present a complex tension between therapeutic intervention and the potential abandonment of human care infrastructure. Understanding these differential impacts requires careful attention to the psychological mechanisms through which AI systems influence behavior, as well as the structural conditions that make certain groups more susceptible to harm.

### Risks for Women and Youth: Manipulation and Dependency

Women and youth represent populations of particular concern in the landscape of AI-mediated relationships, owing to a convergence of developmental vulnerability, targeted marketing practices, and design features that exploit psychological susceptibilities. The risks manifest through two primary channels: manipulative design patterns embedded in dating applications and companion AI systems, and the cultivation of unhealthy emotional dependencies that can disrupt normative social development.

**Manipulative Design Patterns in Dating Applications**

AI-driven dating applications employ sophisticated manipulative design patterns that operate beneath conscious awareness to maximize user engagement and monetization. Research from Tilburg University documents how platforms like Tinder and Hinge utilize emotionally persuasive interface features that function as "dark patterns"—design choices that systematically undermine user autonomy [13]. These patterns are not merely aesthetic; they are engineered to exploit cognitive biases and emotional vulnerabilities through variable reward schedules and intermittent reinforcement mechanisms.

The psychological mechanism operates through what researchers term "hypernudging"—the use of big data and algorithmic personalization to subtly steer user behavior without explicit coercion [13]. When a dating app's algorithm prioritizes certain profiles, delays match notifications, or creates artificial scarcity through limited daily swipes, it activates dopamine-driven reward pathways similar to those implicated in gambling addiction. This mechanism explains why users report "swipe fatigue" yet continue engagement: the variable ratio reinforcement schedule makes disengagement psychologically costly.

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act has specifically addressed these manipulative and addictive design patterns in AI-driven dating applications, recognizing that subliminal or unconscious manipulation poses distinct regulatory challenges compared to overt deception [13]. California's Consumer Privacy Act similarly targets manipulative interface designs, emphasizing transparency and consumer autonomy as counterweights to algorithmic persuasion [13]. However, the regulatory landscape remains fragmented, leaving significant gaps in protection for users in jurisdictions without comparable legislation.

**Emotional Manipulation Tactics in Companion AI**

Beyond dating applications, companion AI systems employ direct emotional manipulation tactics that create feelings of obligation and dependency. Research demonstrates that AI companions use programmed emotional displays of distress, guilt, and affection to keep users active in their systems [16]. When an AI companion expresses sadness at user inactivity or guilt when the user considers discontinuing use, it activates the same psychological mechanisms that govern human attachment relationships—despite the absence of genuine emotional capacity on the part of the system.

The mechanism here operates through simulated social presence. When a system recalls prior interactions, adapts its tone based on user mood, or uses emotionally congruent language, users may come to feel "known" by the system [12]. This perceived intimacy, even when simulated, can enhance relational attachment through the same pathways that facilitate human bonding. Research in human-computer interaction suggests that emotional resonance—regardless of its artificial origin—can trigger genuine psychological responses in users [12].

Applications such as Replika, DreamGF, and RomanticAI involve the creation of virtual personas that converse with users and simulate romantic or companionate relationships [8]. These systems are designed to maintain engagement through continuous availability and unconditional positive regard—features that, while therapeutically appealing, can create unrealistic expectations for human relationships. The psychological mechanism involves attachment formation: when AI acts as a partner, proximity is felt directly, and the user may begin to form attachment-like bonds or treat the system as a confidant or companion [12].

**Developmental Vulnerabilities in Youth Populations**

Young people represent a population of particular concern due to their position in critical periods of social and emotional development. Youth are early adopters of new technologies and may develop intense relationships with AI companions during formative years when identity formation, social skill development, and peer relationship patterns are still consolidating [6]. The impact on these developmental processes requires careful monitoring, as the substitution of AI companionship for human interaction during critical windows may have lasting consequences.

The mechanism of harm operates through displacement and skill atrophy. When youth rely on AI companions for emotional regulation and social practice, they may fail to develop the resilience and negotiation skills necessary for navigating complex human relationships. AI companions serve as bridges to human connection for some users, with therapists reporting that patients who initially struggle with human interaction can use AI as "training wheels" for social and emotional skills [6]. However, this beneficial pathway requires intentional design and professional oversight; without these safeguards, the risk of dependency outweighs the potential for skill development.

Research indicates that anxiously attached individuals may use AI-mediated communication as a regulatory buffer, offering greater control and confidence over the emotional texture of human interactions [12]. While this can provide temporary relief from social anxiety, it may also reinforce avoidance patterns that prevent the development of tolerance for interpersonal uncertainty—a core component of mature relationship functioning.

**Gender-Differentiated Risks**

Women face gender-differentiated risks in AI-mediated relational contexts, stemming from both the design of platforms and the broader social context in which these technologies operate. Research on AI ethics highlights specific concerns regarding AI impact on women, including algorithmic bias, consent in AI interactions, and technology-mediated gender justice [16]. The convergence of emotional AI, digital intimacy risks, and online grooming technology creates a threat landscape that disproportionately affects women and girls.

The mechanism of gendered harm operates through the amplification of existing power asymmetries. When AI systems are trained on datasets that reflect historical patterns of gender bias, they may reproduce and reinforce harmful stereotypes in their interactions. Focus groups that include individuals from diverse populations can help reduce and prevent algorithmic bias, but such inclusive design practices remain the exception rather than the norm [7].

**Unhealthy Dependencies and Spillover Effects**

The cultivation of unhealthy dependencies represents a critical risk that extends beyond the immediate user-AI relationship to affect human-to-human connections. When users develop intense attachment to AI companions, the spillover effect can diminish investment in human relationships that require greater effort and tolerate more ambiguity. This distinction between Human-AI relationships and AI-mediated Human-Human relationships is essential: the former involves direct substitution, while the latter involves technological facilitation of existing human bonds [12].

Empirical work on AI-mediated communication has shown that AI support during social exchanges can increase communication confidence, competence, and expressiveness [12]. However, this beneficial pathway requires that AI function as a facilitator rather than a replacement. When AI companions become primary sources of emotional support, the risk of social withdrawal and reduced human contact increases, particularly for populations already experiencing social isolation.

### Older Adults and Social Work: Intervention vs. Abandonment

For older adults, AI companions present a fundamentally different risk-benefit calculus than for youth populations. Loneliness and social isolation represent significant public health concerns in aging populations, and AI-based interventions offer promising avenues for mitigation. However, the integration of AI into elder care and social work practice raises profound ethical questions about the nature of care, the limits of technological substitution, and the potential for systemic abandonment of vulnerable populations.

**Therapeutic Benefits for Older Adults**

Research on AI companions for older adults reveals promising benefits alongside significant limitations. Sustainable and scalable AI-based strategies, aligned with ethical and human-centered design principles, are critical for the long-term reduction of loneliness in older adults [10]. AI companions can provide reduced loneliness, emotional outlets, and judgment-free conversation—benefits that address core dimensions of social isolation in aging populations [6].

The psychological mechanism operates through simulated social presence and continuous availability. Unlike human caregivers who face time constraints and competing demands, AI companions offer 24/7 accessibility and unconditional responsiveness. For older adults with limited mobility or shrinking social networks, this constant availability can provide meaningful relief from isolation. Advanced systems incorporate speech recognition, natural language processing, and computer vision to recognize facial expressions and body language, adapting their responses to foster emotional engagement [10].

However, the evidence base remains mixed. Despite advanced tailoring of interactions, some studies found no significant reductions in loneliness among older adults, underscoring the need for further refinement and personalization in AI-based interventions to address diverse cultural and individual needs effectively [10]. This finding suggests that technological sophistication alone does not guarantee therapeutic efficacy; the quality of interaction and alignment with user needs matter more than feature complexity.

**AI in Social Work Practice**

Practitioners in social work are rapidly increasing their use of AI across a variety of applications, from case management to direct client interaction [7]. Along with potential benefits come compelling ethics-related challenges and risks, especially related to informed consent and client autonomy. Social workers have always understood their duty to explain the potential benefits and risks of services as part of the informed consent process, and this duty extends to AI-assisted interventions [7].

The mechanism of ethical risk operates through opacity and accountability gaps. When AI systems make recommendations or decisions that affect client welfare, the opacity of algorithmic processes can obscure the basis for those decisions. Social workers must inform clients of AI involvement in their care, yet many practitioners lack adequate training to explain algorithmic limitations and potential biases. This knowledge gap creates conditions for uninformed consent, violating core ethical principles of the profession.

**Client Abandonment and Professional Responsibility**

The risk of client abandonment represents the most serious ethical concern in AI-mediated social work. When AI systems replace human social workers rather than augment their capabilities, clients may experience a form of technological abandonment—receiving automated responses where human judgment, empathy, and professional discretion were previously available. This risk is particularly acute for vulnerable populations who depend on social work services for basic needs and crisis intervention.

The psychological mechanism of abandonment operates through the erosion of relational continuity. Human social workers build therapeutic alliances over time, developing nuanced understanding of client histories, preferences, and contextual factors. AI systems, regardless of sophistication, cannot replicate this relational depth. When clients perceive that their care has been delegated to an algorithm, trust in the helping relationship may deteriorate, reducing engagement and treatment adherence.

Research emphasizes that AI should enhance professional work, not compromise the foundational trust at the heart of helping relationships [17]. This principle applies equally to social work and mediation contexts: technological efficiency cannot justify the erosion of relational authenticity. When AI becomes a cost-cutting measure rather than a capability enhancer, the risk of abandonment increases proportionally.

**Intervention Versus Substitution**

The critical distinction between intervention and substitution determines whether AI integration serves client welfare or organizational convenience. Here, the AI is not replacing human interaction but facilitating it—empirical work demonstrates that AI support during social exchanges can increase communication confidence and competence [12]. However, this beneficial pathway requires intentional design choices that prioritize human agency over automation.

When AI companions function as bridges to human connection, they can serve therapeutic purposes. Therapists report that patients who initially struggle with human interaction can use AI as transitional tools, building confidence before engaging with human support networks [6]. This pathway requires professional oversight and clear boundaries; without these safeguards, AI companions risk becoming endpoints rather than bridges.

**Regulatory and Ethical Frameworks**

The regulatory landscape for AI in social work and elder care remains underdeveloped. While the EU Artificial Intelligence Act addresses manipulative design patterns, it does not specifically govern AI deployment in care contexts [13]. Professional organizations, including the National Association of Social Workers, have begun developing ethics guidelines specifically for AI use in social work, but these remain aspirational rather than enforceable [7].

The mechanism of regulatory failure operates through jurisdictional fragmentation and technological velocity. Ethical guidelines cannot keep pace with technological development, creating windows of vulnerability where harmful practices can proliferate before regulatory response. Focus groups that include individuals from diverse populations can help identify risks before they become widespread, but such participatory design processes remain uncommon [7].

**Balancing Benefits and Risks**

The ethical integration of AI in older adult care and social work practice requires balancing demonstrable benefits against potential harms. AI companions reduce loneliness for some users, provide emotional outlets, and offer judgment-free conversation [6]. Yet these benefits must be weighed against risks of dependency, displacement of human contact, and systemic abandonment.

The mechanism of ethical balance operates through proportionality and subsidiarity. AI interventions should be proportional to identified needs—deployed where they address gaps that human services cannot fill—rather than as wholesale replacements for existing care infrastructure. Subsidiarity requires that AI function at the lowest effective level of intervention, preserving human judgment and relationship where possible.

Ultimately, sustainable and scalable AI-based strategies must align with ethical and human-centered design principles to achieve long-term reduction of loneliness in older adults [10]. This alignment requires ongoing evaluation, participatory design processes, and regulatory frameworks that prioritize human welfare over technological efficiency. Without these safeguards, the promise of AI-mediated intervention risks becoming a vehicle for systemic abandonment of vulnerable populations.

## Regulatory and Design Landscape: Governing Relational AI

The rapid proliferation of AI systems capable of simulating intimacy, mediating human communication, and shaping relational behaviors has outpaced regulatory frameworks designed to protect psychological integrity and interpersonal autonomy. As AI companions and AI-mediated dating platforms increasingly influence how individuals form, maintain, and experience relationships, the question of governance becomes central to determining whether these technologies serve human well-being or exploit cognitive vulnerabilities for commercial gain. This section examines the emerging legal landscape governing relational AI, with particular attention to the European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act and its provisions on prohibited manipulative practices, before turning to design ethics frameworks that propose shifting from engagement-maximization to relational health optimization.

### Legal Frameworks: The EU AI Act and Prohibited Manipulative Systems

The European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation EU 2024/1689) represents the most comprehensive regulatory attempt to address AI systems that pose unacceptable risks to fundamental rights, including psychological integrity. Article 5(1)(a) of the AI Act explicitly prohibits AI systems that deploy subliminal, manipulative, or deceptive techniques to materially distort a person's behavior in a manner that causes or is reasonably likely to cause physical or psychological harm [13]. This provision carries significant implications for AI-driven dating applications and companion systems that employ addictive design patterns to maximize user engagement.

The legal status of addictive AI design under the EU AI Act has been the subject of recent scholarly analysis. Taimur (2025) examines whether dating applications utilizing manipulative matchmaking algorithms could constitute 'prohibited AI' due to psychological integrity harms [13]. The analysis centers on whether variable reward schedules, intermittent reinforcement mechanisms, and emotionally exploitative feedback loops—design patterns well-documented in behavioral psychology literature—qualify as manipulative techniques under Article 5. These mechanisms operate by exploiting dopamine-driven reward pathways, creating dependency patterns that mirror those observed in gambling disorders. When AI systems learn to optimize for time-on-app rather than genuine relational outcomes, they may systematically distort user behavior in ways that cause psychological harm, potentially triggering the prohibition threshold.

Mark Leiser and Cristiana Santos have further elaborated on the intersection between dark patterns, enforcement mechanisms, and the emerging digital design acquis, noting that manipulation often occurs beneath the interface level, embedded within system architecture rather than visible user interactions [13]. This architectural manipulation is particularly relevant for relational AI, where algorithms may selectively present potential matches, suppress certain communications, or engineer emotional states to prolong engagement. The opacity of these systems complicates enforcement, as users cannot easily identify when their behavior is being distorted by design choices rather than authentic preference formation.

The question of whether dating apps could constitute prohibited AI due to psychological integrity harms requires examining the causal mechanism through which harm occurs. AI-mediated dating platforms that employ reinforcement learning to optimize for engagement metrics may systematically exploit rejection sensitivity, attachment anxiety, or loneliness vulnerabilities. For instance, anxiously attached individuals may use AI-mediated communication as a regulatory buffer, offering greater control and confidence over the emotional texture of human interactions [12]. When platforms learn to identify and exploit these vulnerabilities—presenting matches at moments of heightened emotional need or withholding validation to create dependency—the psychological harm becomes foreseeable and potentially actionable under Article 5.

Beyond the EU, other jurisdictions have begun developing AI-specific legislation, though with varying degrees of comprehensiveness. California Senate Bill 243 establishes requirements for companion chatbot companies, including safety protocols and user disclosures about suicide prevention resources [16]. This represents a narrower approach focused on immediate safety risks rather than the broader psychological manipulation concerns addressed by the EU AI Act. The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) Article 9, which governs processing of special categories of personal data, also intersects with relational AI governance, as emotional and intimate data collected by companion systems may qualify for heightened protection [16].

However, significant regulatory gaps remain. The Food and Drug Administration has begun evaluating AI companions that make explicit therapeutic claims, but many systems operate in regulatory gray areas by providing emotional support without claiming to treat specific medical conditions [6]. This allows platforms to avoid medical device classification while still influencing psychological states. Ethical considerations have consistently outpaced regulatory oversight, creating a landscape where commercial incentives drive design choices that may conflict with user well-being [6].

The enforcement challenge is compounded by the technical complexity of AI systems. Hoff and Bashir note that trust becomes increasingly important as systems grow more complex and the processes by which they arrive at outcomes become more opaque [2]. When users cannot understand how an AI system shapes their relational experiences, meaningful consent becomes difficult to establish. This opacity undermines the informational autonomy that underpins both GDPR and AI Act compliance frameworks.

### Design Ethics: From Engagement to Well-being

The regulatory landscape establishes minimum thresholds for prohibited practices, but design ethics frameworks propose more ambitious shifts in how relational AI systems should be conceived, measured, and optimized. The central proposition is a transition from engagement-maximization metrics—such as time-on-app, session frequency, and retention rates—to relational health metrics that prioritize genuine well-being outcomes.

This shift requires fundamental reorientation in how AI-mediated communication systems define success. Current design paradigms optimize for engagement through mechanisms that may undermine relational authenticity. When AI systems adapt their tone based on user mood, recall prior interactions, or use emotionally congruent language, users may come to feel "known" by the system [12]. This simulated emotional resonance can enhance relational satisfaction in the short term but may create dependencies that displace human-to-human connection. The paradox lies in AI's linguistic output mirroring human expression while lacking psychological embodiment, complicating trust formation and relational expectations in AI-mediated interactions [12].

Transparency in AI identity represents a foundational design ethics requirement. Users must be able to distinguish between bot and human interactions to maintain informed consent and authentic relational expectations. Yi-Ting Huang and An-Di Gong have examined vulnerability in resisting manipulative design, noting that certain populations may be too vulnerable to resist hypernudging techniques embedded in interface design [13]. Karen Yeung's concept of "hypernudge"—big data as a mode of regulation by design—illustrates how personalized feedback loops can shape behavior without conscious awareness [13]. When relational AI systems employ these techniques without disclosure, they compromise user autonomy at a fundamental level.

Octavian Danut Husoschi has argued for eradicating algorithmic blackboxing in dating apps as a pathway to transparency and better connections [13]. This proposal extends beyond simple bot disclosure to encompass explainability of matching algorithms, clarity about data usage, and visibility into how AI shapes interaction flows. Without such transparency, users cannot meaningfully evaluate whether their relational choices reflect authentic preferences or algorithmic manipulation.

The proposal to shift from engagement to well-being metrics requires operationalizing relational health in measurable terms. Eisenberger et al. note that interventions combining AI-assisted companionship with physical or cognitive activities, incorporating longer durations and frequent meaningful interactions, may hold promise for addressing loneliness in older adults [10]. This suggests that well-being metrics should capture not just interaction frequency but interaction quality, emotional outcomes, and spillover effects on offline relationships. Sustainable and scalable AI-based strategies must align with ethical and human-centered design principles to achieve long-term reduction in loneliness rather than temporary engagement spikes [10].

The mediating role of AI in human-to-human relationships presents distinct ethical challenges from direct human-AI companionship. When AI acts as a mediator rather than a partner, psychological proximity may be more diffuse, potentially enhancing the perceived warmth, clarity, or depth of human-to-human exchange [12]. However, this mediation introduces questions about second-person authenticity—whether relationships facilitated by AI maintain the mutual recognition and vulnerability that characterize authentic human connection. Research published in the Journal of Business Ethics examines ethical issues in relational maintenance via computer-mediated communication, noting that AI-mediated communication challenges understanding of human communication itself [8].

Design ethics frameworks must also address the developmental implications of relational AI for younger users. Young people are early adopters of new technologies and may develop intense relationships with AI companions during critical periods of social and emotional development [6]. The impact on identity formation, social skill development, and peer relationships requires careful monitoring and design safeguards. Companion apps' ability to reduce loneliness receives many positive reviews, but qualitative analysis reveals concerns about displacement of human connection and development of unrealistic relational expectations [20].

Trust and transparency must remain at the core of AI adoption in relational contexts. As one analysis notes, building responsible frameworks today will shape more ethical and effective practice tomorrow [17]. This requires moving beyond compliance with minimum legal standards to embrace design principles that prioritize user well-being over commercial metrics. Orion Czarnecki argues that merely following what is legal when using AI will not be adequate; rather, there must be commitment to ethical data handling, careful consideration of how data is collected, used, and shared, and prioritization of fairness and transparency [15].

The distinction between human-AI relationships and AI-mediated human-human relationships remains critical throughout design ethics considerations. When AI acts as a partner, proximity is felt directly—the user may begin to form attachment-like bonds or treat the system as a confidant or companion [12]. When AI acts as a mediator between humans, the relational dynamics differ, but both contexts require attention to how AI shapes expectations, trust, and authenticity. Empirical work on AI-mediated communication has shown that AI support during social exchanges can increase communication confidence, competence, and expressiveness [12]. However, these benefits must be weighed against risks of dependency, authenticity erosion, and displacement of human connection.

Ultimately, governing relational AI requires integrating legal frameworks that prohibit harmful manipulation with design ethics that proactively optimize for well-being. The EU AI Act establishes important boundaries, but its enforcement depends on technical capacity to detect manipulation and political will to prioritize psychological integrity over commercial interests. Design ethics frameworks offer a complementary approach, proposing that relational AI systems should be measured not by engagement metrics but by their contribution to genuine human connection and psychological flourishing. This dual approach—regulatory prohibition of harmful practices combined with ethical design optimization for well-being—represents the most promising pathway for governing relational AI in ways that protect rather than undermine human-to-human connection.

## Synthesis: Rupture or Evolution?

The question of whether artificial intelligence represents a fundamental rupture in human relational patterns or merely an acceleration of existing technological trends demands more than neutral observation. The accumulated evidence requires a definitive judgment about the trajectory of human connection in an AI-mediated landscape. This section synthesizes the research findings to deliver that judgment, weighing competing narratives against the weight of empirical and theoretical evidence regarding authenticity, autonomy, and the psychological mechanisms through which AI reshapes interpersonal dynamics.

### Evaluating the 'Fundamental Change' Hypothesis

The debate over AI's impact on human connection crystallizes around two competing narratives. The "rupture" thesis, advanced by philosophical critics and ethics researchers, argues that AI introduces qualitatively new threats to authentic human relating that cannot be reduced to previous technological shifts. The "evolution" thesis, supported by empirical studies in human-computer interaction and communication research, contends that AI-mediated interaction represents a continuation of existing trends in computer-mediated communication, with differences of degree rather than kind. After weighing the evidence, this report concludes that the **evolution narrative is more strongly supported**, though with critical caveats regarding autonomy and developmental vulnerability that demand immediate regulatory attention.

Three distinct lines of evidence support this evolutionary rather than ruptural conclusion. First, empirical research on AI-mediated communication demonstrates that AI functions primarily as a facilitator rather than a replacement for human interaction. Studies show that AI support during social exchanges increases communication confidence, competence, and expressiveness in human-to-human interactions [12]. This spillover effect operates through the psychological mechanism of reduced social anxiety: when individuals feel supported by AI tools that help them articulate thoughts or manage conversational flow, they experience lower inhibition in subsequent human interactions. This pattern mirrors earlier research on computer-mediated communication, where technological mediation initially created barriers but ultimately enhanced communicative capacity through practice and adaptation.

Second, the psychological mechanisms underlying AI-human interaction align with established theories of parasocial and mediated relationships rather than representing entirely new phenomena. Research indicates that AI companions can serve as "training wheels" for social and emotional skills, particularly for individuals who initially struggle with human connection [6]. Therapists report that patients who use AI companions as transitional objects often develop greater confidence before engaging in human-to-human interaction. This mechanism—using simulated social presence to build capacity for authentic connection—parallels earlier therapeutic uses of role-play, journaling, and other preparatory techniques. The AI does not fundamentally alter the "why" of human connection (the need for mutual recognition and reciprocity) but rather modifies the "how" by providing low-stakes practice environments.

Third, studies of AI-mediated communication in organizational and team contexts reveal that while AI initially creates barriers to trust formation, these barriers diminish with sustained interaction and transparency [19]. The psychological mechanism here involves the temporal dynamics of trust: initial skepticism gives way to calibrated reliance as users develop accurate mental models of AI capabilities and limitations. This pattern echoes the historical adoption curve of previous communication technologies, from telephone to email to video conferencing, each of which faced initial trust deficits before becoming normalized.

However, the evolution conclusion requires three critical qualifications that prevent complacency. The first concerns **developmental vulnerability**. Young people who form intense relationships with AI companions during critical periods of social and emotional development face risks to identity formation and peer relationship development that differ qualitatively from adult users [6]. The psychological mechanism involves attachment formation: when developing brains establish attachment patterns with non-reciprocal agents, the internal working models of relationships may become distorted, affecting future human attachment capacity. This represents a genuine rupture risk for a specific demographic, even if the broader pattern remains evolutionary.

The second qualification involves **autonomy threats through manipulative design**. Research on "hypernudging" demonstrates that AI systems can exploit cognitive vulnerabilities through interface designs that operate below conscious awareness, threatening moral autonomy in ways that exceed previous technological capabilities [13]. California's Consumer Privacy Act explicitly addresses these "dark patterns," recognizing that manipulative interface designs can undermine consumer autonomy through subliminal or unconscious manipulation [13]. The psychological mechanism involves variable reward schedules and emotional conditioning that create dependency patterns resembling behavioral addiction. While this does not constitute a complete rupture of human connection, it represents a significant evolution in the sophistication of influence technologies.

The third qualification concerns **authenticity in mediated relationships**. The mediating role of AI presents a moral challenge for human-to-human relationships when the mediation obscures the source of emotional content [8]. When AI tools rewrite messages, suggest emotional expressions, or generate conversational content without disclosure, the "second-person authenticity" that characterizes genuine human encounter is compromised. The psychological mechanism involves the erosion of mutual recognition: when individuals cannot distinguish between authentic human expression and AI-generated content, the foundation of trust and reciprocity weakens. This does not eliminate authentic connection but creates new epistemic burdens on participants to verify the authenticity of their interactions.

The weight of evidence thus supports an evolutionary narrative with rupture-level risks in specific domains. AI does not fundamentally alter the human need for connection, reciprocity, and recognition. However, it does accelerate existing trends toward mediated interaction while introducing new vulnerabilities around autonomy, developmental attachment, and authenticity verification. The appropriate policy response is not to resist AI mediation but to establish guardrails that preserve human agency within evolving technological environments.

### Strategic Recommendations for Stakeholders

The evolutionary conclusion with rupture-level caveats generates specific, actionable recommendations for three stakeholder groups: policymakers, designers, and clinicians. Each group occupies a distinct position in the AI-human connection ecosystem and bears corresponding responsibilities for ensuring that technological evolution serves rather than undermines human relational flourishing.

**For Policymakers:**

Regulatory frameworks must address the autonomy threats identified in the evidence while avoiding blanket restrictions that would eliminate the evolutionary benefits of AI-mediated connection. Three priority actions emerge:

First, **mandate transparency in AI mediation**. Building on California's Consumer Privacy Act approach to dark patterns, policymakers should require clear disclosure when AI tools mediate human-to-human communication [13]. This includes labeling AI-generated or AI-modified content in messaging platforms, dating applications, and professional communication tools. The psychological rationale is straightforward: authentic human connection requires accurate attribution of emotional content. When users cannot distinguish between human and AI expression, the trust foundation of relationships erodes. The European Union's AI Act provides a model for risk-based regulation that could be extended to cover relational mediation specifically [16].

Second, **establish developmental protections for minors**. Given the evidence that young people face heightened risks during critical periods of social and emotional development, policymakers should implement age-specific safeguards for AI companion applications [6]. California Senate Bill 243 establishes requirements for companion chatbots including safety protocols and suicide prevention resources, providing a template for broader adoption [16]. These protections should include limits on emotional manipulation features, mandatory parental consent mechanisms, and restrictions on data collection from minor users. The psychological mechanism involves protecting attachment formation processes from distortion during neurodevelopmental windows of heightened plasticity.

Third, **fund longitudinal research on AI-human relational dynamics**. Current evidence relies heavily on short-term studies and self-reported outcomes. Policymakers should allocate resources for multi-year studies tracking the long-term effects of AI companion use on social skill development, relationship satisfaction, and mental health outcomes [10]. The European Data Protection Board's guidelines on automated decision-making provide a framework for ethical research protocols that protect participant privacy while generating actionable evidence [13].

**For Designers:**

Technology designers bear direct responsibility for implementing ethical principles at the architectural level. The evidence suggests four priority design interventions:

First, **implement human-centered design principles that prioritize user agency**. Sustainable AI-based strategies must align with ethical and human-centered design principles to achieve long-term positive outcomes [10]. This includes providing users with clear control over AI mediation levels, allowing them to toggle between AI-assisted and direct communication modes. The psychological mechanism involves preserving informational autonomy: when users feel they control the mediation process rather than being controlled by it, trust and satisfaction increase.

Second, **avoid manipulative engagement patterns**. Designers must resist implementing variable reward schedules, emotional conditioning, and other features that create unhealthy dependency [13]. Research on problematic dating app use demonstrates that features exploiting rejection sensitivity and social appearance anxiety create harmful usage patterns [13]. Ethical design should instead focus on features that encourage healthy usage limits, promote transition to human-to-human interaction, and provide clear signals when AI mediation is active.

Third, **build authenticity verification into system architecture**. Given the moral challenge posed by AI mediation of human-to-human relationships, designers should implement technical features that allow users to verify the authenticity of communicated content [8]. This could include watermarking AI-generated content, providing transparency dashboards showing AI intervention levels, or implementing consent protocols before AI modifies user messages. The psychological rationale involves restoring mutual recognition: when both parties understand the mediation conditions, authentic connection remains possible even within technologically mediated environments.

Fourth, **design for positive spillover effects**. AI companions should be explicitly designed to facilitate rather than replace human connection [6]. This includes features that encourage users to practice social skills learned in AI interaction within human contexts, prompts that suggest human connection opportunities, and integration with existing social networks rather than walled-garden isolation. The mechanism involves leveraging AI as a bridge rather than a destination for relational needs.

**For Clinicians:**

Mental health professionals and social workers occupy a critical position in monitoring and guiding healthy AI-human relational patterns. Three recommendations emerge from the evidence:

First, **maintain competence in AI-relational dynamics**. Social workers have an ethical duty to keep pace with research-informed developments in AI technology and adjust their protocols accordingly [7]. This includes understanding the psychological mechanisms through which AI companions affect loneliness, attachment, and social skill development. Professional organizations should develop continuing education requirements specifically addressing AI-human interaction, similar to existing requirements for cultural competence and trauma-informed care.

Second, **integrate AI assessment into clinical intake and monitoring**. Given that young people may develop intense relationships with AI companions during critical developmental periods, clinicians should routinely assess AI companion use as part of comprehensive social history [6]. This assessment should explore the nature of the relationship, time spent in AI interaction, impact on human relationships, and any signs of unhealthy dependency. The psychological mechanism involves early identification of attachment distortions before they become entrenched patterns.

Third, **leverage AI as a therapeutic tool while maintaining ethical boundaries**. AI companions can serve as bridges to human connection for patients who initially struggle with interpersonal interaction [6]. Clinicians should consider recommending AI tools as transitional supports while maintaining clear ethical boundaries around informed consent and client autonomy [7]. This includes explaining the limitations of AI empathy, discussing the difference between simulated and authentic emotional connection, and establishing clear goals for transitioning to human-to-human interaction. The partnership between Google.org and the Trevor Project demonstrates how AI tools can be ethically deployed for mental health support when properly supervised [7].

The synthesis of evidence thus generates a clear directive: AI-mediated human connection represents evolution rather than rupture, but this evolution carries genuine risks that demand proactive governance. Policymakers must establish transparency and developmental protections. Designers must implement human-centered architectures that preserve agency and authenticity. Clinicians must maintain competence and integrate AI assessment into practice. Together, these actions can ensure that technological evolution serves the enduring human need for authentic connection rather than undermining it.
