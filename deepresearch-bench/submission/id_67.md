# Safe Reinforcement Learning for Motion Planning under Sparse Rewards: Mechanisms, Constraints, and Trajectory Implications

## Executive Summary and Strategic Context

The integration of safe reinforcement learning (RL) with motion planning under sparse reward conditions represents one of the most pressing challenges in deploying autonomous systems for real-world applications. This section establishes the foundational problem landscape, defining the core tension between exploration requirements and safety guarantees, while delineating the temporal and methodological boundaries of this review. The analysis focuses on post-2020 advances that integrate planning structures with safety-critical learning, excluding general supervised learning approaches and historical RL methods that lack formal safety guarantees.

### Core Challenge: Balancing Exploration Efficiency with Safety Guarantees

The fundamental tension in safe sparse-reward RL arises from conflicting requirements: effective exploration demands aggressive state-space coverage to discover rewarding behaviors, while safety-critical applications require zero-violation constraints throughout the learning process. This dichotomy creates a paradox where the very mechanisms that enable learning in sparse environments inherently increase the probability of constraint violations during training [16][17].

**The Exploration-Safety Trade-off**

In sparse-reward environments, agents receive feedback only upon task completion, providing no gradient signal for intermediate behaviors. Traditional exploration strategies—such as epsilon-greedy policies or entropy maximization—rely on random perturbations that become increasingly inefficient as state-space dimensionality grows. For motion planning applications, this inefficiency compounds with safety requirements: each exploratory action in a physical system carries the risk of collision, mechanical damage, or harm to human operators [13][22].

Recent methods address this tension through distinct mechanistic approaches. ActSafe, published in 2025, guarantees safety during learning while obtaining a near-optimal policy in finite time under regularity assumptions on constraints and dynamics [17]. The mechanism operates by maintaining a safe set of actions derived from learned constraint models, effectively reducing the exploration volume to regions where safety can be formally verified. This approach contrasts with unconstrained exploration methods that rely on post-hoc penalty terms, which provide no guarantees during the learning phase itself.

**Distinguishing Safety Constraints from Task Constraints**

A critical source of ambiguity in the literature involves the conflation of safety constraints with task optimization constraints. This report explicitly distinguishes between these categories:

- **Safety Constraints**: Hard boundaries that must never be violated, including collision avoidance, joint limit protection, and human proximity requirements. These constraints typically employ Control Barrier Functions (CBFs), Lyapunov-based methods, or reachability analysis to provide formal guarantees [12][18].

- **Task Constraints**: Soft optimization objectives related to energy consumption, completion time, or trajectory smoothness. These constraints admit trade-offs and are typically handled through Lagrangian methods or multi-objective optimization frameworks [18][26].

The distinction matters for planning implications: safety constraints require hard enforcement mechanisms (such as action filtering or shield-based intervention), while task constraints can be incorporated into the reward structure through shaping or constrained optimization. Methods like PID Lagrangian approaches handle soft constraints through adaptive multiplier updates, but these provide no formal safety guarantees during exploration [18].

**Mechanisms for Improving Exploration Efficiency**

Recent advances improve exploration efficiency through specific mechanistic pathways rather than empirical performance gains alone. Understanding these mechanisms is essential for method selection in application contexts:

**Reward Machines and Automated Reward Design**: Reward Machines (RMs) convert sparse rewards into dense, structured learning signals by encoding task structure as finite-state automata. The mechanism improves sample efficiency by providing intermediate feedback at state transitions within the automaton, effectively decomposing long-horizon tasks into manageable sub-goals [4]. This approach reduces volume dependence—the requirement to explore the entire state space before receiving any reward signal—by creating dense feedback along semantically meaningful trajectories. The framework scales to diverse environments including grid worlds, complex 3D environments, and robotics with continuous control [4].

**Search-Inspired Exploration (SIERL)**: SIERL actively guides exploration by setting sub-goals based on search principles extracted from existing RL algorithms without leveraging prior information [16]. The mechanism operates by identifying promising state regions through value function analysis, then directing exploration toward these regions systematically rather than randomly. In experiments on challenging sparse-reward environments, SIERL outperforms dominant baselines in both achieving the main task goal and generalizing to reach arbitrary states in the environment [16].

**Directed Sparse-Reward Goal-Conditioned RL (DISCOVER)**: DISCOVER extracts a sense of direction from existing RL algorithms for very long-horizon tasks [2]. The mechanism reduces volume dependence by learning goal-conditioned policies that can be composed to reach distant states, effectively creating a curriculum of progressively challenging sub-goals. This approach addresses the core inefficiency of sparse-reward exploration: the exponential growth of required samples with task horizon length.

**Information-Theoretic Approaches**: The VIB-IG framework learns compact and predictive latent representations through the Information Bottleneck principle, defining intrinsic rewards via information gain [19]. The mechanism improves exploration by directing the agent toward states that maximize predictive information about future outcomes, creating dense intrinsic rewards even when extrinsic rewards remain sparse. This approach decouples policy regularization from reward shaping, allowing independent optimization of exploration and safety objectives [19].

**Exploration-Guided Reward Shaping**: This method shapes rewards based on exploration progress rather than task completion, providing intermediate feedback that guides the agent toward unexplored but potentially rewarding regions [21]. The mechanism addresses the credit assignment problem in sparse-reward environments by creating a gradient signal that reflects exploration novelty rather than task success.

**Safety During Exploration**

The requirement for zero-violation safety during learning fundamentally constrains exploration strategies. Methods like ActSafe build on model-based RL advancements to maintain safety guarantees while exploring [17]. The mechanism operates under regularity assumptions on constraints and dynamics, using learned probabilistic failure surrogates to predict unsafe regions before exploration occurs [15].

Constrained POMDP planning approaches use adaptive safety constraints that tighten or relax based on learned uncertainty about constraint boundaries [15]. This mechanism allows more aggressive exploration in well-understood regions while maintaining conservative behavior near constraint boundaries, effectively balancing exploration efficiency with safety guarantees.

For UAV applications, RL-based collision avoidance systems learn optimal trajectory planning while maintaining separation from obstacles and other aircraft [22]. The mechanism integrates perception uncertainty into the safety constraint, ensuring that exploration remains safe even under sensor noise and partial observability.

## Mechanisms for Efficient and Proactive Exploration

Exploration in sparse-reward reinforcement learning environments represents one of the most persistent challenges in deploying safe RL systems for motion planning. When external rewards arrive infrequently or only upon task completion, agents struggle to identify which actions contribute meaningfully to goal achievement. This section provides a deep technical analysis of three complementary mechanisms that address this fundamental limitation: automated curricula with directed goal selection, search-inspired frontier exploration, and intrinsic motivation with reward shaping. Each mechanism operates on distinct principles but shares the common objective of reducing the effective search volume while maintaining safety guarantees throughout the learning process.

### Automated Curricula and Directed Goal Selection

Automated curriculum learning methods address the exploration problem by systematically structuring the sequence of learning experiences, enabling agents to progress from simpler sub-tasks toward the full target task. The DISCOVER method exemplifies this approach by extracting directional information from existing RL algorithms without requiring prior environmental knowledge [2]. This capability is critical for motion planning applications where pre-specifying useful intermediate goals is often infeasible.

The mechanistic basis for DISCOVER's efficiency gains lies in its reduction of volume dependence. Traditional exploration strategies in high-dimensional state spaces suffer from exponential growth in the number of states requiring visitation before discovering rewarding trajectories. DISCOVER circumvents this by identifying a "sense of direction" embedded within the agent's existing policy gradients and value estimates, then leveraging this directional signal to select exploratory goals that are provably relevant to the target task [2]. Rather than sampling goals uniformly across the state space, the method concentrates exploration effort along trajectories that exhibit non-zero progress signals, effectively collapsing the search volume from the full state space to a lower-dimensional manifold of promising regions.

This directed goal selection operates through a goal-conditioned reinforcement learning framework where the agent learns a policy π(a|s, g) conditioned on both the current state s and a goal g. The curriculum automation emerges from an auxiliary selection mechanism that evaluates candidate goals based on their estimated learnability—goals that are neither trivially achievable nor impossibly distant receive priority. This creates a self-paced learning progression where the agent continuously operates at the boundary of its current capabilities, a principle aligned with the psychological concept of the zone of proximal development.

The efficiency improvement from volume reduction has direct implications for safety-constrained motion planning. When exploration is undirected, agents must traverse large portions of the state space, increasing the probability of encountering unsafe configurations. By contrast, directed exploration along task-relevant manifolds reduces the number of unsafe state visits required to achieve competence. However, this efficiency gain must be balanced against the risk of premature convergence to suboptimal policies if the directional signal is noisy or misleading. Recent work addresses this by maintaining a diversity bonus in the goal selection criterion, ensuring that the curriculum does not collapse to a single narrow trajectory family [9].

For motion planning applications, automated curricula enable a critical distinction between offline pathfinding and online control policy generation. In offline settings, the curriculum can be pre-computed based on environmental models, generating a sequence of waypoints that progressively increase in difficulty. In online settings, the curriculum must adapt in real-time to the agent's learning progress and any environmental changes. DISCOVER's extraction of direction from existing algorithms makes it particularly suitable for online adaptation, as it does not require external supervision or environmental models to identify productive exploration directions [2].

The integration of automated curricula with safety constraints requires careful architectural design. Safety constraints—such as collision avoidance boundaries—must remain invariant throughout the curriculum progression, while task constraints—such as energy consumption limits or time-to-completion targets—may relax or tighten as the agent advances through curriculum stages. Methods like ActSafe demonstrate that active exploration can proceed under safety constraints while still obtaining near-optimal policies in finite time, provided regularity assumptions on the constraints and dynamics hold [17]. This separation between invariant safety constraints and adaptive task constraints is essential for maintaining safety guarantees while enabling efficient curriculum-based exploration.

### Search-Inspired Frontier Exploration

Search-inspired frontier exploration methods draw conceptual inspiration from classical search algorithms, adapting principles like frontier expansion and cost-to-go estimation to the reinforcement learning setting. The SIERL (Search-Inspired Exploration in Reinforcement Learning) method exemplifies this approach by systematically expanding the known state space frontier through sub-goal selection based on learning progress [16].

The frontier, in this context, refers to the boundary between states the agent has successfully visited and states that remain unexplored. SIERL operates by maintaining an explicit representation of this frontier and selecting sub-goals from frontier states at the beginning of each episode [16]. This mechanism ensures that exploration effort is concentrated on expanding the reachable state space rather than revisiting already-mastered regions. The efficiency gain arises from the elimination of redundant exploration—states that have been thoroughly explored receive no additional exploration bonus, directing the agent toward genuinely novel regions.

The novelty-cost-to-go balance represents the core innovation of SIERL. Pure novelty-seeking strategies, such as those based on state visitation counts or prediction error, can lead agents toward high-novelty but low-value regions of the state space. SIERL addresses this by incorporating cost-to-go estimates alongside novelty measures when selecting frontier sub-goals. The cost-to-go estimate provides a heuristic for the expected effort required to reach a candidate sub-goal from the current state, while the novelty measure captures the information gain from visiting that sub-goal. By optimizing a weighted combination of these two factors, SIERL achieves systematic frontier expansion that prioritizes informative yet accessible states [16].

This balance has direct mechanistic implications for exploration efficiency. In high-dimensional motion planning environments, the number of novel states grows exponentially with dimensionality, making pure novelty-seeking computationally intractable. The cost-to-go filter prunes the candidate set to states that are reachable within a reasonable horizon, reducing the effective branching factor of the exploration search. Empirical results demonstrate that SIERL outperforms dominant baselines in both achieving the main task goal and generalizing to reach arbitrary states in sparse-reward environments [16].

The frontier exploration mechanism naturally supports the distinction between safety constraints and task constraints. Safety constraints define hard boundaries on the frontier expansion—states that violate collision avoidance or other safety requirements are excluded from the frontier candidate set entirely. Task constraints, by contrast, influence the cost-to-go estimation but do not absolutely prohibit state visitation. For example, a high-energy trajectory might have elevated cost-to-go but remain within the frontier if it offers significant novelty or progress toward the goal. This architectural separation enables SIERL to maintain safety guarantees while flexibly trading off task performance metrics during exploration.

For trajectory planning applications, SIERL's frontier-based approach supports both offline pathfinding and online control policy generation. In offline settings, the frontier can be expanded using simulated rollouts, building a comprehensive map of reachable states before deployment. In online settings, the frontier updates incrementally as the agent interacts with the environment, enabling real-time adaptation to unexpected obstacles or dynamics changes. The sub-goal selection mechanism operates identically in both cases, providing a unified framework for exploration across planning horizons [16].

A critical consideration for frontier exploration in safety-critical domains is the treatment of frontier states near safety boundaries. Aggressive frontier expansion toward safety constraint boundaries risks constraint violation during the exploration process. Conservative approaches maintain a safety margin around the frontier, excluding states within a specified distance of known unsafe regions. This margin can be adaptive, shrinking as the agent's confidence in its safety predictions increases. The ActSafe framework demonstrates that such adaptive safety margins can guarantee safety during learning while still enabling efficient frontier expansion [17].

### Intrinsic Motivation and Reward Shaping

Intrinsic motivation and reward shaping methods address sparse rewards by generating auxiliary reward signals that provide dense feedback throughout the learning process. Unlike automated curricula and frontier exploration, which structure the sequence of experiences, intrinsic motivation modifies the reward function itself to make the learning signal more informative. This section evaluates three prominent approaches: LLM-driven intrinsic motivation, VAE-based novelty detection, and self-adaptive reward shaping.

LLM-driven intrinsic motivation leverages the semantic understanding encoded in large language models to generate contextually appropriate reward signals. Recent work proposes integrating LLMs with reinforcement learning agents to produce intrinsic rewards based on semantic descriptions of state transitions [3]. The LLM evaluates whether a state transition represents meaningful progress toward task completion, assigning higher intrinsic rewards to transitions that align with task-relevant semantic concepts. This approach is particularly valuable for motion planning tasks where the relationship between low-level actions and high-level task progress is complex and difficult to specify manually.

The mechanistic advantage of LLM-driven rewards lies in their ability to capture task semantics that are difficult to encode in hand-crafted reward functions. Traditional reward shaping requires domain experts to specify potential functions or intermediate rewards, a process that is time-consuming and error-prone. LLMs, having been pre-trained on vast corpora of task descriptions and demonstrations, can infer appropriate reward structures from natural language task specifications alone. This reduces the supervision burden while maintaining reward signal quality. However, LLM-driven rewards introduce computational overhead and potential inconsistency—the LLM's reward assignments may vary across similar states due to the stochastic nature of language model generation.

VAE-based novelty detection offers an alternative intrinsic motivation mechanism grounded in representation learning. The Variational State as Intrinsic Reward (VSIMR) approach uses Variational AutoEncoders to reward state novelty by measuring reconstruction error or latent space distance from previously visited states [3]. States that are difficult to reconstruct or lie far from the centroid of visited states in latent space receive higher intrinsic rewards, encouraging the agent to explore underrepresented regions of the state space.

The efficiency gain from VAE-based novelty arises from its compression of high-dimensional observations into a lower-dimensional latent representation where novelty can be efficiently measured. In motion planning with visual observations, raw pixel-space novelty measures are computationally expensive and sensitive to irrelevant visual variations. VAEs learn to encode task-relevant features while discarding nuisance variables, producing novelty signals that correlate with meaningful state changes rather than superficial visual differences. This representation-based novelty is more sample-efficient than count-based or prediction-error-based alternatives in high-dimensional observation spaces.

Self-Adaptive Success Rate (SASR) represents a third approach that dynamically adjusts reward shaping parameters based on the agent's learning progress [6]. Rather than using fixed intrinsic reward weights, SASR monitors the agent's success rate on sub-tasks and adapts the reward shaping intensity accordingly. When success rates are low, intrinsic rewards are amplified to encourage exploration; when success rates are high, intrinsic rewards are reduced to prevent over-exploration of already-mastered behaviors.

The self-adaptive mechanism addresses a critical limitation of static reward shaping: the optimal balance between extrinsic and intrinsic rewards changes throughout the learning process. Early in training, strong intrinsic rewards are necessary to drive exploration in the absence of extrinsic signal. Later in training, excessive intrinsic rewards can interfere with policy convergence by continuing to encourage novelty after the task-relevant state space has been thoroughly explored. SASR's adaptive adjustment ensures that intrinsic motivation remains aligned with the agent's current learning needs, improving sample efficiency and final performance.

Exploration-Guided Reward Shaping extends this adaptive principle by operating in a fully self-supervised manner, requiring no external supervision beyond the sparse task reward [21]. The method identifies state-action pairs that lead to high-value outcomes and shapes rewards to encourage similar transitions, effectively bootstrapping dense feedback from sparse successes. This approach is particularly valuable for safety-critical motion planning where external supervision is expensive or risky to obtain.

Reward Machines provide a formal framework for automated reward design, converting sparse rewards into dense, structured learning signals through automaton-based reward specifications [4]. The reward machine maintains an internal state that tracks progress through task sub-goals, emitting dense rewards as the agent transitions between automaton states. This structured approach scales to diverse environments including grid worlds, complex 3D environments, and robotics applications, dramatically improving sample efficiency compared to learning from sparse rewards alone.

The integration of intrinsic motivation with safety constraints requires explicit separation between safety and task objectives. Safety constraints must remain inviolate regardless of intrinsic reward magnitude—no amount of novelty bonus should justify unsafe state visitation. This is typically enforced through constrained optimization frameworks where safety constraints define the feasible policy space, and intrinsic rewards optimize within that space. Methods like Control Barrier Functions (CBF) provide formal guarantees that safety constraints are satisfied throughout learning, even when intrinsic rewards strongly encourage exploration toward safety boundaries [18].

Task constraints, by contrast, can be incorporated into the intrinsic reward function as soft penalties. Energy consumption, time-to-completion, and smoothness metrics can be encoded as negative intrinsic rewards that shape behavior without imposing hard feasibility constraints. This distinction enables flexible trade-offs between task performance metrics while maintaining invariant safety guarantees. The PID-Lagrangian approach demonstrates that such constraint handling can reduce failsafe interventions while maintaining task performance, provided cost thresholds are appropriately tuned [18].

For trajectory planning applications, intrinsic motivation methods support both offline and online regimes with different architectural requirements. Offline methods can pre-compute intrinsic reward functions based on environmental models or demonstration data, providing consistent reward signals throughout training. Online methods must compute intrinsic rewards in real-time, requiring efficient novelty estimation and reward computation. VAE-based approaches are particularly well-suited to online computation, as the encoder network can process observations with minimal latency once trained. LLM-driven approaches may require optimization for real-time inference, potentially through model distillation or caching of frequently queried state descriptions.

The convergence properties of intrinsic motivation methods differ from curriculum and frontier approaches. While curricula and frontiers provide explicit exploration schedules that guarantee eventual coverage of the state space, intrinsic motivation relies on the agent's policy optimization to discover rewarding behaviors. This can lead to faster initial progress but risks premature convergence if the intrinsic reward function has local optima that do not align with task completion. Hybrid approaches that combine intrinsic motivation with curriculum-based goal selection or frontier expansion can capture the rapid initial progress of intrinsic rewards while maintaining the systematic coverage guarantees of structured exploration methods.

## Safety Constraints and Risk Mitigation Frameworks

Safe reinforcement learning for motion planning under sparse rewards requires rigorous frameworks that distinguish between safety constraints—such as collision avoidance and physical integrity—and task constraints like energy consumption or time limits. This distinction is critical because safety violations carry irreversible consequences in real-world deployment, whereas task constraint violations typically result in suboptimal performance. Contemporary approaches organize safety mechanisms along a spectrum from soft probabilistic guarantees to hard intervention-based shields, each offering different trade-offs between theoretical assurance and practical flexibility.

### Chance-Constrained and Bi-Level Optimization

Chance-constrained optimization frameworks address safety by bounding the probability of constraint violation rather than enforcing absolute zero-violation guarantees. This probabilistic approach acknowledges that in complex, uncertain environments, absolute safety may be computationally intractable or overly conservative. The fundamental formulation requires that the probability of violating safety constraints remains below a specified threshold ε, expressed as P(constraint violation) ≤ ε, while maximizing expected return. This creates a bi-level optimization structure where the outer level optimizes policy performance and the inner level ensures constraint satisfaction.

Recent advances in bi-level optimization have introduced frameworks that prioritize unsafe probability minimization before return maximization. Methods like MaxSafe employ optimal action masks to filter unsafe actions during policy execution, effectively creating a safety layer that operates independently from the reward optimization objective. This separation allows the agent to explore the action space while maintaining probabilistic safety guarantees throughout the learning process. The action masking mechanism identifies and excludes actions that would violate chance constraints with probability exceeding the threshold, reducing the effective action space to only those actions satisfying safety requirements [18].

Constrained Policy Optimization (CPO) represents a foundational approach in this category, providing theoretical guarantees of near-constraint satisfaction under specific regularity conditions. CPO achieves this by projecting policy updates onto a feasible region defined by linearized constraint boundaries, ensuring that each policy iteration maintains constraint satisfaction within a bounded tolerance. The method's theoretical foundation relies on trust-region optimization, where policy updates are constrained to remain within a KL-divergence ball around the current policy while simultaneously satisfying linearized safety constraints [5]. This approach provides convergence guarantees but requires careful tuning of the trust region size to balance exploration efficiency against constraint adherence.

The distinction between safety constraints and task constraints becomes particularly important in bi-level formulations. Safety constraints—such as maintaining minimum distance from obstacles or avoiding joint limit violations—typically employ stricter probability thresholds (ε < 0.01) compared to task constraints like energy budgets (ε ≈ 0.1). This differentiation reflects the asymmetric cost structure where safety violations incur catastrophic penalties while task constraint violations result in degraded performance. Recent work on Optlayer demonstrates practical constrained optimization for deep reinforcement learning in real-world robotic systems, implementing this hierarchical constraint structure through differentiable safety layers that can be integrated with standard policy gradient methods [18].

ActSafe extends these concepts by providing theoretical guarantees of safety during active exploration while achieving near-optimal policies in finite time. Under regularity assumptions on constraints and dynamics, ActSafe guarantees that the agent remains within safe regions throughout the learning process, addressing a critical limitation of earlier chance-constrained methods that only provided asymptotic guarantees. The framework builds on model-based RL advancements to construct confidence intervals around constraint functions, enabling safe exploration even when constraint boundaries are initially unknown [17]. This approach is particularly valuable for sparse reward environments where the agent must explore extensively before receiving meaningful feedback, as it prevents catastrophic failures during the exploration phase.

The mechanistic advantage of chance-constrained bi-level optimization lies in its ability to reduce the effective search space while maintaining theoretical guarantees. By filtering unsafe actions before policy evaluation, these methods reduce volume dependence—the relationship between state space dimensionality and sample complexity—enabling more efficient exploration in high-dimensional continuous control tasks. However, this efficiency comes at the cost of potential over-conservatism, as the probabilistic bounds must account for worst-case uncertainty realizations, potentially excluding actions that would be safe under typical conditions.

Linear safety signal models provide an alternative formulation where safety constraints are represented as linear functions of state-action pairs, enabling analytical optimization of the safety layer. This approach defines a goal of maintaining zero constraint violations throughout the learning process by constructing a differentiable safety boundary that can be incorporated directly into the policy optimization objective [14]. The linearity assumption simplifies the optimization landscape but may be insufficient for complex, non-convex safety regions encountered in real-world motion planning scenarios.

Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) formulations extend chance-constrained optimization to settings with uncertain state estimation, using learned probabilistic failure surrogates and adaptive safety constraints to handle partial observability. This approach is particularly relevant for motion planning tasks where sensor noise or occlusion creates uncertainty about the agent's true state relative to safety boundaries [15]. The adaptive constraint mechanism adjusts safety thresholds based on estimation confidence, tightening constraints when state uncertainty is high and relaxing them when observations are reliable.

### Extreme Value Theory for Tail Risk Management

Extreme Value Theory (EVT) addresses a fundamental limitation of expectation-based safety constraints: their inability to capture rare but catastrophic "black swan" events that fall in the tail of the cost distribution. Traditional constrained reinforcement learning methods optimize expected cost or constrain average constraint violation, which can overlook low-probability, high-severity safety violations that occur infrequently during training but pose unacceptable risks in deployment. EVT-based methods model the statistical behavior of extreme cost samples separately from the bulk distribution, enabling explicit optimization of tail risk metrics.

Extreme Value Policy Optimization (EVO) represents a recent advancement that leverages EVT to prevent safety violations overlooked by expectation-based constraints. The method pays special attention to rare but risky situations by using EVT as a statistical tool designed specifically for modeling extreme events. Traditional methods often miss these rare black swan events, focusing predominantly on average-case performance, which creates a dangerous blind spot for safety-critical applications [10]. EVO addresses this gap by fitting a Generalized Pareto Distribution to cost samples exceeding a high threshold, enabling estimation of tail probabilities that would require impractically large sample sizes to observe directly.

The mechanistic advantage of EVT-based approaches lies in their sample efficiency for tail risk estimation. Rather than requiring millions of samples to observe rare events empirically, EVT extrapolates tail behavior from a smaller set of extreme observations using asymptotic theory. This reduces the sample complexity of learning safe policies in sparse reward environments where safety violations are intentionally rare by design. The method achieves this by separating the modeling of bulk distribution behavior from tail behavior, applying different statistical techniques to each regime based on their distinct mathematical properties.

EVO has demonstrated reduced numbers of serious safety violations compared to baseline constrained RL methods, particularly in environments where cost distributions exhibit heavy tails or where safety violations cluster in specific regions of the state space. The improvement stems from the method's ability to identify and avoid state-action pairs that, while safe on average, carry non-negligible probability of extreme cost outcomes. This is particularly important for motion planning tasks where a single catastrophic failure—such as a high-speed collision—renders the entire learning episode useless regardless of prior performance [10].

The distinction between safety and task constraints becomes critical when applying EVT. Safety constraints require modeling the upper tail of cost distributions (worst-case outcomes), while task constraints may focus on lower tails (best-case performance) or central tendencies. EVT-based methods typically apply only to safety constraints, as task constraint violations rarely exhibit the catastrophic severity that justifies tail-specific modeling. This selective application reduces computational overhead while focusing tail risk management where it matters most.

Constrained reinforcement learning addresses safety by maximizing reward while limiting constraint violations, but standard CRL formulations optimize expected constraint satisfaction rather than tail risk. EVT extends this framework by replacing expectation-based constraints with Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) or similar tail risk measures that explicitly account for worst-case outcomes [10]. The resulting optimization problem requires specialized algorithms capable of estimating tail risk gradients, as standard policy gradient methods assume differentiability of expected values rather than tail quantiles.

The theoretical foundation of EVT-based safe RL relies on the Fisher-Tippett-Gnedenko theorem, which characterizes the limiting distribution of normalized maxima from independent samples. This theorem enables extrapolation beyond observed data, providing estimates of violation probabilities at levels far below the empirical observation rate. However, this extrapolation depends on the assumption that extreme events follow the same generative process as observed extremes, which may not hold when environment dynamics change or when the agent encounters previously unvisited state regions.

Recent work has integrated EVT with intrinsic motivation frameworks to guide exploration toward regions of high tail risk uncertainty. By treating tail risk estimation error as an intrinsic reward signal, agents can actively explore state-action pairs where extreme cost predictions have high variance, reducing uncertainty about safety boundaries more efficiently than random exploration [25]. This approach addresses the sparse reward challenge by providing dense feedback about safety learning progress even when extrinsic rewards remain unavailable.

### Control Barrier Functions and Safety Shields

Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) provide formal forward invariance guarantees that distinguish them fundamentally from probabilistic safety methods. A CBF defines a safety function h(x) over the state space where the superlevel set {x : h(x) ≥ 0} represents the safe region. Forward invariance guarantees that if the system starts in the safe set, it remains there for all future time under the controlled dynamics. This zero-violation guarantee during operation contrasts with chance-constrained methods that only bound violation probability, making CBFs particularly attractive for safety-critical motion planning where any violation is unacceptable [11].

Learning-based CBFs address the challenge of constructing barrier functions for complex systems where analytical derivation is intractable. Since the function computing the safety margin is often non-differentiable in real-world scenarios, learning-based approaches approximate it with neural networks to ensure differentiability and facilitate integration with CBF constraints. The key technical challenge is achieving bounded learning error and incorporating the upper bound into the CBF formulation to provide formal guarantees despite approximation uncertainty [11]. This requires careful architecture design and training procedures that bound the approximation error over the relevant state space.

Safety shields implement CBF-based intervention through action replacement or projection mechanisms that modify unsafe actions before execution. When the learning-based policy proposes an action that would violate the CBF condition, the safety shield projects the action onto the nearest safe action or replaces it with a pre-computed safe fallback. This intervention reduction technique minimizes the frequency and magnitude of safety interventions while maintaining formal guarantees. The shield operates as a runtime monitor that evaluates CBF conditions at each timestep, intervening only when necessary rather than constraining the policy during training [18].

High-Order Control Barrier Functions (HOCBF) extend the basic CBF framework to systems with relative degree greater than one, where the safety function's time derivative does not directly depend on the control input. HOCBF employs a truncated Taylor-based approach to propagate the barrier condition through multiple time derivatives, enabling safety guarantees for complex robotic systems with non-trivial dynamics [11]. This extension is essential for motion planning tasks where the relationship between control inputs and safety constraints involves multiple integration steps, such as position constraints for velocity-controlled systems.

The general safety framework for learning-based control in uncertain robotic systems integrates CBFs with adaptive uncertainty estimation to handle model mismatch and external disturbances. This framework, developed by Fisac, Akametalu, Zeilinger, Kaynama, Gillula, and Tomlin, provides a systematic approach to combining learning-based controllers with formal safety guarantees [18]. The key insight is that safety guarantees can be maintained even when the learned model has bounded error, provided the CBF condition incorporates worst-case uncertainty bounds into the safety margin calculation.

Continual learning in safety-critical systems leverages Lyapunov-guided reinforcement learning and barrier functions to provide formal guarantees while adapting to changing environments. This approach addresses the challenge of maintaining safety during deployment-time policy adaptation, where the agent must learn new behaviors without violating established safety constraints [12]. The method combines CBF-based safety filters with continual learning algorithms that bound the rate of policy change, ensuring that safety guarantees established during initial training remain valid throughout the adaptation process.

The distinction between offline pathfinding and online control policy generation is particularly relevant for CBF-based approaches. CBFs operate at the online control level, providing real-time safety guarantees for continuous control policies, whereas offline pathfinding methods generate reference trajectories that may not account for real-time disturbances. Integrating CBFs with learned policies enables online safety assurance that adapts to actual system states rather than planned trajectories, addressing the gap between offline planning and online execution [27].

Intervention reduction techniques minimize the frequency of safety shield activations, which is critical for maintaining policy learning efficiency. Excessive intervention prevents the agent from experiencing the consequences of unsafe actions, slowing the learning of inherently safe behaviors. Recent methods reduce interventions by learning CBFs that closely match the true safety boundary, minimizing the volume of the conservative safety margin. This requires balancing approximation accuracy against computational tractability, as tighter bounds demand more complex neural network architectures and longer training times [11].

The theoretical guarantees of CBF-based methods depend on several assumptions that may not hold in practice: accurate system dynamics models, bounded disturbance magnitudes, and sufficient control authority to satisfy the CBF condition. When these assumptions are violated, formal guarantees no longer apply, though the safety shield may still provide practical protection. Recent work addresses this gap by combining CBFs with data-driven uncertainty estimation, adapting the safety margin based on observed model prediction errors to maintain robustness under realistic conditions [17].

Search-Inspired Exploration in Reinforcement Learning (SIERL) complements CBF-based safety by actively guiding exploration through sub-goal setting based on learning progress. While CBFs ensure safety during exploration, SIERL improves exploration efficiency by directing the agent toward frontier states where safety boundaries are uncertain, reducing the sample complexity of learning safe policies in sparse reward environments [16]. This combination addresses both the safety and efficiency challenges inherent in safe RL for motion planning under sparse rewards.

## Integrating Exploration and Safety in Continuous Spaces

The integration of exploration drives with safety constraints in continuous state-action spaces represents one of the most challenging frontiers in safe reinforcement learning. Unlike discrete domains where safety can be enforced through action masking or finite-state verification, continuous spaces require sophisticated mechanisms that simultaneously preserve the agent's capacity to discover novel behaviors while guaranteeing constraint satisfaction throughout the learning process. This section synthesizes three complementary approaches to this problem: model-based methods that plan optimistically for dynamics but pessimistically for constraints, intrinsic motivation frameworks that align curiosity with safety boundaries, and experience replay techniques that prioritize safety-critical samples without compromising exploration breadth. The fundamental tension across all three approaches lies in the sample efficiency trade-off—methods that enforce stricter safety guarantees typically require more interactions to converge, yet unsafe exploration can render learned policies unusable in real-world deployment scenarios.

### Model-Based Safe Exploration Algorithms

Model-based safe exploration algorithms address the continuous space challenge by constructing learned representations of both environment dynamics and constraint functions, enabling the agent to simulate potential trajectories before execution. The ActSafe framework exemplifies this approach, guaranteeing safety during learning while obtaining a near-optimal policy in finite time under regularity assumptions on the constraints and dynamics [17]. The core mechanism operates through a dual-optimism-pessimism principle: the algorithm plans optimistically with respect to the reward function and dynamics model to encourage exploration of potentially high-value regions, while simultaneously planning pessimistically with respect to constraint functions to ensure no safety violations occur during the learning process.

This asymmetric treatment of rewards versus constraints is mechanistically critical for sample efficiency in continuous spaces. By maintaining an optimistic dynamics model, ActSafe reduces the volume of state-action space that must be physically explored, as the agent can reason about unvisited regions through model predictions. The pessimistic constraint model, conversely, shrinks the feasible action set at each state, effectively reducing the exploration burden by eliminating regions that would violate safety requirements. This dual mechanism explains why model-based approaches can achieve better sample efficiency than model-free safe RL methods—the learned model acts as a computational substitute for physical interaction, particularly valuable when constraint violations carry high costs [17].

The distinction between safety constraints and task constraints becomes particularly salient in model-based formulations. Safety constraints, such as collision avoidance or joint limit violations, must be satisfied with high probability at all times during learning and deployment. Task constraints, including energy consumption limits or completion time bounds, may tolerate occasional violations during exploration if they ultimately improve task performance. ActSafe and similar frameworks typically treat safety constraints as hard boundaries that define the feasible region of the policy space, while task constraints may be incorporated into the reward function or handled through softer penalty mechanisms [18]. This distinction matters for trajectory planning implications: safety constraints require online control policy generation with real-time constraint verification, whereas task constraints can often be addressed through offline pathfinding optimization.

Practical variants of ActSafe build on recent model-based RL advancements to handle the computational challenges of continuous action spaces. Traditional Monte Carlo Tree Search methods only work when decisions are selected from a finite set, but space trajectory optimization and robotic manipulation involve continuous parameters that require different treatment [28]. Modern approaches extend MCTS to continuous spaces through action discretization strategies or by integrating gradient-based optimization within the tree search procedure. The computational overhead of maintaining both optimistic and pessimistic models introduces a trade-off: more accurate models reduce the need for physical exploration but increase per-step computation time, which can limit real-time applicability in high-frequency control scenarios.

Foundational work on safe model-based reinforcement learning with stability guarantees established the theoretical underpinnings for these approaches, demonstrating that Lyapunov-based stability constraints can be incorporated into the model learning process [18]. Recent extensions from 2021-2025 have focused on reducing the conservatism of these methods while maintaining formal safety guarantees. The key insight is that overly pessimistic constraint models can unnecessarily restrict exploration, leading to suboptimal policies that never discover high-reward regions near constraint boundaries. Adaptive uncertainty quantification in the constraint model allows the agent to explore closer to safety boundaries as confidence in the model increases, improving sample efficiency without compromising safety [17].

Deployment considerations for model-based safe exploration reveal important distinctions between simulated and real-world environments. In datacenter cooling systems and similar industrial applications, deploying RL requires human oversight on less than 1% of agent interactions while solving complex tasks without explicit reward functions [14]. Model-based methods are particularly well-suited to these scenarios because the learned dynamics model can be validated against historical operational data before deployment, reducing the risk of catastrophic failures during early learning phases. However, model mismatch between simulation and reality remains a critical challenge—constraints that are satisfied in the learned model may be violated in the physical system if the model fails to capture relevant dynamics.

### Constrained Intrinsic Motivation

Constrained Intrinsic Motivation (CIM) addresses a fundamental problem in exploration: standard intrinsic motivation methods encourage novelty-seeking behavior that can lead agents toward unsafe regions of the state space. When intrinsic rewards are derived from prediction error, state visitation counts, or information gain without safety considerations, curiosity-driven agents may systematically explore dangerous configurations that maximize novelty while violating constraints [23]. CIM frameworks align intrinsic objectives with safety constraints to prevent this curiosity-driven risk-taking while preserving the exploration benefits that intrinsic motivation provides in sparse reward environments.

The mechanistic explanation for why constrained intrinsic motivation improves exploration efficiency lies in the reduction of wasted exploration effort. Unconstrained intrinsic motivation drives agents to visit novel states regardless of their safety status, meaning a significant portion of exploration budget may be consumed by states that are ultimately infeasible for the final policy. By incorporating safety constraints directly into the intrinsic reward calculation, CIM methods redirect exploration toward novel but safe regions, effectively increasing the density of useful experiences in the replay buffer. This reallocation of exploration effort explains the sample efficiency gains observed in constrained intrinsic motivation approaches—the agent spends fewer interactions discovering states it cannot use and more interactions refining policies within the feasible region [23].

Information-theoretic formulations of intrinsic motivation provide a principled foundation for constraint integration. Mutual information critics can provide pointwise scores evaluated at the level of individual transitions, making them well-suited for defining information-gain-based intrinsic rewards that respect safety boundaries [19]. When the mutual information between states and skills is maximized subject to constraint satisfaction, the resulting intrinsic reward encourages the agent to discover diverse behaviors that are all safe to execute. This approach grounds both representation learning and exploration in the same information-theoretic objective, providing a principled alternative to heuristic or purely contrastive formulations of curiosity that may ignore safety considerations.

Reward Machines offer a complementary framework for converting sparse rewards into dense, structured learning signals while respecting constraints. Results show that this framework dramatically improves sample efficiency by providing intermediate feedback that guides exploration toward constraint-satisfying trajectories [4]. The automaton structure of reward machines can encode safety constraints as forbidden state transitions, ensuring that intrinsic rewards are only generated for trajectories that maintain constraint satisfaction throughout execution. This is particularly valuable in continuous control tasks where sparse and delayed rewards make it difficult for agents to discover which behaviors lead to task completion without violating safety requirements.

The sample inefficiency problem that affects abstract intrinsic motivation methods stems from the mismatch between intrinsic reward signals and task objectives. When intrinsic rewards dominate the learning signal, agents may optimize for novelty at the expense of task performance, requiring additional interactions to unlearn these behaviors once extrinsic rewards become available [23]. Constrained intrinsic motivation addresses this by annealing the intrinsic reward weight as the agent gains confidence in the constraint model, gradually shifting from exploration-focused to exploitation-focused behavior. This annealing schedule must be carefully tuned: too rapid a decay leaves the agent without sufficient exploration to discover optimal policies, while too slow a decay prolongs the period of suboptimal behavior driven by intrinsic rewards.

Recent work from 2022-2025 has explored deep intrinsically motivated exploration in continuous control, demonstrating that pretrained foundation models can provide semantic feature anchors that remain stable across domains [25]. These semantic features enable intrinsic motivation signals that are more aligned with task-relevant novelty rather than superficial state differences, improving the quality of exploration in continuous spaces. When combined with safety constraints, this approach allows agents to explore semantically diverse behaviors while avoiding unsafe configurations, though the computational overhead of extracting semantic features from foundation models introduces additional complexity.

The distinction between offline pathfinding and online control policy generation becomes relevant for constrained intrinsic motivation in trajectory planning contexts. Offline methods can pre-compute intrinsic reward structures that encode safety constraints into the reward machine automaton, while online methods must compute constraint-satisfying intrinsic rewards in real-time based on the current state and learned constraint model. Online approaches offer greater adaptability to changing environments but require more computational resources per decision cycle, creating a trade-off between flexibility and deployment feasibility [25].

### Safety-Prioritized Experience Replay

Safety-Prioritized Experience Replay techniques modify the standard experience replay buffer to accelerate constraint learning without compromising exploration breadth. The core insight is that safety-critical samples—transitions that occur near constraint boundaries or involve constraint violations—contain disproportionately valuable information for learning safe policies, yet these samples are typically rare in standard replay buffers that prioritize based on temporal difference error or uniform sampling [10]. By explicitly prioritizing these samples during training, agents can learn constraint-satisfying behaviors more efficiently than methods that treat all transitions equally.

The Extreme Value Policy Optimization (EVO) framework demonstrates this approach through an extreme prioritization mechanism during replay that amplifies the learning signal from rare but high-impact extreme samples [10]. Theoretically, this method establishes upper bounds on expected constraint violations during policy updates, guaranteeing strict constraint satisfaction while improving sample efficiency. The mechanistic explanation for this efficiency gain lies in the gradient signal concentration: by sampling safety-critical transitions more frequently, the policy gradient updates receive stronger signals about which actions lead to constraint violations, enabling faster convergence to safe policies. This reduces the number of interactions required to learn the constraint boundary compared to uniform replay, where safety-critical samples may be diluted by large numbers of safe but uninformative transitions.

The prioritization mechanism must balance two competing objectives: sufficient sampling of safety-critical transitions to learn constraints accurately, and adequate sampling of diverse safe transitions to learn high-performing policies within the feasible region. Over-prioritizing safety-critical samples can lead to overly conservative policies that never explore near-optimal regions close to constraint boundaries, while under-prioritizing them risks constraint violations during learning. Adaptive prioritization schemes adjust the sampling probability based on the agent's current constraint violation rate, increasing safety-critical sampling when violations are detected and decreasing it as the policy becomes more reliable [10].

Experience replay prioritization interacts with the distinction between safety constraints and task constraints in important ways. Safety constraints require immediate prioritization because violations during learning can have catastrophic consequences in real-world deployment. Task constraints, such as energy or time limits, may tolerate more relaxed prioritization since occasional violations during exploration do not necessarily render the policy unusable. This distinction affects how replay buffers should be structured: safety-critical samples may require a dedicated buffer with guaranteed minimum sampling rates, while task-constraint samples can be integrated into the standard prioritized replay mechanism with lower priority weights [18].

The integration of safety-prioritized replay with model-based methods creates additional opportunities for sample efficiency improvements. When a learned dynamics model is available, safety-critical samples can be augmented through model-based rollouts that simulate constraint boundary interactions without physical execution. This synthetic data generation reduces the need for dangerous real-world exploration while still providing the agent with training signals about constraint boundaries. However, model bias in these synthetic samples must be carefully managed to avoid learning incorrect constraint boundaries that could lead to violations when the policy is deployed [17].

Empirical evaluations in continuous control domains demonstrate that safety-prioritized replay reduces constraint violations during training while maintaining or improving final policy performance compared to uniform replay baselines. The reduction in violations is particularly pronounced in environments with sparse rewards, where standard RL methods may require thousands of interactions before receiving any feedback about constraint satisfaction [10]. By ensuring that the limited number of constraint-related transitions are sampled frequently during training, safety-prioritized replay enables agents to learn safe behaviors before accumulating significant violation counts.

The computational overhead of safety-prioritized replay is generally modest compared to model-based approaches, as the prioritization mechanism operates on stored transitions rather than requiring online model predictions. This makes safety-prioritized replay particularly attractive for real-time control applications where computational latency must be minimized. However, the method requires careful tuning of prioritization hyperparameters, and performance can degrade if the initial replay buffer contains insufficient safety-critical samples to establish meaningful priority weights [18].

Reducing safety interventions through improved replay prioritization has been demonstrated in provably safe reinforcement learning systems, where aggressive steering behaviors emerge when safety constraints are learned efficiently [18]. This suggests that safety-prioritized replay not only reduces violations during training but also produces policies that are more robust to distribution shift during deployment, as the agent has learned a more accurate representation of the constraint boundary through focused sampling of critical transitions.

## Implications for Trajectory Planning Architectures

The transition from theoretical safe reinforcement learning frameworks to deployable robotic systems necessitates fundamental architectural reconsiderations. Sparse-reward environments compound this challenge by limiting the feedback signal available for policy refinement, requiring planners to balance exploration efficiency with safety guarantees throughout the deployment pipeline. This section examines three critical architectural dimensions: the translation of learned policies into real-time control systems, the role of hierarchical structures and reward machines in decomposing long-horizon tasks, and the mechanisms required for safe sim-to-real transfer in physical deployment scenarios.

### From Policy Generation to Online Control

The distinction between offline pathfinding and online control policy generation represents a foundational architectural consideration for trajectory planning systems. Offline pathfinding computes static trajectories based on known environment models, whereas online control policies must continuously adapt to dynamic conditions while maintaining safety constraints in real-time [18]. This distinction becomes particularly acute in sparse-reward settings where the policy must infer appropriate behavior from limited feedback signals while operating under strict latency requirements.

Continuous action spaces introduce additional complexity for policy deployment. Traditional Monte Carlo Tree Search methods operate on finite action sets, but space trajectory optimization and robotic manipulation involve continuous parameters that require specialized adaptation strategies [28]. The hybrid nature of these action spaces demands architectures that can bridge discrete decision-making with continuous control execution. Recent approaches extend MCTS to continuous domains through parameterized action representations, though this introduces computational overhead that must be managed within real-time control loops.

Policy migration from simulation to physical hardware requires careful consideration of neural network architectures. Tanh neural networks have emerged as a preferred choice for policy representation due to their bounded output characteristics, which facilitate smoother control signals and reduce the risk of actuator saturation during deployment [32]. The bounded activation function provides inherent regularization that proves valuable when policies trained under sparse rewards encounter novel states during real-world operation.

Latency management represents a critical constraint for online control architectures. Control latency must be mitigated through architectural choices that separate high-level decision-making from low-level execution. Recent implementations demonstrate this separation through distributed computing architectures where high-level navigation policies run on embedded computers while low-level actuation and motor feedback execute on microcontrollers using micro-ROS protocols [27]. This division enables real-time communication with motor drivers while maintaining precise control over actuation timing, achieving the sub-millisecond response times required for safety-critical applications.

The integration of safety constraints into online control requires explicit architectural support for constraint enforcement. Control Barrier Functions provide a mechanism for enforcing safety constraints at the control level, independent of the reward signal [11]. When the function computing safety margins is non-differentiable, neural network approximation ensures differentiability and facilitates integration with CBFs while maintaining bounded learning error. This approach separates safety constraints (collision avoidance, joint limits) from task constraints (energy efficiency, completion time), allowing each to be optimized through appropriate mechanisms [26].

Continuous control tasks with sparse and delayed rewards present particular challenges for online policy execution. Frameworks incorporating assistant reward agents demonstrate robustness in these settings by providing auxiliary feedback signals that guide policy behavior without compromising the primary sparse reward structure [20]. The assistant agent generates shaping rewards that accelerate learning while preserving the optimal policy of the original sparse-reward task, enabling more reliable online control performance.

ROS 2 Humble has emerged as a standard platform for implementing these architectural patterns, providing inter-node and sensor communication infrastructure that supports the distributed control architecture required for real-time trajectory tracking [27]. The platform enables kinematics model validation and sensor integration while confirming real-time motor response and correct encoder readings, establishing the communication backbone necessary for policy deployment.

### Hierarchical Planning and Reward Machines

Hierarchical structures address the fundamental challenge of long-horizon planning under sparse rewards by decomposing complex tasks into solvable sub-goals. This decomposition reduces the effective planning horizon at each level, enabling more efficient exploration and more reliable constraint satisfaction throughout the trajectory.

Reward Machines provide a formal language representation of reward functions that enables structured task decomposition [4]. The underlying semantic structure enables shared policies to treat related sub-tasks in similar manners, forming the foundation for skill transfer across related planning problems. This formal representation allows planners to reason about task structure explicitly rather than learning it implicitly through trial and error, which proves particularly valuable when rewards are sparse and feedback is limited.

Automaton-guided reward structures shape agent exploration and learning by constructing finite-state machines that encode task progress and reward conditions [26]. These structures provide dense feedback signals derived from task progression rather than final outcome, effectively densifying the reward signal without altering the optimal policy. The automaton tracks which sub-goals have been achieved and provides intermediate rewards that guide the agent toward task completion, addressing the credit assignment problem inherent in sparse-reward environments.

The ARM-FM framework and related approaches leverage these hierarchical structures to decompose long-horizon planning tasks. Multiagent task decomposition demonstrates how shared semantic structures enable coordinated behavior across multiple agents while maintaining individual policy autonomy [4]. This approach proves particularly relevant for robotic systems where multiple degrees of freedom must be coordinated to achieve complex manipulation or navigation objectives.

The 3 M2ACD algorithm exemplifies hierarchical planning architecture for robotic arm trajectory planning, employing multi-strategy and multi-value network structures designed to meet grasping task requirements [29]. The algorithm divides the planning problem into distinct strategic components, each optimized for different aspects of the trajectory generation process. This decomposition enables more efficient exploration of the action space while maintaining safety constraints throughout the planning horizon.

NURBS (Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines) integration with M2ACD demonstrates how geometric representations can enhance hierarchical planning performance. The positional continuity metrics of TD3, DARC, and DDPG algorithms meet required standards, but tangential continuity improves by 275% when M2ACD incorporates NURBS representations [29]. This improvement reflects the mechanistic benefit of smooth trajectory representations: reduced jerk and acceleration demands on actuators, which translates to more reliable physical execution and reduced wear on mechanical components.

Search-Inspired Exploration in Reinforcement Learning (SIERL) actively guides exploration by setting sub-goals based on the agent's learning progress [16]. At the beginning of each episode, SIERL chooses a sub-goal from the frontier—the boundary between explored and unexplored state space—directing exploration toward the most informative regions. This approach reduces volume dependence by focusing exploration effort on states that maximize information gain rather than uniformly sampling the state space, improving sample efficiency in sparse-reward settings.

Exploration-Guided Reward Shaping provides another mechanism for improving exploration efficiency under sparse rewards [21]. The method shapes rewards based on exploration progress rather than task completion, providing dense feedback that accelerates learning while preserving the optimal policy of the original task. This approach proves particularly effective when combined with hierarchical structures, as exploration bonuses can be applied at multiple levels of the task decomposition.

Continual learning enables skill transfer across related sub-tasks by maintaining shared representations that capture common structure [4]. This underlying semantic structure allows policies to generalize across task variations without requiring complete retraining, reducing the sample complexity of learning new skills. For trajectory planning architectures, this means that skills learned for one manipulation task can transfer to related tasks with minimal additional training, accelerating deployment in new environments.

### Sim-to-Real Transfer for Physical Deployment

The reality gap between simulation and physical deployment represents the final architectural challenge for safe reinforcement learning in trajectory planning. Domain randomization has evolved from simple parameter perturbation to sophisticated strategies that account for the full distribution of possible real-world conditions.

Early sim-to-real transfer relied heavily on domain randomization, randomizing physical parameters such as joint damping, friction coefficients, and sensor noise during training to produce policies robust to real-world variation [26]. However, recent work demonstrates that naive randomization can produce policies that are robust but suboptimal, requiring more targeted approaches that focus randomization on parameters that most significantly affect task performance.

Cyclic Policy Distillation enables sample-efficient sim-to-real transfer by iteratively refining policies through cycles of simulation training and real-world evaluation [26]. This approach reduces the number of real-world samples required for adaptation while maintaining safety throughout the transfer process. The cyclic structure allows the policy to gradually adapt to real-world dynamics without catastrophic forgetting of skills learned in simulation.

Safe Continual Domain Adaptation addresses the challenge of adapting policies after initial sim-to-real transfer [24]. The approach enables policies to continue learning from real-world experience while maintaining safety constraints throughout the adaptation process. This capability proves essential for long-term deployment where environmental conditions may drift or where the robot encounters novel situations not represented in the training distribution.

Foundation model integration provides semantic feature anchors that remain stable across domains, aiding in observation alignment between simulation and reality [26]. Pretrained vision-language models can extract high-level semantic features that are less sensitive to low-level visual differences between simulated and real environments, reducing the observation space mismatch that complicates sim-to-real transfer.

VTOL-UAV offshore docking operations demonstrate successful sim-to-real policy transfer for safety-critical applications [26]. The approach combines domain randomization with careful validation of safety constraints in the target environment, ensuring that collision avoidance behaviors learned in simulation transfer reliably to physical deployment. Energy and completion-time reductions of approximately 10% after robustification have been reported in autonomous surface vehicles, with sub-centimeter trajectory deviations in tactile or manipulator RL applications [26].

Real scene experiments with collaborative robots validate the practical viability of these transfer approaches [29]. The M2ACD algorithm with NURBS trajectory representation demonstrates successful transfer from simulation to physical robotic arms, achieving the continuity requirements necessary for smooth physical execution. This validation confirms that architectural choices made during policy training directly impact deployment success.

Low-level actuation and motor feedback executed on embedded platforms like ESP32 using micro-ROS enable real-time communication with motor drivers while ensuring precise control [27]. This division between high-level decision-making and low-level execution proves essential for sim-to-real transfer, as it allows the policy to operate at a level of abstraction that is less sensitive to hardware-specific dynamics while maintaining the precise timing required for safe operation.

Domain adaptation frameworks that decouple knowledge learning from policy execution enable more flexible transfer strategies [8]. By separating the representation learning component from the policy optimization component, these frameworks allow the representation to adapt to new domains while preserving the safety constraints learned during initial training. This separation reduces the risk of safety violations during adaptation while enabling continued performance improvement.

The evolution from early domain randomization to sophisticated continual adaptation strategies reflects growing understanding of the sim-to-real challenge [32]. Current approaches recognize that transfer requires not just robust policies but architectural support for safe adaptation, observation alignment, and constraint enforcement throughout the deployment lifecycle. This architectural perspective ensures that safety guarantees established during training remain valid during physical operation, enabling reliable deployment of sparse-reward RL policies in safety-critical trajectory planning applications.

## Comparative Analysis of Recent Methodologies

This section provides a structured comparison of recent safe reinforcement learning methodologies for motion planning under sparse rewards. The analysis examines three critical dimensions: the trade-off between safety guarantees and sparse reward handling, computational complexity for real-time deployment, and generalization capabilities across unseen environments. Evidence from 2021-2025 publications forms the basis of this comparative assessment, with earlier methods cited only as foundational context where necessary.

### Performance Trade-offs: Safety vs. Sparsity Handling

Recent methodologies exhibit distinct positioning along the spectrum of safety strictness versus sparse reward robustness. This trade-off fundamentally shapes method selection for motion planning applications, as approaches optimized for rigorous constraint satisfaction often incur exploration inefficiencies, while methods designed for extreme sparsity may relax safety guarantees during learning.

**Safety-First Approaches**

ActSafe represents the current state-of-the-art for safety-critical exploration, providing formal guarantees under regularity assumptions on constraints and dynamics [17]. The method guarantees safety during the learning process while obtaining a near-optimal policy in finite time, distinguishing it from approaches that only ensure safety after convergence. ActSafe builds on model-based RL advancements, incorporating uncertainty quantification into the exploration strategy to maintain constraint satisfaction throughout training [17]. This approach is particularly suited for applications where any constraint violation during learning is unacceptable, such as robotic manipulation in human-occupied spaces or UAV operations in constrained airspace.

Control Barrier Function (CBF)-based methods occupy a similar position on the safety spectrum but introduce different computational characteristics. CBFs enforce safety through quadratic programming optimization at each control step, creating a safety layer that filters or modifies actions from the learned policy [11]. The distinction between safety constraints (collision avoidance, state limits) and task constraints (energy consumption, time optimality) is critical here: CBFs typically address safety constraints through hard barriers, while task constraints may be handled through soft penalties in the reward function. HOCBF (High-Order CBF) extensions examine complexity trade-offs when dealing with systems with higher relative degrees, approximating non-differentiable safety margin functions with neural networks to ensure differentiability and facilitate integration with learning-based controllers [11].

Constrained Policy Optimization (CPO) and PID Lagrangian methods represent the primal-dual optimization approach to constrained RL. CPO uses a constrained Markov decision process formulation with immediate constraint functions, offering the advantage of not requiring knowledge of the behavior policy used in trajectory generation [14]. PID Lagrangian methods modify the standard Lagrangian primal-dual gradient baseline with integral and derivative terms, finding that small modifications to the baseline approach resolve convergence issues in safety-critical benchmarks [5]. These methods typically enforce constraints in expectation rather than state-wise, which provides weaker safety guarantees than ActSafe or CBFs but enables more efficient exploration in sparse reward settings.

**Sparsity-Handling Approaches**

For environments with extreme reward sparsity, recent methods prioritize exploration efficiency while maintaining acceptable (but not formally guaranteed) safety levels. SIERL (Search-Inspired Exploration in Reinforcement Learning) actively guides exploration by setting sub-goals based on the agent's learning progress [16]. At the beginning of each episode, SIERL chooses a sub-goal from the frontier—the boundary between explored and unexplored state space—steering exploration toward the most informative regions. This mechanism reduces volume dependence, meaning the method's efficiency does not degrade proportionally with state space size, a critical advantage for high-dimensional motion planning problems. Experiments demonstrate that SIERL outperforms dominant baselines in both achieving the main task goal and generalizing to reach arbitrary states in challenging sparse-reward environments [16].

DISCOVER (Directed Sparse-Reward Goal-Conditioned Very Long-Horizon RL) extracts a sense of direction necessary for effective exploration from existing RL algorithms without leveraging prior information [2]. The method addresses the fundamental challenge that sparse rewards provide insufficient signal for policy gradient methods to identify productive action sequences. By constructing intermediate goals that maintain progress toward the ultimate objective, DISCOVER reduces the effective horizon that must be solved through undirected exploration.

Reward Machines and LARMs (Language-Associated Reward Machines) convert sparse rewards into dense, structured learning signals through automata-based reward decomposition [4]. This framework dramatically improves sample efficiency by providing intermediate feedback corresponding to sub-task completion. The underlying semantic structure enables a shared policy to treat related sub-tasks similarly, forming the foundation for skill transfer across tasks. Specifically, results show that the framework scales to diverse environments including grid worlds, complex 3D environments, and robotics with continuous control [4].

### Computational Complexity and Real-Time Feasibility

The computational overhead of safety mechanisms and exploration strategies determines whether methods are suitable for online control policy generation versus offline pathfinding. This distinction is critical for motion planning applications, as online control requires decisions at control frequencies (typically 100-1000 Hz for robotic systems), while offline pathfinding can tolerate longer computation times.

**Safety Layer Overhead**

CBF-based safety filters introduce quadratic programming (QP) optimization at each control step, creating a computational bottleneck that scales with the number of constraints and system dimensions. The QP must be solved online to project unsafe actions onto the safe set, which introduces latency that may be unacceptable for high-frequency control loops. HOCBF extensions compound this issue by requiring higher-order derivatives of the barrier function, increasing both computation time and numerical sensitivity [11]. For systems with relative degree greater than one, the approximation of non-differentiable safety margin functions with neural networks adds forward pass latency to the QP solve time.

ActSafe's model-based approach shifts computational burden from online execution to planning-time computation. The method builds Gaussian process or neural network models of dynamics and constraints, then uses these models for safe exploration planning [17]. This architecture enables the safety verification to occur during the planning phase rather than at control frequency, making it more suitable for systems where planning can be performed at lower frequency than control execution. However, the model learning and uncertainty quantification introduce significant training-time computational costs that may be prohibitive for applications requiring rapid deployment.

CPO and PID Lagrangian methods introduce minimal online computational overhead, as constraint handling occurs through modified policy gradient updates during training rather than online optimization [5][14]. Once trained, the policy executes at the same computational cost as unconstrained methods like PPO. This makes these approaches attractive for real-time applications where control frequency is critical, though the weaker safety guarantees (constraint satisfaction in expectation rather than state-wise) may be insufficient for safety-critical deployments.

**Exploration Mechanism Costs**

SIERL's sub-goal selection mechanism requires maintaining and updating a frontier representation of the explored state space, introducing memory and computation overhead proportional to the complexity of the state space representation [16]. The sub-goal selection process itself involves evaluating learning progress metrics across candidate frontier states, which adds computation at the episode level rather than the step level. This episodic overhead is generally acceptable for motion planning applications where episodes correspond to task attempts rather than control cycles.

DISCOVER's direction extraction mechanism operates on existing RL algorithm outputs, adding minimal computational overhead beyond the base algorithm [2]. The method does not require separate model learning or complex optimization, making it computationally efficient relative to model-based alternatives. However, the effectiveness of the direction signal depends on the quality of the underlying value function estimates, which may require substantial training computation in high-dimensional spaces.

Reward Machines introduce automaton-based computation at each step to determine the current reward machine state and corresponding dense reward signal [4]. For simple task specifications, this overhead is negligible. However, for complex tasks requiring large automata or language model-based reward machine generation (as in ARM-FM approaches), the per-step computation may become significant. The framework's effectiveness in converting sparse rewards to dense signals typically justifies this overhead through improved sample efficiency, reducing total training computation despite increased per-step cost.

**Real-Time Feasibility Assessment**

PPO maintains a balance between training stability and computational efficiency, making it a common baseline for real-time applications [27]. The clipped surrogate objective constrains policy update magnitude without introducing online optimization, enabling control frequencies compatible with robotic systems. When combined with lightweight safety filters or trained with constraint-aware objectives (CPO, PID Lagrangian), PPO-based approaches offer the most straightforward path to real-time deployment.

AstroZero demonstrates integration of RL with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for space trajectory planning, avoiding computational challenges of explicitly expanding continuous decision spaces by integrating local optimization [28]. This hybrid approach leverages MCTS for discrete decision components while using learned policies for continuous control, achieving computational efficiency suitable for trajectory optimization problems where planning time is less constrained than control frequency.

For online control policy generation, methods with minimal online computation (CPO, PID Lagrangian, PPO with safety layers) are preferred. For offline pathfinding, methods with higher planning-time computation but stronger guarantees (ActSafe, CBF-MPC hybrids) become viable. The choice fundamentally depends on whether the application requires the policy to generate actions at control frequency or whether actions can be computed through slower planning processes.

### Generalization Across Environments and Tasks

Zero-shot generalization and adaptability to unseen planning scenarios represent critical capabilities for deploying safe RL methods in real-world environments where training conditions cannot encompass all deployment scenarios. Recent methodologies exhibit varying degrees of generalization capability, with approaches leveraging structured representations and language grounding showing particular promise.

**Zero-Shot Transfer Mechanisms**

LARMs (Language-Associated Reward Machines) achieve zero-shot generalization on unseen tasks by associating language embeddings with reward machine automata states [4]. This architecture enables the framework to generalize across tasks by treating related sub-tasks similarly based on semantic similarity in the language embedding space. The underlying semantic structure forms the foundation for skill transfer, allowing policies trained on a set of tasks to generalize to new tasks with similar sub-goal structures. Experiments demonstrate higher success rates than learning from sparse rewards alone, with the framework scaling to diverse environments including grid worlds, complex 3D environments, and robotics with continuous control [4].

ARM-FM (Automaton-Guided Reward Structures with Foundation Models) extends this approach by using foundation models to automatically generate reward machines from natural language specifications [4]. This capability reduces the manual effort required to specify reward machines for new tasks while maintaining the generalization benefits of the reward machine framework. The empirical evidence demonstrates effectiveness across a diverse suite of environments, suggesting that language-grounded reward structures provide a robust mechanism for task generalization.

SIERL demonstrates generalization capability beyond the primary task objective, achieving strong performance in reaching arbitrary states in the environment [16]. This generalization emerges from the frontier-based exploration strategy, which does not optimize for a specific goal state but rather for comprehensive state space coverage. The method's ability to generalize to arbitrary states suggests applicability to settings where task specifications may change at deployment time.

**Sim-to-Real Transfer**

Deep reinforcement learning methods face significant challenges in sim-to-real transfer due to mismatches in physical dynamics, sensory inputs, and environmental variability [32]. Recent approaches address this gap through robustified controllers, domain randomization, and adaptive mechanisms. VTOL-UAV offshore docking operations demonstrate successful sim-to-real policy transfer for collision avoidance and trajectory planning, validating that safe RL methods can transition from simulation to physical deployment [26].

Safe continual domain adaptation after sim-to-real transfer represents an emerging direction, where policies adapt to real-world conditions while maintaining safety constraints [24]. This approach acknowledges that perfect simulation is unattainable and instead focuses on safe adaptation mechanisms that allow policies to adjust to real-world dynamics without violating safety constraints during the adaptation process.

**Environment and Task Adaptability**

The M2ACD algorithm demonstrates strong generalization ability in the face of new environments or unseen scenes, performing excellently in practical applications for robotic arm grasping tasks [29]. The multi-strategy and multi-value network structure enables the algorithm to satisfy real-time requirements while maintaining adaptability across environmental variations. This suggests that architectural choices in the policy network itself, independent of the reward structure, contribute significantly to generalization capability.

Autotelic RL frameworks emphasize the need for unified approaches supporting open-ended environments, language-guided exploration, and task specification via learned or human-like goal and intrinsic reward representations for broader adaptability [25]. The generalization to open worlds and language represents a critical frontier, as current methods often require task-specific tuning that limits deployment flexibility.

**Generalization Limitations**

Despite progress, significant limitations remain. Methods providing strong safety guarantees (ActSafe, CBFs) often require environment-specific constraint modeling that does not transfer directly to new environments. The regularity assumptions underlying ActSafe's safety guarantees may not hold in substantially different deployment environments, requiring re-verification or conservative constraint tightening. Similarly, CBFs require accurate models of the constraint functions, which may not generalize across environments with different obstacle configurations or dynamics.

Reward machine-based approaches show stronger generalization potential due to their semantic grounding, but require that new tasks can be expressed within the reward machine formalism. Tasks requiring fundamentally different skill sets or environmental interactions may not benefit from transfer without reward machine restructuring. The language grounding in LARMs and ARM-FM mitigates this limitation by enabling automatic reward machine generation, but the quality of generated reward machines depends on foundation model capabilities and may require human verification for safety-critical applications.

The evidence suggests that no single method currently achieves strong performance across all three dimensions of safety, sparsity handling, and generalization. Method selection for motion planning applications requires explicit trade-off analysis based on application requirements, with hybrid approaches combining safety layers (CBFs) with sparsity-handling mechanisms (reward machines, sub-goal exploration) representing a promising direction for future development.

## Future Directions and Strategic Recommendations

The field of safe reinforcement learning for motion planning under sparse rewards stands at a critical inflection point. Recent advances in foundation models, theoretical safety guarantees, and practical implementation frameworks offer promising pathways forward, yet significant challenges remain. This section synthesizes emerging trends, identifies unresolved theoretical gaps, and provides actionable recommendations for practitioners deploying safe RL systems in robotic planning contexts.

### Emerging Trends: LLMs and Foundation Models in RL

The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) and Foundation Models (FMs) into reinforcement learning represents a paradigm shift in how sparse reward problems are approached. Traditional RL methods struggle when reward signals are infrequent or delayed, requiring agents to explore vast state spaces without meaningful feedback. Foundation models address this fundamental limitation by leveraging their pretrained reasoning capabilities to convert sparse rewards into dense, structured learning signals [4]. This transformation dramatically improves sample efficiency across diverse environments, including grid worlds, complex 3D environments, and robotics with continuous control requirements.

A critical mechanism underlying this improvement is the ability of FMs to decompose high-level task descriptions into concrete sub-goals that serve as intermediate reward signals. Recent work on LLM-Driven Intrinsic Motivation for Sparse Reward Reinforcement Learning demonstrates that foundation models can generate intrinsic motivation signals that guide exploration toward states likely to yield task completion [3]. This approach reduces the volume dependence of exploration—the agent no longer needs to randomly traverse the entire state space but instead follows semantically meaningful pathways suggested by the foundation model's understanding of task structure.

Reward Machines (RMs) provide a formal framework for translating abstract task understanding into structured reward signals. RMs function as automata-based formalisms that encode reward functions as state machines, enabling the specification of complex temporal reward structures that would be difficult to express through scalar rewards alone [4]. While Foundation Models excel at interpreting and decomposing tasks from natural language, a critical gap exists in translating this abstract understanding into the concrete structured reward signals necessary for RL. Consequently, high-level plans generated by FMs often fail to produce the dense learning signals required for efficient policy optimization. Recent work addresses this by exploring FM-driven automata that bridge semantic understanding with formal reward specifications.

The two-stage reward (TSR) strategy exemplifies how foundation model insights can be operationalized. In robotic manipulator trajectory planning, the TSR strategy speeds up training and overcomes learning complexity caused by sparse or delayed reward signals by separating task completion rewards from intermediate progress indicators [29]. This mechanistic separation allows the agent to receive frequent feedback about trajectory quality while maintaining the ultimate task objective as a sparse terminal reward.

Exploration-Guided Reward Shaping represents another promising direction where reward functions are dynamically shaped based on the agent's exploration patterns [21]. Rather than using static reward shaping that may introduce unintended optimization pressures, this approach adapts reward signals based on which state-action pairs the agent has visited, ensuring that exploration is systematically guided toward unexplored but potentially valuable regions of the state space.

The VIB-IG framework demonstrates how information-theoretic principles can generate intrinsic rewards without requiring explicit task knowledge. By learning compact and predictive latent representations through the Information Bottleneck principle and defining intrinsic rewards via information gain, VIB-IG improves both solution quality and convergence speed in complex routing problems with capacity constraints and sparse, delayed rewards [19]. The learned mutual information critic provides a pointwise score that can be evaluated at the level of individual transitions, making it well-suited for defining information-gain-based intrinsic rewards.

For practitioners, the strategic implication is clear: foundation models should not be viewed as replacements for RL algorithms but as complementary systems that provide semantic scaffolding for reward specification and exploration guidance. The most effective deployments will combine FM-based task decomposition with traditional RL optimization, using the foundation model to generate dense intermediate rewards while the RL algorithm handles policy optimization under safety constraints.

### Unresolved Challenges in Convergence and Guarantees

Despite significant progress in safe reinforcement learning, substantial gaps remain in theoretical guarantees for constrained sparse environments and continuous action spaces. The fundamental tension between exploration efficiency and safety assurance creates mathematical challenges that existing frameworks have not fully resolved.

ActSafe represents a notable advance in providing formal safety guarantees during the learning process. Under regularity assumptions on the constraints and dynamics, ActSafe guarantees safety during learning while also obtaining a near-optimal policy in finite time [17]. However, the regularity assumptions required—typically Lipschitz continuity of dynamics and constraints—may not hold in many real-world robotic systems where contact dynamics, friction, and actuator saturation introduce discontinuities. A practical variant of ActSafe builds on latest model-based RL advancements, but the theoretical guarantees weaken when model uncertainty exceeds certain bounds.

The distinction between safety constraints and task constraints remains a source of ambiguity in theoretical analysis. Safety constraints (collision avoidance, joint limits, torque bounds) require hard guarantees that violations never occur during learning or deployment. Task constraints (energy consumption, time limits, path smoothness) can tolerate occasional violations in exchange for improved task performance. Current theoretical frameworks often treat both constraint types identically in Constrained Markov Decision Process (CMDP) formulations, obscuring the fundamentally different guarantee requirements. Learning-Based Control in Safety-Critical Systems emphasizes Lyapunov-Guided Reinforcement Learning and Barrier Functions to provide formal guarantees, but the integration of these methods with sparse reward exploration remains underdeveloped [12].

Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) provide safety guarantees through forward invariance—ensuring that once the system state enters a safe set, it cannot leave under the controlled dynamics [11]. However, CBFs require differentiable safety margin functions, which are often unavailable in complex robotic environments. Recent work approximates non-differentiable safety margins with neural networks to ensure differentiability and facilitate integration with CBFs, describing how bounded learning error can be incorporated into the CBF to provide robustness guarantees. The challenge is that approximation errors compound over time, and existing convergence proofs do not adequately account for this accumulation in sparse reward settings where the agent receives limited feedback about constraint violations.

Extreme Value Policy Optimization for Safe Reinforcement Learning addresses safety by leveraging Extreme Value Theory to model tail risks in constraint violations [10]. Constrained Reinforcement Learning (CRL) addresses safety by maximizing reward subject to constraint satisfaction, but standard CRL methods focus on expected constraint satisfaction rather than worst-case guarantees. EVT-based methods provide probabilistic bounds on extreme constraint violations, offering a middle ground between hard guarantees and soft constraints. However, convergence proofs for EVT-based safe RL in continuous action spaces remain incomplete, particularly when the underlying distribution of constraint violations is non-stationary due to policy updates.

Search-Inspired Exploration in Reinforcement Learning (SIERL) actively guides exploration by setting sub-goals based on search heuristics, outperforming dominant baselines in challenging sparse-reward environments [16]. While SIERL demonstrates empirical improvements in both achieving the main task goal and generalizing to reach arbitrary states, theoretical analysis of convergence rates under safety constraints is limited. The sub-goal generation mechanism introduces additional complexity into the convergence analysis, as the effective reward function changes dynamically based on exploration progress.

For continuous action spaces, the function approximation error in actor-critic methods introduces additional convergence challenges. Addressing function approximation error in actor-critic methods remains critical for continuous control, as approximation errors can cause policy oscillations that violate safety constraints even when the nominal policy satisfies them [32]. Existing convergence proofs typically assume tabular settings or linear function approximation, neither of which applies to deep RL methods used in modern robotic planning.

Constrained POMDP planning using learned probabilistic failure surrogates and adaptive safety constraints offers a framework for handling partial observability [15]. However, the convergence guarantees for these methods depend on accurate belief state estimation, which is itself a learning problem in sparse reward environments. The interaction between belief convergence and policy convergence creates coupled dynamics that existing theoretical frameworks do not fully characterize.

The gap between theory and practice is particularly pronounced in multi-agent settings where safety constraints must be satisfied collectively. A general safety framework for learning-based control in uncertain robotic systems provides theoretical foundations, but experimental comparisons reveal that provably safe methods often underperform heuristic approaches in terms of task completion rates [18]. This performance gap suggests that current theoretical guarantees are overly conservative, excluding policies that would be safe in practice but cannot be proven safe under worst-case analysis.

### Recommendations for Trajectory Planning Implementation

Practitioners deploying safe reinforcement learning for robotic trajectory planning must navigate a complex landscape of methodological choices. The following recommendations provide actionable guidance on selecting and combining exploration and safety methods for specific use cases, explicitly distinguishing between offline pathfinding and online control policy generation.

**For Offline Pathfinding Applications:**

Offline trajectory planning, where a complete path is computed before execution, benefits from methods that prioritize global optimality with safety verification. The M2ACD algorithm demonstrates effective DRL-based trajectory planning for robotic manipulators by combining multi-strategy and multi-value network structures [29]. The algorithm transforms the inverse kinematics problem into a general Newton-MP iterative method to improve solution efficiency and accuracy, then applies DRL for trajectory optimization. For offline applications, practitioners should:

- **Use reward shaping with assistant reward agents** for continuous control tasks with sparse and delayed rewards, as this approach demonstrates robustness and superiority over existing methods [20]. The assistant agent provides dense intermediate rewards while the primary policy optimizes for task completion.

- **Implement two-stage reward strategies** that separate trajectory quality metrics from task completion rewards. This approach speeds up training and overcomes learning complexity caused by sparse or delayed reward signals [29].

- **Apply Monte Carlo Tree Search adaptations** for hybrid action spaces. Traditional MCTS only works when decisions are selected from a finite set, but space trajectory optimization involves parameters like thrust magnitude and direction that are continuous [28]. Recent adaptations extend MCTS to continuous spaces through discretization strategies that preserve theoretical guarantees.

- **Leverage foundation models for task decomposition** in complex multi-stage planning problems. Foundation models can decompose high-level mission specifications into sequences of waypoints and constraints that serve as input to traditional trajectory optimization algorithms.

**For Online Control Policy Generation:**

Online policy generation, where the robot continuously computes actions based on sensor feedback, requires methods that guarantee safety during both learning and deployment. The distinction is critical: offline planning can use computationally intensive verification, while online control must satisfy real-time constraints.

- **Deploy ActSafe or similar safe exploration frameworks** when learning must occur in the physical environment. ActSafe guarantees safety during learning while obtaining a near-optimal policy in finite time under regularity assumptions [17]. For applications where regularity assumptions may not hold, use the practical variant that builds on model-based RL advancements with conservative uncertainty estimates.

- **Implement Control Barrier Functions with neural network approximations** for systems where analytical CBFs are unavailable. The neural network approximation ensures differentiability and facilitates integration with learning-based controllers, with bounded learning error incorporated into the CBF to provide robustness [11].

- **Use PPO-Mask or similar constrained policy variants** for sim-to-real transfer. When policies were transferred to robotic platforms, PPO broadly reproduced its simulated trajectory, whereas PPO-Mask behaved more stably and smoothly, avoiding obstacles without abrupt corrections [27]. Both policies reached the goal within acceptable thresholds, but PPO-Mask's stability is critical for physical deployment.

- **Apply trajectory-level SE(3) equivariant diffusion policies** for manipulation tasks requiring geometric consistency. ET-SEED provides efficient trajectory-level SE(3) equivariant diffusion policy that respects the geometric structure of robotic manipulation [8].

**Method Selection by Use Case:**

| Use Case | Recommended Exploration Method | Recommended Safety Method | Rationale |
|----------|-------------------------------|---------------------------|-----------|
| Warehouse robot navigation | Search-Inspired Exploration (SIERL) | Control Barrier Functions | SIERL guides exploration through sub-goals; CBFs provide hard collision avoidance guarantees [16][11] |
| Robotic arm manipulation | Two-Stage Reward + Foundation Model decomposition | Neural CBF with bounded error | TSR handles sparse task rewards; neural CBF accommodates complex kinematics [29][11] |
| UAV trajectory planning | MCTS with continuous action adaptation | Extreme Value Policy Optimization | MCTS handles hybrid discrete-continuous actions; EVT provides probabilistic safety bounds [28][10] |
| Sim-to-real transfer | PPO-Mask with domain randomization | Lyapunov-guided constraints | PPO-Mask provides stable transfer; Lyapunov functions ensure stability margins [27][12] |
| Multi-robot coordination | Intrinsic motivation with information gain | Constrained POMDP with failure surrogates | Information gain encourages synergistic exploration; POMDP handles partial observability [19][15] |

**Implementation Priorities:**

1. **Distinguish safety from task constraints explicitly** in system design. Safety constraints (collision avoidance, joint limits) require hard guarantees enforced through CBFs or shield mechanisms. Task constraints (energy, time) can be incorporated into the reward function with penalty terms. This distinction prevents over-constraining the optimization problem while maintaining critical safety assurances.

2. **Prioritize methods with mechanistic explanations** for exploration efficiency improvements. For example, VIB-IG improves sample efficiency by reducing volume dependence through information-theoretic exploration bonuses, not merely by adding heuristic rewards [19]. Understanding the mechanism enables practitioners to diagnose failures and adapt methods to new domains.

3. **Validate sim-to-real transfer with robustified controllers**. Sim-to-real transfer learning using robustified controllers in robotic tasks involving complex dynamics demonstrates that policies trained with domain randomization and robustness constraints transfer more reliably than nominal policies [32].

4. **Implement online verification for deployed systems**. Online verification of multiple safety criteria for robot trajectories provides runtime assurance that planned trajectories satisfy all constraints before execution [18]. This verification layer acts as a final safety check independent of the learning algorithm.

5. **Plan for continual learning with safety preservation**. Continual learning methods must preserve safety guarantees across task sequences. Prevalence of negative transfer in continual reinforcement learning indicates that naive task sequencing can degrade both performance and safety [8]. Implement task isolation mechanisms or safety buffers when deploying continual learning systems.

The strategic recommendation for practitioners is to adopt a layered safety architecture: foundation models for semantic task understanding and reward shaping, constrained RL algorithms for policy optimization under safety constraints, and runtime verification systems for final safety assurance. This layered approach acknowledges that no single method provides complete safety guarantees while leveraging the strengths of each component.
